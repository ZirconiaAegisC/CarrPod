# 01 — TECHNOLOGY FUNDAMENTALS: PEROVSKITE-SILICON TANDEM ARCHITECTURE

## Executive Summary

Perovskite-silicon tandem photovoltaic technology represents the most consequential advance in commercial solar since the transition from multicrystalline to monocrystalline silicon. Oxford PV, founded in 2010 by Professor Henry Snaith at the University of Oxford, has established the foundational intellectual property, manufacturing capability, and commercial pathway to bring this technology to market. The tandem architecture stacks a thin-film metal-halide perovskite top cell atop a conventional crystalline silicon bottom cell, enabling single-junction efficiency limits to be surpassed without requiring fundamental redesign of established silicon manufacturing infrastructure.

## Physical Principles

The tandem configuration exploits the complementary bandgap characteristics of the two absorber materials. Silicon, with a bandgap of approximately 1.12 eV, efficiently converts near-infrared and red photons but leaves substantial energy unharvested in the blue-green portion of the spectrum. The perovskite top cell, engineered with a tunable bandgap typically in the range of 1.60–1.75 eV through compositional engineering of the halide and cation constituents, captures these higher-energy photons with reduced thermalization losses.

The two sub-cells are connected electrically in series through a recombination junction or tunnel junction. Photocurrent matching between the top and bottom cells is essential for maximizing tandem efficiency. The perovskite layer thickness, typically 400–600 nm, is optimized to balance absorption in the top cell against transmission to the silicon bottom cell. Optical management through textured interfaces, anti-reflection coatings, and index-matched interlayers further enhances photon utilization across the 300–1200 nm wavelength range.

## Perovskite Material System

Oxford PV's proprietary perovskite composition is a multi-cation, mixed-halide formulation based on the general stoichiometry (Cs,FA,MA)Pb(I,Br)₃, where cesium, formamidinium, and methylammonium cations occupy the A-site of the ABX₃ perovskite crystal lattice. The precise composition is a closely guarded trade secret protected by over 400 granted patents. Key performance attributes of the Oxford PV formulation include:

**Bandgap Tunability**: The iodide-to-bromide ratio in the halide sublattice governs the optical bandgap. Oxford PV has demonstrated precise bandgap control within ±0.02 eV across full-scale deposition runs, achieved through advanced precursor chemistry and in-line optical monitoring.

**Phase Stability**: The multi-cation approach suppresses the undesirable tetragonal-to-hexagonal phase transition that plagues pure FAPbI₃ compositions. Cesium incorporation at the A-site stabilizes the photoactive black phase, while methylammonium provides crystallization kinetics favorable to large-grain, low-defect films.

**Defect Tolerance**: Metal-halide perovskites exhibit intrinsic defect tolerance owing to their electronic band structure, wherein dominant point defects form shallow rather than deep trap states. This property enables high open-circuit voltages despite solution-processed or vacuum-deposited fabrication routes that would be incompatible with traditional III-V semiconductors.

## Silicon Bottom Cell Compatibility

A critical strategic advantage of Oxford PV's tandem approach is its compatibility with existing high-efficiency silicon cell technologies. The company has demonstrated tandem integration with both heterojunction (HJT) and tunnel oxide passivated contact (TOPCon) architectures. This compatibility is commercially decisive: it allows Oxford PV to partner with established silicon cell manufacturers rather than competing against them, reducing capital requirements and accelerating market adoption.

The perovskite deposition process is designed as a post-processing step applied to finished silicon cells, with minimal modification to the silicon production line. The transparent conductive oxide layers that form the top contact of the tandem stack are deposited by physical vapor deposition methods already standard in HJT manufacturing, further reducing integration complexity.

## Competitive Landscape

Oxford PV occupies a unique position as the only company to have shipped commercial perovskite-silicon tandem modules. Competitors in the tandem space include LONGi Green Energy (China), Hanwha Qcells (South Korea), and several early-stage ventures. However, Oxford PV's patent portfolio, manufacturing head-start, and exclusive licensing relationships provide durable competitive moats. The company's strategy of licensing rather than competing on silicon cell production further differentiates it from vertically integrated Asian manufacturers.

## Historical Context and Founding Narrative

Oxford PV was incorporated in December 2010 by Professor Henry Snaith, Dr. Kevin Arthur, and Dr. Christopher Case, with seed funding from the University of Oxford's technology transfer office (Oxford University Innovation) and angel investors including Dr. John Mills and Dr. Michael Birch. The company's founding thesis was that metal-halide perovskites — a class of materials first explored for photovoltaic applications by Tsutomu Miyasaka at Toin University of Yokohama in 2009 — could be engineered into a commercially viable thin-film photovoltaic technology that would surpass the cost-performance frontier of crystalline silicon.

The early years (2010–2014) focused on fundamental perovskite materials science: compositional engineering of the ABX₃ lattice to improve stability and optoelectronic quality, development of solution-based and vacuum-based deposition processes suitable for industrial scale-up, and demonstration of perovskite cell efficiencies progressing from single digits to above 15%. The decision to pursue a tandem architecture — rather than a standalone perovskite thin-film module — was crystallized in 2014, driven by the recognition that pairing perovskite with silicon offered a faster path to commercial deployment than attempting to displace silicon entirely.

Key personnel were recruited throughout the 2014–2018 period to build the company's technical and commercial capabilities. Dr. Ed Crossland joined from Oxford's Department of Materials to lead the technology development team. Dr. Chris Case, with a background in semiconductor capital equipment, provided early strategic guidance on manufacturing scale-up. The engineering team expanded substantially with the opening of the Oxford R&D center in 2015 (initially 30 personnel, now approximately 120) and the establishment of Oxford PV Germany GmbH following the Brandenburg facility acquisition.

The company's fundraising history reflects the capital intensity of bringing a new photovoltaic technology to market. Seed and Series A rounds (2010–2014) raised approximately GBP 20 million from angel investors and early-stage venture capital. Series B (2015, GBP 30 million) and Series C (2018, GBP 65 million) rounds brought in institutional investors including Legal & General Capital, Equinor, and Goldwind. The Series D round (2021, EUR 110 million) was led by Reliance Industries with participation from existing investors. The Series E round (early 2025, GBP 80 million) was led by a consortium of European institutional investors and sovereign wealth funds, bringing total equity raised to over GBP 250 million.

## Regulatory and Policy Landscape

The commercialization of lead-containing perovskite photovoltaics operates within an evolving regulatory framework. The European Union's Restriction of Hazardous Substances (RoHS) Directive currently exempts photovoltaic modules from its restrictions on lead in electronic equipment, but this exemption is subject to periodic review. Oxford PV actively engages with the European Commission's regulatory process through industry associations, presenting data on the life-cycle lead inventory, encapsulation effectiveness, and end-of-life recycling pathways for tandem modules.

The lead content of a typical Oxford PV tandem module (approximately 0.8 g/m² of lead in the perovskite layer) is lower than the lead content of the solder used in the module's junction box and interconnect ribbons (approximately 15–20 g/module for cells interconnected with SnPbAg-coated ribbon). An end-of-life recycling process developed in partnership with ROSI Solar (France) recovers over 99% of the perovskite lead content through a selective delamination and chemical leaching process, with the recovered lead iodide re-purified to semiconductor-grade quality for re-use in new perovskite precursor synthesis. This closed-loop lead management pathway addresses a key environmental concern and provides Oxford PV with a defensible position in RoHS exemption renewal proceedings.

In the United Kingdom, Oxford PV benefits from the UK government's commitment to net-zero emissions by 2050 and the associated policy support for domestic clean energy technology development. The UK Infrastructure Bank has expressed interest in providing debt financing for an eventual UK-based tandem module manufacturing facility, though no timeline for such a facility has been announced.

## Technology Transfer and Knowledge Management

Oxford PV's knowledge management system is structured to preserve and systematically build upon the company's accumulated technical expertise. The perovskite deposition process alone involves over 200 discrete process parameters that interact in complex, partially characterized ways. A centralized knowledge repository captures process recipes, metrology data, failure analysis reports, and corrective action records, indexed against specific process steps, material batches, and equipment configurations. This repository is the foundation for both continuous process improvement at Brandenburg and the technology transfer packages delivered to licensees.

The company's Oxford R&D center maintains a capability gradient from fundamental materials research (combinatorial perovskite synthesis, advanced characterization, durability science) through process development (pilot-scale deposition, accelerated reliability testing) to product integration (module design, field testing). This gradient enables the efficient translation of laboratory discoveries into manufacturing process improvements, with a typical laboratory-to-production transfer timeline of 12–18 months for a new perovskite composition or process innovation.
