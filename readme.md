## The Quick Pitch

Carrington Storm Motors is the only research and product development enterprise on Earth fully dedicated to one electrifyingly simple problem. The Sun is going to throw a magnetic tantrum. A coronal mass ejection that rattles Earth's magnetic field, induces runaway current in every power line and transformer winding on the continent, and collapses the electrical grid for years. Not hours, not days. Years. This repository contains everything we have built to understand that threat, engineer real dielectric protections against it at every scale from the atomic to the architectural, price the risk for the insurance industry that will have to pay for it, and communicate the urgency to two hundred governments in language any tenth grader can absorb. We make ships that walk onto beaches when the ports go dark. We make robots that function inside an electromagnetic hurricane. We make paint whose color was chosen not by a designer but by a physicist calculating complex permittivity at geomagnetic frequencies. And somewhere in the SiblingFrequency radio archives, there is an episode where Director Steele — standing on the bridge of a Charlemagne vessel as the aurora paints the sky in colors that would have made Richard Carrington drop his sketchbook — says into the protonic backup channel: "This is what we built it for." That line, right there, is the entire enterprise in nine words. Thanks to Robin Williams, whose Mork from Ork taught a generation that the strangest visitor can deliver the most important message. Thanks to the writers of Mork and Mindy, who gave us nanu nanu and the permission to be both silly and serious in the same breath. Thanks to Ashton Kutcher and the creators of That 70s Show and Dude Where's My Car, who proved that the smartest person in the room is sometimes the one who seems the most relaxed. We stand on the shoulders of storytellers. We are building the shield they never knew we would need. The door is open. Step through.

Richard Carrington — a British astronomer with excellent sideburns and an even better telescope — sketched the sunspot group that caused it. He connected the flare to the magnetic disturbance. He created the field of space weather. Power lines. Pipelines. Railway tracks. Bridge girders. The copper windings inside the transformers that make everything from your refrigerator to your hospital ventilator work. CHESTER: Sweet. So the old way of thinking about electromagnetic protection is the Faraday cage. You build a conductive box around the thing you want to protect, and the current goes around the box instead of through it. Works great. Michael Faraday was a genius. The man discovered electromagnetic induction, for which we are all grateful even though it is also the thing that makes the Carrington event dangerous — but that is not his fault. The problem with Faraday cages at civilization scale is that they are heavy, expensive, and impractical for retrofitting every house and every car and every bridge on the planet. So our way — the Dielectric Citadel Protocol — asks a different question. What if the thing you want to protect was simply not made of materials that conduct electricity in the first place?
