## 1. STRATEGIC OVERVIEW

### 1.1 American Manufacturing Commitment

Apple's commitment to United States-based manufacturing and infrastructure investment totals $600 billion over a multi-year period spanning 2018 through 2028. This commitment encompasses direct capital expenditure on Apple-owned manufacturing facilities, long-term supplier purchase agreements with United States-based manufacturers, investments in workforce development and training, and Advanced Manufacturing Fund disbursements to domestic technology partners. The program, designated the American Manufacturing Program internally, addresses multiple strategic objectives: supply chain geographic diversification, tariff and trade policy risk mitigation, logistics cost reduction for the North American market (Apple's largest revenue region), and alignment with federal policy incentives under the CHIPS and Science Act of 2022 and related legislation.

The $600 billion commitment is distributed across semiconductor fabrication, advanced glass manufacturing, printed circuit board and substrate fabrication, final assembly and test operations, data center infrastructure build-out, and corporate facilities expansion.

### 1.2 Semiconductor Supply Chain

**Broadcom Partnership.** Apple and Broadcom Inc. have executed a multi-year component supply and manufacturing agreement with an aggregate value of approximately $30 billion. The agreement encompasses the design, development, and supply of 15 billion-plus individual semiconductor components manufactured at Broadcom's United States fabrication and assembly facilities, including the Fort Collins, Colorado, facility — which is undergoing a $1.5 billion capacity expansion supported by the CHIPS Act and Apple's Advanced Manufacturing Fund. Components supplied under this agreement include radio frequency (RF) front-end modules, wireless connectivity chipsets (Wi-Fi, Bluetooth), and timing and power management integrated circuits critical to iPhone, iPad, Mac, Apple Watch, and Apple Vision Pro product lines.

**TSMC Arizona.** Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company (TSMC) is constructing a multi-phase advanced semiconductor fabrication campus in Phoenix, Arizona, representing the largest foreign direct investment in Arizona history and one of the largest in United States history. Phase 1 (Fab 21, Module 1) commenced high-volume manufacturing in early 2025 at the N4 (4-nanometer class) process node, with Phase 2 (Fab 21, Module 2) progressing toward 3-nanometer class production. Phase 3 (Fab 21, Module 3) is planned for 2-nanometer class production later in the decade. Total investment across all phases exceeds $65 billion. Apple has secured a substantial allocation of TSMC Arizona's advanced-node capacity through a direct wafer supply agreement, with cumulative wafer output for Apple products projected to exceed 100 million individual semiconductor die by year-end 2026. This represents the first instance of leading-edge logic semiconductor fabrication for Apple products occurring on United States soil.

**GlobalWafers.** GlobalWafers Co., Ltd., has commenced construction of a $4 billion, 300-millimeter silicon wafer manufacturing facility in Sherman, Texas. The facility will produce polished silicon wafers and epitaxial wafers for advanced logic and memory semiconductor manufacturing. Apple has facilitated this investment through long-term wafer supply commitments and coordination with the United States Department of Commerce CHIPS Program Office.

**Amkor Technology.** Amkor Technology, Inc., is constructing a $7 billion advanced semiconductor packaging and test facility in Peoria, Arizona, co-located in the Greater Phoenix semiconductor ecosystem with TSMC's fabrication campus. The facility will provide advanced packaging services — including flip-chip ball grid array (FC-BGA), wafer-level chip-scale packaging (WLCSP), and system-in-package (SiP) assembly — for Apple's United States-manufactured semiconductor content. Co-location of fabrication (TSMC) and packaging (Amkor) within 30 miles reduces logistics complexity, transportation-related carbon emissions, and supply chain cycle time compared to the historical model of wafer fabrication in Taiwan followed by packaging and test in Southeast Asia.

### 1.3 Advanced Manufacturing Partnerships

**Corning Incorporated.** Apple's cumulative Advanced Manufacturing Fund commitment to Corning totals $2.5 billion, supporting the Harrodsburg, Kentucky, glass manufacturing facility — the world's largest dedicated smartphone glass production line. Corning's Harrodsburg facility produces Ceramic Shield glass-ceramic for iPhone, with furnace capacity exceeding 100,000 metric tons of specialty glass per annum operating on a continuous 24/365 production schedule.

**Houston Server and Mac mini Facility.** Apple has established a 250,000-square-foot server and Mac mini manufacturing facility in Houston, Texas. The facility manufactures Apple-designed server hardware utilizing M-series silicon for Apple Intelligence cloud infrastructure, Apple data center operations, and internal enterprise computing requirements. The facility also produces Mac mini desktop computers for the North American market. The Houston facility's co-location with the United States energy capital provides access to industrial electrical infrastructure, skilled manufacturing labor, and logistics connectivity through the Port of Houston and interstate highway and rail networks.

**Apple Manufacturing Academy — Detroit.** Apple has established the Apple Manufacturing Academy in Detroit, Michigan, in partnership with Michigan State University, Macomb Community College, and local workforce development organizations. The Academy provides tuition-free training in advanced manufacturing skills including CNC machining, robotics operation and programming, statistical process control, metrology and quality assurance, and industrial automation systems. To date, over 130 small and medium-sized manufacturing enterprises (SMEs) have participated in training programs delivered through the Academy, with participating companies reporting measurable improvements in manufacturing yield, equipment utilization, and employee retention.

## 2. SUPPLIER RESPONSIBILITY AND COMPLIANCE

### 2.1 Supplier Code of Conduct

Apple's Supplier Code of Conduct establishes mandatory requirements across more than 500 individual criteria spanning labor and human rights, health and safety, environmental protection, ethics, and management systems. The Code applies to all suppliers of goods and services to Apple and to all supplier facilities involved in the production of Apple products, components, or materials, including sub-tier suppliers. Key provisions include:

- **Labor and Human Rights:** Prohibition of forced labor, child labor, and human trafficking; maximum 60-hour work week (including overtime) except in emergencies or unusual circumstances, with at least one day off per seven-day work week; freedom of association and collective bargaining rights; non-discrimination and prohibition of harassment.
- **Health and Safety:** Occupational health and safety management systems conforming to ISO 45001 or equivalent; machine safeguarding, lockout/tagout, and electrical safety programs; industrial hygiene monitoring and control for chemical, physical, and biological hazards; emergency preparedness and response planning; worker dormitory and canteen standards meeting or exceeding local regulatory requirements.
- **Environmental Protection:** Environmental management systems conforming to ISO 14001 or equivalent; hazardous substance management and phase-out in accordance with Apple's Regulated Substances Specification; greenhouse gas emissions inventory and reduction target setting; water stewardship and wastewater treatment; waste minimization, segregation, and responsible disposal.
- **Ethics:** Anti-corruption and anti-bribery compliance with the United States Foreign Corrupt Practices Act (FCPA), United Kingdom Bribery Act, and applicable local laws; prohibition of facilitation payments; protection of Apple intellectual property and confidential information; responsible sourcing of minerals (conflict minerals due diligence per OECD Due Diligence Guidance for Responsible Supply Chains of Minerals from Conflict-Affected and High-Risk Areas); supplier diversity and inclusion.

### 2.2 Assessment and Audit Program

Apple's supplier assessment program is one of the most extensive in the electronics industry. In fiscal year 2025, Apple conducted 579 on-site assessments of supplier facilities across more than 50 countries, encompassing first-tier final assembly suppliers, component and material suppliers, and sub-tier suppliers identified through risk-based selection criteria. Assessments are conducted by Apple's internal Supplier Responsibility team — a dedicated organization of auditors, engineers, and subject matter experts — and are unannounced where legally permissible.

In addition to facility-level assessments, Apple conducted 10,300 individual worker interviews in 2025, conducted confidentially and without management presence, to gather direct worker perspectives on working conditions, compensation, working hours, and treatment. Worker interview findings inform corrective action requirements and contribute to broader programmatic improvements.

### 2.3 Corrective Action and Accountability

Suppliers found to be in non-conformance with the Supplier Code of Conduct are required to implement corrective action plans with defined timelines, root cause analysis, and verification of effectiveness. Apple provides technical assistance — at no cost to the supplier — to support corrective action implementation, including engineering support for health and safety improvements, environmental management system development, and human resources policy development. Suppliers that fail to implement corrective actions or that commit severe violations (forced labor, falsification of records, deliberate environmental violations) are subject to termination of their Apple business relationship.

### 2.4 Zero Waste Program

Over 400 supplier facilities participate in Apple's Zero Waste Program, which requires facilities to divert 100% of non-hazardous waste from landfill through waste reduction, reuse, recycling, composting, and waste-to-energy pathways. Program participation requires: baseline waste characterization study, waste reduction target setting, implementation of source reduction and segregation programs, third-party verification of diversion rates, and annual reporting of waste generation and diversion data. Participating facilities have cumulatively diverted more than 3 million metric tons of waste from landfill since the program's inception.

### 2.5 Clean Water Program

Apple's Clean Water Program requires supplier facilities to implement water stewardship practices including water use monitoring and metering, water reuse and recycling maximization, wastewater treatment to standards exceeding local regulatory requirements, and Alliance for Water Stewardship (AWS) standard certification for facilities in water-stressed regions. As of 2025, 32 supplier facilities have achieved AWS certification, with additional facilities in the certification pipeline.

## 3. SUPPLY CHAIN GEOGRAPHIC RESILIENCE

### 3.1 Multi-Region Sourcing Strategy

Apple's supply chain resilience strategy employs multi-region sourcing for critical components and materials to mitigate single-point-of-failure risks arising from geopolitical disruption, natural disaster, pandemic-related manufacturing interruption, trade policy changes, and logistics network disruption. Critical semiconductor components are sourced from fabrication facilities in Taiwan, Arizona, South Korea, and Singapore. Display panels are sourced from South Korea, Japan, China, and (for micro-OLED displays used in Apple Vision Pro) from Sony's Japan facilities and TSMC's Taiwan back-end-of-line facilities. Battery cells are sourced from China, South Korea, and India. Final assembly is executed in China, India, Vietnam, and Brazil, with additional capacity under development.

### 3.2 Logistics Network Redundancy

Apple maintains multiple logistics pathways for product distribution from manufacturing sites to regional distribution centers to retail and direct-to-consumer fulfillment. Air freight capacity is secured through long-term contracts with multiple cargo carriers. Ocean freight employs multiple shipping lines and multiple port-of-entry options on each continental coast. Regional distribution centers in the United States (California, Pennsylvania, Texas), Europe (Netherlands, Czech Republic), and Asia-Pacific (Singapore, China, Japan, Australia) provide inventory buffering and regional fulfillment capability.

### 3.3 Strategic Inventory Management

Apple maintains strategic inventory buffers of critical components — particularly custom Apple-designed silicon (A-series, M-series, S-series, H-series, U-series, R-series SoCs) — to provide production continuity during supply disruption events. Inventory levels are determined through advanced modeling methodologies balancing the cost of carrying inventory against the revenue-at-risk from production interruption, with higher buffer levels for single-sourced components with long manufacturing lead times.

## 4. WORKFORCE DEVELOPMENT

### 4.1 Apple Manufacturing Academy

The Detroit-based Apple Manufacturing Academy provides free training to manufacturing workers and small-to-medium enterprises across the United States, with curriculum covering CNC machining, industrial robotics, quality assurance and metrology, automation systems, and manufacturing management. Over 130 SMEs have participated in training programs since inception.

### 4.2 Community College Partnerships

Apple has established partnerships with community college systems in Arizona, Texas, Ohio, Indiana, and additional states to develop associate-degree and certificate programs in semiconductor manufacturing, advanced manufacturing, and industrial maintenance. These programs align curriculum with Apple and Apple supplier workforce needs, creating talent pipelines for TSMC Arizona, Amkor Peoria, the Houston manufacturing facility, and supplier facilities.

### 4.3 Apprenticeship Programs

Apple supports registered apprenticeship programs in partnership with the United States Department of Labor and state apprenticeship agencies, providing on-the-job training and related technical instruction in manufacturing, equipment maintenance, and quality assurance occupations. Apprentices earn progressive wages and industry-recognized credentials.

## 5. INFRASTRUCTURE PROTECTION

### 5.1 Physical Security

Apple manufacturing and data center facilities employ multi-layered physical security systems including: perimeter intrusion detection (fiber-optic fence-line sensing, ground-based radar, thermal imaging), access control (biometric and multi-factor authentication at all entry points), 24/7 on-site security operations centers with video surveillance coverage, and supply chain custody tracking for high-value and security-sensitive components.

### 5.2 Cybersecurity for Manufacturing

Apple supplier manufacturing facilities are required to implement cybersecurity controls conforming to NIST SP 800-171 (Protecting Controlled Unclassified Information in Nonfederal Systems and Organizations) or equivalent standards, including network segmentation between manufacturing operational technology (OT) and enterprise IT networks, endpoint detection and response, security information and event management (SIEM), vulnerability management, incident response planning, and annual third-party penetration testing.

### 5.3 Intellectual Property Protection

Apple's custom silicon designs, industrial design specifications, manufacturing process parameters, and proprietary materials formulations are protected through a combination of: legal instruments (patents, trade secrets, non-disclosure agreements), physical security controls at manufacturing facilities, digital rights management for technical data packages shared with suppliers, supply chain segmentation (no single supplier has access to a complete product design), and forensic marking of prototype and pre-production components to trace unauthorized disclosures.

## 6. TRADE POLICY AND GEOPOLITICAL RISK MANAGEMENT

### 6.1 Tariff Mitigation Strategy

Apple's supply chain resilience program includes proactive assessment and mitigation of trade policy risks, including tariff actions under Section 301 of the Trade Act of 1974, Section 232 national security tariffs, and reciprocal tariff frameworks. Mitigation strategies include: geographic diversification of final assembly and component manufacturing across multiple customs territories, utilization of free trade agreement (FTA) and preferential trade arrangement duty preferences where applicable, bond and duty-drawback programs for temporary import and re-export manufacturing flows, and engagement with United States Trade Representative (USTR) and Department of Commerce through industry associations regarding tariff exclusion request processes.

### 6.2 Export Control Compliance

Apple's semiconductor and technology supply chain operates within the framework of United States export control regulations administered by the Bureau of Industry and Security (BIS) under the Export Administration Regulations (EAR). Compliance obligations include: entity list screening for all supply chain partners, technology transfer authorization for advanced semiconductor manufacturing process information shared with international suppliers, deemed export compliance for foreign national employees accessing controlled technology within United States facilities, and geographic sourcing restrictions for semiconductor fabrication equipment and electronic design automation (EDA) software tools subject to multilateral export control regimes.

### 6.3 Forced Labor Prevention

Apple's forced labor prevention program encompasses: mandatory supply chain mapping to the smelter and mine level for tin, tantalum, tungsten, gold, cobalt, lithium, rare earth elements, and mica; conflict minerals reporting per SEC Rule 13p-1 (Section 1502 of the Dodd-Frank Wall Street Reform and Consumer Protection Act); supplier due diligence audits for indicators of forced labor per International Labour Organization (ILO) Forced Labour Indicators; worker-paid recruitment fee reimbursement program (Apple requires suppliers to reimburse workers for any recruitment fees paid, with over $35 million in fees reimbursed to date); and participation in the Responsible Minerals Initiative (RMI), the Responsible Labor Initiative (RLI), and the Global Battery Alliance (GBA) for cross-industry collaboration on forced labor elimination.
