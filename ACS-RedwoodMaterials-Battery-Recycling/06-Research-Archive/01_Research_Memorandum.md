## 12.1 US Battery Supply Chain: Strategic Vulnerability Assessment

The United States currently produces less than 1% of global lithium, less than 1% of global cobalt, and approximately 0.5% of global nickel sulfate — the three metals that collectively constitute the cathode active material in every commercial lithium-ion battery chemistry. China controls an estimated 60–70% of global lithium refining, 80% of cobalt refining, and over 90% of manganese sulfate production. This concentration creates a structural dependency that the Department of Defense, Department of Energy, and multiple bipartisan congressional reports have characterized as a national security vulnerability.

Redwood Materials represents the most operationally advanced domestic initiative to address this dependency. The company's hydrometallurgical refining capacity, once scaled to 500 GWh of CAM production, would supply sufficient lithium, nickel, and cobalt to equip approximately five million electric vehicles annually from domestically recycled feedstock.

## 12.2 End-of-Life Battery Volume Projections

Battery end-of-life volumes are projected to grow exponentially through 2040 as the first generation of mass-market electric vehicles reaches decommissioning age. Key projection parameters:

| Year | US EOL EV Batteries (GWh) | Global EOL EV Batteries (GWh) |
|------|---------------------------|-------------------------------|
| 2025 | ~15 GWh | ~50 GWh |
| 2030 | ~80 GWh | ~300 GWh |
| 2035 | ~300 GWh | ~1,000 GWh |
| 2040 | ~600 GWh | ~2,000 GWh |

At Redwood's projected 500 GWh recycling capacity by 2030, the company would have sufficient domestic feedstock to operate at full utilization through at least 2035. The addition of manufacturing scrap (5–15% of annual cell production) provides an additional feedstock stream that grows in proportion to US gigafactory output.

## 12.3 Second-Life Battery Storage Market: 2026–2033

The global second-life battery storage market is projected to reach $62.7 billion by 2033, expanding at a 28.6% compound annual growth rate from a 2025 base of approximately $8 billion. North America is projected to represent 35–40% of the addressable market, driven by:

- Rapid renewable energy penetration (wind + solar projected to represent 40–50% of US generation by 2030)
- Federal Investment Tax Credit (ITC) for standalone energy storage (30% through 2032, phasing to 10% by 2035)
- State-level storage mandates (California 12 GW by 2030, New York 6 GW by 2030)
- Data center power demand growth (projected 35 GW of new data center capacity additions in the US by 2030)
- Aging thermal generation retirements (50+ GW of coal and gas plant retirements forecast by 2030)

Second-life batteries offer a 30–50% cost reduction relative to new-build lithium-ion storage systems on a per-kWh basis, making them economically compelling for applications where energy density is not the binding constraint (stationary storage vs. vehicle propulsion).

## 12.4 IRA Policy Analysis: Material Provisions Affecting Redwood

| Provision | Section | Relevance to Redwood |
|-----------|---------|----------------------|
| Advanced Manufacturing Production Credit | 45X | 10% of production cost credit for CAM produced in US; direct-payable |
| Consumer EV Credit — Domestic Content | 30D | Requires increasing % of battery critical minerals from US/FTA countries for vehicle to qualify; drives OEM demand for domestic CAM |
| DOE ATVM Loan Expansion | 50143 | $3B additional credit subsidy; eliminates $25B cap |
| DOE Battery Material Processing Grants | 40207 | $3B in grants for domestic battery material processing; Redwood eligible |
| Standalone Storage ITC | 48(a) | 30% ITC for energy storage projects; supports Redwood Energy project economics |

The IRA creates a regulatory moat: competitor recycled materials imported from China do not qualify for 45X credits or contribute to OEM Section 30D compliance, creating a structural cost advantage for domestically produced CAM estimated at $35–$45 per kWh of downstream cell production.

## 12.5 Competitive Intelligence: Ascend Elements Bankruptcy

Ascend Elements, Inc., a Westborough, Massachusetts-based battery recycling and cathode precursor company, filed for Chapter 11 bankruptcy protection in April 2026. Ascend Elements had raised approximately $1.2 billion in private capital, including a $542 million Series D in 2024, and was constructing a cathode precursor facility in Hopkinsville, Kentucky. The bankruptcy filing cited construction cost overruns, delays in DOE grant disbursement, and working capital constraints. Implications for Redwood:

- **Market concentration:** Redwood is now the sole vertically integrated battery recycler and CAM producer at commercial scale in North America.
- **Talent acquisition:** Ascend's technical workforce — including process engineers and metallurgists with hydrometallurgical experience — represents a pool of specialized talent.
- **Customer consolidation:** OEM partners previously evaluating dual-supplier strategies may now have reduced alternative supply options, strengthening Redwood's commercial negotiating position.
- **Asset acquisition opportunity:** Ascend's Hopkinsville facility and equipment may become available through bankruptcy asset sales at a discount to replacement cost.

## 12.6 Workforce Restructuring Context

The April 2026 reduction of 135 positions (approximately 10% of workforce) followed a prior reduction of 5% in November 2025. These actions are consistent with a growth-stage company transitioning from R&D-intensive operations to commercial production. The restructurings are understood to reflect reallocation toward energy storage deployment and CAM production scaling rather than financial distress — the company closed a $425 million equity round three months prior to the April 2026 layoffs.
