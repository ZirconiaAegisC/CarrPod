## I. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Carrington Storm Motors occupies a unique position in the insurance landscape: we are simultaneously an insured (we need insurance for our own operations, products, and people) and an insurance-industry partner (our products reduce insurance losses, and we work with carriers to create premium reduction frameworks). This dual role creates unusual complexity in CSM's own risk management.

This document outlines: (a) the insurance coverage CSM needs and the procurement strategy, (b) the self-insurance considerations where commercial insurance is unavailable or uneconomical, (c) the meta-risk of CSM's unique position, and (d) the relationship management with the insurance industry as both customer and insured.

Under the Williams Heuristic: we sell protection against an event that could bankrupt insurance companies. We also buy insurance from those same companies. This creates an interesting set of conversations.

### B. Insurance Procurement Strategy

**Broker Selection:** CSM will engage a large commercial insurance broker (Marsh, Aon, or Willis Towers Watson) to place its insurance program. The broker's market access, risk management services, and claims advocacy are valuable for a company with CSM's unusual risk profile. Selection by Q4 2026 (immediately post-Series A), with coverage bound by Q1 2027.

**Carrier Selection:** CSM will seek coverage from carriers with:
- Experience insuring advanced manufacturing and technology companies
- Financial strength rating of A or better (A.M. Best)
- Willingness to understand CSM's unique risk profile rather than applying generic manufacturing rates
- Potential interest in CSM's products as insureds themselves (creating a relationship that extends beyond CSM as a policyholder)

**Coverage Gaps and Exclusions:** CSM's novel products may be subject to exclusions or sublimits in standard policies. The following must be specifically negotiated:
- **Product Liability — "Protection Product" Exclusion:** Standard product liability policies may exclude or limit coverage for products sold as "protection" against specific events. CSM must ensure that protection against geomagnetic storms is not excluded.
- **Professional Liability (Errors & Omissions):** If CSM provides engineering services, consulting, or installation design, E&O coverage is needed. Typically separate from CGL. CSM will evaluate E&O need based on whether installation services are performed by CSM employees or certified third-party installers.
- **Business Interruption — Off-Premises Power Failure:** Standard business interruption policies exclude loss due to off-premises power failure. Given that CSM's business could be interrupted by the very event we protect against (a Carrington Event causing grid failure), this exclusion is directly relevant. CSM will negotiate a sublimit or exception for off-premises power failure caused by geomagnetic storm — or self-fund this exposure.

## III. SELF-INSURANCE CONSIDERATIONS

### A. Risks Not Commercially Insurable

Some risks CSM faces are not insurable at any price or are insurable only with exclusions that defeat the purpose:

**Risk 1 — Business Interruption from Carrington Event:**
Commercial business interruption insurance typically excludes "earth movement" and "flood" — and would almost certainly exclude or severely sublimit loss from a continent-scale power outage. Even if not explicitly excluded, the aggregation risk for insurers (every policyholder in North America would claim simultaneously) means that coverage, if available, would be subject to industry-wide loss caps that make recovery uncertain.

**Self-Insurance Approach:** CSM cannot insure against the event it exists to prevent. This is not a failure of risk management. It is the rationale for the company's existence. CSM will:
- Maintain sufficient working capital (18+ months of operating expenses) to survive an extended revenue interruption
- Pre-position emergency power generation (solar + battery + backup generator) at Montana headquarters to maintain operations during grid outages
- Maintain off-site backup of all critical documents and data (the 6,302-document corpus exists in multiple physical and cloud locations)
- Accept this risk as fundamentally uninsurable

**Risk 2 — Reputational Harm from Product Failure During Carrington Event:**
Reputational harm insurance exists but is difficult to underwrite and limited in scope. The specific reputational scenario — CSM's product fails during the event we promised to protect against — could not be adequately insured at any reasonable premium.

**Self-Insurance Approach:** CSM's reputation is protected by product quality, not insurance. The mitigation is: conservative design margins, extensive testing, transparent communication about product limitations, and a crisis communication plan for the worst-case scenario.

**Risk 3 — Intellectual Property Litigation:**
Patent infringement defense and trade secret litigation can cost $2-10M per matter. IP litigation insurance exists but is expensive and often includes coverage limitations for known prior art and validity challenges.

**Self-Insurance Approach:** CSM will maintain an IP litigation reserve ($500,000 from Series A proceeds) separate from the general operating budget. As the company grows, the reserve will be increased. For high-stakes litigation, CSM may explore third-party litigation funding.

### B. Captive Insurance (Future Consideration)

As CSM grows (Year 4+, revenue >$200M), a captive insurance company — a wholly-owned subsidiary that insures the parent company's risks — may become economical. Benefits include:
- Direct access to reinsurance markets (lower cost than primary insurance)
- Ability to insure risks that commercial markets will not cover
- Premium and investment income retained within the corporate group
- Potential tax advantages (premium deductibility, reserve accumulation)

This is not a near-term initiative but will be evaluated as CSM scales. The preferred captive domicile (Vermont, Delaware, or Bermuda) will be selected based on regulatory environment, capitalization requirements, and tax considerations.

## IV. THE META-RISK: CSM'S UNIQUE POSITION IN THE INSURANCE ECOSYSTEM

### A. The Dual Relationship

CSM is simultaneously:
1. **An Insured:** We purchase insurance from carriers to cover our own risks.
2. **An Insurance Partner:** We work with carriers to reduce their losses by protecting their insureds' assets.
3. **A Potential Competitor to Insurance:** In a sense, CSM competes with insurance — we prevent the loss rather than indemnifying it. A fully Dielectric Citadel-protected grid would eliminate the need for GIC-related insurance coverage. This is a philosophical tension, not a commercial conflict, but it colors our relationship with the industry.

### B. Managing the Dual Relationship

**Transparency:** CSM will be transparent with its insurance carriers about its dual role. The carriers who provide CSM's CGL and product liability coverage will be aware that CSM is also working with other carriers (potentially their competitors) on premium reduction frameworks.

**Separation of Relationships:** Fenna Welles (Insurance Relations Director) manages the insurance partnership side (premium reduction frameworks, reinsurance facility, carrier engagement). Chester (Financial Architect) manages the insurance purchasing side (CSM's own coverage procurement). These functions are deliberately separated to avoid conflicts of interest.

**The "Interesting Conversation":** When CSM sits across the table from an insurance underwriter to purchase product liability coverage, the underwriter will ask: "What does your product protect against?" The answer — "It prevents the largest uninsured catastrophic loss in our industry's history" — will either reassure or alarm the underwriter. CSM's job is to ensure it reassures.

### C. The Insurance Industry as Both Distribution Channel and Risk-Bearer

The insurance industry is CSM's primary distribution channel (through premium reduction frameworks) and simultaneously the bearer of the risk that CSM's products mitigate ($4-6 trillion in GIC-related exposure). This alignment of interest is strategically advantageous — the industry most motivated to see CSM succeed is also the industry most capable of driving demand for CSM's products.

But it also creates a dependency: if the insurance industry's assessment of GIC risk changes — if carriers collectively decide that the risk is overblown or that other mitigation approaches are preferable — CSM's primary distribution channel evaporates. This risk is mitigated by our multi-channel strategy (insurance, regulation, consumer awareness, military) but not eliminated.

## VII. CONCLUSION: THE PARADOX WE EMBRACE

CSM is in the business of making certain types of insurance unnecessary — specifically, insurance against GIC-related transformer failure and cascading grid collapse. We are also a customer of the insurance industry, buying the coverage we need to operate as a manufacturing company.

This paradox is not a problem. It is our value proposition.

The insurance industry has a term for what we provide: "risk mitigation." They prefer it to "risk transfer" (insurance) because it eliminates the loss rather than just paying for it after the fact. When CSM prevents a transformer from failing during a geomagnetic storm, the insurer saves the cost of the claim. The insured saves the cost of the downtime. Society saves the cost of the cascading infrastructure failures.

We are in the business of making catastrophic losses not happen. That is a good business to be in, and it is a business the insurance industry wants to support. Our own insurance program — the coverage we buy to protect CSM while we pursue this mission — is a necessary operating expense, not a strategic differentiator. The strategic differentiator is what we sell, not what we buy.
