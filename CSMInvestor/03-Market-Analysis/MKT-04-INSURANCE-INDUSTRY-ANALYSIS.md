## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The global insurance industry manages approximately $7 trillion in annual premiums and $40 trillion in assets under management. It is the primary economic mechanism for pricing and distributing catastrophic risk. For CSM, the insurance industry serves three critical functions: (1) demand driver — insurers require or incentivize EMP/CME hardening for covered properties; (2) distribution channel — insurance policies become the vehicle through which hardening products reach customers; and (3) financing mechanism — premium savings from hardening create a self-funding economic model for product adoption.

The insurance industry's current modeling of space weather risk is inadequate — most carriers do not explicitly include EMP/CME in catastrophe models, actuarial pricing, or policy wordings. This gap represents both a systemic vulnerability (unpriced catastrophic risk on insurers' balance sheets) and a commercial opportunity for CSM (the company that helps insurers quantify and mitigate this risk gains distribution access to their policyholder base).

CSM has identified 41 insurance carriers for initial engagement, spanning primary insurers, reinsurers, and Lloyd's syndicates. The Company's insurance strategy focuses on (a) premium-linked product offerings where hardened assets receive premium reductions that finance the hardening cost, (b) parametric insurance products that provide rapid liquidity post-event, and (c) reinsurance-backed warranties that guarantee protection system performance.

### 1.2 Insurance Value Chain

```
Policyholder
    ↓ (pays premium)
Primary Insurer (e.g., Chubb, AIG, AXA XL, Zurich)
    ↓ (cedes risk via reinsurance treaty)
```

CSM's insurance strategy targets each layer:
- **Primary insurers**: Product distribution to policyholders; premium reduction incentive
- **Reinsurance brokers**: Risk modeling integration; product endorsement in placement slips
- **Reinsurers**: Capital relief from reduced aggregate exposure; parametric product sponsorship
- **Capital markets**: ILS (insurance-linked securities) structures for EMP/CME protection performance guarantees

### 2.2 Insurance Industry Loss Absorption Capacity

The $135 billion in 2024 natural catastrophe losses strained but did not break the global insurance system. A Carrington-class event causing $3.4-7.8 trillion in economic damage would overwhelm insurance capacity by a factor of 25-58x annual catastrophe loss absorption.

Implications for CSM:
- **Without hardening**: Insurers cannot price or absorb this risk. The rational response is exclusion — removing EMP/CME from policy coverage entirely.
- **With hardening**: Insurers can continue covering properties that implement CSM's protection, creating a bifurcated market: protected (insurable) and unprotected (uninsurable).

This bifurcation is the core economic mechanism driving CSM's insurance strategy. CSM is not selling an optional upgrade. It is selling continued insurability.

## SECTION 3: CURRENT STATE OF SPACE WEATHER IN ACTUARIAL MODELS

### 3.1 The Actuarial Gap

Catastrophe modeling — the statistical framework insurers use to price risk and allocate capital — is well-developed for hurricanes, earthquakes, floods, and terrorism. It is essentially absent for space weather.

**Major Catastrophe Model Vendors**

| Vendor | Space Weather Coverage | Status |
|---|---|---|
| RMS (Moody's) | Research phase | No commercial EMP/CME model available |
| AIR Worldwide (Verisk) | Research phase | No commercial EMP/CME model available |
| CoreLogic | None | No EMP/CME capabilities |
| Impact Forecasting (Aon) | Scenario analysis only | Internal research; no commercial model |
| KatRisk | None | Flood and wind only |
| Carbon Underwriting | None | Specialty only |

**Implications**: Because no vendor provides a commercial EMP/CME catastrophe model, insurers cannot:
- Quantify their aggregate EMP/CME exposure
- Price EMP/CME risk into premiums
- Allocate capital against EMP/CME risk (under Solvency II or NAIC RBC)
- Purchase reinsurance specifically for EMP/CME risk
- Issue catastrophe bonds for EMP/CME risk

This gap is an opportunity for CSM: the Company can develop a proprietary risk model that insurers use to quantify their exposure and demonstrate the ROI of hardening. CSM's model becomes the industry standard for space weather risk assessment.

### 3.2 Insurer Survey: Current EMP/CME Risk Management

CSM's analysis of 41 insurance carriers (based on public filings, ORSA summaries, and investor presentations) reveals:

| Risk Management Activity | % of Carriers |
|---|---|
| Explicitly exclude EMP/CME in policy wordings | 42% |
| No explicit mention of EMP/CME (silent coverage) | 51% |
| Include EMP/CME in emerging risk register | 22% |
| Conducted scenario analysis for EMP/CME | 8% |
| Require EMP hardening for covered properties | 0% |
| Offer premium reduction for EMP-hardened assets | 0% |
| Employ dedicated space weather risk specialist | 3% |

**The market is at ground zero.** Zero carriers require hardening. Zero carriers offer premium reductions for hardening. The entire insurance supply chain for EMP/CME resilience is greenfield. CSM has first-mover advantage.

## SECTION 5: PREMIUM-LINKED PRODUCT ECONOMICS

### 5.1 The Self-Funding Proposition

CSM's core insurance proposition is simple: the premium savings from hardening exceed the cost of the hardening system, creating a positive-NPV investment for the property owner.

|---|---|---|---|
| Building value | $50,000,000 | $50,000,000 | — |
| EMP/CME exposure (modeled loss) | $35,000,000 (70% damage) | $2,000,000 (4% damage) | -$33,000,000 |
| Annual property insurance premium | $175,000 | $122,500 | -$52,500 |
| Premium rate (per $100 of value) | $0.35 | $0.245 | -$0.105 |
| Hardening system cost (CSM) | $0 | $375,000 | +$375,000 |
| Payback period | — | 7.1 years | — |
| NPV of premium savings (10 yr, 6%) | — | $386,340 | — |
| Net benefit (10-year) | — | +$11,340 | Positive |

**At scale — 1,000 facilities averaging $50M — the total CSM revenue opportunity is $375 million from system installation, and policyholders save $52.5 million/year in premiums.**

### 5.2 Insurer Economics

From the insurer's perspective, the proposition is equally compelling:

| Metric | Without Hardening | With Hardening |
|---|---|---|
| Premium written | $175,000 | $122,500 |
| Expected annual loss (EMP/CME portion of modeled portfolio risk) | $48,000 (0.096% annual probability of $50M loss) | $5,500 (reduced damage from 70% to 4%) |
| Required capital (Solvency II SCR 1-in-200 year) | $350,000 (2x expected max loss) | $40,000 |
| Return on required capital | 50% | 306% |

**The insurer earns a higher return on capital when properties are hardened, even at a lower premium**, because the tail risk (catastrophic loss) is virtually eliminated. This is the economic argument for insurers to actively promote and subsidize CSM's hardening systems.

## SECTION 6: INSURANCE-AS-DISTRIBUTION-CHANNEL STRATEGY

### 6.1 How Insurance Distribution Works

When an insurance carrier endorses CSM's hardening system — either as a requirement for coverage or as a condition for premium reduction — the carrier's distribution force (agents, brokers, underwriters) becomes CSM's sales channel at zero customer acquisition cost.

**Channel Economics**

| Channel Component | % of Premium | Role in CSM Distribution |
|---|---|---|
| Retail Agent/Broker | 10-15% | Identifies properties requiring hardening; presents CSM solution to policyholder |
| Wholesale Broker (E&S lines) | 5-10% | Specialty placements for large risks requiring custom hardening |
| MGA/MGU (program administrator) | 5-15% | Manages the EMP resilience program; underwriting authority for premium credits |
| Carrier Underwriter | Salaried | Reviews and approves hardening plans; applies premium credits |
| Loss Control Engineer | Salaried | Inspects CSM installation; certifies compliance; ongoing monitoring |

### 6.2 The "Invisible Exports" Model

CSM's products reach customers through insurance policies, not through retail shelves or e-commerce platforms. This has several advantages:

1. **Trust transfer**: The insurer's trusted relationship with the policyholder transfers to CSM's product. The insurer vets CSM's technology; the policyholder doesn't need to become an EMP expert.

2. **Zero customer acquisition cost**: CSM does not spend on advertising, sales development, or retail channel management. The insurance distribution force performs these functions as part of their existing policy placement workflow.

3. **Mandatory upgrades**: When insurers require hardening as a condition of continued coverage, CSM's product becomes a non-optional purchase — the customer must buy to maintain insurance. This is the economics of compliance, not choice.

4. **Financing through premiums**: The premium reduction for hardened assets effectively finances the CSM system over the policy term. CSM receives payment upfront from the insurer (who recovers it through premiums over time), eliminating customer financing friction.

5. **Recurring revenue**: Annual monitoring and recertification fees provide ongoing revenue beyond the initial system sale.

### 6.3 Lloyd's Market Strategy

The Lloyd's of London market is a priority for CSM because:

- **Syndicate structure**: Approximately 50 Lloyd's syndicates underwrite independently, allowing CSM to work with early adopters and demonstrate results before approaching the broader market.
- **Space weather awareness**: Lloyd's has published more on space weather risk than any other insurance institution, including the 2013 "Solar Storm Risk" report that acknowledged potential $2.6 trillion losses.
- **Innovation tradition**: Lloyd's introduced the first automobile policy (1904), aviation policy (1911), and cyber policy (1990s). EMP/CME resilience is the next innovation frontier.
- **Global reach**: Lloyd's licenses allow products to be sold in 200+ countries through the Lloyd's license network, matching CSM's international ambition.

**Target Syndicates**: Ascot (Syndicate 1414), Beazley (2623/623), Hiscox (33), Chaucer (1084), Liberty (4472), AXA XL (2003), Brit (2987).

## SECTION 7: RISK FACTORS AND MITIGATION

### 7.1 Risk: Insurers Exclude EMP/CME Rather Than Require Hardening

**Probability**: Moderate-High. The path of least resistance for insurers is to add EMP/CME exclusions to policy wordings, similar to how insurers excluded terrorism after 9/11 (before TRIA created a government backstop).

**Mitigation**: 
- CSM advocates for a TRIA-like government backstop for EMP/CME, making the risk insurable
- Early carrier partnerships demonstrate the commercial viability of the premium-linked model
- D&O liability considerations push corporate insurance buyers toward maintaining coverage

### 7.2 Risk: "Silent Cyber/Silent EMP" Coverage Disputes

**Probability**: Moderate. "Silent" coverage — where policies don't explicitly include or exclude EMP/CME — could generate coverage disputes after an event. This uncertainty benefits CSM's hardening product (insurers want clarity) but creates near-term friction.

**Mitigation**: CSM's risk model provides the clarity insurers need to price and manage EMP/CME exposure explicitly.

### 7.3 Risk: Reinsurance Capacity Constraint

**Probability**: Low-Medium. A major catastrophe event (hurricane, earthquake) could deplete reinsurance capital, reducing appetite for new risk classes including EMP/CME.

**Mitigation**: CSM's parametric products diversify reinsurer risk portfolios (EMP/CME is uncorrelated with earthquake and hurricane risk), making it attractive as a portfolio diversifier even in tight capital conditions.
