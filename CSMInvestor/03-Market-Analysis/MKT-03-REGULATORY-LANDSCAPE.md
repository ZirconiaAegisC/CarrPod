## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Regulatory developments are the single most powerful demand driver for CSM's products. Unlike consumer products that must create demand through marketing, EMP/CME hardening is increasingly mandated by regulation, creating non-discretionary demand that customers must satisfy regardless of economic conditions. This document catalogs the regulatory frameworks — existing, pending, and anticipated — that create mandatory or de facto mandatory demand for dielectric protection products.

The regulatory landscape spans four domains: (1) electric grid reliability regulation (FERC/NERC), (2) critical infrastructure protection (DHS/CISA), (3) space weather monitoring and warning (NOAA/DoD), and (4) insurance regulatory frameworks (NAIC/state insurance commissioners). Each domain is evolving toward stricter EMP/CME hardening requirements. CSM's strategy is to engage early in regulatory proceedings, participate in standards development, and position its technology as the compliance pathway of choice.

The most consequential pending action is FERC's anticipated Notice of Proposed Rulemaking (NOPR) on geomagnetic disturbance (GMD) standards, which would extend existing GMD planning requirements to include mandatory hardening of critical substations. If finalized as expected in 2027-2028, this would create binding demand for grid hardening products covering approximately 2,847 critical substations identified by NERC's 2024 Long-Term Reliability Assessment.

## SECTION 1: FERC/NERC GRID RELIABILITY STANDARDS

### 1.1 FERC Authority and Jurisdiction

The Federal Energy Regulatory Commission (FERC) regulates the interstate transmission of electricity, natural gas, and oil. Under the Energy Policy Act of 2005, FERC designated the North American Electric Reliability Corporation (NERC) as the Electric Reliability Organization (ERO) with authority to develop and enforce mandatory reliability standards for the bulk power system.

FERC Order 779 (2013) directed NERC to develop geomagnetic disturbance (GMD) reliability standards. This initiated a cascade of regulatory requirements that continues to expand.

### 1.2 Existing NERC GMD Standards

**EOP-010-1 (Geomagnetic Disturbance Operations)**

Effective: January 2024
Scope: All reliability coordinators and transmission operators with bulk power system assets above 200 kV.
Requirements:
- Develop and implement GMD operating plans
- Monitor space weather conditions
- Coordinate GMD response actions across interconnected systems
- Conduct GMD operating drills and exercises

Impact on CSM: Creates awareness and operational readiness. Does not yet mandate hardware hardening, but establishes the operational framework that hardening equipment would operate within.

**TPL-007-4 (Transmission System Planned Performance for GMD Events)**

Effective: April 2023 (updated January 2026)
Scope: All transmission planners and planning coordinators.
Requirements:
- Conduct GMD vulnerability assessments every 5 years
- Assess impact of a benchmark GMD event (100-year geomagnetic disturbance)
- Identify transformers at risk of thermal damage from geomagnetically induced currents (GIC)
- Develop corrective action plans for vulnerable assets

Impact on CSM: This standard generates the vulnerability assessments that identify specific assets requiring hardening. CSM's products address the "corrective action plan" requirement by eliminating GIC pathways through dielectric substitution. **CSM should target utilities with the highest GIC vulnerability scores from their TPL-007 assessments.**

**CIP-014 (Physical Security) — GMD Extension Anticipated**

The NERC Critical Infrastructure Protection (CIP) standards address physical and cyber security. An extension to include GMD/EMP hardening for critical CIP-identified substations is under industry discussion, with a standards authorization request (SAR) expected in 2027.

### 1.3 Pending and Anticipated FERC/NERC Actions

**FERC GMD NOPR (Expected 2027-2028)**

The most consequential regulatory action for CSM is FERC's anticipated Notice of Proposed Rulemaking on comprehensive GMD standards. Based on FERC's 2024 technical conference on GMD, the NOPR is expected to include:

- Mandatory GIC blocking or mitigation at all bulk power system substations with high-side voltage ≥ 200 kV (approximately 4,700 substations in the US)
- Required hardening of control buildings and communication systems at critical substations
- GMD monitoring equipment at all reliability coordinator control centers
- Annual GMD readiness certification

**Estimated Economic Impact**: Mandatory hardening of 4,700 substations at an average cost of $2.5 million per substation = **$11.75 billion in mandated spending**. CSM's dielectric approach targets 15-20% of this market, representing $1.76-2.35 billion in potential revenue.

**NERC 2024 Long-Term Reliability Assessment — GMD Findings**

- Identified 2,847 critical substations where GMD-induced transformer damage would cause cascading outages affecting >500,000 customers each
- Recommended mandatory GIC mitigation for all critical substations within 5 years
- Estimated cost of $1.2-3.8 million per substation for GMD protection (EPRI estimate, referenced in NERC report)

**NERC GMD Task Force — 2026 Work Plan**

- Developing performance requirements for GIC blocking devices
- Establishing testing and certification standards for GMD protection equipment
- Creating a GMD event severity scale for operational response
- Evaluating long-term hardening alternatives including dielectric and superconducting technologies

**CSM Engagement Strategy**: Participate in NERC GMD Task Force proceedings. Submit technical comments on dielectric protection performance characteristics. Seek inclusion of dielectric materials as an accepted compliance technology in NERC guidance documents.

## SECTION 2: DHS/CISA CRITICAL INFRASTRUCTURE PROTECTION

### 2.1 DHS EMP/GMD Program

The Department of Homeland Security's Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency (CISA) is the lead federal agency for critical infrastructure protection. Under Executive Order 13865 (2019, Trump Administration) and continued under EO 14110 (2024), CISA is directed to:

- Identify critical infrastructure vulnerable to EMP/CME events
- Develop and disseminate protection guidance
- Coordinate with sector-specific agencies (DOE for energy, DOT for transportation, DOD for defense)
- Conduct EMP/CME exercises and training

### 2.2 CISA EMP/GMD Guidance Documents

**CISA EMP Protection Guidance for Critical Infrastructure (2023)**

This guidance document, developed with input from the EMP Commission and national laboratories, recommends:
- Layered protection approach (shielding, filtering, isolation)
- Priority hardening for assets whose failure would cause cascading consequences
- Integration of EMP protection into new construction and major renovations
- Annual assessment of EMP protection adequacy

The guidance references but does not mandate dielectric materials specifically. **CSM's strategy: Work with CISA to include dielectric protection as a recommended best practice in future revisions.**

**CISA National Critical Functions Framework (2024 Update)**

Identifies 55 National Critical Functions (NCFs) whose disruption would cause national catastrophe. Of these, 31 are electricity-dependent. The framework specifically identifies "Provide Electricity" and "Provide Communications" as Tier 1 functions requiring EMP/CME resilience.

### 2.3 DHS Grants for EMP Protection

The following DHS grant programs fund critical infrastructure protection:

| Program | Annual Funding | EMP Relevance | CSM Opportunity |
|---|---|---|---|
| Homeland Security Grant Program (HSGP) | $1.12B (FY2025) | State homeland security priorities; EMP hardening eligible | State-level pilot funding |
| Urban Area Security Initiative (UASI) | $615M | High-threat urban areas; grid resilience | Urban substation hardening projects |

## SECTION 3: NOAA SPACE WEATHER PREDICTION CENTER

### 3.1 NOAA SWPC Role

The Space Weather Prediction Center (SWPC) in Boulder, Colorado, is the nation's official source of space weather alerts and warnings. Established in 1965, SWPC operates 24/7 and provides:

- Geomagnetic storm watches and warnings (G-scale, 1-5)
- Solar radiation storm alerts (S-scale, 1-5)
- Radio blackout forecasts (R-scale, 1-5)
- GIC (geomagnetically induced current) modeling for power grid operators

### 3.2 PROSWIFT Act (2020)

The Promoting Research and Observations of Space Weather to Improve the Forecasting of Tomorrow (PROSWIFT) Act, signed into law in October 2020, directs:

- NOAA to maintain and enhance space weather monitoring capabilities
- DOD to integrate space weather into military planning
- DHS to incorporate space weather into critical infrastructure protection
- Establishment of a Space Weather Advisory Group (SWAG) with industry participation
- Interagency coordination through the Space Weather Operations, Research, and Mitigation (SWORM) working group

**Impact on CSM**: PROSWIFT institutionalizes space weather as a national security concern, creating sustained funding for monitoring and mitigation. CSM's products are the mitigation component of this framework.

### 3.3 NOAA Commercial Data Program

NOAA's Commercial Data Program, which purchases weather data from commercial providers, could be extended to space weather. If CSM deploys monitoring equipment as part of hardened installations, this creates a potential data-as-a-service revenue stream.

## SECTION 4: EXECUTIVE ORDERS AND NATIONAL SECURITY DIRECTIVES

### 4.1 Executive Order 13865 (2019) — Coordinating National Resilience to Electromagnetic Pulses

Signed: March 26, 2019 (Trump Administration)
Key provisions:
- Established EMP as a national security priority
- Directed DHS to coordinate EMP resilience across critical infrastructure sectors
- Directed DOD to assess EMP vulnerability of military installations
- Directed DOE to assess EMP vulnerability of the electric grid
- Required annual reporting on EMP preparedness progress

**Status**: Fully in effect. Annual reports continue through 2026. EO 13865 established the policy framework; subsequent executive orders have expanded scope.

### 4.2 Executive Order 14110 (2024) — Safe, Secure, and Trustworthy Development and Use of Artificial Intelligence

While primarily about AI, EO 14110 includes a section on critical infrastructure AI applications that references EMP/GMD as a threat to AI-enabled grid control systems. This creates additional regulatory pressure for hardening the control systems that manage modernized, AI-optimized power grids.

### 4.3 National Security Presidential Memorandum 40 (NSPM-40)

Signed: March 2023
Classification: Unclassified summary published
Key provisions:
- Designated space weather as a national intelligence priority
- Directed integration of space weather into National Intelligence Priorities Framework
- Established space weather threat information sharing between intelligence community and critical infrastructure operators
- Created a classified annex on EMP/CME defense priority locations

**Impact on CSM**: Classified priority location list effectively creates a mandatory hardening requirement for those locations, even if not publicly specified. CSM will need facility security clearances (FCL) to serve these customers.

### 4.4 EMP Commission Reports

The Congressional EMP Commission (2001-2017, periodically reauthorized) produced the foundational technical assessments that underpin current policy. Its 2008 Critical National Infrastructures Report remains the most-cited source on EMP vulnerability and estimated that a nationwide EMP event would cause:

- 4-10 year recovery timeline for the electric grid
- 65-90% population loss within 12 months due to cascading infrastructure failures (food, water, medicine, transportation)
- $5-8 trillion in economic damage

The Commission's estimates, while debated, have shaped every federal EMP policy since publication. The Commission was reauthorized in the FY2025 NDAA, with a new report expected in 2027.

## SECTION 5: EU CRITICAL ENTITIES RESILIENCE DIRECTIVE

### 5.1 CER Directive (2022/2557)

Effective: January 2024 (EU member states must transpose by October 2024)
Scope: Eleven sectors including energy, transport, banking, financial market infrastructure, health, drinking water, wastewater, digital infrastructure, public administration, space, and food.

Key requirements:
- Member states must identify critical entities providing essential services
- Critical entities must conduct risk assessments including "natural hazards"
- Critical entities must implement resilience measures, including physical protection
- Incident reporting to national competent authorities
- Cross-border coordination for entities serving multiple member states

**EM in the CER Framework**: "Natural hazards" as defined in the CER Directive explicitly includes "extreme weather events" and "space weather phenomena" (Recital 18, Annex). This makes EMP/CME protection a compliance requirement for critical entities in the energy and digital infrastructure sectors across all 27 EU member states.

### 5.2 EU Network Codes on Cybersecurity (Expected 2026-2027)

The EU is developing a Network Code on Cybersecurity for the electricity sector under the 2019 Electricity Regulation. Expected provisions include:
- Mandatory risk assessment for electromagnetic threats
- Resilience requirements for grid control systems
- Certification requirements for protection equipment

**Impact on CSM**: Creates a 27-country mandatory market for grid hardening products with EU-wide certification standards. CSM should pursue EU certification early to establish first-mover advantage.

### 5.3 European Space Weather Services

The European Space Agency (ESA) operates the Space Weather Service Network, providing forecasting and alerting for European critical infrastructure operators. ESA's Vigil mission (scheduled launch 2028) will position a spacecraft at the L5 Lagrange point for improved CME detection. This enhanced warning capability will increase demand for mitigation products — knowing a CME is coming creates obligation to protect against it.

## SECTION 6: INSURANCE REGULATORY FRAMEWORK

### 6.1 NAIC (National Association of Insurance Commissioners)

The NAIC coordinates insurance regulation across 50 states, DC, and 5 territories. Key NAIC initiatives relevant to EMP/CME:

**NAIC Catastrophe Risk Subgroup**
- Developing catastrophe risk capital requirements including "emerging perils"
- Space weather has been discussed as an emerging peril requiring capital allocation
- If NAIC adopts space weather-related risk-based capital charges, insurers would need to either (a) exclude space weather coverage, (b) hold additional capital, or (c) require policyholders to implement hardening measures
- CSM's products enable option (c), which is the lowest-cost path for insurers

**NAIC Climate and Resiliency Task Force**
- 2024 Work Plan includes "assessment of insurance coverage gaps for non-modeled catastrophic perils"
- Space weather is the quintessential non-modeled catastrophic peril
- Task force is expected to recommend regulatory incentives for resilience investments

**NAIC ORSA (Own Risk and Solvency Assessment)**
- Large insurers must conduct annual ORSA reports identifying material risks
- EMP/CME exposure in commercial property portfolios increasingly appears in ORSA filings
- Regulators are beginning to question insurers about space weather risk management

### 6.2 Solvency II (EU)

The EU's Solvency II insurance regulatory framework requires capital adequacy for all material risks. Key developments:

- **2024 Solvency II Review**: The review process is examining whether "emerging catastrophic risks" including space weather require explicit capital charges
- **EIOPA (European Insurance and Occupational Pensions Authority)**: Published a 2024 thematic review on catastrophe risk modeling, identifying space weather as a "significant gap in current modeling"
- If Solvency II imposes space weather capital charges, European insurers will have strong incentive to require EMP/CME hardening for large commercial properties

### 6.3 IAIS (International Association of Insurance Supervisors)

The IAIS coordinates insurance regulation globally. Its 2024 Global Monitoring Exercise identified "protection gaps for non-modeled catastrophic risks" as a systemic vulnerability. Space weather was specifically referenced.

## SECTION 8: HOW REGULATIONS CREATE MANDATORY DEMAND

### 8.1 The Regulatory Demand Cascade

Regulatory demand for EMP/CME protection follows a predictable cascade:

```
Space weather monitoring (NOAA/ESA)
        ↓
Threat assessment (EMP Commission, NERC assessments)
        ↓
Vulnerability identification (TPL-007, CISA NCF)
        ↓
Standards development (NERC, IEEE, IEC)
        ↓
Mandatory compliance requirements (FERC rules, CER Directive)
        ↓
Procurement of protection equipment (CSM products)
        ↓
Enforcement and audit (NERC compliance audits, insurance inspections)
        ↓
Ongoing maintenance and upgrade cycle
```

CSM is positioned at step 5 (procurement). The preceding steps are occurring now and will produce mandatory, non-discretionary demand by 2028-2030.

### 8.2 Regulatory Demand vs. Economic Demand

Unlike products that depend on customer ROI calculations, mandated products have demand curves that are:
- **Inelastic**: Demand does not decrease if prices rise (within reason)
- **Non-cyclical**: Not affected by economic downturns
- **Legislated**: Timing determined by regulatory calendars, not market conditions
- **Penalty-backed**: Non-compliance carries fines, operating restrictions, or liability exposure

This regulatory demand profile is attractive to investors because it reduces revenue uncertainty and competitive pricing pressure.

### 8.3 Director and Officer Liability

A secondary demand driver is D&O (director and officer) liability. Public company boards that fail to address known catastrophic risks may face shareholder derivative lawsuits. The 2023 Caremark standard (Delaware Chancery Court) requires boards to exercise oversight of "mission-critical" risks. As EMP/CME risk becomes better understood, utility board members and officers face increasing personal liability exposure for failing to implement available protective measures. CSM's products are the most direct way for utilities to demonstrate reasonable oversight of EMP/CME risk.

## SECTION 9: RISK FACTORS

1. **Regulatory delay**: The FERC GMD NOPR could be delayed by political changes or industry opposition. Each year of delay shifts CSM's revenue curve right by 1-2 years.

2. **Regulatory capture**: Large incumbent utilities and grid equipment manufacturers may influence standards to favor their existing products or create compliance pathways that don't require new technology.

3. **Preemption risk**: Federal grid resilience standards could preempt more stringent state standards, reducing overall demand.

4. **Technology specification risk**: If NERC standards specify particular technologies (e.g., "neutral blocking devices" vs. "GIC mitigation"), they could exclude dielectric approaches from compliance.

5. **International divergence**: If US and EU standards diverge, CSM would need separate product lines for each market, increasing development cost.

6. **Enforcement weakness**: Standards without enforcement are suggestions. If NERC compliance audits are underfunded, mandated demand may not materialize.

7. **Insurance market withdrawal**: If insurers simply exclude EMP/CME from coverage rather than requiring hardening, the insurance distribution channel disappears.
