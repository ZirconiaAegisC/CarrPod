## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

CSM's pricing strategy is built on value-based pricing — the price of a dielectric protection system is set as a fraction of the economic loss it prevents, not as a markup on manufacturing cost. This approach captures a portion of the $2-7.8 trillion in averted catastrophe loss as revenue for CSM, while leaving the customer with a clear positive economic return.

The average CSM system is priced at 5-15% of the avoided loss over a 10-year period, producing gross margins of 55-65% at scale while providing customers with ROI periods of 3-8 years (or zero years when financed through insurance premium savings).

This document details pricing by customer segment, competitive positioning, volume discount structures, licensing models, geographic variations, and price elasticity estimates.

## SECTION 1: VALUE-BASED PRICING METHODOLOGY

### 1.1 The Economic Rationale

CSM's pricing philosophy: **A product that prevents a $10 million loss is worth something less than $10 million.** The question is how much less. CSM's goal is to capture 5-15% of the value created, consistent with pricing for enterprise software, medical devices, and other high-value-add technology products.

**Value-Based Price Formula**

```
CSM System Price = (Avoided Economic Loss × Value Capture Rate) ÷ (1 + Discount Rate)^t
```

Where:
- Avoided Economic Loss = cost of damage + business interruption + insurance premium increase + regulatory penalty
- Value Capture Rate = CSM's target share of value (5-15%)
- Discount Rate = customer's cost of capital (5-12%)
- t = time until first event with protection (probability-weighted)

**Example: Utility Substation ($75M replacement value, 2% annual EMP/CME loss probability)**

| Parameter | Value |
|---|---|
| Substation replacement cost | $75,000,000 |
| Equipment vulnerable to EMP/CME (% of replacement) | 55% |
| Direct damage cost (avoided) | $41,250,000 |
| Business interruption (6 months at $2M/day grid revenue) | $365,000,000 |
| Regulatory penalty for outage | $5,000,000 |
| Total avoided economic loss | $411,250,000 |
| Probability-weighted annual avoided loss (2% event probability) | $8,225,000 |
| CSM value capture rate | 10% |
| **CSM system price** | **$822,500** |

Against an EPRI-estimated $1.2-3.8 million for traditional GMD protection, CSM's $800K-$1.2M pricing is competitive while delivering superior protection (dielectric vs. conductive) and lower life-cycle cost (no corrosion maintenance). This is the core pricing narrative: **better protection at lower total cost.**

## SECTION 3: COMPETITIVE PRICING ANALYSIS

### 3.1 Pricing vs. Faraday Cage Alternatives

| Application | Traditional Faraday Solution (Estimated) | CSM Dielectric Solution | Price Premium (CSM) | Life-Cycle Savings (CSM) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Substation control building (500 sq ft) | $250K-$500K (copper mesh room) | $175K-$350K (dielectric panels) | -30% to -30% | $50K-$150K (no corrosion maintenance over 30 years) |
| C4I facility (2,000 sq ft) | $6M-$20M (MIL-SPEC welded steel) | $1.5M-$4M (dielectric structural) | -75% to -80% | $1M-$3M (maintenance + weight savings in foundation) |
| Vessel bridge electronics | $500K-$2M (copper mesh + surge) | $200K-$1.5M (dielectric enclosure + integrated protection) | -60% to -25% | $50K-$200K (corrosion + weight) |
| Home electronics protection | $2K-$10K (Faraday bags + surge strips — incomplete) | $5K-$30K (whole-home integrated) | +150% to +200% | Vastly superior protection; Faraday bags protect only bagged items |

**Key Insight**: For large-scale applications (C4I facilities, substations, vessels), CSM's dielectric solution is price-competitive or significantly cheaper than traditional Faraday approaches, even before accounting for life-cycle maintenance savings. The residential price premium is real but justified by the inadequacy of consumer Faraday alternatives.

## SECTION 4: LICENSING AND ALTERNATIVE PRICING MODELS

### 4.1 Technology Licensing Model

For defense contractors and international partners where direct CSM product sales are impractical:

| License Type | Description | Fee Structure |
|---|---|---|
| Component License | License to manufacture CSM dielectric panels under CSM patent and quality control | 8-12% royalty on licensee's selling price + $2M-$10M upfront technology transfer fee |
| Application License | Rights to use CSM dielectric technology in specified application (e.g., military ground vehicles) | 5-8% royalty + $5M-$20M upfront |
| Geographic License | Exclusive territory rights for non-US markets (e.g., India, Southeast Asia) | $10M-$50M upfront + 10-15% royalty |
| OEM Supply Agreement | CSM manufactures; partner integrates and sells under partner's brand | CSM sells at 35-45% gross margin to partner |

### 4.2 Government Contract Pricing Models

| Contract Type | Description | CSM Margin | Risk Profile |
|---|---|---|---|
| Firm Fixed Price (FFP) | Single price for defined deliverable | 45-65% | CSM bears performance risk; rewards efficiency |
| Cost-Plus-Fixed-Fee (CPFF) | Reimbursement of allowable costs + fixed fee (5-15%) | 5-15% (fee only) | Low risk; used for R&D and first-of-kind projects |
| Cost-Plus-Incentive-Fee (CPIF) | Cost reimbursement + incentive fee for meeting/exceeding targets | 10-20% (combined) | Moderate risk; incentive for performance |
| Time & Materials (T&M) | Hourly rates + material costs | 25-40% on labor; pass-through on materials | Moderate risk; appropriate for installation services |
| IDIQ Task Orders | Individual task orders under umbrella contract | Varies by task order type | Mixed; each task order is independent |

**Recommended Mix**: 70% FFP (production contracts), 20% CPFF/CPIF (R&D and development), 10% T&M (installation services).

### 5.1 Price Sensitivity by Segment

| Segment | Price Elasticity Estimate | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| DOD Installation Hardening | -0.2 to -0.5 (inelastic) | Mandated by policy; budgets allocated; few alternatives |
| Utility IOU Substation | -0.5 to -0.8 (moderately inelastic) | Regulatory mandate drives compliance; some cost sensitivity |
| Utility Cooperative | -0.8 to -1.2 (unit elastic) | Smaller budgets; alternative of self-insurance considered |
| Commercial Property (via insurance) | -0.3 to -0.6 (inelastic) | Insurance requirement creates non-discretionary demand |
| Maritime Vessel | -0.6 to -1.0 (moderately elastic) | Classification society requirement drives compliance; retrofit timing discretionary |
| Home Builder | -0.8 to -1.5 (elastic) | Optional upgrade; price-sensitive consumer market |
| Homeowner (Direct) | -1.0 to -2.0 (highly elastic) | Discretionary purchase; premium savings soften elasticity |
| Dielectric Robot Components | -0.3 to -0.7 (inelastic) | No substitute products exist for dielectric functionality |

### 5.2 Revenue Optimization

Given these elasticities, CSM's revenue-optimal pricing strategy varies by segment:

- **Inelastic segments (Government, Utility, Insurance-linked)**: Price at 75-100% of WTP; value capture rate of 10-15%. These segments are revenue anchors.
- **Moderately elastic segments (Maritime, Robot Components)**: Price at 60-80% of WTP; value capture of 8-12%. Volume growth potential.
- **Elastic segments (Home Builder, Homeowner)**: Price at 40-60% of WTP; value capture of 5-8%. Market share growth prioritized over per-unit margin; insurance premium savings create effective price reduction.

## SECTION 8: PRICING RISK FACTORS

1. **Price transparency risk**: As CSM products become specified in government and utility RFPs, pricing may need to be disclosed (FAR requirement for FFP contracts), potentially creating pricing pressure across segments.

2. **Commoditization risk**: If dielectric panels become commodity items (multiple manufacturers, interchangeable specifications), margins compress toward manufacturing cost. Mitigation: CSM's systems integration, monitoring, and certification layers capture value beyond the material.

3. **Insurance premium fluctuation**: If insurance premiums for hardened assets don't decline as modeled, the PaaS model's economics break. CSM's risk is limited to PaaS contracts; direct-sale customers bear premium fluctuation risk.

4. **Regulatory price pressure**: If FERC or state PUCs impose cost caps on EMP hardening (analogous to rate caps on medical devices), margins could compress. CSM's value-based pricing narrative argues against price controls — the product prevents losses that dwarf its cost.

5. **Currency risk on international contracts**: Long-duration international contracts denominated in local currency carry exchange rate risk. CSM will attempt to denominate in USD and include FX adjustment clauses.
