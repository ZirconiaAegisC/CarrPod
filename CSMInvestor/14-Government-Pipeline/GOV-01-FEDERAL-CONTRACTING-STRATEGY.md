## 1.0 EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The United States federal government is CSM's largest potential customer, with an estimated $1.25B+ addressable market over 10 years spanning the Department of Defense, Department of Energy, Department of Homeland Security, General Services Administration, NASA, Department of Transportation, and Department of Health and Human Services.

This document outlines CSM's federal contracting strategy: the agencies to target, the contract vehicles to pursue, the small business programs to leverage, the sole-source justification pathway, and the compliance infrastructure required. Federal procurement is complex, slow, and compliance-heavy — but also the most stable, creditworthy, and mission-aligned customer base in the world.

As El Segundo frames it: "The federal government moves at the speed of procurement regulation. CSM must be patient enough to navigate that pace and urgent enough to push where push is possible. The grid doesn't wait for the Federal Acquisition Regulation."

## 3.0 CONTRACTING VEHICLES

### 3.1 SBIR/STTR Program

**Small Business Innovation Research (SBIR) and Small Business Technology Transfer (STTR):**

| Program | Phase I Funding | Phase II Funding | Phase III |
|---------|----------------|------------------|-----------|
| SBIR | $50K-250K (feasibility, 6-12 months) | $750K-1.5M (prototype, 2 years) | Commercialization (sole-source eligible) |
| STTR | $50K-250K (requires university/research institution partner) | $750K-1.5M | Commercialization |

**CSM SBIR Strategy:**
- Phase I (Year 1): "MXene-Based Dielectric Shielding for Transformer GIC Protection" — DTRA or DOE topic
- Phase II (Year 2): Prototype Charlemagne system for DOD/DOE facility
- Phase III (Year 3-5): Sole-source production contract

**Key SBIR Agencies for CSM:**
- DOD (DTRA, AFWERX, SOFWERX, DIU)
- DOE (ARPA-E, CESER, Office of Electricity)
- DHS (CISA, S&T Directorate)
- NASA
- DOT

### 3.2 GSA Schedule Contracting

**GSA Multiple Award Schedule (MAS):** A pre-negotiated contract vehicle that simplifies federal purchasing. Once CSM has a GSA Schedule contract, any federal agency can purchase CSM products through a streamlined process.

**Timeline:** 6-12 months to obtain GSA Schedule (requires commercial sales history, which CSM needs first — target Year 3-4).

### 3.3 Other Transaction (OT) Agreements

OT agreements are flexible, non-FAR contracting vehicles used by DOD for rapid prototyping and research. DIU (Defense Innovation Unit) specializes in OT agreements for commercial technology companies. Timeline: 60-90 days from proposal to award (much faster than traditional FAR-based contracts).

### 3.4 Broad Agency Announcements (BAAs)

BAAs are research funding solicitations from DOD and DOE. CSM can respond to BAAs related to EMP hardening, grid resilience, and advanced materials.

## 4.0 SOLE-SOURCE JUSTIFICATION PATHWAY

### 4.1 The Sole-Source Argument

CSM's MXene-based dielectric shielding is unique. No other company produces GIC blocking devices with 99.97% reduction at comparable cost, weight, and form factor. This uniqueness can support sole-source justifications under FAR 6.302-1 ("Only one responsible source and no other supplies or services will satisfy agency requirements").

### 4.2 Requirements for Sole-Source

| Requirement | How CSM Satisfies |
|------------|-------------------|
| Unique capability | MXene shielding at 92dB/45μm; no competitor achieves comparable performance |
| No alternative sources | Patent-protected (once filed); trade secret manufacturing process; 6,302-document knowledge base |
| Reasonable price | Cost-based pricing with government cost accounting standards |
| Justification & Approval (J&A) | Prepared by contracting officer; CSM provides technical justification |

### 4.3 Sole-Source vs. Competitive

CSM should not rely solely on sole-source contracting. Competitive contracting is the default in federal procurement, and CSM should be prepared to compete — while maintaining that its technology is superior enough to win competitions on technical merit. The sole-source pathway is a fallback for situations where CSM's uniqueness is incontrovertible.

## 6.0 COMPLIANCE INFRASTRUCTURE

### 6.1 Required Systems

| System | Description | Timeline to Implement |
|--------|-----------|--------------------|
| Government cost accounting | Per FAR Part 31 (cost principles) and Cost Accounting Standards (CAS) if applicable | Year 2-3 |
| DCAA-compliant accounting system | Defense Contract Audit Agency requirements for timekeeping, cost allocation, billing | Year 3-4 |
| Purchasing system (CPSR) | Contractor Purchasing System Review compliance for subcontracting | Year 4-5 |
| Property management system | Government-furnished property tracking | As needed |
| Estimating system | For cost-plus and T&M contracts | Year 3-4 |

### 6.2 Key FAR/DFARS Requirements

| Regulation | Description | Impact on CSM |
|-----------|------------|-------------|
| FAR Part 9 (Contractor Qualifications) | Organizational conflict of interest (OCI); responsibility determinations | Must avoid OCI in advisory + product roles |
| FAR Part 15 (Contracting by Negotiation) | Cost or pricing data; TINA (Truth in Negotiations Act) | Required for non-competitive contracts >$2M |
| FAR Part 31 (Contract Cost Principles) | Allowable vs. unallowable costs | Must segregate unallowable costs (e.g., entertainment, alcohol, lobbying) |
| FAR Part 52.204-21 (Basic Safeguarding) | Cybersecurity for federal contract information | Required for all federal contracts |
| DFARS 252.204-7012 (NIST SP 800-171) | Controlled unclassified information protection | Required for DOD contracts |
| DFARS 252.204-7020 (NIST SP 800-171 Assessment) | Self-assessment and DOD assessment | Required for DOD contracts |
| CMMC (Cybersecurity Maturity Model Certification) | Third-party cybersecurity certification | Required for DOD contracts (phased rollout) |

### 6.3 Facility Security Clearance

If CSM works on classified DOD programs (likely, given EMP's relevance to nuclear and strategic systems), a Facility Security Clearance (FCL) and personnel security clearances (PCLs) are required. Timeline: 12-18 months for initial FCL. CSM should sponsor key personnel for clearances early.

## 7.0 AGENT VOICES

**Government Domain Agent:**
"The government moves at the speed of the Federal Acquisition Regulation. A standard procurement — from requirements definition to contract award — is 12-18 months for a commercial item, 24-36 months for a development program. CSM's technology timeline (7 years to IPO) must factor in that government revenue ramps slowly. The SBIR/STTR pathway is the fastest entry point. GSA Schedule is the scalable path. Don't confuse government interest with government procurement — they are separated by 18 months of paperwork."

**El Segundo (Patience + Urgency):**
"The federal government is simultaneously the best and worst customer. Best because: unlimited budget, stable payments, mission alignment, long-term contracts. Worst because: slow, bureaucratic, compliance-heavy, politically vulnerable. The El Segundo approach: start government engagement now (CSMReach), pursue SBIR immediately, but don't let government revenue be the make-or-break for the company's survival. Government can be the cherry on top, not the cake."

**The Accountant (Compliance Discipline):**
"Government contracting compliance is not optional. DCAA will audit your accounting system. DCMA will audit your quality system. DCSA will audit your security. None of these audits are friendly conversations — they are adversarial examinations by trained professionals looking for non-compliance. You either build compliant systems from the beginning, or you pay 10x to retrofit them later. The Accountant Heuristic demands: build government compliance into the DNA, not bolted on after."
