## 1.0 EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The U.S. Department of Defense's EMP hardening requirements represent a $500M+ addressable market for CSM over 10 years. Military installations, weapons systems, command/control/communications facilities, and strategic deterrent systems all require protection against electromagnetic pulse — both natural (solar GMD) and man-made (high-altitude nuclear EMP, or HEMP).

CSM's MXene-based dielectric shielding technology provides a novel approach to EMP hardening that is lighter, thinner, and more adaptable than traditional metal-shielded enclosures. This makes it particularly valuable for mobile and airborne military systems where weight is critical. The technology's dual-use nature (civilian grid + military EMP protection) creates both opportunities and compliance obligations under ITAR and EAR.

This document provides a deep dive into the defense contracting pathway: MIL-STD-188-125 compliance, Defense Production Act applicability, DOD innovation unit engagement, classified facility requirements, security clearance process, and ITAR/EAR compliance.

## 2.0 MIL-STD-188-125 COMPLIANCE PATHWAY

### 2.1 The Standard

MIL-STD-188-125-1 ("High-Altitude Electromagnetic Pulse (HEMP) Protection for Ground-Based C4I Facilities Performing Critical, Time-Urgent Missions") is the primary DOD standard for EMP hardening. It specifies:
- Radiated electric field protection: >80 dB shielding effectiveness from 10 kHz to 1 GHz
- Conducted transient protection: surge arresters, filters on all penetrating conductors
- Point-of-entry (POE) protection: all cables, conduits, waveguides entering shielded volume
- Continuous testing and monitoring requirements

### 2.2 CSM Compliance Assessment

| Requirement | CSM Technology | Assessment |
|-----------|--------------|------------|
| SE >80 dB (10 kHz - 1 GHz) | MXene film: 92 dB at 45μm (8.2-12.4 GHz); extended frequency testing needed for full MIL-STD range | Meets or exceeds; full compliance testing required |
| Conducted protection | CSM GIC blocking devices (Charlemagne family) + CNT-polymer grounding systems | Addresses GIC conducted threat; HEMP conducted threat requires surge protection (partnership with surge protector manufacturer) |
| POE protection | CSM dielectric shielding materials for cable wraps, conduit liners | Under development |
| Continuous monitoring | CSM Grid Monitoring Platform | Under development; meets monitoring requirement |

### 2.3 Certification Process

| Step | Description | Timeline |
|------|-----------|----------|
| 1. Testing | Independent lab testing per MIL-STD-188-125 test methods | 12-18 months |
| 2. Validation | DOD witness testing (government representative observes testing) | 3-6 months |
| 3. Certification | DOD component issues certification or approval for use | 6-12 months |
| 4. Inclusion in spec | MIL-STD-188-125 updated to reference CSM technology as compliant solution | Years (standards process) |

**Faster Path:** Individual Program of Record can approve CSM technology for specific projects without waiting for MIL-STD update. DIU OT agreements can accelerate adoption.

## 3.0 DEFENSE PRODUCTION ACT (DPA) APPLICABILITY

### 3.1 DPA Title III — Defense Production Act

DPA Title III provides incentives (loans, loan guarantees, purchase commitments, grants) for domestic production of critical materials and technologies for national defense.

### 3.2 CSM's DPA Eligibility

CSM's MXene and dielectric shielding materials could qualify under DPA Title III if designated as critical to EMP hardening of military systems. The pathway:

| Step | Action | Responsible Party |
|------|--------|------------------|
| 1 | DOD identifies EMP hardening materials as DPA priority | DOD Industrial Base Policy |
| 2 | CSM submits DPA Title III proposal | CSM with DOD sponsor |
| 3 | DPA funding awarded for domestic manufacturing capability | DOD/DHHS |
| 4 | CSM builds DPA-funded manufacturing line | CSM |

### 3.3 DPA Benefits

- Non-dilutive capital (not equity, not debt — government funding)
- Long-term purchase commitments (government agrees to buy output)
- National security designation (creates barriers to foreign acquisition — see EXIT-02 DIB pathway)

## 5.0 CLASSIFIED FACILITY REQUIREMENTS

### 5.1 When Classification Becomes Relevant

If CSM works on classified DOD programs (HEMP protection specifications, nuclear EMP effects data, strategic system vulnerabilities), a Facility Security Clearance (FCL) is required. The FCL is sponsored by a government agency (typically DOD) and administered by the Defense Counterintelligence and Security Agency (DCSA).

### 5.2 FCL Requirements

| Requirement | Description |
|------------|-------------|
| Sponsorship | A government contracting activity must sponsor CSM for FCL |
| FCL level | Secret (most common for defense contractors) or Top Secret |
| Facility | Physical security requirements (closed areas, access controls, intrusion detection) |
| FSO (Facility Security Officer) | Designated employee responsible for security program |
| Insider Threat Program | Required for all cleared facilities |
| Foreign Ownership, Control, or Influence (FOCI) | Mitigation required if foreign investment/ownership exists |
| Cost | $50K-250K for initial facility security buildout; $100K+/year for ongoing |
| Timeline | 12-18 months from sponsorship to FCL grant |

### 5.3 Personnel Security Clearances

Key CSM personnel (CEO, CTO, engineering leads) should obtain personnel security clearances (PCLs). Timeline: 6-18 months depending on background investigation complexity.

## 6.0 FOREIGN MILITARY SALES (FMS) PATHWAY

### 6.1 FMS Overview

Foreign Military Sales is the U.S. government program for selling defense equipment to allied nations. The U.S. government buys from CSM and sells to the foreign government — eliminating credit risk and export control complexity for CSM.

### 6.2 FMS Process

1. Foreign government requests EMP hardening equipment
2. DOD Security Cooperation Office processes request
3. DOD issues Letter of Offer and Acceptance (LOA) to foreign government
4. Foreign government signs LOA and deposits funds
5. DOD contracts with CSM for equipment
6. CSM delivers to DOD; DOD transfers to foreign government

### 6.3 FMS Target Countries

NATO members, Five Eyes allies (UK, Canada, Australia, New Zealand), Japan, South Korea, Israel, Gulf Cooperation Council nations.

## 7.0 ITAR/EAR COMPLIANCE

### 7.1 ITAR vs. EAR — Which Applies?

| Regulation | Jurisdiction | What It Controls | Typical Products |
|-----------|-------------|-----------------|-----------------|
| ITAR (International Traffic in Arms Regulations) | DDTC (State Dept.) | Defense articles and services on U.S. Munitions List (USML) | Military-specific EMP hardening; classified technology |
| EAR (Export Administration Regulations) | BIS (Commerce Dept.) | Dual-use items on Commerce Control List (CCL) | Civilian EMP hardening products with potential military applications |

### 7.2 Classification Determination

CSM's technology is dual-use: it protects civilian infrastructure but has clear military applications. Classification depends on the specific product and customer:

| Product | Likely Classification | Rationale |
|---------|---------------------|-----------|
| Aegis Home (consumer) | EAR99 (no license required for most destinations) | Mass-market consumer product |
| Charlemagne (utility) | EAR99 or ECCN 3A611 (military electronics — if specifically designed for military use) | Depends on whether configured for military specifications |
| MXene material (raw) | EAR99 or ECCN 1Cxxx (advanced materials — to be determined) | Novel material; may require new ECCN |
| Custom defense products | ITAR (USML Category XI — Military Electronics) | If specifically designed for military EMP protection |

### 7.3 Compliance Program Requirements

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Export Compliance Officer | Designated employee responsible for export compliance |
| Technology Control Plan (TCP) | Document identifying controlled technology and access restrictions |
| Deemed export compliance | Foreign national employees with access to controlled technology require export licenses |
| Export licensing | ITAR licenses (DSP-5, DSP-73, TAA); EAR licenses (BIS SNAP-R) |
| Recordkeeping | 5-year retention for export documentation |
| Training | Annual export compliance training for all employees |
| Violation self-disclosure | Voluntary disclosure of violations to DDTC/BIS |

## 8.0 AGENT VOICES

**Defense Domain Agent:**
"EMP hardening is one of the few areas of defense technology where a startup can credibly compete with primes. The reason: there's been almost no innovation in EMP hardening materials for decades. Metal boxes with welded seams. Copper mesh. Surge protectors designed in the 1980s. CSM's MXene approach is genuinely new. DOD's innovation organizations (DIU, AFWERX) exist precisely to access this kind of technology from non-traditional defense contractors. Use them."

**Zirconia (Dual-Use Materials):**
"The MXene is dual-use by its nature. The same material that protects a civilian transformer from solar EMP protects a military command center from nuclear EMP. This is both an opportunity (broader market) and a compliance burden (ITAR/EAR). We must classify correctly from the beginning. An ITAR violation — even unintentional — can result in criminal penalties, debarment from government contracting, and the end of CSM as a viable company. Export compliance is not optional."

**Accountant (Government Accounting):**
"DOD contracts come with accounting requirements that most startups cannot meet: DCAA-compliant accounting system, incurred cost submissions, forward pricing rate proposals, cost allowability rules. Build these systems before you need them. The cost of implementing government accounting is $250K-500K upfront and $100K-250K/year ongoing. Compare that to the cost of losing a $50M DOD contract because your accounting system failed a DCAA audit. It's the cheapest insurance you'll ever buy."
