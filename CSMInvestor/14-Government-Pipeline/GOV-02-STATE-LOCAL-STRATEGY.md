## 1.0 EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

State and local governments represent a $700M+ addressable market for CSM over 10 years. While individual state procurements are smaller than federal contracts, they are often faster (6-12 months vs. 18-36 months for federal), more numerous (50 states × multiple agencies each), and less prone to the political gridlock that can delay federal programs.

State energy offices, public utility commissions, emergency management agencies, municipal utilities, and state transportation departments all have procurement authority for grid resilience equipment. Additionally, FEMA grant programs (BRIC, HMPG) flow through state governments, making them the pass-through for significant federal funding.

This document outlines CSM's state and local government strategy, including pilot state identification, procurement processes, grant program navigation, and the 50-state outreach program (a subset of CSM's 200-country CSMReach network).

## 3.0 MUNICIPAL AND LOCAL GOVERNMENT

### 3.1 Municipal Utilities

2,000+ municipal utilities in the US serve 15% of the population. They are locally governed (city council or utility board), making procurement faster than state-regulated utilities.

**CSM Municipal Strategy:**
- Partner with American Public Power Association (APPA) for collective procurement
- Offer volume discounts for municipal utility consortia
- Municipal leasing program (spread hardware cost over 5-10 years through municipal bonds or lease-purchase)

### 3.2 Rural Electric Cooperatives

900+ rural electric co-ops (NRECA members) serve 42 million Americans across 56% of the US landmass. Many are in high-GIC-exposure regions (northern plains, upper Midwest).

**CSM Rural Co-op Strategy:**
- Partner with NRECA for cooperative-wide procurement
- Leverage USDA Rural Utilities Service (RUS) loan and grant programs for co-op infrastructure investment
- Co-op business model aligns with CSM mission (member-owned, not profit-maximizing)

### 3.3 Large Cities

Cities with their own emergency management and infrastructure budgets:

| City | Rationale | Procurement Pathway |

## 4.0 STATE PROCUREMENT PROCESSES

### 4.1 Typical State Procurement Flow

```
Requirements Definition (30-90 days)
→ RFP/RFQ Issuance (30-60 days public notice)
→ Proposal Submission (30-60 days)
→ Evaluation (30-90 days)
→ Negotiation (30-60 days)
→ Contract Award (15-30 days)
→ Protest Period (10-30 days)
→ Notice to Proceed
```

**Total:** 6-12 months for standard state procurement (faster than federal).

### 4.2 Cooperative Purchasing

Many states participate in cooperative purchasing agreements where one state's contract can be used by other states. Key cooperatives:
- NASPO ValuePoint (National Association of State Procurement Officials)
- Sourcewell (MN-based; national reach)
- OMNIA Partners
- HGACBuy (TX-based)

**CSM Strategy:** Secure a contract through one cooperative (e.g., Sourcewell) and make it available to all participating states and local governments — one procurement, 50-state reach.

### 4.3 Emergency Procurement

Most states have emergency procurement provisions that can bypass normal RFP processes during declared emergencies. A Carrington Event would trigger these provisions — but CSM must have products ready to deploy when the emergency occurs. Pre-positioning through normal procurement is the goal; emergency procurement is the fallback.

## 5.0 FEMA GRANT PROGRAMS

### 5.1 Building Resilient Infrastructure and Communities (BRIC)

BRIC provides pre-disaster mitigation grants to states, tribes, and territories. CSM products qualify under "utility and infrastructure mitigation" and "wind/storm retrofitting" categories.

**BRIC Funding:**
- Annual appropriation: $500M-$1B+ (varies)
- Federal share: 75% (25% non-federal match required)
- Eligible applicants: States, tribes, territories; pass-through to local governments and utilities
- Application cycle: Annual (typically fall)

**CSM Strategy:** Partner with state emergency management agencies to include CSM hardening in BRIC applications. Provide template grant language and cost-benefit analysis (BCA) to support applications.

### 5.2 Hazard Mitigation Grant Program (HMPG)

HMPG provides post-disaster mitigation funding. Available after a presidential disaster declaration. Post-Carrington, HMPG would be a massive funding source for CSM products — but CSM's goal is to prevent the disaster, not profit from its aftermath.

### 5.3 State Infrastructure Banks

Many states operate infrastructure banks that provide low-interest loans for infrastructure projects, including grid resilience. CSM can work with utilities to access State Revolving Fund (SRF) programs.

## 6.0 PUBLIC UTILITY COMMISSION ENGAGEMENT

### 6.1 The PUC Role

PUCs regulate investor-owned utilities. They approve (or reject) utility capital expenditure plans and rate recovery requests. For CSM to sell to IOUs, the PUC must allow the utility to recover the cost from ratepayers.

**CSM Strategy:**
- Educate PUC commissioners on GMD threat and CSM solution
- Provide cost-benefit analysis demonstrating that hardening is cheaper than replacement
- Support utility rate case filings with technical testimony
- Advocate for PUC orders requiring utilities to assess and mitigate GMD risk

### 6.2 Model PUC Engagement

For each target state, CSM will:
1. Request informational meeting with PUC staff
2. Present GMD threat overview and CSM solution
3. Provide written testimony-quality documentation
4. Offer to participate in PUC workshops or technical conferences on grid resilience
5. Support utility-specific proceedings with intervener testimony

## 7.0 THE 50-STATE CSMREACH PROGRAM

### 7.1 State-Level CSMReach

CSM's 200-country CSMReach program includes a 50-state component:

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Governor's Office Letter | Formal introduction to each governor's energy/homeland security policy advisor |
| State Energy Office Briefing | Customized GMD threat assessment for each state's grid |
| PUC Commissioner Briefing | Technical presentation to each state's PUC |
| State Emergency Management Agency | Integration with state emergency operations plans |
| State Legislature Briefing | Education for relevant legislative committees (Energy, Utilities, Homeland Security) |
| Municipal League / Association of Counties | Outreach to local government associations |

### 7.2 State-Specific Customization

Each state's CSMReach package includes:
- GIC exposure map for the state (magnetic latitude overlay on grid topology)
- Count of at-risk transformers and substations
- Economic impact model (GDP loss, jobs lost, tax revenue loss) for a Carrington Event in that state
- CSM product recommendations (which products address which vulnerabilities)
- Cost estimate for comprehensive state hardening
- FEMA BRIC/HMPG funding opportunity analysis
