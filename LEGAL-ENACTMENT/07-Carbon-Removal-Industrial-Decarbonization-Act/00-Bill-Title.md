## A BILL

To accelerate the deployment of carbon dioxide removal technologies, to establish a comprehensive framework for the decarbonization of the industrial sector, to authorize appropriations for direct air capture hubs, point-source carbon capture retrofit programs, green cement and steel production tax credits, industrial heat electrification, carbon removal measurement and verification infrastructure, clean industrial manufacturing parks, and a carbon border adjustment mechanism, and for other purposes.

### SECTION 2. FINDINGS AND PURPOSES.

**(a) Findings.** — Congress finds the findings set forth in the Findings Document of this Act to be accurate and material to the purposes of this legislation.

**(b) Purposes.** — The purposes of this Act are to —

(1) achieve not less than 1 gigaton per year of carbon dioxide removal capacity within the United States by calendar year 2035;

(2) reduce industrial sector greenhouse gas emissions by not less than 50 percent from 2022 levels by calendar year 2035;

(3) establish the United States as the global leader in carbon removal and clean industrial manufacturing technologies;

(4) create not fewer than 300,000 domestic jobs in carbon removal, clean manufacturing, and related supply chains;

(5) prevent carbon leakage through a carbon border adjustment mechanism consistent with international trade obligations;

(6) ensure that the benefits of carbon removal and industrial decarbonization investments flow to all communities, including environmental justice communities and energy transition communities; and

(7) catalyze not less than $400 billion in domestic industrial output through public and private investment in carbon removal and clean industrial infrastructure.

### SECTION 3. DEFINITIONS.

In this Act:

**(1) CARBON DIOXIDE REMOVAL (CDR).** — The term "carbon dioxide removal" means anthropogenic activities that remove carbon dioxide from the atmosphere and durably store it in geological, terrestrial, or ocean reservoirs, or in products, where the storage is reasonably expected to persist for a period of not less than 100 years.

**(2) DIRECT AIR CAPTURE (DAC).** — The term "direct air capture" means the process of separating carbon dioxide from ambient air using chemical, physical, or biological means to produce a concentrated stream of carbon dioxide for utilization or permanent storage.

**(3) POINT-SOURCE CARBON CAPTURE.** — The term "point-source carbon capture" means the separation and concentration of carbon dioxide from flue gas or process emissions at an industrial facility, power plant, or other stationary source.

**(4) CLEAN INDUSTRIAL MANUFACTURING PARK.** — The term "clean industrial manufacturing park" means a geographically co-located industrial facility or set of facilities sharing common infrastructure for carbon dioxide transport, hydrogen supply, renewable energy, and carbon capture and storage.

**(5) GREEN CEMENT.** — The term "green cement" means cement produced using processes that reduce lifecycle greenhouse gas emissions by not less than 70 percent compared to conventional ordinary portland cement, including but not limited to calcium silicate-based cement produced without limestone calcination and cement produced using carbon mineralization of industrial wastes.

**(6) GREEN STEEL.** — The term "green steel" means steel produced using processes that reduce lifecycle greenhouse gas emissions by not less than 70 percent compared to conventional blast furnace-basic oxygen furnace steelmaking, including but not limited to direct reduced iron using green hydrogen and electrolytic steel production.

**(7) THERMAL BATTERY.** — The term "thermal battery" means a stationary energy storage system that stores electricity as sensible or latent heat in solid media, such as carbon blocks or graphite, and discharges thermal energy on demand for industrial process heat applications.

**(8) CARBON BORDER ADJUSTMENT.** — The term "carbon border adjustment" means a mechanism that imposes fees on imported goods based on the greenhouse gas emissions associated with their production, equivalent to the carbon costs borne by domestic producers.
