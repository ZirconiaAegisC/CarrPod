# 119TH CONGRESS — CARBON REMOVAL AND INDUSTRIAL DECARBONIZATION ACT OF 2026
# DOCUMENT 02: CONGRESSIONAL FINDINGS

---

## CONGRESSIONAL FINDINGS

### PURSUANT TO THE CARBON REMOVAL AND INDUSTRIAL DECARBONIZATION ACT OF 2026

---

**The Congress finds the following:**

### PART A — CLIMATE IMPERATIVE

**Finding 1.** The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC), in its Sixth Assessment Report (AR6) published in 2021 through 2023, concluded that global net anthropogenic greenhouse gas emissions must decline by approximately 43 percent by 2030 relative to 2019 levels, reach net zero by 2050, and achieve net negative emissions thereafter to limit global warming to 1.5 degrees Celsius above pre-industrial levels without significant overshoot.

**Finding 2.** The IPCC AR6 further concluded that all modeled pathways limiting warming to 1.5 degrees Celsius require carbon dioxide removal at a scale of 5 to 10 gigatons of CO2 per year by 2050, exceeding current global carbon dioxide removal capacity of approximately 0.04 gigatons per year by more than two orders of magnitude.

**Finding 3.** The United States Global Change Research Program, in its Fifth National Climate Assessment published in 2023, found that the United States is already experiencing significant climate-related damages, with the annual cost of extreme weather events exceeding $150 billion in multiple recent years, and that these costs will accelerate without aggressive emissions reductions and carbon dioxide removal deployment.

### PART B — INDUSTRIAL EMISSIONS PROFILE

**Finding 4.** The industrial sector accounts for approximately 30 percent of total United States greenhouse gas emissions, representing the largest-emitting sector when electricity-related emissions are allocated to end-use sectors, according to the Environmental Protection Agency Inventory of U.S. Greenhouse Gas Emissions and Sinks.

**Finding 5.** Cement production contributes approximately 8 percent of global carbon dioxide emissions, with process emissions from limestone calcination accounting for approximately 60 percent of cement's carbon footprint — emissions that cannot be eliminated through energy efficiency or fuel switching alone.

**Finding 6.** Iron and steel production contributes approximately 7 to 9 percent of global carbon dioxide emissions, with the dominant blast furnace-basic oxygen furnace route emitting approximately 1.85 tons of CO2 per ton of crude steel produced.

**Finding 7.** The United States currently has approximately 100 cement plants and 110 steel production facilities in operation, many of which are located in or near environmental justice communities that bear disproportionate burdens of air pollution and would benefit from industrial decarbonization investments.

**Finding 8.** Industrial process heat — the thermal energy used in manufacturing processes such as melting, forming, drying, and chemical reactions — accounts for approximately 50 percent of industrial energy consumption and is predominantly supplied by fossil fuel combustion at temperatures that can be achieved by emerging thermal battery and electrification technologies.

### PART C — CURRENT CARBON REMOVAL CAPACITY AND TECHNOLOGY READINESS

**Finding 9.** Global direct air capture capacity in 2026 stands at approximately 0.00004 gigatons per year. The largest operational facilities include Climeworks' Orca plant in Iceland (4,000 tons CO2 per year) and Mammoth plant (36,000 tons CO2 per year), with Carbon Engineering's STRATOS facility in Texas (500,000 tons CO2 per year capacity) currently under construction and expected to commence operations by 2028.

**Finding 10.** Direct air capture costs currently range from $600 to $1,000 per ton of CO2 captured. Engineering analysis and technology learning curves, including those published by the National Academies of Sciences, Engineering, and Medicine, indicate that costs must decline to approximately $100 per ton for widespread commercial deployment at gigaton scale.

**Finding 11.** The Department of Energy, in its 2022 report "Pathways to Commercial Liftoff: Carbon Management," identified carbon management as a critical pathway to achieve net zero emissions and recommended deployment of 400 to 1,800 million tons of annual carbon management capacity by 2050.

**Finding 12.** The DOE DAC Hubs program, authorized at $3.5 billion under the Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act of 2021 (Public Law 117-58), has funded four initial DAC Hub projects. The DOE received applications representing more than $10 billion in project proposals, demonstrating substantial unmet demand for carbon removal deployment support.

### PART D — EXISTING POLICY FRAMEWORK

**Finding 13.** Section 45Q of the Internal Revenue Code, as amended by the Inflation Reduction Act of 2022 (Public Law 117-169), provides a tax credit of $180 per metric ton for qualified carbon dioxide captured via direct air capture and disposed of in secure geologic storage. While this incentive has stimulated investment, analysis by the Department of the Treasury indicates that additional policy support is needed to achieve gigaton-scale deployment and to meet the cost reduction targets identified by the DOE Earthshots Initiative.

**Finding 14.** The Inflation Reduction Act of 2022 significantly enhanced the Section 45Q credit by lowering capture thresholds, extending the commence-construction window to January 1, 2033, and providing direct pay optionality for tax-exempt entities. These reforms, together with expanded credit values, provide a foundation upon which this Act builds.

**Finding 15.** The European Union has adopted a Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (Regulation (EU) 2023/956) that entered into force on May 17, 2023, with transitional reporting requirements beginning October 1, 2023, and full implementation expected in 2026. The absence of a comparable United States carbon border adjustment mechanism places domestic industry at competitive disadvantage and risks carbon leakage, whereby emissions-intensive production shifts to jurisdictions with less stringent climate policies.

### PART E — TECHNOLOGY AND INNOVATION FINDINGS

**Finding 16.** Breakthrough cement technologies developed by United States-based companies, including Brimstone Energy (calcium silicate-based cement produced without limestone calcination) and Sublime Systems (electrochemical cement production), have demonstrated at pilot scale the technical viability of cement production with near-zero process emissions, achieving up to 100 percent reduction in process-related CO2 emissions compared to conventional ordinary portland cement.

**Finding 17.** Boston Metal's molten oxide electrolysis technology has demonstrated at pilot scale the ability to produce steel without coal or coke, using electricity to directly reduce iron ore, with the potential to eliminate all direct CO2 process emissions from steelmaking when powered by zero-carbon electricity.

**Finding 18.** CarbonCure Technologies has deployed CO2 mineralization in concrete at over 750 concrete plants worldwide, demonstrating that injection of captured CO2 into fresh concrete during mixing permanently mineralizes the CO2 while improving concrete compressive strength and enabling reductions in cement content.

**Finding 19.** Antora Energy has developed thermal battery technology using solid carbon blocks heated to temperatures exceeding 1,500 degrees Celsius by renewable electricity, capable of delivering industrial-grade heat for cement kilns, steel furnaces, and chemical processing at costs competitive with fossil fuels.

**Finding 20.** Mainspring Energy has developed a linear generator technology that converts chemical energy in zero-carbon fuels such as hydrogen and ammonia to electricity with high efficiency and ultra-low emissions, suitable for flexible on-site power generation at industrial facilities and integration with intermittent renewable energy sources.

### PART F — WORKFORCE AND ECONOMIC OPPORTUNITY

**Finding 21.** The Department of Energy, in its 2023 United States Energy and Employment Report, found that the clean energy and carbon management sectors employed approximately 3.1 million workers in 2022 and that these sectors are growing faster than the overall economy.

**Finding 22.** International Energy Agency analysis indicates that the global market for carbon dioxide removal and clean industrial technologies is projected to exceed $1 trillion annually by 2050, and that countries achieving early-mover advantage in these technologies will capture disproportionate economic benefits.

**Finding 23.** Environmental justice communities — defined as communities disproportionately burdened by environmental pollution and climate change impacts — face cumulative health and economic burdens from industrial emissions, and stand to benefit from the air quality improvements, job creation, and infrastructure investments associated with industrial decarbonization.

### PART G — GEOLOGIC STORAGE POTENTIAL

**Finding 24.** The United States Geological Survey, in its 2013 assessment of geologic carbon dioxide storage resources, estimated that the United States possesses between 2,400 and 3,700 gigatons of subsurface carbon dioxide storage capacity in saline formations, sufficient to store more than 500 years of total United States CO2 emissions at current rates.

**Finding 25.** The Carbfix method — in which CO2 dissolved in water is injected into reactive basaltic rock formations, where it mineralizes into stable carbonate minerals within approximately two years — has been successfully demonstrated in Iceland at commercial scale since 2014. The United States possesses substantial basaltic formations, including the Columbia River Basalt Group in the Pacific Northwest, representing a large-scale permanent storage opportunity.

---

*These findings are incorporated by reference into Section 2 of the Carbon Removal and Industrial Decarbonization Act of 2026 and shall inform the interpretation and implementation of all provisions of this Act.*
