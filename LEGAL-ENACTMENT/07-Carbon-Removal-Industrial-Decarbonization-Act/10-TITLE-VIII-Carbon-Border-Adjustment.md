# 119TH CONGRESS — CARBON REMOVAL AND INDUSTRIAL DECARBONIZATION ACT OF 2026
# DOCUMENT 10: TITLE VIII — CARBON BORDER ADJUSTMENT

---

## TITLE VIII — CARBON BORDER ADJUSTMENT

### SECTION 801. UNITED STATES CARBON BORDER ADJUSTMENT MECHANISM.

**(a) Short Title.** — This title may be cited as the "United States Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism Act of 2026" or "U.S. CBAM Act."

**(b) Establishment.** — Not later than January 1, 2028, the Secretary of the Treasury, in coordination with the Secretary of Commerce, the Administrator of the Environmental Protection Agency, and the United States Trade Representative, shall establish a United States Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM).

**(c) Purpose.** — The purposes of the U.S. CBAM are to:

(1) prevent carbon leakage whereby emissions-intensive production shifts from the United States to jurisdictions with less stringent climate policies;

(2) ensure that imported goods bear a carbon cost equivalent to the carbon cost borne by domestic producers;

(3) incentivize trading partners to adopt and enforce robust greenhouse gas emission reduction policies; and

(4) generate revenue to support domestic carbon removal and industrial decarbonization programs.

**(d) Covered Goods.** — The U.S. CBAM shall apply to imports of the following goods:

(1) cement and cement clinker classified under Harmonized Tariff Schedule heading 2523;

(2) iron and steel products classified under HTS chapters 72 and 73;

(3) aluminum and aluminum products classified under HTS chapter 76;

(4) fertilizers classified under HTS chapter 31;

(5) hydrogen classified under HTS heading 2804.10; and

(6) electricity transmitted across United States borders.

**(e) Covered Emissions.** — The CBAM shall account for:

(1) direct emissions from production of covered goods, including process emissions and combustion emissions;

(2) indirect emissions from electricity consumption during production; and

(3) upstream emissions from extraction, processing, and transportation of feedstocks and fuels, to the extent that such emissions are accounted for in the carbon costs borne by domestic producers.

**(f) Exemptions.** — The CBAM shall not apply to:

(1) goods originating from least-developed countries as designated by the United Nations;

(2) goods originating from countries with a domestic explicit carbon price, including a carbon tax or emissions trading system, that the Secretary of the Treasury has determined is equivalent to or higher than the effective carbon price borne by United States domestic producers for the relevant sector; and

(3) goods with embedded emissions below de minimis thresholds to be established by the Secretary.

### SECTION 802. IMPORT FEES FOR CARBON-INTENSIVE GOODS.

**(a) CBAM Certificate Requirement.** — Beginning on January 1, 2029, importers of covered goods shall be required to purchase CBAM certificates corresponding to the embedded emissions in imported goods.

**(b) Certificate Price.** — The price of CBAM certificates shall be equal to the average weekly auction settlement price for allowances under the most stringent state or regional greenhouse gas emissions trading program in the United States, or a price of $50 per metric ton of CO2-equivalent, whichever is higher, adjusted annually for inflation based on the Consumer Price Index.

**(c) Embedded Emissions Calculation.** — The quantity of CBAM certificates required shall be determined based on:

(1) for goods originating from countries that have third-party verified facility-level emissions data: actual verified emissions of the producing facility;

(2) for goods from countries without verified facility-level data: default values based on the average emission intensity of the worst-performing 10 percent of facilities in the exporting country; and

(3) if country-specific data is unavailable: default values based on the average emission intensity of the worst-performing 10 percent of facilities in the United States for the relevant sector.

**(d) Credit for Foreign Carbon Price.** — Importers may deduct from the CBAM certificate requirement an amount corresponding to the explicit carbon price already paid in the country of origin, provided that the carbon price is mandatory, verifiable, and not subject to rebate upon export.

**(e) CBAM Certificate Management.** — CBAM certificates shall:

(1) be electronic and tracked in a registry maintained by the Secretary of the Treasury;

(2) not be tradable, transferable, or bankable for future compliance periods;

(3) be purchased by importers on a quarterly basis corresponding to imports in the preceding quarter; and

(4) be priced as of the date of purchase.

### SECTION 803. PREVENTION OF CARBON LEAKAGE.

**(a) Carbon Leakage Risk Assessment.** — The Secretary of Commerce, in coordination with the United States Trade Representative, shall conduct an annual assessment of carbon leakage risk for each covered sector, which shall include:

(1) analysis of changes in domestic production, imports, and exports for covered goods;

(2) analysis of greenhouse gas emissions trends in covered sectors domestically and internationally;

(3) assessment of the carbon pricing and climate policies of major trading partners; and

(4) identification of sectors at elevated risk of carbon leakage not currently covered by the CBAM.

**(b) Sectoral Expansion.** — Upon a finding by the Secretary of Commerce that a sector not currently covered by the CBAM faces significant carbon leakage risk, the Secretary of the Treasury shall, within 18 months, issue regulations expanding the CBAM to cover imports of goods from such sector.

**(c) Export Rebate Mechanism.** — Domestic producers of covered goods that export to countries without equivalent carbon pricing policies shall be eligible for a rebate of the embedded carbon cost, equivalent to the CBAM certificate price, to be applied as a refundable tax credit against the exporter's federal income tax liability.

**(d) WTO Consistency.** — The Secretary of the Treasury, in coordination with the United States Trade Representative, shall ensure that the U.S. CBAM is designed and implemented in a manner consistent with the obligations of the United States under the Marrakesh Agreement Establishing the World Trade Organization.

### SECTION 804. INTERNATIONAL COOPERATION AND EQUIVALENCE AGREEMENTS.

**(a) Equivalence Determinations.** — The Secretary of the Treasury, in coordination with the Secretary of State and the United States Trade Representative, may determine that a foreign country's carbon pricing system, emissions trading system, or regulatory emission reduction program is equivalent to the carbon costs borne by United States domestic producers for purposes of this title.

**(b) Criteria for Equivalence.** — In making an equivalence determination, the Secretary shall consider:

(1) the stringency of carbon pricing or emission reduction requirements;

(2) the coverage of sectors and emissions;

(3) the enforceability and monitoring, reporting, and verification requirements;

(4) the degree of exemption, rebate, or compensation provided to trade-exposed sectors; and

(5) the country's compliance with its Nationally Determined Contribution under the Paris Agreement.

**(c) Climate Club Participation.** — The Secretary of State shall pursue negotiations toward the establishment of an international Climate Club of countries that apply comparable carbon pricing mechanisms, as proposed by the G7, to facilitate mutual equivalence determinations, harmonize embedded emissions calculation methodologies, and coordinate carbon leakage prevention measures.

**(d) EU CBAM Coordination.** — The United States Trade Representative shall pursue discussions with the European Commission toward a mutual recognition agreement that would allow goods that have complied with either the U.S. CBAM or the EU CBAM to be exempt from the other jurisdiction's mechanism, based on a determination of equivalent carbon costs and embedded emissions methodologies.

---

### SECTION 805. CBAM REVENUE ALLOCATION.

**(a) Revenue Distribution.** — Revenues generated from the sale of CBAM certificates shall be deposited in the Carbon Removal Trust Fund established under subsection (b) and allocated as follows:

(1) 50 percent shall be available for appropriations to carry out Titles I through VII of this Act;

(2) 25 percent shall be available for appropriations for international climate finance, including contributions to the Green Climate Fund, adaptation assistance to climate-vulnerable developing countries, and technical assistance for developing countries to establish carbon pricing systems and MRV infrastructure;

(3) 15 percent shall be available for transition assistance to energy communities and workers in trade-exposed industries, including income support, health care continuation, pension protection, and retraining; and

(4) 10 percent shall be available for the development and maintenance of the CBAM administrative infrastructure, including the CBAM certificate registry, verification systems, and enforcement.

**(b) Carbon Removal Trust Fund.** — There is established in the Treasury of the United States a Carbon Removal Trust Fund. Amounts in the Fund shall be available, as provided in appropriations Acts, for the purposes specified in subsection (a).

**(c) Revenue Projection.** — The Congressional Budget Office shall estimate annual CBAM revenues and report such estimates to the Committees on the Budget of the House of Representatives and the Senate annually. Based on modeling of current import volumes and emission intensities for covered goods, CBAM revenues are projected to exceed $50,000,000,000 per year at steady state.

*End of Title VIII*
