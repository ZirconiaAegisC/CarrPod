# DOCUMENT 01: EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

### CARBON REMOVAL AND INDUSTRIAL DECARBONIZATION ACT (CRIDA) OF 2026

**Prepared for the Committee on Energy and Commerce, Committee on Ways and Means, Committee on Science, Space, and Technology, and Committee on Environment and Public Works**

---

### I. OVERVIEW

The Carbon Removal and Industrial Decarbonization Act of 2026 (CRIDA) establishes a comprehensive statutory framework to position the United States as the global leader in carbon dioxide removal (CDR) technologies and clean industrial manufacturing. The Act authorizes $80 billion in federal investment over a ten-year period and projects the mobilization of an additional $320 billion in private capital, yielding total industrial output exceeding $400 billion. The legislation targets achievement of 1 gigaton per year of domestic carbon removal capacity by 2035 and a 50 percent reduction in industrial sector greenhouse gas emissions from 2022 baseline levels within the same timeframe.

### II. PROBLEM STATEMENT

The industrial sector accounts for approximately 30 percent of United States greenhouse gas emissions. Cement production alone contributes roughly 8 percent of global carbon dioxide emissions, while steelmaking accounts for an additional 7 to 9 percent. Current global carbon dioxide removal capacity stands at approximately 0.04 gigatons per year — orders of magnitude below the 5 to 10 gigatons per year that the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) Sixth Assessment Report identifies as necessary by 2050 to limit warming to 1.5 degrees Celsius.

Direct air capture costs remain at $600 to $1,000 per ton of CO2, well above the estimated $100 per ton threshold required for widespread commercial deployment. The United States possesses substantial advantages — abundant geologic storage capacity, a skilled industrial workforce, world-leading research institutions, and existing carbon capture incentives under Internal Revenue Code Section 45Q — yet lacks the coordinated policy framework, infrastructure investment, and demand-side certainty required to achieve gigaton-scale carbon removal and deep industrial decarbonization.

### III. PROGRAM ARCHITECTURE

CRIDA is organized into eight substantive titles and supporting titles for funding, implementation, and economic analysis:

| Title | Subject | Authorization |
|-------|---------|---------------|
| Title I | Direct Air Capture Scale-Up | $30 billion |
| Title II | Point-Source Carbon Capture | $15 billion |
| Title III | CO2 Utilization and Permanent Storage | Included in Titles I/II/IV |
| Title IV | Green Cement and Steel Deployment | $15 billion |
| Title V | Industrial Heat Decarbonization | $10 billion |
| Title VI | Carbon Removal Verification (MRV) | $5 billion |
| Title VII | Clean Industrial Parks | $5 billion |
| Title VIII | Carbon Border Adjustment (CBAM) | Revenue-positive |

**Total Authorization: $80 billion over fiscal years 2027 through 2036.**

### IV. KEY PROVISIONS

**Title I — Direct Air Capture Scale-Up.** Expands the Section 45Q tax credit to $250 per metric ton of CO2 captured via direct air capture and stored in secure geologic formations. Authorizes expansion of the Department of Energy DAC Hubs program from the current four hubs to ten hubs, building upon the $3.5 billion program established by the Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act. Establishes a federal government procurement commitment serving as anchor buyer for verified carbon removal credits, providing demand certainty to accelerate private investment.

**Title II — Point-Source Carbon Capture.** Expands Section 45Q to $100 per ton for industrial point-source capture at cement, steel, chemical, and refinery facilities. Authorizes $15 billion for competitive grants covering up to 50 percent of capital costs for carbon capture retrofit projects at existing industrial facilities. Establishes a carbon dioxide pipeline transportation infrastructure program, including federal siting authority and cost-sharing for trunk pipeline development connecting capture facilities to storage sites.

**Title III — CO2 Utilization and Permanent Storage.** Creates a performance-based tax credit of $50 per ton of CO2 permanently mineralized in concrete products, modeled on the CarbonCure Technologies approach. Establishes a regulatory framework for permanent geologic storage via in-situ mineral carbonation based on the Carbfix method. Mandates EPA rulemaking to reduce Class VI well permit processing time to not more than 18 months from application submission.

**Title IV — Green Cement and Steel Deployment.** Establishes production tax credits for green cement ($40 per ton) and green steel ($85 per ton) that achieve lifecycle emission reductions of at least 70 percent compared to conventional production. Creates federal procurement preferences under the Buy Clean America program requiring federal agencies to prioritize low-carbon cement, steel, and other construction materials. Authorizes $5 billion in transition grants for legacy cement and steel plants to retool for clean production.

**Title V — Industrial Heat Decarbonization.** Establishes a 30 percent investment tax credit for thermal battery systems, such as those developed by Antora Energy, that displace fossil fuel combustion for industrial process heat. Creates a 30 percent credit for clean distributed power systems including linear generator technology as developed by Mainspring Energy. Authorizes the $10 billion Industrial Heat Transformation Fund to provide grants, loans, and loan guarantees for electrification of industrial boilers, furnaces, kilns, and process heating systems.

**Title VI — Carbon Removal Verification.** Establishes a National Measurement, Reporting, and Verification (MRV) Standard for carbon dioxide removal, incorporating digital MRV methodologies utilizing ground-based sensors, aerial monitoring, and satellite-based observation. Creates a Federal Carbon Credit Registry administered by the Department of Energy in coordination with the Environmental Protection Agency. Establishes criminal penalties for fraudulent carbon credit issuance or trading, including fines of up to $1 million per violation and imprisonment of up to 10 years.

**Title VII — Clean Industrial Parks.** Authorizes $5 billion for the establishment of not fewer than ten clean industrial manufacturing parks co-locating cement, steel, chemical, and carbon removal facilities with shared green hydrogen pipeline infrastructure, CCUS backbone networks, and dedicated renewable energy generation. Parks will be selected through a competitive process administered by the Department of Energy.

**Title VIII — Carbon Border Adjustment.** Establishes a United States Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM) modeled on the European Union CBAM. Importers of cement, steel, aluminum, fertilizers, hydrogen, and electricity will be required to purchase CBAM certificates corresponding to the embedded emissions in imported goods, at a price equivalent to the effective carbon price borne by domestic producers. Projected to generate not less than $50 billion in annual federal revenue.

### VI. IMPLEMENTATION TIMELINE

| Phase | Years | Key Milestones |
|-------|-------|---------------|
| Phase I — Program Establishment | FY 2027-2028 | Rulemaking, program guidance, initial grant solicitations |
| Phase II — Early Deployment | FY 2028-2031 | First DAC Hubs operational, first industrial parks under construction, MRV system operational |
| Phase III — Scale-Up | FY 2031-2034 | Carbon removal capacity reaches 500 Mt/yr, CBAM fully phased in, green materials procurement mandatory |
| Phase IV — Full Capacity | FY 2034-2036 | 1 Gt/yr carbon removal achieved, industrial emissions 50% below baseline, program review and reauthorization |

### VII. FUNDING MECHANISM

The $80 billion authorization of appropriations shall be funded through a combination of general revenues, CBAM certificate revenues, and reprogramming of certain fossil fuel subsidies identified by the Government Accountability Office. CBAM revenues in excess of program costs shall be deposited in a Carbon Removal Trust Fund to be used for long-term monitoring of geologic storage sites, community transition assistance, and contributions to international climate finance commitments.
