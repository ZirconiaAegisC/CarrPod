                             SECTION 2
================================================================================

    SEC. 2. FINDINGS AND DECLARATIONS.

    The Congress finds and declares the following:

================================================================================

                           PART A — IMPORT DEPENDENCE

    (1) The United States currently imports approximately ninety-seven
    percent (97%) of its lithium requirements, with the majority of
    imports originating from or transiting through nations in which the
    People's Republic of China exercises substantial influence over
    extraction and processing operations.

    (2) The United States Geological Survey's Mineral Commodity
    Summaries for 2025 and 2026 confirm that domestic lithium mine
    production in calendar year 2025 totaled approximately 5,000 metric
    tons, representing less than three percent (3%) of global output,
    and that the sole domestic producing facility, Silver Peak in Esmeralda
    County, Nevada, has an annual production capacity of approximately
    6,000 metric tons of lithium carbonate equivalent.

    (3) Global lithium demand is projected to increase from approximately
    180,000 metric tons of lithium carbonate equivalent in 2025 to between
    1,200,000 and 1,800,000 metric tons by 2035, an increase of more than
    600 percent (600%), driven primarily by electric vehicle battery
    manufacturing, grid-scale energy storage deployment, and defense
    procurement requirements.

    (4) The domestic content requirements established under Section
    45X of the Internal Revenue Code of 1986, as amended by the Inflation
    Reduction Act of 2022 (Public Law 117-169), and the associated battery
    component sourcing requirements for the clean vehicle credit under
    Section 30D of the Internal Revenue Code, cannot be fully satisfied
    without a substantial expansion of domestic lithium mining, processing,
    and manufacturing capacity.

================================================================================

                PART B — FOREIGN ADVERSARY CONTROL OF SUPPLY CHAIN

    (5) The People's Republic of China currently controls more than
    sixty-five percent (65%) of global lithium refining and processing
    capacity, including approximately seventy-two percent (72%) of
    lithium hydroxide conversion capacity and approximately sixty-eight
    percent (68%) of lithium carbonate refining capacity.

    (6) Chinese state-owned and state-directed enterprises have acquired
    controlling interests in lithium mining operations in Australia,
    Chile, Argentina, Zimbabwe, the Democratic Republic of the Congo,
    Mali, and other nations, through a coordinated strategy of
    equity investment, infrastructure-for-resources agreements, and
    concessional lending through the Belt and Road Initiative.

    (7) The concentration of lithium processing capacity within the
    People's Republic of China creates a structural vulnerability in
    global supply chains that could be exploited through export
    restrictions, as demonstrated by PRC export controls on rare earth
    elements in 2010 and on graphite and gallium in 2023, and through
    similar actions affecting germanium, antimony, and other critical
    minerals.

    (8) The International Energy Agency has classified lithium as a
    mineral facing "high" or "very high" supply risk due to geographic
    concentration of reserves, processing capacity concentration in
    a single nation, long lead times for new mine development, and
    declining ore grades at existing operations.

================================================================================

                     PART C — DOMESTIC RESOURCE POTENTIAL

    (9) The Smackover Formation, a Jurassic-age brine aquifer extending
    across southern Arkansas, eastern Texas, and northern Louisiana,
    contains an estimated total lithium resource exceeding 4,000,000
    metric tons of lithium carbonate equivalent, representing the
    largest known lithium brine resource in North America and one of
    the largest such resources in the world.

    (10) The Thacker Pass lithium deposit in Humboldt County, Nevada,
    operated by Lithium Americas Corp., holds measured and indicated
    resources of approximately 13,700,000 metric tons of lithium
    carbonate equivalent, making it the largest known sedimentary
    lithium deposit in the United States and the third-largest such
    deposit globally.

    (11) The Salton Sea Known Geothermal Resource Area in Imperial
    County, California, contains an estimated 18,000,000 metric tons
    of lithium carbonate equivalent dissolved in geothermal brines,
    representing a potential annual production capacity of 600,000
    metric tons of lithium carbonate equivalent through Direct Lithium
    Extraction technology coupled with existing geothermal power
    generation infrastructure.

    (12) Additional domestic lithium resources exist in the Clayton
    Valley of Nevada, the Great Salt Lake of Utah, the Paradox Basin,
    spodumene-bearing pegmatites in North Carolina and South Dakota,
    and lithium-bearing clays in Oregon and Nevada, collectively
    representing sufficient domestic resources to meet projected
    United States lithium demand through 2050 and beyond.

================================================================================

                   PART D — NATIONAL SECURITY IMPLICATIONS

    (13) The Department of Defense has identified lithium as essential
    to every major weapons system and platform modernization program,
    including the F-35 Joint Strike Fighter, the Columbia-class
    submarine, unmanned aerial and underwater systems, portable power
    for dismounted operations, directed energy weapons, and the
    electrification of the non-tactical vehicle fleet.

    (14) The Defense Production Act of 1950 (50 U.S.C. 4501 et seq.)
    authorizes the President to ensure the availability of critical
    materials for national defense, and the Department of Defense has
    previously exercised Defense Production Act Title III authority for
    rare earth elements and other strategic materials, confirming the
    precedent and necessity of government intervention in critical
    mineral supply chains.

    (15) The Quadrennial Defense Review, the National Defense Strategy,
    and the Department of Defense Critical Minerals Strategy each
    identify supply chain dependence on foreign adversaries for
    critical minerals as a Tier One national security vulnerability
    requiring immediate and sustained corrective action.

================================================================================

                  PART E — ECONOMIC COMPETITIVENESS

    (16) The domestic critical minerals industry, if developed to
    the scale authorized under this Act, is projected to support
    approximately eighty-five thousand (85,000) direct jobs and an
    additional one hundred fifty thousand (150,000) indirect and
    induced jobs in mining, chemical processing, advanced manufacturing,
    construction, transportation, and professional services.

    (17) Battery pack costs represent approximately thirty to forty
    percent (30-40%) of electric vehicle manufacturing costs, and
    domestic lithium supply chain independence is projected to reduce
    battery pack costs by fifteen to twenty-two percent (15-22%)
    relative to import-dependent pricing, accelerating consumer
    adoption of electric vehicles and enhancing the competitiveness
    of the domestic automotive industry.

    (18) The Inflation Reduction Act of 2022 has catalyzed more than
    one hundred twenty billion dollars ($120,000,000,000) in announced
    private sector investment in domestic battery manufacturing,
    electric vehicle assembly, and related supply chain facilities,
    and the full realization of these investments requires a reliable,
    competitively priced domestic supply of lithium and other critical
    battery minerals.

================================================================================

                        PART F — DECLARATION OF POLICY

    (19) It is the policy of the United States to achieve domestic
    lithium and critical minerals supply chain independence by calendar
    year 2035, such that no less than eighty-five percent (85%) of
    annual domestic lithium demand is satisfied by domestically
    extracted, processed, manufactured, and recycled materials.

    (20) It is the policy of the United States that the development
    of domestic critical mineral resources shall be conducted in a
    manner that protects the environment, respects tribal sovereignty
    and consultation rights, provides community benefits to affected
    populations, maintains high labor and safety standards, and
    contributes to the long-term economic prosperity of the Nation.
