                        FINDINGS AND DECLARATIONS

               ADVANCED NUCLEAR ENERGY DEPLOYMENT ACT OF 2026

     Pursuant to Section 3 of the Act, the Congress of the United
     States makes the following findings and declarations:

================================================================================

PART I — FINDINGS OF FACT

     SECTION 3(a). CONTRIBUTION OF EXISTING NUCLEAR FLEET.

     Whereas, nuclear energy currently provides approximately 20 percent
     of the total electricity generated in the United States, and more
     than 50 percent of the Nation's carbon-free electricity, making it
     the single largest source of zero-emission power in the country;

     Whereas, the existing fleet of 92 operating commercial nuclear
     reactors in the United States has maintained an average capacity
     factor exceeding 92 percent over the past decade, demonstrating
     unparalleled reliability among all generating technologies;

     Whereas, the continued operation of the existing nuclear fleet
     prevents the emission of approximately 470 million metric tons of
     carbon dioxide annually, equivalent to removing 100 million
     passenger vehicles from the road each year;

     Whereas, premature closure of existing nuclear plants has resulted
     in measurable increases in greenhouse gas emissions and regional
     air pollution, as documented by the Department of Energy and the
     Energy Information Administration;

     Whereas, maintaining and expanding the nuclear generating fleet
     is essential to achieving the Nation's decarbonization objectives
     while preserving grid reliability and energy affordability.

     SECTION 3(b). NEED FOR ADVANCED NUCLEAR TECHNOLOGIES.

     Whereas, the National Renewable Energy Laboratory, the Electric
     Power Research Institute, and the Massachusetts Institute of
     Technology have each independently concluded that achieving
     economy-wide net-zero emissions by 2050 will require a substantial
     expansion of firm, dispatchable, zero-carbon generating capacity
     beyond what variable renewable resources alone can provide;

     Whereas, small modular reactors (SMRs) offer the potential for
     factory fabrication, reduced construction timelines, enhanced
     safety through passive and inherent safety features, and siting
     flexibility that enables deployment at existing fossil fuel
     plant sites, industrial facilities, and remote communities;

     Whereas, Generation IV advanced reactor designs — including molten
     salt reactors, sodium-cooled fast reactors, and high-temperature
     gas-cooled reactors — offer higher operating temperatures suitable
     for industrial process heat applications, improved fuel utilization,
     reduced waste volumes, and further enhanced safety characteristics
     compared to current light-water reactor designs;

     Whereas, the Nuclear Regulatory Commission, in December 2023,
     issued its first construction permit for a non-light-water reactor
     design in more than 50 years — the Kairos Power Hermes molten salt
     test reactor in Oak Ridge, Tennessee — signaling the beginning of
     a new era in American nuclear innovation;

     Whereas, NuScale Power Corporation's VOYGR SMR design received
     Standard Design Approval from the Nuclear Regulatory Commission
     in August 2020 and full Design Certification in January 2023,
     establishing the regulatory pathway for factory-fabricated small
     modular reactors in the United States;

     Whereas, the TerraPower Natrium sodium-cooled fast reactor with
     integrated molten-salt energy storage, planned for deployment
     at a retiring coal plant site in Kemmerer, Wyoming, represents
     a pioneering coal-to-nuclear transition project with a target
     operational date of 2030;

     Whereas, X-energy LLC's Xe-100 high-temperature gas-cooled pebble
     bed reactor, utilizing TRISO fuel particles, has been selected
     for deployment under the Department of Energy's Advanced Reactor
     Demonstration Program (ARDP), with a first unit targeted at a
     Dow Chemical industrial site in Seadrift, Texas;

     Whereas, major technology companies, including Google LLC and
     Microsoft Corporation, have entered into power purchase agreements
     specifically for advanced nuclear and fusion energy, reflecting
     commercial confidence in these technologies and the growing demand
     for 24/7 carbon-free electricity to power data centers and
     artificial intelligence infrastructure.

     SECTION 3(c). FUSION ENERGY PROGRESS.

     Whereas, global private investment in fusion energy has exceeded
     $15,200,000,000 as of 2026, with more than 40 private fusion
     companies operating worldwide, demonstrating accelerating
     commercial momentum toward fusion power plants;

     Whereas, Helion Energy, Inc., has executed the world's first
     power purchase agreement for fusion-generated electricity with
     Microsoft Corporation, targeting delivery of not less than 50
     megawatts of fusion power by 2029, establishing a commercial
     pathway for fusion energy deployment;

     Whereas, Commonwealth Fusion Systems (CFS), in collaboration
     with the Massachusetts Institute of Technology Plasma Science
     and Fusion Center, has successfully demonstrated high-temperature
     superconducting magnet technology using 18 HTS magnets producing
     magnetic field strengths exceeding 20 tesla, a critical milestone
     for compact tokamak fusion devices, and is constructing the SPARC
     net-energy demonstration facility in Devens, Massachusetts;

     Whereas, TAE Technologies, Inc., has demonstrated plasma
     temperatures exceeding 75 million degrees Celsius using a
     field-reversed configuration with neutral beam injection, an
     approach that avoids the need for deuterium-tritium fuel and
     the associated neutron activation challenges;

     Whereas, General Fusion, Inc., is advancing magnetized target
     fusion technology with support from the Canadian and United
     Kingdom governments, and has announced plans for a demonstration
     facility at the United Kingdom Atomic Energy Authority's Culham
     campus, with projected completion by 2027;

     Whereas, the National Ignition Facility at Lawrence Livermore
     National Laboratory achieved scientific breakeven — producing
     more fusion energy output than the laser energy delivered to the
     target — in December 2022, and has since repeated this achievement
     multiple times, validating inertial confinement fusion as a
     technically viable energy pathway.

     SECTION 3(d). FUEL SUPPLY VULNERABILITY.

     Whereas, the United States currently lacks domestic commercial
     enrichment capability for high-assay low-enriched uranium (HALEU),
     which is required as fuel for the majority of advanced reactor
     designs under development;

     Whereas, the Russian Federation currently controls a dominant
     share of global HALEU enrichment capacity, creating an acute
     national security vulnerability and supply chain risk for the
     deployment of American advanced nuclear technology;

     Whereas, the Inflation Reduction Act of 2022 appropriated
     $700,000,000 for the HALEU Availability Program, but additional
     investment is required to establish a fully independent and
     commercially sustainable domestic HALEU supply chain.

     SECTION 3(e). NRC LICENSING CHALLENGES.

     Whereas, the Nuclear Regulatory Commission was originally
     established under the Energy Reorganization Act of 1974 with
     a regulatory framework designed exclusively for large light-water
     reactors, and the Commission's existing regulations, guidance
     documents, and review practices have not been adequately adapted
     to the unique characteristics of advanced reactor and fusion
     technologies;

     Whereas, the ADVANCE Act of 2024 (enacted as part of the Fire
     Grants and Safety Act, Public Law 118-67) directed the Commission
     to modernize its licensing framework for advanced reactors, but
     additional statutory direction, resources, and timelines are
     required to achieve the regulatory efficiency necessary to meet
     deployment targets.

PART II — DECLARATIONS OF POLICY

     SECTION 3(f). NATIONAL POLICY DECLARATIONS.

     It is hereby declared to be the policy of the United States that:

          (1) NUCLEAR ENERGY LEADERSHIP. — The United States shall
          maintain and strengthen its global leadership in nuclear
          energy technology, from advanced fission through commercial
          fusion, as a matter of national security, economic
          competitiveness, and environmental stewardship.

          (2) REGULATORY EFFICIENCY. — The Nuclear Regulatory Commission
          shall conduct its licensing and oversight activities in a
          manner that is efficient, predictable, technology-inclusive,
          and commensurate with the actual radiological risk posed by
          the facility or activity under review, while preserving the
          Commission's paramount commitment to public health and safety
          and the common defense and security.

          (3) FUEL SUPPLY INDEPENDENCE. — The United States shall
          establish and maintain a commercially viable, fully domestic
          supply chain for all nuclear fuel components required by the
          Nation's existing and advanced reactor fleet, and shall
          eliminate reliance on any foreign adversary for such supply.

          (4) COMMUNITY PARTNERSHIP. — The deployment of advanced
          nuclear energy facilities shall proceed in genuine partnership
          with host communities, with mandatory community benefit
          agreements, robust environmental justice protections, and
          meaningful consultation with affected Tribal Nations.

          (5) WORKFORCE DEVELOPMENT. — The United States shall invest
          in the education, training, and credentialing of the next
          generation of nuclear scientists, engineers, technicians,
          and skilled tradespeople necessary to support a tripling of
          domestic nuclear generating capacity.

          (6) FUSION COMMERCIALIZATION. — The United States shall
          establish a clear, predictable, and technology-appropriate
          regulatory framework for commercial fusion energy systems,
          recognizing that fusion technologies present fundamentally
          different safety, security, and waste characteristics than
          fission reactors and shall not be regulated under the same
          statutory or regulatory provisions.

          (7) INTERNATIONAL COOPERATION. — The United States shall
          cooperate with allied and partner nations on advanced
          nuclear and fusion research, development, demonstration,
          and deployment, including through regulatory harmonization,
          joint research programs, and export promotion, consistent
          with nonproliferation obligations and national security
          requirements.
