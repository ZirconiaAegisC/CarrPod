                     ECONOMIC IMPACT STATEMENT

            ADVANCED NUCLEAR ENERGY DEPLOYMENT ACT OF 2026

================================================================================

I.   EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

     This Economic Impact Statement provides an analysis of the
     projected employment, gross domestic product, electricity cost,
     Federal fiscal, and international trade impacts of the Advanced
     Nuclear Energy Deployment Act of 2026 (ANEDA). The analysis is
     based on modeling conducted by the Department of Energy's Office
     of Nuclear Energy, the Energy Information Administration, and
     independent assessments by the National Renewable Energy
     Laboratory and the Massachusetts Institute of Technology.

     The analysis projects that ANEDA will support approximately
     200,000 construction jobs and 50,000 permanent operations and
     maintenance jobs over the fifteen-year deployment period,
     contribute an estimated $340 billion in cumulative GDP growth,
     and position the United States to capture a substantial share
     of the projected $1 trillion global market for advanced nuclear
     and fusion technologies over the next three decades.

II.  EMPLOYMENT IMPACTS

     (a) CONSTRUCTION EMPLOYMENT.

          (1) Each gigawatt of new nuclear capacity requires
          approximately 3,000 to 5,000 construction workers at peak,
          with construction durations of 3 to 7 years depending on
          reactor type and deployment model.

          (2) Deployment of 50 GW of new nuclear capacity by 2040 is
          projected to support an annual average of 200,000 direct
          construction jobs, concentrated in building trades including:

               (A) electricians and electrical engineers;
               (B) pipefitters, steamfitters, and boilermakers;
               (C) structural iron and steel workers;
               (D) concrete finishers and reinforcing iron workers;
               (E) heavy equipment operators and crane operators;
               (F) welders with nuclear-grade certifications;
               (G) quality assurance and quality control inspectors;
               (H) construction project managers and engineers.

          (3) The indirect and induced employment multiplier for
          nuclear construction is estimated at 2.5 to 3.0, meaning
          each direct construction job supports an additional 1.5 to
          2.0 jobs in the broader economy, for a total employment
          impact of 500,000 to 600,000 jobs during construction
          periods.

     (b) PERMANENT OPERATIONS EMPLOYMENT.

          (1) A typical advanced nuclear facility of 300 to 500 MW
          employs 150 to 300 permanent operations and maintenance
          personnel, including:

               (A) licensed reactor operators and senior operators;
               (B) maintenance technicians (mechanical, electrical,
               instrumentation and control);
               (C) radiation protection and health physics technicians;
               (D) chemistry and radiochemistry technicians;
               (E) security personnel;
               (F) engineering and technical support staff;
               (G) administrative and support personnel.

          (2) Deployment of 50 GW of advanced nuclear capacity is
          projected to support approximately 50,000 permanent
          operations jobs with an average annual compensation of
          $120,000 to $150,000, significantly above the national
          median household income.

          (3) The indirect and induced employment multiplier for
          nuclear operations is estimated at 3.0 to 4.0, reflecting
          the high wages and stable employment associated with
          nuclear plant operations and the resulting local economic
          activity, for a total employment impact of 150,000 to
          200,000 jobs during operations periods.

     (c) MANUFACTURING EMPLOYMENT.

          (1) The factory fabrication model for small modular reactors
          creates substantial manufacturing employment at centralized
          production facilities. Each SMR factory producing 4 to 8
          reactor modules per year is projected to employ 1,000 to
          2,500 manufacturing workers.

          (2) The domestic HALEU and TRISO fuel supply chain is
          projected to support an additional 3,000 to 5,000
          manufacturing and processing jobs.

          (3) The total manufacturing employment impact is estimated
          at 10,000 to 20,000 direct jobs over the deployment period.

III. GROSS DOMESTIC PRODUCT (GDP) IMPACTS

     (a) DIRECT GDP CONTRIBUTION.

          (1) The construction of 50 GW of new nuclear capacity
          represents a capital investment of approximately $300
          billion to $400 billion in 2026 dollars, based on an
          assumed overnight capital cost of $6,000 to $8,000 per
          kilowatt for early deployments, declining to $3,000 to
          $4,000 per kilowatt as manufacturing economies of scale
          are achieved.

          (2) The cumulative direct GDP contribution of this capital
          investment is estimated at $340 billion over the fifteen-year
          deployment period.

     (b) INDIRECT AND INDUCED GDP CONTRIBUTION.

          (1) Using a GDP multiplier of 2.0 to 2.5 for nuclear
          construction, the total GDP impact (direct plus indirect
          and induced) is estimated at $680 billion to $850 billion
          over the deployment period.

     (c) OPERATIONS GDP CONTRIBUTION.

          (1) Once in operation, 50 GW of nuclear capacity will
          generate approximately 400 billion kilowatt-hours of
          electricity annually, with a market value of $20 billion
          to $40 billion per year depending on wholesale electricity
          prices.

IV.  ELECTRICITY COST PROJECTIONS

     (a) LEVELIZED COST OF ELECTRICITY (LCOE).

          (1) The Energy Information Administration's Annual Energy
          Outlook 2025 projects an LCOE for advanced nuclear of
          $60 to $80 per megawatt-hour for Nth-of-a-kind deployments,
          declining from first-of-a-kind costs of $100 to $140 per
          megawatt-hour.

          (2) The NuScale VOYGR SMR design projects an LCOE of $55
          to $65 per megawatt-hour at Nth-of-a-kind, benefiting from
          factory fabrication, modular construction, and design
          standardization.

          (3) Fusion energy is expected to enter the market at a
          premium, with first commercial plants estimated at $100
          to $200 per megawatt-hour, declining rapidly as the
          technology matures and multiple commercial providers
          compete.

     (b) SYSTEM COST BENEFITS.

          (1) As a source of clean, firm, dispatchable power, nuclear
          energy provides system-level benefits not captured in LCOE
          comparisons, including:

               (A) avoided costs of overbuilding variable renewable
               generation and energy storage to achieve reliable
               24/7 decarbonized electricity;

               (B) avoided transmission costs through siting at
               existing fossil fuel plant locations with existing
               transmission infrastructure;

               (C) provision of essential reliability services
               including inertia, frequency response, and voltage
               support that are not provided by inverter-based
               resources.

          (2) A 2018 MIT study estimated that the system cost of
          achieving deep decarbonization is 20 to 40 percent lower
          when clean firm resources including nuclear are available,
          compared to scenarios relying exclusively on variable
          renewables and storage.

     (c) CONSUMER ELECTRICITY PRICES.

          (1) The deployment of 50 GW of cost-competitive nuclear
          capacity, combined with existing nuclear, renewable, and
          natural gas resources, is projected to reduce average
          retail electricity prices by 5 to 15 percent compared to
          a scenario in which nuclear deployment stagnates and the
          grid relies primarily on variable renewables plus storage
          for decarbonization.

V.   FEDERAL FISCAL IMPACTS

     (a) DIRECT SPENDING. — This Act authorizes $100,000,000,000 in
     discretionary appropriations over fiscal years 2027 through 2041.

     (b) TAX EXPENDITURES. — The Joint Committee on Taxation estimates
     that the tax provisions of this Act (SMR PTC at $0.03/kWh, Fusion
     PTC at $0.05/kWh, and the expanded ITC at 30 percent) will reduce
     Federal revenue by approximately $30,000,000,000 to $60,000,000,000
     over the scoring window, depending on the pace of deployment and
     electricity production.

     (c) LOAN GUARANTEE EXPOSURE. — The $50,000,000,000 in additional
     Title XVII loan guarantee authority carries credit subsidy costs
     estimated at $5,000,000,000 to $15,000,000,000, depending on
     project risk profiles and recovery rates.

     (d) REVENUE OFFSETS. — Federal revenue is projected to increase
     due to:

          (1) income and payroll taxes from 200,000 construction and
          50,000 permanent jobs;

          (2) corporate income taxes from nuclear project developers,
          operators, and supply chain companies;

          (3) reduced Federal expenditures on energy assistance
          programs due to lower electricity prices;

          (4) export-related tax revenues from the sale of American
          nuclear technology and fuel to international customers.

VI.  INTERNATIONAL TRADE AND EXPORT MARKETS

     (a) GLOBAL MARKET SIZE. — The global market for advanced nuclear
     power plants, including SMRs, Generation IV reactors, and fusion
     systems, is projected to reach $1,000,000,000,000 over the next
     three decades (2026-2056), driven by:

          (1) decarbonization commitments by more than 140 countries
          representing over 90 percent of global GDP;

          (2) growing demand for 24/7 carbon-free electricity to power
          data centers, artificial intelligence infrastructure, and
          industrial electrification;

          (3) retirement of coal and natural gas generating capacity
          in major economies;

          (4) increasing recognition of the limitations of variable
          renewable-only decarbonization pathways.

     (b) UNITED STATES COMPETITIVE POSITION. — ANEDA positions the
     United States to capture a substantial share of the global advanced
     nuclear market by:

          (1) establishing a robust domestic manufacturing base and
          supply chain that can serve both domestic and export demand;

          (2) creating an NRC regulatory framework that serves as the
          global gold standard for advanced reactor licensing, enabling
          U.S.-certified designs to be accepted by foreign regulators;

          (3) supporting American companies in competing for
          international nuclear projects against state-supported
          competitors from the Russian Federation and the People's
          Republic of China;

          (4) developing a domestic HALEU supply chain that can serve
          both U.S. and allied nation advanced reactor fleets.

     (c) EXPORT PROJECTIONS. — The Department of Commerce projects that
     United States exports of advanced nuclear technology, equipment,
     fuel, and services could reach $50,000,000,000 in cumulative value
     by 2041 under the policies established by this Act, supporting an
     additional 25,000 to 50,000 domestic manufacturing and services
     jobs.
