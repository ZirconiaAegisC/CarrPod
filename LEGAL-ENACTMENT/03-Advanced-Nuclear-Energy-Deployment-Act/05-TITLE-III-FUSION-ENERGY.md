                  TITLE III — FUSION ENERGY DEVELOPMENT

================================================================================

SECTION 301. ESTABLISHMENT OF FUSION ENERGY REGULATORY FRAMEWORK.

     (a) FINDINGS AND DECLARATIONS. —

          (1) The Congress finds that commercial fusion energy systems
          present fundamentally different safety, security, waste, and
          proliferation risk profiles compared to nuclear fission
          reactors and shall not be regulated under the same statutory
          or regulatory provisions applicable to fission facilities.

          (2) It is the sense of Congress that fusion energy systems
          merit a technology-appropriate, risk-proportionate regulatory
          framework that protects public health and safety and the
          common defense and security without imposing regulatory
          burdens designed for fission reactors on fundamentally
          different fusion technologies.

     (b) FUSION DIVISION ESTABLISHMENT. — Not later than 180 days after
     the date of enactment of this Act, the Commission shall establish
     a Fusion Energy Division with the following responsibilities:

          (1) developing and promulgating, not later than January 1,
          2029, a technology-inclusive, risk-informed, performance-based
          regulatory framework specifically for commercial fusion
          energy systems;

          (2) reviewing and issuing licenses, permits, and approvals
          for fusion energy systems;

          (3) conducting inspections and oversight of licensed fusion
          facilities;

          (4) coordinating with the Department of Energy, the
          Environmental Protection Agency, and state regulatory
          authorities on matters relating to fusion energy regulation;

          (5) engaging with international counterparts to promote
          regulatory harmonization for fusion energy systems.

     (c) FUSION REGULATORY PRINCIPLES. — In developing the fusion
     regulatory framework, the Commission shall adhere to the
     following principles:

          (1) REGULATORY SEPARATION. — Fusion energy systems shall be
          regulated under a distinct statutory and regulatory
          framework, separate from the provisions of the Atomic Energy
          Act of 1954 applicable to utilization facilities that employ
          nuclear fission.

          (2) RISK-PROPORTIONATE REGULATION. — The stringency of
          regulatory requirements shall be proportionate to the actual
          radiological and chemical hazards presented by the fusion
          facility, with particular consideration of:

               (A) the absence of self-sustaining chain reactions and
               the inherent incapacity of fusion systems to experience
               reactivity-initiated accidents or core melt events
               comparable to fission reactors;

               (B) the type and quantity of radioactive materials
               present, including tritium and activated structural
               materials, and the inventory of such materials that
               could be mobilized under accident conditions;

               (C) the waste characteristics of fusion systems,
               including the absence of spent nuclear fuel and
               actinide-bearing waste streams.

          (3) TECHNOLOGY NEUTRALITY. — The regulatory framework shall
          accommodate all credible fusion approaches, including magnetic
          confinement (tokamaks, stellarators, field-reversed
          configurations), inertial confinement, magnetized target
          fusion, and other approaches that may be demonstrated in
          the future.

          (4) BYPRODUCT MATERIAL REGULATION. — Fusion facilities shall
          be regulated with respect to byproduct materials, including
          tritium, under a framework consistent with the existing
          Agreement State program under section 274 of the Atomic
          Energy Act of 1954, with appropriate modifications to account
          for the quantities and forms of tritium utilized in fusion
          systems.

     (d) INTERIM GUIDANCE. — Pending promulgation of the final fusion
     regulatory framework, the Commission shall, not later than 90 days
     after the date of enactment of this Act, issue interim guidance
     describing the licensing pathway that fusion developers may utilize
     under existing Commission authority for activities not involving
     the possession or use of special nuclear material in quantities
     of strategic significance.

     (e) AUTHORIZATION. — There is authorized to be appropriated
     $250,000,000 for fiscal years 2027 through 2031 for the
     establishment and operation of the Fusion Energy Division and
     the development of the fusion regulatory framework.

SECTION 302. FUSION ENERGY PRODUCTION TAX CREDIT.

     (a) CREDIT ESTABLISHED. — Section 45 of the Internal Revenue Code
     of 1986, as amended by section 102 of this Act, is further amended
     by adding at the end the following new subsection:

          "(BB) FUSION ENERGY PRODUCTION CREDIT. —

               "(1) IN GENERAL. — For purposes of subsection (a), a
               qualified fusion energy facility shall be treated as a
               qualified facility described in paragraph (__) of
               subsection (d).

               "(2) CREDIT AMOUNT. — The credit determined under this
               subsection for any taxable year is equal to the product
               of —

                    "(A) 5 cents, multiplied by

                    "(B) the kilowatt-hours of electricity produced by
                    the taxpayer at a qualified fusion energy facility
                    during the 15-year period beginning on the date the
                    facility is originally placed in service and sold
                    by the taxpayer to an unrelated person during such
                    taxable year.

               "(3) QUALIFIED FACILITY. — The term 'qualified fusion
               energy facility' means a facility that —

                    "(A) generates electricity through nuclear fusion
                    reactions and demonstrates net energy gain, defined
                    as a fusion energy output exceeding the total energy
                    input to the facility;

                    "(B) is placed in service after December 31, 2026,
                    and before January 1, 2046;

                    "(C) is located in the United States; and

                    "(D) meets the prevailing wage and apprenticeship
                    requirements of section 45(b)(7)."

     (b) EFFECTIVE DATE. — The amendment made by this section shall
     apply to facilities placed in service after December 31, 2026.

SECTION 303. FUSION ENERGY RESEARCH AND DEVELOPMENT PROGRAM.

     (a) FUSION R&D PROGRAM. — The Secretary, acting through the Office
     of Fusion Energy Sciences and in coordination with the Office of
     Science, shall establish and carry out a comprehensive Fusion
     Energy Research and Development Program that includes:

          (1) continued support for the ITER international fusion
          project and the United States' in-kind hardware contributions
          thereto;

          (2) a Domestic Fusion Pilot Plant Program, with the goal of
          demonstrating net electricity production from a fusion
          facility by December 31, 2035, through a competitive,
          milestone-based public-private partnership model;

          (3) a Fusion Materials and Technology Program addressing:

               (A) development and qualification of low-activation
               structural materials, including reduced-activation
               ferritic-martensitic steels, oxide dispersion-
               strengthened alloys, and silicon carbide composites;

               (B) tritium breeding blanket technology, including
               lead-lithium, lithium ceramic, and molten salt breeder
               concepts;

               (C) plasma-facing component materials capable of
               withstanding steady-state heat fluxes exceeding
               10 megawatts per square meter;

          (4) a Fusion Enabling Technologies Program, supporting
          high-temperature superconducting magnets, high-power
          neutral beam and radio-frequency heating systems, inertial
          fusion driver technologies, and advanced fuel cycle
          technologies;

          (5) continued operation of and upgrades to the DIII-D
          National Fusion Facility at General Atomics in San Diego,
          California, and the National Spherical Torus Experiment
          Upgrade (NSTX-U) at the Princeton Plasma Physics Laboratory.

     (b) MILESTONE-BASED FUNDING. — The Secretary shall utilize
     milestone-based funding mechanisms, modeled on the ARDP cost-share
     program, for the Fusion Pilot Plant Program, with participating
     private entities contributing not less than 50 percent of total
     project costs and receiving Federal payments upon achievement of
     specified technical and programmatic milestones.

     (c) AUTHORIZATION. — There is authorized to be appropriated
     $8,000,000,000 for fiscal years 2027 through 2036 to carry out
     this section.

SECTION 304. ARPA-E FUSION PROGRAMS AND MILESTONES.

     (a) ARPA-E FUSION. — The Advanced Research Projects Agency-Energy
     (ARPA-E) shall establish and carry out a dedicated fusion energy
     program focused on accelerating the commercial viability of fusion
     through targeted investments in enabling technologies and
     alternative confinement approaches.

     (b) PROGRAM ELEMENTS. — The ARPA-E fusion program shall include:

          (1) a Fusion Enabling Technologies program supporting
          breakthroughs in high-temperature superconducting magnets,
          advanced plasma diagnostics, and fusion materials;

          (2) a Fusion Performance Target program establishing specific
          technical milestones for fusion companies to achieve on
          defined timelines, with funding awarded upon successful
          milestone demonstration;

          (3) a Fusion Innovation Research program supporting early-
          stage, high-risk, high-reward fusion concepts that have not
          yet attracted substantial private investment.

     (c) AUTHORIZATION. — There is authorized to be appropriated
     $2,500,000,000 for fiscal years 2027 through 2036 to carry out
     this section.

SECTION 305. PUBLIC-PRIVATE PARTNERSHIP AUTHORITY FOR FUSION.

     (a) FUSION P3 AUTHORITY. — The Secretary is authorized to enter
     into public-private partnerships with United States-domiciled
     entities for the purpose of accelerating the development and
     commercial deployment of fusion energy systems.

     (b) PARTNERSHIP STRUCTURES. — Partnerships under this section may
     take the form of:

          (1) cooperative research and development agreements
          (CRADAs) pursuant to section 12 of the Stevenson-Wydler
          Technology Innovation Act of 1980 (15 U.S.C. 3710a);

          (2) other transaction agreements pursuant to section 646(g)
          of the Department of Energy Organization Act (42 U.S.C.
          7256(g));

          (3) technology investment agreements;

          (4) cost-share agreements with milestone-based payments;

          (5) user facility access agreements at the national
          laboratories.

     (c) INTELLECTUAL PROPERTY. — In negotiating partnership agreements
     under this section, the Secretary shall apply intellectual property
     provisions that protect the commercial interests of private
     participants while ensuring that the Federal Government receives
     appropriate benefits from its investment, consistent with the
     Bayh-Dole Act (35 U.S.C. 200 et seq.) and the Stevenson-Wydler
     Act.

     (d) NATIONAL LABORATORY SUPPORT. — The Secretary shall direct the
     national laboratories to prioritize support for fusion public-
     private partnerships, including through:

          (1) access to computational resources for plasma simulation
          and fusion systems modeling;

          (2) utilization of existing experimental facilities for
          component testing and validation;

          (3) technical expertise in fusion science, engineering, and
          safety analysis;

          (4) shared use of tritium handling facilities and expertise.

     (e) COMMUNITY BENEFITS. — Any partnership agreement under this
     section involving Federal funding exceeding $50,000,000 shall
     require the private participant to negotiate and execute a
     community benefit agreement with the host community, consistent
     with section 801 of this Act.
