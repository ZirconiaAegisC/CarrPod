# APPENDIX — REFERENCES AND SUPPORTING DOCUMENTS

## CLEAN INDUSTRIAL MANUFACTURING TAX CREDIT ACT OF 2026

### I. STATUTORY REFERENCES — INTERNAL REVENUE CODE OF 1986

**(a) Section 45V — Clean Hydrogen Production Credit.** Established
by section 13204 of the Inflation Reduction Act of 2022 (Pub. L.
117-169). Provides a production tax credit for qualified clean
hydrogen produced at a qualified facility, with credit amounts
ranging from $0.60 to $3.00 per kilogram based on lifecycle
greenhouse gas emissions rates. The CIMTCA (Title III) amends
section 45V to extend the credit period, tighten emission rate
thresholds, add a domestic electrolyzer manufacturing bonus, and
add an industrial heat end-use bonus.

**(b) Section 45Q — Carbon Oxide Sequestration Credit.** Originally
enacted by section 41119 of the Bipartisan Budget Act of 2018
(Pub. L. 115-123) and amended by section 13104 of the Inflation
Reduction Act of 2022. Provides a credit for qualified carbon oxide
captured and disposed of in secure geological storage or utilized
for enhanced oil recovery or other qualified uses. The CIMTCA
(Title V) increases credit rates, extends the commence-construction
window, reduces capture thresholds, and adds a direct air capture
and CO2 utilization bonus.

**(c) Section 45X — Advanced Manufacturing Production Credit.**
Established by section 13501 of the Inflation Reduction Act of 2022.
Provides a production tax credit for domestic manufacturing of
eligible components, including solar and wind components, inverters,
battery cells and modules, and applicable critical minerals. The
CIMTCA (Title VI) expands section 45X to include advanced composites,
additive manufacturing components, nano-engineered coatings, thermal
energy storage media, and electrolyzer components.

**(d) Section 48 — Energy Investment Tax Credit.** Codified at 26
U.S.C. § 48. Provides an investment tax credit for energy property,
including solar, wind, geothermal, fuel cell, microturbine, combined
heat and power, and waste energy recovery property. The CIMTCA
(Title IV) adds industrial heat decarbonization property as a new
category of energy property eligible for the ITC.

**(e) Section 48C — Qualifying Advanced Energy Project Credit.**
Established by section 1302 of the American Recovery and Reinvestment
Act of 2009 (Pub. L. 111-5) and expanded by section 13501 of the
Inflation Reduction Act of 2022. Provides a 30 percent investment
tax credit for qualifying advanced energy manufacturing projects.
The CIMTCA (Title III) expands eligibility to hydrogen infrastructure
projects.

**(f) Section 48D — Advanced Manufacturing Investment Credit.**
Established by the CHIPS Act of 2022 (Pub. L. 117-167). Provides
a 25 percent investment tax credit for semiconductor manufacturing
facilities. The CIMTCA (Title VI) extends section 48D to cover
industrial automation equipment.

**(g) Sections 6417 and 6418 — Direct Pay and Transferability.**
Established by sections 13801 and 6418 of the Inflation Reduction
Act of 2022. Section 6417 allows tax-exempt entities to receive
direct payment in lieu of tax credits. Section 6418 allows
taxpayers to transfer all or a portion of certain tax credits to
unrelated taxpayers for cash. The CIMTCA makes the new credits
established by this Act eligible for direct pay and transferability.

### II. STATUTORY REFERENCES — OTHER FEDERAL LAWS

**(a) Inflation Reduction Act of 2022 (Pub. L. 117-169).** The
landmark climate and clean energy legislation that established the
credits amended by the CIMTCA, including sections 45V, 45Q, 45X,
48, and 48C. The success of IRA manufacturing credits in
accelerating domestic solar, battery, and wind manufacturing
investment provides the policy model for the CIMTCA.

**(b) Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act of 2021 (Pub. L. 117-58).**
The IIJA established the Build America, Buy America Act procurement
requirements (sections 70901–70953), which serve as the foundation
for the Buy America clean industrial procurement requirements in
Title VIII of the CIMTCA.

**(c) Buy America Act (41 U.S.C. §§ 8301–8305).** The original Buy
America statute governing domestic procurement preferences for
federal contracts. Title VIII of the CIMTCA amends chapter 83 of
title 41 to add clean industrial materials procurement requirements.

**(d) Energy Policy Act of 2005 (Pub. L. 109-58).** Establishes the
base framework for energy tax credits that the CIMTCA builds upon.

**(e) Davis-Bacon Act (40 U.S.C. 3141 et seq.).** Requires payment
of prevailing wages on federal construction contracts. The CIMTCA
applies prevailing wage requirements to all facilities claiming
credits under the Act.

### III. DEPARTMENT OF ENERGY REFERENCES

**(a) Industrial Decarbonization Roadmap (DOE/EE-2635, September 2022).**
The foundational analytical document identifying four key pathways for
industrial decarbonization: energy efficiency, industrial
electrification, low-carbon fuels and feedstocks, and carbon capture
and utilization. The CIMTCA credit structure directly implements the
DOE roadmap's recommendations.

**(b) Pathways to Commercial Liftoff: Industrial Decarbonization
(DOE, September 2023).** Analysis of the commercial readiness of
industrial decarbonization technologies, including green steel,
low-carbon cement, clean hydrogen, and industrial heat solutions.

**(c) Hydrogen Shot (DOE Hydrogen Program, 2021).** The DOE's Earthshot
initiative targeting $1 per kilogram of clean hydrogen by 2031.
Title III of the CIMTCA supports this goal through production credits
and electrolyzer manufacturing incentives.

**(d) Industrial Heat Shot (DOE, 2022).** DOE initiative targeting
an 85 percent reduction in industrial heating emissions by 2035.
Title IV of the CIMTCA directly supports the Industrial Heat Shot
through the industrial heat decarbonization ITC.

**(e) Carbon Negative Shot (DOE, 2021).** DOE initiative targeting
gigaton-scale carbon dioxide removal at less than $100 per metric ton.
Title V of the CIMTCA supports the Carbon Negative Shot through
enhanced direct air capture and CO2 utilization credits.

### IV. INDUSTRY EMISSIONS DATA

**(a) Cement Sector.** According to the International Energy Agency
(IEA), global cement production accounts for approximately 8 percent
of global CO2 emissions. The United States produces approximately
100 million metric tons of cement annually, emitting approximately
0.9 metric tons of CO2 per metric ton of cement. Process emissions
from limestone calcination account for approximately 60 percent of
total emissions.

_Source: IEA, "Cement" (Tracking Clean Energy Progress, 2023);
USGS, "Mineral Commodity Summaries: Cement" (2024); EPA, "Inventory
of U.S. Greenhouse Gas Emissions and Sinks: 1990–2022" (2024)._

**(b) Steel Sector.** According to the IEA, iron and steel production
accounts for approximately 7 to 9 percent of global CO2 emissions.
The United States produces approximately 80 million metric tons of
crude steel annually. Integrated blast furnace-basic oxygen furnace
(BF-BOF) production emits approximately 2.0 metric tons CO2 per metric
ton of steel; electric arc furnace (EAF) production emits approximately
0.4 metric tons CO2 per metric ton.

_Source: IEA, "Iron and Steel" (Tracking Clean Energy Progress, 2023);
American Iron and Steel Institute, "Annual Statistical Report" (2023);
World Steel Association, "Sustainability Indicators" (2023)._

**(c) Industrial Sector Total.** According to the EPA, the industrial
sector was responsible for 30 percent of total United States greenhouse
gas emissions in 2022 (1,885 million metric tons CO2e out of
6,338 million metric tons CO2e total). Industrial process emissions
alone accounted for 7.6 percent of total emissions.

_Source: EPA, "Inventory of U.S. Greenhouse Gas Emissions and
Sinks: 1990–2022" (April 2024), Tables ES-2 and 2-1._

### VI. INTERNATIONAL FRAMEWORKS AND COMPARATIVE ANALYSIS

**(a) European Union Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM).**
Regulation (EU) 2023/956, establishing a carbon border adjustment
mechanism. From 2026, EU importers of cement, iron and steel,
aluminum, fertilizers, electricity, and hydrogen must purchase CBAM
certificates corresponding to the embedded emissions. The CIMTCA
enables United States producers to meet EU emissions benchmarks,
avoiding border adjustment costs.

**(b) First Movers Coalition.** World Economic Forum initiative in
which member companies commit to purchasing a percentage of their
industrial materials from near-zero-emission sources. Commitments
include: steel (10 percent near-zero by 2030), cement/concrete
(10 percent near-zero by 2030), and aluminum (10 percent near-zero
by 2030).

**(c) Industrial Deep Decarbonisation Initiative (IDDI).** Clean
Energy Ministerial initiative coordinated by the United Nations
Industrial Development Organization (UNIDO) in which member
governments commit to green public procurement of steel and cement.
