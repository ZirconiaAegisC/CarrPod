================================================================================
                              EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
                        WATER INFRASTRUCTURE AND
                     DROUGHT RESILIENCE ACT OF 2026
================================================================================

I. PURPOSE AND SCOPE

  The Water Infrastructure and Drought Resilience Act of 2026 (WIDRA)
  constitutes a comprehensive legislative framework authorizing $120 billion
  in Federal investment over a ten-year period to modernize the nation's
  deteriorating water infrastructure, eliminate lead service lines from public
  drinking water systems, address perfluoroalkyl and polyfluoroalkyl substance
  (PFAS) contamination, build long-term drought resilience across the Western
  United States, and advance water recycling, desalination, and agricultural
  efficiency technologies. WIDRA represents the most significant Federal
  investment in water infrastructure since the Clean Water Act of 1972.

II. STRATEGIC CONTEXT

  The United States water infrastructure faces an existential crisis. An
  estimated 2.2 million miles of aging underground pipes distribute drinking
  water and collect wastewater across the nation, much of it installed in the
  early to mid-20th century and now well beyond its designed service life.
  The American Society of Civil Engineers awarded United States drinking
  water infrastructure a grade of C-minus in its 2021 Report Card, and the
  American Water Works Association estimates that restoring and expanding
  existing water systems will require at least $1 trillion over the next 25
  years. Approximately 6 billion gallons of treated water are lost daily
  through leaks in distribution systems, equivalent to the daily water
  consumption of 15 million households. An estimated 240,000 water main breaks
  occur annually, one every two minutes, costing municipalities and utilities
  over $2.6 billion per year in emergency repairs.

  The lead service line crisis affects an estimated 9.2 million lead service
  lines still in operation across the United States, disproportionately
  concentrated in low-income communities and communities of color. Lead
  exposure, even at low levels, causes irreversible neurological damage in
  children, contributing to reduced IQ, behavioral disorders, and lifelong
  health disparities. The Congressional Budget Office estimates the economic
  cost of lead exposure in the United States at approximately $50 billion per
  year in lost lifetime earnings, increased healthcare costs, and educational
  underperformance.

  The Colorado River Basin, which supplies water to 40 million people and
  irrigates 5.5 million acres of farmland across seven Western States, has
  experienced a 20 percent decline in average annual flow since 2000 due to
  a combination of climate-driven aridification and chronic over-allocation
  under the 1922 Colorado River Compact. Lake Mead, the nation's largest
  reservoir by capacity, reached its lowest recorded elevation of 1,040.58
  feet above sea level in July 2022, triggering the first-ever Tier 2a
  shortage declaration and mandatory water delivery reductions to Arizona,
  Nevada, and Mexico.

  The United States recycles less than 1 percent of its municipal wastewater,
  compared with approximately 85 percent in Israel and over 40 percent in
  Singapore. With more than 18,000 desalination plants operating globally, only
  approximately 400 are located in the United States, primarily in California,
  Florida, and Texas. Meanwhile, PFAS contamination has been detected at over
  3,000 sites nationwide, including drinking water sources serving tens of
  millions of Americans, with health effects linked to cancer, thyroid disease,
  and developmental disorders.

III. KEY PROVISIONS AND FUNDING ALLOCATION

  The WIDRA authorizes $120 billion across eight substantive titles plus
  program administration, oversight, and economic impact assessment:

  TITLE I — LEAD PIPE ELIMINATION ($45 billion): Mandates comprehensive lead
  service line inventory and replacement over a ten-year period, with priority
  for disadvantaged communities; provides low-income household assistance;
  capitalizes State revolving funds; funds school and childcare facility
  remediation; and supports workforce training for lead pipe replacement.

  TITLE II — WATER RECYCLING AND REUSE ($20 billion): Establishes national
  water recycling targets modeled after Israel's successful program; funds
  direct potable reuse demonstration projects; provides industrial and
  agricultural reuse incentives; deploys advanced treatment technologies
  including reverse osmosis and advanced oxidation; and funds public education
  campaigns to build acceptance of recycled water.

  TITLE III — PFAS REMEDIATION ($15 billion): Establishes enforceable national
  primary drinking water regulations for PFAS; funds research and deployment
  of PFAS destruction technologies; provides remediation grants for
  contaminated sites; supports drinking water utilities with monitoring and
  treatment costs; and funds biomonitoring and health impact studies.

  TITLE IV — WESTERN DROUGHT RESILIENCE ($25 billion): Funds Colorado River
  Basin conservation programs; supports groundwater management and managed
  aquifer recharge; facilitates interstate water banking agreements; expands
  drought early warning systems; and provides rural community drought
  assistance.

  TITLE V — AGRICULTURAL WATER EFFICIENCY ($10 billion): Provides irrigation
  modernization grants; deploys precision agriculture water technologies;
  supports crop transition assistance; promotes regenerative agriculture
  and soil water retention practices; and funds livestock water supply
  resilience programs.

  TITLE VI — SMART WATER METERING: Funds nationwide smart metering
  deployment; supports advanced leak detection technologies; establishes
  real-time distribution system monitoring requirements; and mandates
  non-revenue water reduction targets.

  TITLE VII — DESALINATION RESEARCH AND DEVELOPMENT: Funds advanced membrane
  research; establishes brackish water desalination demonstration facilities;
  addresses brine management challenges; supports renewable energy-powered
  desalination; and funds cost reduction and commercialization efforts.

  TITLE VIII — TRIBAL WATER RIGHTS ($5 billion): Establishes a Tribal water
  rights settlement fund; funds water infrastructure on Indian reservations;
  requires meaningful Tribal consultation; provides technical assistance;
  and addresses emergency water supply needs for Tribal communities.

  An additional $5 billion is allocated for program administration, oversight,
  and economic impact assessment across all program areas.

IV. ECONOMIC PROJECTIONS

  The Department of the Interior and the Environmental Protection Agency
  estimate that WIDRA implementation will:

  1. Eliminate all 9.2 million remaining lead service lines within 10 years,
     preventing an estimated 200,000 childhood lead poisoning cases annually;

  2. Create approximately 350,000 direct and indirect domestic jobs in water
     infrastructure construction, engineering, manufacturing, and operations;

  3. Generate approximately $180 billion in cumulative economic benefits
     through avoided water loss, reduced public health costs, increased
     agricultural productivity, and enhanced property values;

  4. Increase United States municipal wastewater recycling from less than
     1 percent to no less than 10 percent by 2036; and

  5. Establish resilient water supplies for 40 million Americans in the
     Colorado River Basin and other drought-affected regions.

V. NATIONAL SECURITY AND PUBLIC HEALTH IMPERATIVE

  WIDRA finds that the nation's deteriorating water infrastructure, widespread
  lead contamination, emerging PFAS threats, and intensifying drought
  conditions collectively constitute a national emergency requiring Federal
  action commensurate with the scale of the challenge. Access to safe,
  reliable, and affordable drinking water is a fundamental human right and a
  prerequisite for public health, economic prosperity, and community vitality.
  This legislation operationalizes the consensus view across the Environmental
  Protection Agency, the Department of the Interior, the Centers for Disease
  Control and Prevention, and the National Academies of Sciences, Engineering,
  and Medicine that water infrastructure investment must be prioritized at a
  level comparable to national transportation and defense spending.
