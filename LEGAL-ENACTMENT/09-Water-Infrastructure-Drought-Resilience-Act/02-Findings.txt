================================================================================
                          FINDINGS AND DECLARATIONS
                     WATER INFRASTRUCTURE AND DROUGHT
                        RESILIENCE ACT OF 2026
================================================================================

SECTION 101. FINDINGS.

  Congress finds the following:

    (1) The United States drinking water infrastructure comprises an estimated
        2.2 million miles of underground pipes, many of which were installed
        between 1900 and 1970 and have exceeded their designed service life
        of 50 to 75 years, creating an escalating risk of catastrophic system
        failures.

    (2) According to the American Society of Civil Engineers, there is a
        water main break approximately every two minutes in the United States,
        totaling an estimated 240,000 breaks per year, costing municipalities
        and ratepayers more than $2.6 billion annually in emergency repairs.

    (3) An estimated 6 billion gallons of treated drinking water are lost
        daily through leaks in aging distribution systems, representing
        approximately 16 percent of all treated water produced in the
        United States and an annual economic loss of $7.6 billion.

    (4) The American Water Works Association estimates that restoring and
        expanding existing water infrastructure will require at least
        $1 trillion in investment over the next 25 years.

    (5) Approximately 9.2 million lead service lines remain in operation
        across the United States, as documented by the Environmental
        Protection Agency's 7th Drinking Water Infrastructure Needs Survey
        and Assessment.

    (6) There is no safe level of lead exposure in children, according to
        the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention, the American Academy
        of Pediatrics, and the World Health Organization; lead exposure
        causes irreversible neurological damage, reduced IQ, behavioral
        disorders, and increased risk of cardiovascular disease in adults.

    (7) The environmental justice implications of lead service line
        prevalence are severe: communities of color and low-income
        communities are disproportionately served by water systems with
        lead service lines, perpetuating systemic health inequities.

    (8) The Flint, Michigan, water crisis of 2014-2019, in which an estimated
        100,000 residents were exposed to elevated lead levels, demonstrates
        the catastrophic consequences of deferred water infrastructure
        investment and inadequate regulatory oversight.

    (9) Perfluoroalkyl and polyfluoroalkyl substances (PFAS) have been
        detected in drinking water sources serving at least 200 million
        Americans across all 50 States, with contamination documented at
        over 3,000 sites as of 2026.

    (10) PFAS are linked to serious adverse health effects including kidney
         and testicular cancer, thyroid disease, ulcerative colitis,
         developmental delays in children, and reduced vaccine response,
         according to the National Academies of Sciences, Engineering, and
         Medicine.

    (11) The Western United States is experiencing the most severe
         megadrought in at least 1,200 years, with the period 2000-2025
         representing the driest 25-year period in the region since at
         least 800 CE, as documented in studies published in the journal
         Nature Climate Change.

    (12) The Colorado River Basin, which supplies water to 40 million people
         and supports $1.4 trillion in annual economic activity across seven
         States and 30 Tribal nations, has experienced a 20 percent decline
         in average annual flow since 2000.

    (13) Lake Mead dropped to its lowest recorded elevation of 1,040.58 feet
         above sea level in July 2022, representing a decline of more than
         180 feet since January 2000, triggering the first-ever Tier 2a
         shortage declaration under the 2019 Drought Contingency Plan.

    (14) Agriculture accounts for approximately 80 percent of consumptive
         water use in the Western United States, yet many irrigation systems
         date to the mid-20th century and operate at efficiency rates below
         50 percent.

    (15) The United States recycles less than 1 percent of its municipal
         wastewater for beneficial reuse, compared with 85 percent in
         Israel and over 40 percent in Singapore, representing an
         opportunity to dramatically expand water supplies through
         proven technology.

    (16) While more than 18,000 desalination plants operate globally
         producing over 25 billion gallons of fresh water daily, only
         approximately 400 plants are located in the United States,
         primarily in California, Florida, and Texas.

    (17) Many Native American Tribal communities lack access to reliable
         drinking water and wastewater infrastructure: an estimated 48
         percent of Tribal households lack access to reliable water
         sources or adequate sanitation facilities, compared with less
         than 1 percent of non-Native American households.

    (18) The economic cost of deferred water infrastructure investment
         is substantial: water main breaks, water loss, public health
         impacts, bottled water expenditures, and reduced economic
         activity in water-insecure communities collectively impose
         an estimated $100 billion annual economic burden on the
         United States economy.

SECTION 102. DECLARATIONS OF POLICY.

  Congress declares that it is the policy of the United States to:

    (1) Guarantee access to safe, clean, affordable drinking water and
        wastewater services for all Americans as a matter of public
        health, environmental justice, and national priority.

    (2) Eliminate all lead service lines from public drinking water systems
        within ten years, prioritizing disadvantaged communities and those
        with the highest concentrations of lead service lines.

    (3) Dramatically expand water recycling and reuse capacity, with a
        goal of recycling at least 10 percent of municipal wastewater
        by 2036 and 25 percent by 2046.

    (4) Remediate PFAS contamination at drinking water sources, establish
        enforceable national standards for PFAS in drinking water, and
        eliminate industrial discharges of PFAS into waterways.

    (5) Build long-term drought resilience in the Western United States
        through conservation, water banking, groundwater management,
        aquifer recharge, and interstate cooperation.

    (6) Modernize agricultural irrigation infrastructure to improve
        water use efficiency, with a goal of reducing agricultural
        consumptive water use in the Colorado River Basin by 20 percent
        by 2036.

    (7) Deploy smart water metering and leak detection technologies
        nationwide, with a target of reducing non-revenue water loss
        from 16 percent to no more than 10 percent by 2036.

    (8) Advance desalination technology through research, development,
        and demonstration, with a target of achieving cost-competitive
        desalination at an energy consumption of 2.0 kilowatt-hours
        or less per cubic meter of water produced by 2036.

    (9) Fulfill the Federal trust responsibility to Native American
        Tribes by ensuring access to safe, reliable water supplies
        and wastewater infrastructure on all Indian reservations.

    (10) Prioritize Buy America requirements for all water infrastructure
         materials, equipment, and components funded under this Act.

    (11) Implement all programs under this Act in accordance with the
         principles of environmental justice, ensuring that disadvantaged
         communities receive no less than 40 percent of the overall
         benefits of Federal water infrastructure investments.
