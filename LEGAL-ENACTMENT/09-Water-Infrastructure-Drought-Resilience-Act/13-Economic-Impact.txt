================================================================================
                       ECONOMIC IMPACT ASSESSMENT
                     WATER INFRASTRUCTURE AND DROUGHT
                        RESILIENCE ACT OF 2026
================================================================================

SECTION 1. MACROECONOMIC PROJECTIONS.

  (a) GROSS DOMESTIC PRODUCT. — The Council of Economic Advisers, in
      consultation with the Department of the Treasury and the Office of
      Management and Budget, projects that the $120 billion authorized
      by the Water Infrastructure and Drought Resilience Act of 2026
      will generate cumulative gross domestic product (GDP) contributions
      of approximately $180 billion over the ten-year authorization
      period, representing a fiscal multiplier of approximately 1.5.

  (b) EMPLOYMENT IMPACTS. — Direct, indirect, and induced employment
      effects are projected to generate:

    (1) TITLE I (Lead Pipe Elimination): .................. 150,000 jobs
        Construction trades, plumbing, engineering, materials
        manufacturing, and environmental testing;

    (2) TITLE II (Water Recycling and Reuse): .............. 55,000 jobs
        Advanced treatment plant construction and operation,
        membrane manufacturing, pipeline construction;

    (3) TITLE III (PFAS Remediation): ...................... 35,000 jobs
        Environmental remediation, laboratory analysis,
        treatment system installation and operation;

    (4) TITLE IV (Western Drought Resilience): ............. 45,000 jobs
        Conservation program administration, groundwater
        recharge construction, monitoring technology;

    (5) TITLE V (Agricultural Water Efficiency): ........... 30,000 jobs
        Irrigation system installation, precision agriculture
        technology manufacturing, extension services;

    (6) Titles VI-VIII: ................................... 35,000 jobs
        Smart metering manufacturing and installation,
        desalination research, Tribal infrastructure;

    (7) TOTAL PROJECTED EMPLOYMENT: ....................... 350,000 jobs

  (c) WAGE ANALYSIS. — The weighted average wage for jobs created under
      this Act is projected at $32.50 per hour, approximately 15 percent
      higher than the national median wage, reflecting the skilled
      nature of water infrastructure construction and technology
      deployment.

SECTION 2. SECTORAL AND REGIONAL ECONOMIC ANALYSIS.

  (a) CONSTRUCTION AND MANUFACTURING. — The pipe manufacturing, plumbing
      fixture, water meter, membrane, treatment chemical, monitoring
      equipment, and related manufacturing sectors are projected to
      experience annual revenue growth of 8-12 percent above baseline
      forecasts, supporting expansion of domestic manufacturing capacity
      for water infrastructure products.

  (b) WESTERN STATES ECONOMIC BENEFITS. — California, Arizona, Nevada,
      Colorado, New Mexico, Utah, and Wyoming are projected to realize:

    (1) Avoided economic disruption valued at $15 billion annually from
        water shortage-related agricultural losses, tourism declines,
        and emergency water supply costs;

    (2) Agricultural productivity gains of $3.5 billion annually from
        improved irrigation efficiency and drought-resistant cropping
        systems;

    (3) Enhanced water supply reliability supporting sustained
        population and economic growth in the Colorado River Basin.

  (c) MIDWEST AND NORTHEAST. — States with the highest concentration of
      lead service lines, including Illinois, Ohio, Michigan, New York,
      Pennsylvania, and New Jersey, are projected to receive approximately
      40 percent of Title I funding, generating regional employment and
      economic activity in construction trades and associated sectors.

  (d) TRIBAL ECONOMIC DEVELOPMENT. — Investment in Tribal water
      infrastructure is projected to enable economic development on
      Indian reservations that has been constrained by inadequate
      water supply and wastewater infrastructure, including:

    (1) Agricultural development and food sovereignty initiatives;
    (2) Tourism and recreation enterprises;
    (3) Housing construction and community development;
    (4) Business attraction and retention.

SECTION 3. PUBLIC HEALTH AND SOCIAL COST AVOIDANCE.

  (a) LEAD EXPOSURE ELIMINATION. — The replacement of all remaining
      9.2 million lead service lines is projected to:

    (1) Prevent approximately 200,000 cases of childhood lead poisoning
        annually, as measured by blood lead levels exceeding the CDC
        reference level of 3.5 micrograms per deciliter;

    (2) Avoid an estimated $50 billion per year in societal costs
        associated with lead exposure, including:
        (A) $30 billion in lost lifetime earnings due to cognitive
            impairment;
        (B) $12 billion in increased healthcare costs;
        (C) $5 billion in special education expenditures;
        (D) $3 billion in criminal justice system costs attributable
            to lead exposure-related behavioral effects.

    (3) Increase aggregate lifetime earnings of affected cohorts by an
        estimated $400 billion over 40 years.

  (b) PFAS HEALTH BENEFITS. — The remediation of PFAS-contaminated
      drinking water sources and reduction in human exposure is
      projected to:

    (1) Avoid approximately 6,500 cases of kidney and testicular
        cancer annually attributable to PFAS exposure in drinking
        water;

    (2) Reduce the incidence of thyroid disease, ulcerative colitis,
        and developmental disorders attributable to PFAS exposure;

    (3) Generate public health cost savings of approximately
        $12 billion annually.

  (c) WATERBORNE DISEASE PREVENTION. — Modernization of aging water
      distribution systems reduces the risk of contamination events
      and waterborne disease outbreaks, projected to avoid:

    (1) Approximately 500,000 cases of waterborne illness annually;
    (2) Emergency boil-water notices and associated economic disruption;
    (3) Bottled water expenditures by affected households.

SECTION 4. RETURN ON INVESTMENT ANALYSIS.

  (a) COST-BENEFIT RATIO. — The overall social return on investment for
      WIDRA programs is projected as:

    (1) TITLE I (Lead Pipe Elimination): Each dollar invested returns
        an estimated $4.00 in public health, cognitive, and economic
        benefits, yielding a total return of $180 billion;

    (2) TITLE II (Water Recycling and Reuse): Each dollar invested
        returns an estimated $2.50 in water supply reliability, avoided
        infrastructure costs, and environmental benefits, yielding a
        total return of $50 billion;

    (3) TITLE III (PFAS Remediation): Each dollar invested returns an
        estimated $3.00 in public health benefits and avoided healthcare
        costs, yielding a total return of $45 billion;

    (4) TITLE IV (Western Drought Resilience): Each dollar invested
        returns an estimated $2.00 in avoided economic losses, ecosystem
        services, and water supply reliability, yielding a total return
        of $50 billion;

    (5) Aggregate return on investment: .......................... $325 billion
        over the 10-year authorization period, representing an overall
        benefit-cost ratio of 2.7 to 1.

  (b) COMPARISON WITH DEFERRED INVESTMENT. — The Congressional Budget
      Office estimates that deferring the investments authorized by this
      Act for 10 years would increase total costs by approximately 40
      percent, as continued infrastructure deterioration compounds,
      lead exposure continues to exact public health costs, and drought
      conditions intensify under climate change projections.

SECTION 5. FISCAL IMPACT ESTIMATES.

  (a) DIRECT SPENDING. — The Congressional Budget Office estimates that
      this Act will increase direct spending by $120 billion over the
      period of fiscal years 2027-2036, distributed as follows:

    FY2027: $8 billion
    FY2028: $10 billion
    FY2029: $12 billion
    FY2030-$2036: $90 billion (average $12.86 billion per year)

  (b) REVENUE EFFECTS. — This Act is projected to increase Federal
      revenues by approximately $30 billion over the authorization
      period through:

    (1) Increased payroll and income tax receipts from employment
        generated by program expenditures;

    (2) Increased corporate tax receipts from manufacturing and
        construction activity;

    (3) Reduced Federal expenditures on public health, special
        education, and criminal justice attributable to lead
        exposure reduction.

  (c) STATE AND LOCAL FISCAL EFFECTS. — States and local governments
      are projected to realize:

    (1) Increased tax revenues of approximately $25 billion from
        economic activity generated by program expenditures;

    (2) Reduced emergency response and bottled water distribution
        costs of approximately $3 billion annually;

    (3) Avoided infrastructure replacement costs of approximately
        $15 billion from deferred investment escalation.

  (d) LONG-TERM FISCAL OUTLOOK. — Over a 30-year horizon, WIDRA is
      projected to be approximately cost-neutral on a Federal fiscal
      basis due to the public health cost savings, economic growth
      effects, and avoided emergency expenditures that offset the
      initial Federal investment.
