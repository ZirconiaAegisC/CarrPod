# CRITICAL INFRASTRUCTURE PROTECTION ACT OF 2026
## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

---

### I. PURPOSE AND SCOPE

The Critical Infrastructure Protection Act of 2026 (CIPA) establishes the most
comprehensive Federal framework for the assessment, rehabilitation, and
long-term protection of United States critical infrastructure ever enacted by
the Congress. This legislation addresses the systemic deferred maintenance
crisis affecting bridges, electrical transmission and distribution systems,
data centers, water supply and treatment networks, ports and inland waterways,
and pipeline systems.

The Act is predicated on extensive evidentiary findings documenting: (a) an
estimated $13.6 billion in annual direct corrosion costs to bridge
infrastructure alone, with total indirect economic impacts exceeding $38
billion per year; (b) deferred maintenance obligations exceeding $8.6 billion
across the California State University system and $9.1 billion across the
University of California system, reflecting a nationwide pattern of public
infrastructure disinvestment; (c) catastrophic wildfire mitigation costs
exceeding $73 billion incurred by a single investor-owned utility due to
deferred grid hardening; (d) annual water main failures exceeding 240,000
events causing $7.6 billion in non-revenue water losses; and (e) projected
data center electricity demand reaching 35 gigawatts by 2030, with cooling
infrastructure consuming up to 40 percent of facility energy.

### II. LEGISLATIVE STRUCTURE

The Act comprises ten Titles addressing distinct infrastructure sectors and
cross-cutting provisions:

**Title I — Protection of Bridge Infrastructure** — Establishes mandatory
bridge condition assessment protocols, lifecycle protective coating
standards requiring thermal spray zinc (TSZ) duplex systems for all new
and rehabilitated steel bridge construction, a comprehensive Bridge Fleet
Protection Fund, and a formula-based funding allocation mechanism.

**Title II — Electrical Grid Resilience** — Mandates wildfire mitigation
standards for electrical transmission and distribution systems in high-fire-
threat districts, establishes undergrounding incentive programs, requires
deployment of distributed control and detection (DCD) systems, creates a
federally funded weather sensor network program, and authorizes microgrid
and distributed energy resource integration grants.

**Title III — Data Center Infrastructure Protection** — Sets energy
efficiency standards for data center cooling systems, provides investment
tax credits for immersion cooling deployment, establishes mandatory Power
Usage Effectiveness (PUE) targets, and creates a photovoltaic-thermal (PVT)
assisted cooling demonstration program.

**Title IV — Water System Infrastructure** — Authorizes $75 billion for
nationwide pipe replacement, mandates complete elimination of lead service
lines by 2036, requires deployment of advanced leak detection technologies,
establishes water recycling and reuse mandates for water-stressed regions,
and creates emergency water system resilience programs.

**Title V — Ports and Waterways** — Authorizes port electrification
infrastructure, mandates cathodic protection systems for all maritime
steel structures, reforms the Harbor Maintenance Trust Fund disbursement
process, and establishes enhanced port security requirements.

**Title VI — Funding and Financing** — Authorizes appropriations totaling
$500 billion over a ten-year period, establishes an Infrastructure
Protection Bond program, creates a statutory framework for public-private
partnerships, sets state matching fund requirements, and establishes the
National Infrastructure Protection Bank.

**Title VII — Regulatory Standards** — Creates the National Infrastructure
Protection Rating (NIPR) system, a uniform numerical rating methodology for
all covered infrastructure assets; establishes mandatory inspection intervals;
codifies material and coating performance standards aligned with AMPP and ISO
12944 specifications; and creates a professional certification program for
infrastructure protection assessors.

**Title VIII — Economic Provisions** — Requires five-year cost-benefit
analyses for all funded projects, authorizes $12 billion for workforce
development and apprenticeship programs, imposes Buy America requirements
for all materials procured under the Act, and establishes small business
participation targets.

**Title IX — Enforcement and Compliance** — Grants expanded Inspector
General authority for infrastructure protection oversight, establishes
civil penalty structures up to $10 million per violation for willful
non-compliance, creates comprehensive whistleblower protections, authorizes
state enforcement grants, and mandates annual public reporting.

**Title X — Miscellaneous Provisions** — Contains severability, effective
date, and administrative authorization provisions.

### III. ECONOMIC JUSTIFICATION

Independent economic analysis projects that full implementation of CIPA will:

1. Create approximately 2.1 million direct and indirect jobs over the
ten-year authorization period, with sustained employment of approximately
850,000 positions in ongoing maintenance and inspection roles.

2. Generate cumulative GDP impact of approximately $1.2 trillion through
direct construction spending, supply chain activity, and productivity gains
from reduced infrastructure failure and disruption.

3. Yield a long-term benefit-cost ratio of 4.7:1 over a 50-year analysis
period, driven primarily by avoided catastrophic failure costs, reduced
emergency repair expenditures, and enhanced economic productivity from
reliable infrastructure.

4. Reduce annual bridge corrosion costs by an estimated $9.2 billion
per year by 2036 through the deployment of lifecycle protective coating
systems on the National Bridge Inventory.

5. Prevent projected wildfire-related liabilities exceeding $150 billion
over 30 years through mandated grid hardening and undergrounding programs
in high-fire-threat districts.

6. Reduce non-revenue water losses by an estimated 60 percent by 2036,
saving approximately $4.5 billion annually in treated water loss and
associated energy costs.

7. Achieve cumulative energy savings of approximately 120 terawatt-hours
by 2036 through the data center efficiency standards established under
Title III, equivalent to the annual electricity consumption of 11 million
households.

### IV. IMPLEMENTATION APPROACH

The Act adopts a phased implementation strategy:

**Phase I (Years 1–2):** Comprehensive condition assessment of all covered
infrastructure assets; establishment of the NIPR rating system methodology;
promulgation of initial regulatory standards by the FIPA Administrator;
creation of state-level infrastructure protection offices.

**Phase II (Years 3–5):** Prioritized rehabilitation of assets rated
NIPR-3 (Critical) or below; deployment of initial lifecycle coating systems
on high-priority bridges; commencement of lead service line replacement
programs; initial grid undergrounding in Tier 1 high-fire-threat districts.

**Phase III (Years 6–10):** Full deployment of lifecycle coating systems
across the National Bridge Inventory; completion of grid hardening in all
designated fire-threat districts; achievement of PUE targets for covered
data centers; substantial completion of lead service line elimination.

### V. ADMINISTRATION

The Act establishes the Federal Infrastructure Protection Authority (FIPA)
as an independent agency within the executive branch, governed by a
five-member Board of Directors appointed by the President with the advice
and consent of the Senate. FIPA shall have authority to promulgate
regulations, administer grant and loan programs, conduct inspections,
impose civil penalties, and coordinate with State and local infrastructure
protection agencies.
