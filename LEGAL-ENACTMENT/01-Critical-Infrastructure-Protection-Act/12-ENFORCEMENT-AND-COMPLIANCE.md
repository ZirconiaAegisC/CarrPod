# TITLE IX — ENFORCEMENT AND COMPLIANCE

---

## SUBTITLE A — INSPECTOR GENERAL AUTHORITY

**§ 901. Office of Inspector General for Infrastructure Protection.**

(a) ESTABLISHMENT.—There is established within the Federal Infrastructure
Protection Authority an Office of Inspector General, to be headed by an
Inspector General appointed by the President, by and with the advice and
consent of the Senate, in accordance with the Inspector General Act of
1978 (5 U.S.C. App.).

(b) DUTIES.—The Inspector General shall:

(1) Conduct audits and investigations relating to the programs and
operations of the Authority;

(2) Review existing and proposed legislation and regulations relating
to the programs and operations of the Authority and make recommendations
concerning the impact of such legislation and regulations on the economy
and efficiency of the Authority's programs and operations and on the
prevention and detection of fraud and abuse in such programs and
operations;

(3) Recommend policies for, and conduct, supervise, or coordinate
other activities carried out or financed by the Authority for the
purpose of promoting economy and efficiency in the administration
of, or preventing and detecting fraud and abuse in, its programs and
operations;

(4) Receive and investigate complaints or information from any person
concerning the possible existence of an activity constituting a
violation of law, rules, or regulations, or mismanagement, gross waste
of funds, abuse of authority, or a substantial and specific danger to
the public health and safety related to programs and operations of
the Authority;

(5) Refer matters for criminal prosecution to the Attorney General
when the Inspector General has reasonable grounds to believe there
has been a violation of Federal criminal law.

(c) SUBPOENA POWER.—The Inspector General may require by subpoena the
production of all information, documents, reports, answers, records,
accounts, papers, and other data in any medium (including electronically
stored information), as well as any tangible thing and documentary
evidence, necessary in the performance of the functions assigned by
this Act. Subpoenas shall be enforceable by order of any appropriate
United States district court.

(d) WHISTLEBLOWER COMPLAINT REVIEW.—The Inspector General shall, in
accordance with section 7 of the Inspector General Act of 1978, review
complaints and information concerning improper activities or violations
of law, rules, or regulations related to programs administered by the
Authority. The Inspector General shall not, after receipt of a complaint
or information from an employee of the Authority or a contractor,
subcontractor, or grantee of the Authority, disclose the identity of
the employee without the consent of the employee, unless the Inspector
General determines such disclosure is unavoidable during the course of
the investigation.

**§ 902. Civil Penalties.**

(a) VIOLATIONS RELATING TO NIPR REPORTING.—Any owner or operator of a
covered infrastructure asset who knowingly fails to conduct a required
NIPR assessment within the prescribed interval, or who knowingly submits
false or misleading NIPR assessment data to the FIPA Administrator, shall
be subject to a civil penalty not to exceed:

(1) $50,000 per day for each day beyond the prescribed assessment
interval that the assessment remains incomplete, subject to a maximum
penalty of $5,000,000 per assessment interval; and

(2) $500,000 per false or misleading submission, subject to a maximum
penalty of $10,000,000 for a pattern or practice of false submissions.

(b) VIOLATIONS RELATING TO COATING AND MATERIAL STANDARDS.—Any owner,
operator, contractor, or subcontractor who knowingly installs protective
coating materials on a covered infrastructure asset that do not meet the
standards established under section 703, or who knowingly falsifies coating
application inspection records, shall be subject to a civil penalty not to
exceed the greater of:

(1) The cost of removing and replacing the non-compliant coating system;
or

(2) $2,500,000.

(c) VIOLATIONS RELATING TO DATA CENTER PUE.—Any owner or operator of a
covered data center that fails to meet the applicable PUE target
established under section 303 shall be subject to a civil penalty
calculated as $0.05 per kilowatt-hour of excess energy consumption
attributable to PUE exceeding the target, as determined by the FIPA
Administrator, not to exceed $5,000,000 per calendar year for any single
covered data center.

(d) VIOLATIONS RELATING TO GRID HARDENING.—Any electrical utility that
fails to comply with the mandatory hardening standards established under
section 203 shall be subject to a civil penalty not to exceed:

(1) $10,000 per day per non-compliant circuit-mile for violations of
undergrounding and covered conductor requirements, subject to a maximum
penalty of $10,000,000 per utility per year; and

(2) $5,000 per day per non-compliant feeder for violations of DCD
system deployment requirements and fast-acting fault interruption
requirements, subject to a maximum penalty of $5,000,000 per utility
per year.

(e) VIOLATIONS RELATING TO WATER INFRASTRUCTURE.—Any community water system
that fails to comply with the lead service line replacement requirements of
section 403 shall be subject to a civil penalty of $10,000 per lead service
line per year that remains in service beyond the applicable replacement
deadline, subject to a maximum penalty of $5,000,000 per water system per
year.

(f) PENALTY CONSIDERATIONS.—In determining the amount of a civil penalty
under this section, the FIPA Administrator shall consider:

(1) The nature, circumstances, extent, and gravity of the violation;
(2) The degree of culpability of the violator, including any history of
prior violations;
(3) The ability of the violator to pay the penalty;
(4) The economic benefit, if any, resulting from the violation;
(5) The violator's efforts to achieve compliance after receiving
notification of the violation; and
(6) Such other matters as justice may require.

(g) JUDICIAL REVIEW.—Any person against whom a civil penalty is assessed
under this section may obtain review in the United States Court of Appeals
for the District of Columbia Circuit or for the circuit in which the
violation occurred, by filing a notice of appeal within 60 days of the
date of the order assessing the penalty.

(h) FAILURE TO PAY.—If any person fails to pay a civil penalty assessed
under this section after the order assessing the penalty has become final,
the FIPA Administrator may commence a civil action in an appropriate
United States district court to recover the amount assessed, plus
interest at the rate established under section 6621 of the Internal
Revenue Code of 1986 accruing from the date the penalty was due.

**§ 903. Criminal Penalties.**

(a) KNOWING ENDANGERMENT.—Any person who knowingly violates any requirement
of this Act, or any regulation promulgated under this Act, and thereby
places another person in imminent danger of death or serious bodily injury
shall, upon conviction, be subject to a fine under title 18, United States
Code, or imprisonment for not more than 15 years, or both.

(b) FALSE STATEMENTS.—Any person who knowingly and willfully makes any
material false statement, representation, or certification in any
application, record, report, plan, or other document filed or required
to be maintained under this Act shall, upon conviction, be subject to a
fine under title 18, United States Code, or imprisonment for not more
than five years, or both.

(c) DESTRUCTION OF RECORDS.—Any person who knowingly destroys, alters,
mutilates, or conceals any record required to be maintained under this
Act with the intent to impair the record's integrity or availability for
use in any proceeding under this Act shall, upon conviction, be subject
to a fine under title 18, United States Code, or imprisonment for not
more than 10 years, or both.

(d) ORGANIZATIONAL LIABILITY.—An organization convicted of an offense
under this section shall be subject to a fine not exceeding the greater
of $25,000,000 or three times the gross pecuniary gain derived from the
offense, or three times the gross pecuniary loss caused to any person by
the offense.

**§ 904. Whistleblower Protections.**

(a) PROHIBITION ON RETALIATION.—No employer, including any Federal, State,
or local government agency, contractor, subcontractor, or grantee, may
discharge, demote, suspend, threaten, harass, or in any other manner
discriminate against an employee because of any lawful act done by the
employee:

(1) To provide information, cause information to be provided, or
otherwise assist in an investigation regarding any conduct that the
employee reasonably believes constitutes a violation of this Act, or
any regulation or order issued under this Act, when the information or
assistance is provided to or the investigation is conducted by: (A) a
Federal, State, or local regulatory or law enforcement agency; (B) any
Member of Congress or any committee of Congress; (C) the Inspector
General of the Authority; or (D) a person with supervisory authority
over the employee or such other person working for the employer who
has the authority to investigate, discover, or terminate misconduct;

(2) To file, cause to be filed, testify, participate in, or otherwise
assist in a proceeding filed or about to be filed, with respect to any
violation of this Act or any regulation or order issued under this Act;
or

(3) To refuse to participate in any activity that the employee
reasonably believes would result in a violation of this Act or any
regulation or order issued under this Act.

(b) ENFORCEMENT ACTION.—

(1) An employee who alleges discharge or other discrimination in violation
of subsection (a) may file a complaint with the Secretary of Labor not
later than 180 days after the date on which the violation occurs.

(2) Within 90 days of receipt of a complaint filed under paragraph (1),
the Secretary of Labor shall conduct an investigation and determine
whether there is reasonable cause to believe that a violation of
subsection (a) has occurred.

(3) If the Secretary of Labor concludes that there is reasonable cause
to believe that a violation has occurred, the Secretary shall issue a
preliminary order providing the relief prescribed by subsection (c) and
may also order preliminary reinstatement of the employee.

(4) If the Secretary of Labor has not issued a final decision within
180 days of the filing of the complaint, and there is no showing that
such delay is due to the bad faith of the claimant, the claimant may
bring an action at law or equity for de novo review in the appropriate
district court of the United States, which shall have jurisdiction over
such an action without regard to the amount in controversy.

(c) REMEDIES.—

(1) An employee prevailing in an action under subsection (b) shall be
entitled to all relief necessary to make the employee whole, including:
(A) reinstatement with the same seniority status that the employee
would have had, but for the discrimination; (B) back pay, with
interest; (C) compensatory damages, including compensation for
emotional distress, litigation costs, expert witness fees, and
reasonable attorney's fees; and (D) punitive damages in an amount not
to exceed $250,000 where the employer's conduct is found to be
willful or egregious.

(2) An employee who prevails in an action under subsection (b) and who
is not ordered reinstated shall receive, in addition to other remedies,
front pay in an amount that compensates the employee for the reasonably
anticipated loss of future earnings resulting from the discriminatory
discharge.

(d) CONFIDENTIALITY.—The Secretary of Labor, the Inspector General of the
Authority, and any other Federal official receiving a whistleblower
complaint under this section shall protect the confidentiality of the
complainant to the maximum extent permitted by law, including by
withholding the complainant's identity from the employer until such
time as disclosure is necessary for an effective investigation or
enforcement proceeding.

(e) WHISTLEBLOWER REWARD PROGRAM.—

(1) The FIPA Administrator shall establish a whistleblower reward
program under which individuals who voluntarily provide original
information leading to the successful enforcement of a civil penalty
or criminal fine under this Act may receive an award of not less than
10 percent and not more than 30 percent of the monetary sanctions
collected.

(2) Awards under this subsection shall be paid from the amounts
collected in connection with the enforcement action.

**§ 905. State Enforcement Grants.**

(a) GRANT PROGRAM.—The FIPA Administrator shall establish a State
Infrastructure Protection Enforcement Grant Program, authorized at
$3,000,000,000 for the period of fiscal years 2027 through 2036, to
assist State agencies with the costs of:

(1) Hiring and training infrastructure protection inspectors and
assessors;
(2) Procuring inspection equipment, including coating thickness gauges,
adhesion testers, corrosion potential measurement equipment, and
unmanned aerial vehicles for bridge and transmission line inspection;
(3) Developing and maintaining information technology systems for
inspection data management, NIPR rating, and compliance tracking;
(4) Conducting enforcement proceedings and prosecuting violations of
State infrastructure protection laws and regulations.

(b) MAINTENANCE OF EFFORT.—As a condition of receiving a grant under this
section, a State shall maintain its aggregate expenditures for
infrastructure protection enforcement activities at not less than the
average level of such expenditures for the three fiscal years preceding
the date of enactment.

(c) ANNUAL REPORT.—Each State receiving a grant under this section shall
submit to the FIPA Administrator an annual report describing:

(1) The number of infrastructure protection inspections conducted;
(2) The number of violations identified and enforcement actions
initiated;
(3) The amount of civil penalties assessed and collected;
(4) The condition of covered infrastructure assets within the State,
as measured by the NIPR rating distribution; and
(5) Improvements in infrastructure condition attributed to enforcement
activities.

**§ 906. Annual Reporting Requirements.**

(a) FIPA ANNUAL REPORT.—Not later than March 1 of each year, the FIPA
Administrator shall submit to the President and to the Congress a
comprehensive report on the implementation of this Act, including:

(1) The status of all programs and activities authorized under this Act,
including obligations and expenditures by program and by State;
(2) The NIPR rating distribution for each covered asset class;
(3) A summary of enforcement actions, including civil penalties
assessed and collected, criminal referrals made, and criminal
prosecutions initiated;
(4) An assessment of whether the programs authorized under this Act are
achieving the purposes set forth in section 2.

(b) PUBLIC AVAILABILITY.—The annual report required under subsection (a)
shall be made publicly available on the FIPA website, in a format that
is searchable, machine-readable, and accessible to individuals with
disabilities.
