# TITLE III — DATA CENTER INFRASTRUCTURE PROTECTION

---

## SUBTITLE A — COOLING EFFICIENCY STANDARDS

**§ 301. Findings and Purpose.**

(a) FINDINGS.—The Congress finds that:

(1) The United States data center sector consumed approximately 90
terawatt-hours of electricity in 2024, and the Federal Energy Regulatory
Commission projects that data center peak electricity demand will reach
35 gigawatts by 2030, representing approximately 8 percent of total
national electricity demand and placing unprecedented strain on
electrical generation, transmission, and distribution infrastructure.

(2) Data center cooling systems typically account for 25 to 40 percent
of total facility energy consumption, depending on climate zone,
facility design, and cooling technology deployed, and improvements in
cooling efficiency represent the single largest achievable reduction in
data center energy intensity.

(3) Single-phase and two-phase immersion cooling technologies have been
demonstrated to reduce data center cooling energy consumption by 70 to 95
percent compared to conventional computer room air conditioning (CRAC)
and computer room air handler (CRAH) systems, while simultaneously
increasing information technology equipment density, improving equipment
reliability through reduced thermal cycling, and enabling waste-heat
recovery for beneficial use.

(4) Photovoltaic-thermal (PVT) hybrid solar collectors can simultaneously
generate electricity and provide low-grade thermal energy for absorption
chiller systems, desiccant dehumidification, or direct liquid cooling
heat rejection, offering a pathway to net-zero cooling energy for data
centers in appropriate climate zones.

(5) Power Usage Effectiveness (PUE), defined as the ratio of total
facility energy to information technology equipment energy, is the
internationally recognized metric for data center infrastructure
efficiency, and facilities employing best-in-class liquid cooling
systems have demonstrated sustained annualized PUE values below 1.10.

(b) PURPOSE.—The purpose of this Title is to establish mandatory efficiency
standards for data center cooling infrastructure, provide investment
incentives for advanced cooling technology deployment, and align national
data center infrastructure policy with the energy security, economic
competitiveness, and environmental objectives of the United States.

**§ 302. Definitions.**

In this Title:

(1) COVERED DATA CENTER.—The term "covered data center" means any facility
with total information technology equipment design power exceeding 500
kilowatts that is used primarily for the processing, storage, or
networking of digital data for commercial, government, or institutional
purposes, excluding telecommunications central offices and facilities
used exclusively for the transmission of information.

(2) POWER USAGE EFFECTIVENESS (PUE).—The term "Power Usage Effectiveness"
means the ratio of total facility energy consumption to information
technology equipment energy consumption, measured over a 12-month period
and calculated in accordance with the methodology published by the
International Organization for Standardization in ISO/IEC 30134-2:2016,
or any successor standard.

(3) IMMERSION COOLING.—The term "immersion cooling" means a cooling
methodology in which information technology equipment is directly
immersed in a dielectric fluid that removes heat through single-phase
natural or forced convection, two-phase boiling and condensation, or
a combination thereof, without intervening heat exchangers at the
component level.

(4) PHOTOVOLTAIC-THERMAL (PVT) SYSTEM.—The term "photovoltaic-thermal
system" means a solar energy conversion system that simultaneously
generates electricity through photovoltaic cells and captures thermal
energy through a heat transfer fluid circulated behind or through the
photovoltaic module, for use in cooling, heating, or industrial process
applications.

(5) INFORMATION TECHNOLOGY EQUIPMENT DESIGN POWER.—The term "information
technology equipment design power" means the aggregate nameplate power
rating of all servers, storage systems, network equipment, and
uninterruptible power supplies installed or planned for installation
in the data center.

**§ 303. Mandatory Power Usage Effectiveness Targets.**

(a) NEW DATA CENTERS.—Each covered data center for which construction is
commenced after the date that is two years after the date of enactment of
this Act shall be designed and operated to achieve the following annualized
Power Usage Effectiveness:

(1) For data centers located in ASHRAE climate zones 1 through 4:
PUE not to exceed 1.20, measured as an annualized average over each
calendar year.

(2) For data centers located in ASHRAE climate zones 5 through 8:
PUE not to exceed 1.25, measured as an annualized average over each
calendar year.

(3) For covered data centers with information technology equipment design
power exceeding 10 megawatts, regardless of climate zone: PUE not to
exceed 1.15, measured as an annualized average over each calendar year.

(b) EXISTING DATA CENTERS.—

(1) Each covered data center in operation as of the effective date of this
section shall achieve the applicable PUE targets established under
subsection (a) not later than December 31, 2036.

(2) The FIPA Administrator may grant a compliance deadline extension of
not more than three years to an owner or operator of an existing covered
data center upon a showing that: (A) physical constraints of the existing
facility prevent achievement of the PUE target without structural
modification; (B) a capital improvement plan to achieve the PUE target
has been approved and funded; and (C) interim efficiency improvements
have reduced PUE by not less than 15 percent from the baseline year.

(c) MEASUREMENT AND REPORTING.—

(1) Each owner or operator of a covered data center shall install
permanent metering equipment capable of measuring and recording total
facility energy consumption and information technology equipment
energy consumption at intervals not exceeding 15 minutes.

(2) Annual PUE calculations, together with supporting metering data,
shall be submitted to the FIPA Administrator not later than March 31
of the year following the reporting year, in an electronic format
prescribed by the Administrator.

(3) The FIPA Administrator shall publish an annual report summarizing
PUE compliance data for covered data centers, in a manner that does
not disclose proprietary or commercially sensitive information of
individual data center operators.

(d) PENALTIES.—An owner or operator of a covered data center that fails to
meet the applicable PUE target established under this section shall be
subject to a civil penalty of $0.05 per kilowatt-hour of excess energy
consumption attributable to PUE exceeding the target, as calculated by
the FIPA Administrator, not to exceed $5,000,000 in any calendar year
for any single covered data center.

**§ 304. Immersion Cooling Deployment Incentives.**

(a) INVESTMENT TAX CREDIT.—For purposes of section 48 of the Internal Revenue
Code of 1986, qualified immersion cooling property placed in service after
December 31, 2026, shall be treated as energy property eligible for the
energy credit at the rate of 30 percent in the case of property the
construction of which begins before January 1, 2032, and at the rate of 25
percent in the case of property the construction of which begins after
December 31, 2031, and before January 1, 2035.

(b) QUALIFIED IMMERSION COOLING PROPERTY.—The term "qualified immersion
cooling property" means:

(1) Dielectric fluid tanks, pumps, heat exchangers, filtration systems,
and associated piping and instrumentation used in a single-phase or
two-phase immersion cooling system;

(2) Dielectric heat transfer fluids specifically formulated for use in
immersion cooling of information technology equipment;

(3) Monitoring and control systems required for safe and efficient
operation of immersion cooling equipment;

(4) Dry coolers, cooling towers, or liquid-to-liquid heat exchangers
used for rejection of heat from an immersion cooling system to the
environment; and

(5) Building modifications, including structural reinforcement, fluid
containment, and fire suppression systems, required to accommodate
immersion cooling equipment.

(c) ACCELERATED DEPRECIATION.—Qualified immersion cooling property shall be
classified as 5-year property under section 168(e)(3)(B) of the Internal
Revenue Code of 1986.

(d) DOE LOAN GUARANTEES.—The Secretary of Energy shall make available not
less than $3,000,000,000 in loan guarantees under title XVII of the Energy
Policy Act of 2005 for projects involving the deployment of immersion cooling
systems at covered data centers, with streamlined application procedures for
projects with total capital cost below $50 million.

**§ 305. Photovoltaic-Thermal Assisted Cooling Program.**

(a) PROGRAM ESTABLISHMENT.—The Secretary of Energy shall establish a program
of competitive grants for the demonstration and deployment of
photovoltaic-thermal (PVT) assisted cooling systems at covered data centers.

(b) GRANT ELIGIBILITY.—Grants shall be available for:

(1) Engineering feasibility studies and system design for PVT-assisted
cooling systems;

(2) Capital costs of PVT collector procurement and installation;

(3) Integration of PVT thermal output with absorption chiller, adsorption
chiller, desiccant dehumidification, or direct liquid cooling heat
rejection systems;

(4) Monitoring equipment and data acquisition systems for performance
verification and optimization.

(c) FEDERAL COST SHARE.—The Federal share of costs for projects funded under
this section shall not exceed:

(1) 50 percent for feasibility studies and design;
(2) 35 percent for capital costs of PVT system procurement and
installation;
(3) 40 percent for projects located in low-income communities or
energy communities as defined by the Secretary of Energy.

(d) AUTHORIZATION.—There is authorized to be appropriated $750,000,000
for the period of fiscal years 2027 through 2036 to carry out this section.

**§ 306. Data Center Infrastructure Security.**

(a) PHYSICAL SECURITY REQUIREMENTS.—Each covered data center shall implement
physical security measures including:

(1) Perimeter intrusion detection systems;
(2) Multi-factor access control for all entry points;
(3) Continuous video surveillance of all exterior and interior common areas,
with retention of recordings for not less than 90 days;
(4) Redundant power supply with on-site backup generation capable of
operating at full load for not less than 48 hours without refueling, or
72 hours for data centers classified as critical national infrastructure
by the Secretary of Homeland Security;
(5) Electromagnetic pulse (EMP) protection meeting MIL-STD-188-125-1
standards for data centers classified as critical national infrastructure.

(b) CYBERSECURITY REQUIREMENTS.—Each covered data center shall maintain
cybersecurity controls meeting the standards established under the National
Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) Special Publication 800-53,
Revision 5, or any successor standard, for the High impact baseline.

(c) SECURITY ASSESSMENT REQUIREMENT.—Each covered data center shall undergo
an independent security assessment, including physical security, cybersecurity,
and operational resilience, at intervals not to exceed two years. Assessment
reports shall be submitted to the FIPA Administrator and, for data centers
classified as critical national infrastructure, to the Secretary of Homeland
Security.

**§ 307. Waste Heat Recovery Standards.**

(a) FEASIBILITY ASSESSMENT.—For each covered data center for which
construction is commenced after the date of enactment of this Act, the
owner or operator shall complete a waste heat recovery feasibility
assessment evaluating:

(1) Proximity to potential waste heat consumers, including district
heating systems, industrial process heat users, greenhouse agriculture,
and aquaculture facilities;
(2) Technical feasibility of heat recovery and delivery;
(3) Economic cost-benefit analysis of waste heat recovery, including
avoided cooling energy costs;
(4) Potential for integration with thermal energy storage to match
heat supply with demand.

(b) RECOVERY MANDATE.—If the waste heat recovery feasibility assessment
demonstrates a positive net present value over a 15-year analysis period
using a discount rate of 7 percent, the owner or operator shall implement
waste heat recovery measures at the time of initial construction or
within five years of commencing operations, whichever is later.

(c) REPORTING.—Waste heat recovery feasibility assessments shall be
submitted to the FIPA Administrator and shall be made publicly available,
excluding proprietary or commercially sensitive information.

**§ 308. Research and Development.**

(a) The Secretary of Energy, in coordination with the National Science
Foundation and the National Institute of Standards and Technology, shall
establish a Data Center Infrastructure Research Program to advance:

(1) Next-generation dielectric fluids with improved thermal conductivity,
reduced global warming potential, and enhanced material compatibility;
(2) Two-phase immersion cooling systems with passive circulation;
(3) Direct-to-chip liquid cooling with microchannel and jet impingement
technologies;
(4) Thermal energy storage integration for data center demand response;
(5) Artificial intelligence-driven cooling system optimization;
(6) Advanced heat exchanger materials and designs for data center
heat rejection.

(b) There is authorized to be appropriated $250,000,000 for the period of
fiscal years 2027 through 2031 to carry out this section.
