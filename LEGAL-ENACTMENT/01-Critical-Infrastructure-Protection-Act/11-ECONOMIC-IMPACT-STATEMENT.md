# TITLE VIII — ECONOMIC PROVISIONS

---

## § 801. Congressional Budget Office Cost Estimate.

(a) FINDING.—The Congress finds that the Congressional Budget Office has
provided a preliminary cost estimate for the Critical Infrastructure
Protection Act of 2026 in accordance with section 402 of the Congressional
Budget Act of 1974 (2 U.S.C. 653).

(b) ESTIMATED DIRECT SPENDING AND REVENUES.—

(1) The CBO estimates that enacting this Act would increase direct
spending by $500 billion over the 2027–2036 period.

(2) The CBO estimates that the infrastructure protection surcharge
established under section 602(e) would increase Federal revenues by
approximately $42 billion over the 2027–2036 period, based on current
import volume projections and the 0.1 percent ad valorem rate.

(3) The CBO estimates that loan repayments to the National Infrastructure
Protection Bank would offset approximately $18 billion in direct spending
over the 2027–2036 period.

(4) The CBO estimates a net increase in the Federal deficit of
approximately $440 billion over the 2027–2036 period attributable to
this Act, excluding macroeconomic feedback effects.

(c) MACROECONOMIC FEEDBACK.—

(1) The CBO estimates that the provisions of this Act would increase
Gross Domestic Product by an average of 0.3 percent per year over the
2027–2036 period, yielding approximately $90 billion in additional
Federal revenues from income, payroll, and corporate taxes attributable
to increased economic activity.

(2) Inclusive of macroeconomic feedback effects, the CBO estimates a net
increase in the Federal deficit of approximately $350 billion over the
2027–2036 period.

(3) Over a 50-year analysis period, the CBO projects that maintenance
cost savings, avoided catastrophic failure costs, and sustained
productivity gains would yield a net fiscal benefit to the Federal
Government, with the Act becoming deficit-neutral by approximately
fiscal year 2047 and producing cumulative net fiscal savings of
approximately $280 billion by fiscal year 2076.

## § 802. Employment and Workforce Impact.

(a) DIRECT EMPLOYMENT.—The Department of Labor estimates that full
implementation of the programs authorized under this Act would create:

(1) Approximately 750,000 direct construction, manufacturing, and
installation jobs during Phase II and Phase III (fiscal years 2029–2036),
including:

(A) 180,000 jobs in bridge coating application and structural
rehabilitation;
(B) 220,000 jobs in electrical grid undergrounding, conductor
replacement, and substation construction;
(C) 120,000 jobs in water pipe replacement, treatment plant
construction, and leak detection deployment;
(D) 95,000 jobs in port electrification, cathodic protection
installation, and channel dredging;
(E) 75,000 jobs in data center cooling system installation and
retrofit;
(F) 60,000 jobs in manufacturing of protective coating materials,
electrical equipment, pipe, and construction materials.

(2) An additional 300,000 direct jobs in ongoing inspection, assessment,
maintenance, and monitoring roles sustained throughout the authorization
period.

(b) INDIRECT AND INDUCED EMPLOYMENT.—

(1) The Department of Commerce estimates that the programs authorized
under this Act would support an additional 1,050,000 indirect and induced
jobs through supply chain activity and consumer spending by directly
employed workers, for a total employment impact of approximately 2.1
million jobs.

(2) Of the total employment impact, an estimated 35 percent would be in
manufacturing, 30 percent in construction, 15 percent in professional
and technical services, 10 percent in transportation and logistics, and
10 percent in all other sectors.

(c) WORKFORCE DEVELOPMENT.—To ensure that an adequately trained workforce
is available to implement the provisions of this Act, the Secretary of
Labor, in coordination with the FIPA Administrator, shall:

(1) Establish a National Infrastructure Workforce Development Program,
authorized at $12,000,000,000 for the period of fiscal years 2027 through
2031, providing grants to:

(A) Community colleges and technical schools for curriculum development
and equipment acquisition in corrosion engineering, protective coating
application, cathodic protection, grid construction, and leak detection
technology;
(B) Labor-management apprenticeship programs registered with the
Department of Labor for infrastructure protection trades;
(C) Non-profit workforce development organizations serving
underrepresented populations in the construction and manufacturing
trades;
(D) Industry associations for the development of competency-based
training standards and portable credentials recognized across
States and employers;

(2) Require that not less than 20 percent of total labor hours on
projects receiving Federal funds under this Act be performed by
apprentices enrolled in a registered apprenticeship program;

(3) Require that recipients of grants and contracts under this Act
demonstrate compliance with local hire provisions that prioritize
employment of workers residing within the economic development region
or metropolitan statistical area where the project is located.

## § 803. Buy America Provisions.

(a) DOMESTIC CONTENT REQUIREMENT.—

(1) All iron, steel, manufactured products, and construction materials
used in projects receiving Federal financial assistance under this Act
shall be produced in the United States.

(2) The requirement of paragraph (1) shall apply to: (A) all processes
from initial melting through application of coatings for iron and steel
products; (B) all manufacturing processes from initial fabrication
through final assembly for manufactured products; and (C) all mining,
production, and manufacturing processes for construction materials.

(b) WAIVER AUTHORITY.—The head of the Federal agency providing financial
assistance may waive the requirements of subsection (a) upon a written
finding that:

(1) Applying the requirement would be inconsistent with the public
interest;
(2) The iron, steel, manufactured products, or construction materials
are not produced in the United States in sufficient and reasonably
available quantities or of satisfactory quality; or
(3) Inclusion of domestically produced materials would increase the
cost of the overall project by more than 25 percent.

(c) WAIVER PUBLICATION.—Any waiver issued under subsection (b) shall be
published in the Federal Register with a detailed justification, and
shall be posted on a publicly accessible website maintained by the FIPA
Administrator.

(d) PENALTY FOR FALSE CERTIFICATION.—A contractor or subcontractor that
knowingly certifies false domestic content information shall be subject
to debarment from Federal contracting for a period of not less than three
years and shall be liable to the United States for damages equal to three
times the value of the non-compliant materials.

## § 804. Small Business Participation.

(a) SMALL BUSINESS SET-ASIDES.—Not less than 23 percent of the total value
of prime contracts awarded under this Act shall be awarded to small business
concerns as defined under section 3 of the Small Business Act (15 U.S.C. 632).

(b) SUBCONTRACTING TARGETS.—Each prime contractor receiving a contract
exceeding $1,500,000 under this Act shall establish a subcontracting plan
that sets a goal of not less than 35 percent of the total subcontracted
value for awards to small business concerns, including specific goals
for small disadvantaged businesses, women-owned small businesses,
service-disabled veteran-owned small businesses, and HUBZone small
businesses.

(c) SMALL BUSINESS TECHNICAL ASSISTANCE.—The FIPA Administrator shall
establish a Small Business Infrastructure Protection Center to provide
technical assistance, bonding assistance, and mentorship to small business
concerns seeking to participate in programs authorized under this Act.

## § 805. Cost-Benefit Analysis Requirements.

(a) PROJECT-LEVEL ANALYSIS.—For any project receiving Federal funding under
this Act with total project costs exceeding $50,000,000, the project sponsor
shall submit to the FIPA Administrator a cost-benefit analysis prepared in
accordance with Office of Management and Budget Circular A-94, including:

(1) A lifecycle cost analysis over a minimum 50-year analysis period
using a real discount rate of 3 percent and sensitivity analysis at
discount rates of 1 percent and 7 percent;
(2) Quantification of direct benefits, including: (A) avoided repair and
replacement costs; (B) reduced maintenance expenditures; (C) extended
asset service life; (D) reduced service disruption frequency and duration;
(3) Quantification of indirect benefits, including: (A) reduced traffic
delay costs; (B) reduced supply chain disruption costs; (C) reduced
environmental remediation costs; (D) avoided emergency response costs;
(4) Monetization of non-market benefits to the extent feasible, including
public health improvements, ecosystem service preservation, and community
resilience enhancement.

(b) PROGRAM-LEVEL ANALYSIS.—The FIPA Administrator shall submit to Congress,
not later than December 31, 2031, a comprehensive cost-benefit analysis of
all programs established under this Act, covering the first five years of
implementation, and updating the analysis every five years thereafter.

## § 806. Long-Run Economic Impact Assessment.

(a) 50-YEAR BENEFIT-COST RATIO.—Independent economic analysis commissioned
by the FIPA Administrator projects the following long-run economic impacts:

(1) A cumulative benefit-cost ratio of 4.7:1 over 50 years, with $2.35
trillion in total benefits against $500 billion in total costs (undiscounted).

(2) Annual net benefits to the United States economy reaching $95 billion
by fiscal year 2046, driven primarily by: (A) avoided bridge corrosion costs
of $9.2 billion per year; (B) reduced non-revenue water losses of $4.5
billion per year; (C) avoided wildfire-related costs of $25 billion per year;
(D) energy efficiency savings in data centers of $12 billion per year; and
(E) reduced emergency infrastructure repair costs, improved supply chain
efficiency, and enhanced national security resilience.

(3) A net present value of $520 billion in Federal, State, and local fiscal
benefits over 50 years when accounting for increased tax revenues from
economic growth, reduced emergency spending, and avoided catastrophic
failure costs.

(b) PRODUCTIVITY GAINS.—Infrastructure-related supply chain delays are
estimated to cost the United States economy approximately $120 billion
per year. The infrastructure protection investments authorized under this
Act are projected to reduce these costs by 15 to 25 percent through
improved bridge reliability, port efficiency, and grid resilience, yielding
annual productivity gains of $18 billion to $30 billion by fiscal year 2036.

(c) NATIONAL SECURITY VALUE.—The Department of Defense has assessed that
the degradation of critical infrastructure reduces military readiness by
approximately $4 billion per year through impaired mobilization capability,
degraded installation resilience, and increased logistics costs. Full
implementation of this Act is projected to eliminate 80 percent of this
readiness penalty.
