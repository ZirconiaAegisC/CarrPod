# TITLE II — ELECTRICAL GRID RESILIENCE

---

## SUBTITLE A — WILDFIRE MITIGATION AND GRID HARDENING

**§ 201. Findings and Purpose.**

(a) FINDINGS.—The Congress finds that:

(1) Electrical transmission and distribution infrastructure has been
identified as the ignition source for six of the ten most destructive
wildfires in California history, including the 2018 Camp Fire, which
caused 85 fatalities, destroyed 18,804 structures, and resulted in
insurer losses exceeding $12.5 billion.

(2) Investor-owned utilities in California incurred wildfire-related
liabilities exceeding $73 billion through bankruptcy proceedings,
settlements, and ongoing mitigation expenditures, demonstrating the
catastrophic financial consequence of deferred investment in
electrical grid protection.

(3) Post-ignition wildfire costs are substantially higher than the
cost of preventative grid hardening measures: the Department of Energy's
2023 Grid Resilience Study estimates that comprehensive undergrounding
and conductor replacement in high-fire-threat districts costs
approximately $3.5 million per mile, while the societal cost of a
single catastrophic wildfire ignition exceeds $15 billion when
accounting for property loss, business interruption, environmental
damage, healthcare costs from smoke exposure, and loss of life.

(4) The threat of utility-caused wildfire is not limited to California:
the National Interagency Fire Center has documented expanding wildland-
urban interface areas in 38 States, and the USDA Forest Service projects
that climate-driven changes in temperature, precipitation, and vegetation
dryness will double the area at high risk of catastrophic wildfire by 2050.

(b) PURPOSE.—The purpose of this Title is to establish mandatory minimum
standards for wildfire risk mitigation on electrical transmission and
distribution systems, provide financial incentives for system hardening,
and deploy advanced detection and response technologies to eliminate
utility-ignited wildfire as a threat to life, property, and the environment.

**§ 202. High-Fire-Threat District Designation.**

(a) MAPPING REQUIREMENT.—The Secretary of Energy, in coordination with the
Secretary of Agriculture, the Secretary of the Interior, the Administrator
of the Federal Emergency Management Agency, and the Director of the United
States Geological Survey, shall develop and publish a national High-Fire-
Threat District (HFTD) map within 18 months of enactment.

(b) HFTD TIERS.—The HFTD map shall designate areas into the following
three tiers based on wildfire ignition probability, potential fire
consequence, and wildland-urban interface exposure:

(1) TIER 1 — EXTREME FIRE THREAT.—Areas where: (A) historical fire
frequency exceeds 0.5 ignitions per square mile per year; (B) vegetation
fuel loading exceeds 50 tons per acre; (C) population density within the
wildland-urban interface exceeds 250 persons per square mile; and (D)
topographic slope in a substantial portion of the area exceeds 30 percent.

(2) TIER 2 — HIGH FIRE THREAT.—Areas meeting two or more of the Tier 1
criteria but not all four.

(3) TIER 3 — ELEVATED FIRE THREAT.—Areas meeting one of the Tier 1
criteria or areas adjacent to Tier 1 or Tier 2 areas where fire spread
modeling projects ember transport distances would carry ignition sources
into Tier 1 or Tier 2 areas under typical fire weather conditions.

(c) UPDATE REQUIREMENT.—The HFTD map shall be updated at intervals not to
exceed five years and shall incorporate updated climate projections, fire
history data, and land-use changes.

**§ 203. Mandatory Grid Hardening Standards.**

(a) TIER 1 HFTD REQUIREMENTS.—Not later than December 31, 2036, each
electrical utility operating distribution or transmission infrastructure
within a Tier 1 High-Fire-Threat District shall:

(1) UNDERGROUND DISTRIBUTION.—Complete undergrounding of not less than
85 percent of distribution circuit miles within the Tier 1 area, or
demonstrate to the satisfaction of the FIPA Administrator that
alternative mitigation measures provide equivalent wildfire ignition
risk reduction.

(2) COVERED CONDUCTOR REPLACEMENT.—Replace all remaining overhead
distribution conductors with covered conductor meeting the requirements
of IEEE Standard 2748-2023 for tree-wire and spacer-cable configurations
rated for fault-current limiting at the circuit protection device setting.

(3) FAST-ACTING FAULT INTERRUPTION.—Deploy fast-acting fault interruption
devices on all distribution feeders serving HFTD areas, with trip settings
configured to limit line-to-ground fault energy to less than 2,500
ampere-squared-seconds (I²t) under worst-case fault conditions at the
conductor location furthest from the substation.

(4) RECLOSER POLICY.—Configure automatic reclosing logic to require
manual patrol verification before any reclosing attempt on circuits
serving Tier 1 HFTD areas when fire weather conditions exceed the 95th
percentile of historical fire-weather index values for that location.

(5) VEGETATION MANAGEMENT.—Maintain minimum conductor-to-vegetation
clearance of 15 feet in all directions under worst-case wind and
conductor sag conditions, and conduct vegetation management patrols
at intervals not to exceed six months.

(b) TIER 2 HFTD REQUIREMENTS.—Not later than December 31, 2041, each
electrical utility operating infrastructure within a Tier 2 HFTD shall
meet the requirements of subsection (a)(2), (a)(3), (a)(4), and (a)(5),
with undergrounding targets of not less than 50 percent of distribution
circuit miles.

(c) FEDERAL COST-SHARING.—For utilities subject to rate-of-return regulation
by a State public utility commission, the Federal Government shall provide
cost-sharing for HFTD hardening measures as follows:

(1) TIER 1 UNDERGROUNDING — 40 percent Federal cost share;
(2) TIER 2 UNDERGROUNDING — 30 percent Federal cost share;
(3) COVERED CONDUCTOR REPLACEMENT — 25 percent Federal cost share;
(4) FAST-ACTING FAULT INTERRUPTION — 50 percent Federal cost share.

**§ 204. Weather Sensor Network Deployment.**

(a) NATIONAL NETWORK ESTABLISHMENT.—The Secretary of Energy, in coordination
with the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration and the United
States Geological Survey, shall establish a National Grid Weather Sensor
Network (NGWSN) for the purpose of providing real-time, high-resolution
meteorological data to support grid operations and wildfire risk
management.

(b) SENSOR DENSITY REQUIREMENTS.—

(1) IN TIER 1 HFTD AREAS, weather stations shall be deployed at an average
density of not less than one station per 20 circuit-miles of overhead
electrical infrastructure, measuring at minimum: wind speed, wind gust,
wind direction, temperature, relative humidity, solar radiation, and
fuel moisture at two heights.

(2) IN TIER 2 HFTD AREAS, weather stations shall be deployed at an
average density of not less than one station per 40 circuit-miles.

(3) IN ALL OTHER AREAS, weather stations shall be deployed at a density
sufficient to support the grid situational awareness requirements of the
North American Electric Reliability Corporation.

(c) DATA INTEGRATION.—Data from the NGWSN shall be integrated into a
publicly accessible, real-time data platform operated by the Department
of Energy, with application programming interfaces (APIs) available for
integration into utility control systems, fire agency dispatch systems,
and public safety alerting platforms.

(d) FEDERAL FUNDING.—There is authorized to be appropriated to the
Secretary of Energy $2,500,000,000 for the period of fiscal years 2027
through 2036 for procurement, installation, and operation of the NGWSN.

**§ 205. Distributed Control and Detection System Deployment.**

(a) DEFINITION.—In this section, the term "distributed control and
detection system" or "DCD system" means a network of intelligent
electronic devices deployed on distribution feeders that provides:

(1) Real-time monitoring of voltage, current, phase angle, and power
quality at multiple points along each feeder;
(2) High-impedance fault detection using harmonic analysis and
waveform recognition;
(3) Sectionalizing capability enabling fault isolation to the
smallest possible circuit segment;
(4) Communication with the utility's supervisory control and data
acquisition (SCADA) and distribution management system (DMS); and
(5) Fail-safe operation that defaults to the most protective state
upon loss of communication.

(b) DEPLOYMENT REQUIREMENT.—Not later than December 31, 2031, each
electrical utility operating distribution infrastructure in a Tier 1 or
Tier 2 HFTD shall deploy DCD systems on all distribution feeders serving
those areas.

(c) DCD SYSTEM GRANTS.—The Secretary of Energy shall administer a
competitive grant program providing up to 50 percent Federal cost share
for DCD system deployment. Priority shall be given to applications
serving Tier 1 HFTD areas, low-income communities, and areas with
limited evacuation routes.

**§ 206. Microgrid and Distributed Energy Resource Integration.**

(a) MICROGRID RESILIENCE GRANTS.—The Secretary of Energy shall establish
a Microgrid Resilience Grant Program, authorized at $5,000,000,000 for
the period of fiscal years 2027 through 2036, providing grants for:

(1) Feasibility studies and design for community microgrids serving
critical facilities, including hospitals, fire stations, police stations,
emergency shelters, water and wastewater treatment facilities, and
telecommunications infrastructure;

(2) Capital costs for microgrid construction, including distributed
generation resources, energy storage systems, microgrid controllers,
and associated switchgear and protection equipment;

(3) Interconnection of microgrids with the distribution system in a
manner that enables islanded operation for not less than 72 hours
without external fuel resupply.

(b) CRITICAL FACILITY PRIORITIZATION.—Not less than 60 percent of funds
appropriated under this section shall be allocated to microgrids serving
critical facilities in Tier 1 or Tier 2 HFTD areas or in areas designated
by FEMA as having high or very high natural hazard risk ratings.

(c) DISTRIBUTED ENERGY RESOURCE INTERCONNECTION.—The Federal Energy
Regulatory Commission, in coordination with the National Association of
Regulatory Utility Commissioners, shall develop and promulgate a model
interconnection standard for distributed energy resources that:

(1) Establishes maximum interconnection review timelines not exceeding
30 days for systems under 50 kilowatts and 60 days for systems under
500 kilowatts;

(2) Requires utilities to publish hosting capacity maps identifying
locations on the distribution system with available capacity for
distributed generation interconnection;

(3) Establishes standardized technical screens for interconnection
review that avoid the need for detailed impact studies for the
majority of applications.

**§ 207. Public Safety Power Shutoff Standards.**

(a) PSPS MINIMIZATION.—Each electrical utility that implements public
safety power shutoffs (PSPS) as a wildfire risk mitigation measure shall:

(1) Employ sectionalizing devices and DCD systems to limit the scope of
any PSPS event to the smallest feasible geographic area and the minimum
feasible duration;

(2) Provide not less than 48 hours advance notice to affected customers,
public safety agencies, and local governments before implementing a
proactive PSPS, and not less than 72 hours advance notice when fire
weather forecasting supports such notice;

(3) Maintain and publish a list of critical facilities and medical
baseline customers that shall receive prioritized power restoration
and, where feasible, temporary backup generation during PSPS events;

(4) Submit to the State public utility commission a PSPS after-action
report within 30 days of each event, documenting the area affected,
duration, customer impacts, and measures taken to minimize impacts.

(b) PSPS LIABILITY SHIELD.—An electrical utility that implements a PSPS
event in full compliance with the standards established under subsection
(a) and all applicable State regulatory requirements shall not be liable
for damages alleged to result from the de-energization, provided that the
PSPS was implemented in good faith based on objective fire-weather criteria
and that restoration was completed as expeditiously as safety allowed.

**§ 208. Workforce Development for Grid Resilience.**

(a) The Secretary of Energy, in coordination with the Secretary of Labor,
shall establish a Grid Resilience Workforce Training Program to address the
skilled workforce shortage for undergrounding, substation construction,
advanced metering deployment, and DCD system installation and maintenance.

(b) There is authorized to be appropriated $500,000,000 for the period of
fiscal years 2027 through 2031 for grants to community colleges, technical
schools, labor-management apprenticeship programs, and non-profit training
organizations for grid resilience workforce development.
