# CRITICAL INFRASTRUCTURE PROTECTION ACT OF 2026
## SECTION 2 — CONGRESSIONAL FINDINGS AND DECLARATIONS

---

The Congress of the United States, having conducted extensive hearings,
received expert testimony, and reviewed voluminous documentary evidence
concerning the condition of the Nation's critical infrastructure, hereby
makes the following findings and declarations:

---

### SUBPART A — BRIDGE INFRASTRUCTURE FINDINGS

**Finding 1. Corrosion Cost to the National Economy.** Whereas the NACE
International Corrosion Cost Study (IMPACT Report, 2023) determined that
the total annual direct cost of corrosion in the United States is
approximately $452 billion, representing 2.5 percent of Gross Domestic
Product; and whereas corrosion of highway bridges alone accounts for
$13.6 billion in direct annual costs, including $8.3 billion for repair
and rehabilitation of structurally deficient bridge elements, $4.4 billion
in additional maintenance expenditures necessitated by inadequate corrosion
protection systems, and $0.9 billion in capital costs for replacement of
bridges that failed prematurely as a direct result of corrosion-related
deterioration;

**Finding 2. Indirect Economic Consequences of Bridge Deterioration.**
Whereas the indirect costs of bridge corrosion, including traffic
congestion from unplanned lane closures, supply chain delay costs,
detour-related fuel consumption, environmental damage from corroded
steel runoff, and accident costs attributable to deteriorated bridge
decks and expansion joints, are estimated to exceed an additional $25
billion per year, yielding a total annual bridge corrosion cost to
the United States economy of not less than $38 billion;

**Finding 3. BATA Bridge Fleet Condition Assessment.** Whereas the Bay
Area Toll Authority (BATA) operates seven major toll bridges carrying
an average daily traffic volume of approximately 830,000 vehicles across
the San Francisco Bay; and whereas the BATA 2024 Capital Improvement
Program documents a weighted average Bridge Health Index of 85.7 across
the fleet, with individual bridge ratings as follows: Golden Gate Bridge
(BHI 91.9), San Francisco–Oakland Bay Bridge (BHI 92.3), Antioch Bridge
(BHI 88.6), Benicia–Martinez Bridge (BHI 87.4), Carquinez Bridge (BHI
84.8), Dumbarton Bridge (BHI 82.5), and Richmond–San Rafael Bridge
(BHI 73.1); and whereas the Richmond–San Rafael Bridge alone requires
an estimated $1.1 billion in rehabilitation over the next 15 years to
maintain safe operational condition;

**Finding 4. Deferred Maintenance in California Public University
Infrastructure.** Whereas the California State University (CSU) system,
comprising 23 campuses serving approximately 460,000 students, has
accumulated deferred maintenance obligations totaling approximately
$8.6 billion as documented in the CSU 2024 Facilities Condition
Assessment; and whereas the University of California (UC) system,
comprising 10 campuses and five medical centers, has accumulated deferred
maintenance obligations totaling approximately $9.1 billion as documented
in the UC Office of the President 2024 Capital Financial Plan; and whereas
these deferred maintenance figures are representative of the systemic
disinvestment in public infrastructure assets that has occurred across
all levels of government over the past four decades;

**Finding 5. Lifecycle Cost Advantages of Advanced Protective Coating
Systems.** Whereas the National Cooperative Highway Research Program
Report 398 (NCHRP 398), "Durability of Bridge Coatings," and the
subsequent AMPP/NACE SP21412-2023 standard establish through extensive
empirical testing that thermal spray zinc (TSZ) duplex coating systems,
consisting of a metallized zinc or zinc-aluminum alloy layer sealed with
an organic topcoat, can provide 75 to 100 years of corrosion protection
for structural steel with minimal maintenance intervention; and whereas
conventional liquid-applied zinc-rich epoxy coating systems typically
require complete removal and reapplication at intervals of 15 to 25
years; and whereas lifecycle cost analysis demonstrates that TSZ duplex
systems yield a net present value savings of 40 to 60 percent over a
100-year analysis period compared to conventional coating systems,
notwithstanding higher initial application costs;

### SUBPART B — ELECTRICAL GRID FINDINGS

**Finding 6. Wildfire Mitigation Costs and Grid Vulnerability.** Whereas
Pacific Gas and Electric Company (PG&E) incurred wildfire mitigation
costs and settlement obligations exceeding $73 billion through its Chapter
11 bankruptcy proceeding (filed January 29, 2019, in the United States
Bankruptcy Court for the Northern District of California, Case No. 19-30088),
its subsequent reorganization, and ongoing system hardening expenditures;
and whereas post-bankruptcy wildfire mitigation investment by California's
investor-owned utilities exceeded $25 billion in the period 2020 through
2025; and whereas these costs represent the catastrophic financial
consequences of deferred investment in electrical transmission and
distribution system protection;

**Finding 7. Transmission and Distribution Infrastructure Age Profile.**
Whereas approximately 70 percent of the Nation's electrical transmission
lines and power transformers are 25 years of age or older, and
approximately 60 percent of circuit breakers are 30 years of age or
older, according to the Department of Energy's 2024 Quadrennial Energy
Review; and whereas the average annual capital expenditure for
transmission infrastructure has been less than 55 percent of the level
that the North American Electric Reliability Corporation (NERC) has
determined to be necessary for long-term reliability;

**Finding 8. Grid Hardening Cost-Effectiveness.** Whereas the Department
of Energy's 2023 "Grid Resilience and Wildfire Mitigation Study" found
that undergrounding of electrical distribution lines in high-fire-threat
districts reduces wildfire ignition probability by 98 percent compared
to overhead conductors; and whereas the deployment of distributed control
and detection (DCD) systems with fast-acting fault interruption can reduce
the energy released during line-to-ground faults by more than 90 percent,
substantially eliminating the ignition source for catastrophic wildfires;

### SUBPART C — DATA CENTER INFRASTRUCTURE FINDINGS

**Finding 9. National Energy Consumption of Data Center Sector.** Whereas
the United States data center sector consumed approximately 90 terawatt-hours
of electricity in 2024, representing approximately 2.2 percent of total
national electricity consumption; and whereas the Federal Energy Regulatory
Commission projects that data center electricity consumption will reach
35 gigawatts of peak demand by 2030, representing approximately 8 percent
of total national electricity demand; and whereas cooling systems account
for 25 to 40 percent of total data center energy consumption depending on
climate zone and facility design;

**Finding 10. Immersion Cooling Efficiency.** Whereas single-phase immersion
cooling technology has been demonstrated to reduce data center cooling
energy consumption by up to 90 percent compared to conventional air-cooling
systems, and enables Power Usage Effectiveness (PUE) values below 1.05,
approaching the thermodynamic minimum; and whereas the Lawrence Berkeley
National Laboratory's 2023 "Advanced Cooling Technologies for Data Centers"
study documents that widespread adoption of immersion cooling could reduce
national data center electricity consumption by 25 terawatt-hours annually
by 2035;

### SUBPART D — WATER INFRASTRUCTURE FINDINGS

**Finding 11. Water System Asset Condition.** Whereas the Environmental
Protection Agency's 2023 "Drinking Water Infrastructure Needs Survey and
Assessment" identified $472.6 billion in capital improvement needs for
drinking water infrastructure over the 20-year period from 2023 to 2043;
and whereas the American Water Works Association estimates that restoring
and expanding the Nation's buried drinking water infrastructure will
require approximately $1 trillion over 25 years;

**Finding 12. Water Loss and Pipe Failure Rates.** Whereas the United
States experiences approximately 240,000 water main breaks annually
resulting in the loss of approximately 2.1 trillion gallons of treated
drinking water, valued at approximately $7.6 billion per year at average
municipal water rates; and whereas approximately 6 billion gallons of
treated water are lost daily, representing approximately 16 percent of
total treated water production nationally;

**Finding 13. Lead Service Line Health Impacts.** Whereas the EPA
estimates that 9.2 million lead service lines remain in service across
the United States, serving approximately 22 million people; and whereas
the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention has determined that there
is no safe level of lead exposure for children; and whereas the total
economic cost of lead exposure, including lost lifetime earnings,
increased healthcare expenditures, and special education costs, is
estimated to exceed $50 billion per year;

### SUBPART E — PORT AND WATERWAY FINDINGS

**Finding 14. Economic Significance of Maritime Infrastructure.** Whereas
United States ports and inland waterways support approximately 30.8 million
jobs and generate approximately $5.4 trillion in annual economic activity,
representing 26 percent of Gross Domestic Product; and whereas the American
Society of Civil Engineers awarded the Nation's port infrastructure a grade
of C+ in its 2025 Report Card, citing congestion delays exceeding 12 hours
at major container ports and backlogged maintenance dredging requirements;

**Finding 15. Harbor Maintenance Funding Deficit.** Whereas the Water
Resources Reform and Development Act of 2014 (WRRDA) authorized
incremental increases in Harbor Maintenance Trust Fund (HMTF) expenditures
toward the full-use level; and whereas actual HMTF disbursements for
harbor maintenance have averaged approximately 65 percent of the full-use
level, resulting in an annual maintenance dredging deficit exceeding
$1.8 billion and compounding channel depth reductions at major ports;

### SUBPART F — CROSS-CUTTING FINDINGS

**Finding 16. Systemic Disinvestment in Public Infrastructure.** Whereas
Federal, State, and local government investment in infrastructure as a
percentage of Gross Domestic Product has declined from approximately 3.0
percent in 1960 to approximately 1.5 percent in 2024; and whereas this
sustained disinvestment has resulted in an estimated aggregate
infrastructure investment gap of $2.6 trillion over the next decade;

**Finding 17. Return on Infrastructure Investment.** Whereas the
Congressional Budget Office has determined that Federal investment in
physical infrastructure yields an average return on investment of
approximately $1.80 for every dollar expended, through direct construction
employment, supply chain activity, and long-term productivity improvements;
and whereas investments in preventative maintenance and lifecycle
protection yield returns of $3.00 to $6.00 per dollar expended when
catastrophic failure costs are included in the analysis;

**Finding 18. National Security Implications.** Whereas the Department of
Homeland Security and the Department of Defense have identified critical
infrastructure degradation as a Tier 1 national security threat in the
2024 National Infrastructure Protection Plan; and whereas infrastructure
failures caused by deferred maintenance have been documented to impair
military mobility, disrupt defense supply chains, and compromise the
resilience of installations critical to national defense;

**Finding 19. Climate Adaptation Imperative.** Whereas the Fourth National
Climate Assessment (2018, 2023 update) projects that climate-related
stressors, including sea level rise, increased precipitation intensity,
wildfire frequency, and temperature extremes, will accelerate the
deterioration rate of existing infrastructure by 30 to 50 percent over
baseline projections; and whereas infrastructure protection investments
made under this Act are designed to account for these projected stressors
through enhanced design standards and material specifications;

**Finding 20. Constitutionally Enumerated Federal Responsibility.** Whereas
Article I, Section 8 of the Constitution of the United States vests in the
Congress the power to provide for the common defense and general welfare
of the United States and to regulate commerce among the several States;
and whereas the protection, rehabilitation, and modernization of critical
infrastructure assets essential to national security, interstate commerce,
and public health and safety constitutes a core Federal responsibility
under these enumerated powers;

---

### DECLARATIONS

Be it therefore declared by the Congress of the United States that:

(1) It is the policy of the United States to protect, rehabilitate, and
modernize the critical infrastructure of the Nation through a comprehensive,
scientifically grounded, and adequately funded Federal program of condition
assessment, life-cycle protective treatment, and ongoing monitoring;

(2) The Federal Government shall establish uniform standards for
infrastructure condition assessment, protective material specification,
and inspection frequency applicable to all covered infrastructure assets;

(3) The Federal Government shall provide financial and technical
assistance to State and local governments, public utilities, port
authorities, water districts, and private infrastructure owners to
achieve compliance with the standards established under this Act;

(4) The Federal Government shall prioritize investment in infrastructure
protection measures that achieve the greatest lifecycle cost-effectiveness,
including the deployment of advanced protective coating systems, grid
hardening and undergrounding, and preventative maintenance programs;

(5) The Federal Government shall maintain a continuing program of
research, development, and demonstration to advance the state of
infrastructure protection science and technology; and

(6) Nothing in this Act shall be construed to preempt more stringent
State or local infrastructure protection standards, provided such
standards are not inconsistent with the minimum requirements
established under this Act.
