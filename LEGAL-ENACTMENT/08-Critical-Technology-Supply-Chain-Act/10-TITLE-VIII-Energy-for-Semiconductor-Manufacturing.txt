================================================================================
               TITLE VIII — ENERGY FOR SEMICONDUCTOR MANUFACTURING
               CTSCRA OF 2026
================================================================================

SECTION 801. SHORT TITLE.

  This title may be cited as the "Semiconductor Manufacturing Energy
  Security Act of 2026."

SECTION 802. FINDINGS.

  Congress finds that:

    (1) Advanced semiconductor fabrication facilities are among the most
        energy-intensive industrial facilities in the world, with a single
        leading-edge logic fabrication facility consuming 100 to 150
        megawatts of continuous electrical power—equivalent to the
        consumption of approximately 100,000 households—and next-generation
        facilities projected to require 200 megawatts or more.

    (2) Semiconductor manufacturing requires power of exceptional quality
        and reliability; voltage sags, frequency deviations, or momentary
        interruptions lasting even milliseconds can destroy entire wafer
        batches valued at millions of dollars, requiring fabrication
        facilities to maintain multiple redundant uninterruptible power
        supply systems and on-site backup generation.

    (3) The projected expansion of domestic semiconductor manufacturing
        under the CHIPS Act and CTSCRA—including the TSMC Arizona campus
        (three fabs totaling an estimated 500 megawatts at full buildout),
        the Intel Ohio mega-fab campus (potentially eight fabs exceeding
        1 gigawatt total demand), Samsung's Taylor, Texas facility, and
        Micron's planned Boise, Idaho and Clay, New York facilities—will
        require approximately 5 to 8 gigawatts of new, firm, 24-hour-
        per-day, 365-day-per-year electrical generation and transmission
        capacity by 2035.

    (4) Existing electrical transmission infrastructure in many regions
        identified for semiconductor manufacturing expansion—including
        central Ohio, Maricopa County, Arizona, and upstate New York—is
        insufficient to accommodate projected load growth and requires
        substantial investment in new high-voltage transmission corridors,
        substations, and grid interconnections.

    (5) Nuclear power and advanced Small Modular Reactors (SMRs) offer
        unique advantages for semiconductor manufacturing, including
        continuous baseload operation independent of weather conditions,
        high power density permitting co-location with fabrication
        facilities, zero-carbon operation aligning with corporate
        sustainability commitments, and exceptionally high power quality
        characteristics suitable for sensitive manufacturing processes.

SECTION 803. FABRICATION FACILITY POWER GUARANTEE PROGRAM.

  (a) ESTABLISHMENT. — The Secretary of Energy shall establish a Fabrication
      Facility Power Guarantee Program to ensure that semiconductor
      fabrication facilities constructed with Federal incentives under
      this Act or the CHIPS and Science Act of 2022 receive reliable,
      24-hour-per-day electrical power at rates competitive with those
      available to comparable industrial facilities in Asia.

  (b) PROGRAM ELEMENTS. — The Program shall:

    (1) Enter into long-term power purchase agreements or provide
        guarantees for power purchase agreements between fabrication
        facility operators and electrical generation and transmission
        providers;

    (2) Provide financial support for dedicated transmission infrastructure
        connecting semiconductor manufacturing clusters to generation
        sources, including new high-voltage direct current (HVDC) and
        extra-high-voltage alternating current (EHVAC) transmission lines;

    (3) Establish a Semiconductor Manufacturing Energy Rate Stabilization
        Fund to offset electrical rate differentials where domestic
        industrial power rates exceed a benchmark based on rates available
        to comparable facilities in Taiwan, South Korea, and Japan;

    (4) Provide grants for on-site energy storage systems to enhance
        power quality and provide backup capability for critical
        manufacturing processes.

  (c) AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS. — There is authorized to be
      appropriated $8,000,000,000 for the period of fiscal years 2027
      through 2036 to carry out this section.

SECTION 804. NUCLEAR AND SMALL MODULAR REACTOR CO-LOCATION.

  (a) ESTABLISHMENT. — The Secretary of Energy shall establish a Nuclear
      Co-Location Incentive Program for Semiconductor Manufacturing, in
      coordination with the Nuclear Regulatory Commission, to:

    (1) Provide direct grants and loan guarantees for the deployment of
        advanced nuclear reactors, including Small Modular Reactors and
        microreactors, at sites proximate to or co-located with
        semiconductor manufacturing clusters;

    (2) Fund pre-application and licensing activities for SMR deployments
        serving semiconductor facilities;

    (3) Support feasibility studies, site characterization, and
        environmental review for nuclear co-location with semiconductor
        manufacturing;

    (4) Provide economic incentives including production tax credits
        and accelerated depreciation for nuclear facilities supplying
        dedicated power to semiconductor fabrication facilities.

  (b) REGULATORY COORDINATION. — The Nuclear Regulatory Commission shall
      establish an expedited licensing track for SMR and advanced reactor
      projects supplying power to semiconductor manufacturing facilities
      designated as critical national security infrastructure.

  (c) TARGETS. — The Secretary shall establish a goal of not fewer than
      five operational nuclear or SMR facilities co-located with or
      dedicated to semiconductor manufacturing clusters by December 31,
      2035.

  (d) AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS. — There is authorized to be
      appropriated $6,000,000,000 for the period of fiscal years 2027
      through 2036 to carry out this section.

SECTION 805. TRANSMISSION CORRIDOR BUILDOUT.

  (a) DESIGNATION OF SEMICONDUCTOR ENERGY CORRIDORS. — The Secretary of
      Energy, in coordination with the Federal Energy Regulatory
      Commission, shall designate not fewer than six Semiconductor
      Energy Corridors connecting semiconductor manufacturing clusters
      to existing and planned generation resources.

  (b) FEDERAL BACKSTOP SITING AUTHORITY. — For transmission projects within
      designated Semiconductor Energy Corridors, the Federal Energy
      Regulatory Commission shall have backstop siting authority
      consistent with section 216 of the Federal Power Act, with
      expedited procedures requiring final determination not later than
      one year after application.

  (c) TRANSMISSION INVESTMENT TAX CREDIT. — An investment tax credit of
      30 percent shall be available for qualifying transmission
      infrastructure investments located within designated Semiconductor
      Energy Corridors.

  (d) AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS. — There is authorized to be
      appropriated $1,000,000,000 for the period of fiscal years 2027
      through 2036 to carry out this section.

SECTION 806. RENEWABLE ENERGY INTEGRATION.

  (a) IN GENERAL. — The Secretary of Energy shall establish a grant
      program to support integration of renewable energy generation,
      including solar, wind, geothermal, and long-duration energy
      storage, with semiconductor manufacturing facilities to reduce
      Scope 2 emissions and comply with corporate sustainability
      commitments while maintaining power quality and reliability
      standards.

  (b) AUTHORIZATION OF APPROPRIATIONS. — There is authorized to be
      appropriated $500,000,000 for the period of fiscal years 2027
      through 2036 to carry out this section.
