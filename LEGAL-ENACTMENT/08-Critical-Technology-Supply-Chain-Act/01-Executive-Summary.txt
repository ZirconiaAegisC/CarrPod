================================================================================
                      EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
                      CRITICAL TECHNOLOGY SUPPLY CHAIN
                      RESILIENCE ACT OF 2026
================================================================================

I. PURPOSE AND SCOPE

  The Critical Technology Supply Chain Resilience Act of 2026 (CTSCRA)
  constitutes a comprehensive legislative framework authorizing $200 billion
  in Federal investment over a ten-year period to restore United States
  leadership in critical technology supply chains. This legislation builds
  upon the foundational CHIPS and Science Act of 2022 (Public Law 117-167),
  which authorized $52.7 billion for semiconductor manufacturing incentives
  and research and development. CTSCRA addresses the urgent national and
  economic security imperatives arising from persistent vulnerabilities in
  semiconductor manufacturing, advanced packaging, rare earth materials,
  artificial intelligence compute infrastructure, and related sectors.

II. STRATEGIC CONTEXT

  The United States share of global semiconductor manufacturing capacity has
  declined from approximately 37 percent in 1990 to approximately 12 percent
  in 2024. An estimated 92 percent of the world's most advanced semiconductor
  chips are manufactured in Taiwan, an island territory facing significant
  geopolitical risk from the People's Republic of China. The semiconductor
  shortages of 2020-2023 exposed the fragility of global supply chains,
  causing an estimated $240 billion in lost United States GDP in 2021 alone
  and disrupting critical industries including automotive manufacturing,
  defense systems, medical devices, and consumer electronics. The People's
  Republic of China controls approximately 60 percent of global rare earth
  element production and approximately 90 percent of rare earth permanent
  magnet manufacturing, while advanced semiconductor packaging is concentrated
  over 90 percent in Asia, primarily in Taiwan and South Korea.

III. KEY PROVISIONS AND FUNDING ALLOCATION

  The CTSCRA authorizes $200 billion across eight titles:

  TITLE I — CHIPS ACT 2.0 ($100 billion): Doubles the initial CHIPS Act
  investment to $100 billion, expanding semiconductor fabrication incentives;
  incorporating semiconductor equipment and materials manufacturing into the
  subsidy framework; establishing an advanced packaging subsidy program; and
  providing enhanced research and development tax credits.

  TITLE II — AI COMPUTE INFRASTRUCTURE ($20 billion): Creates a National AI
  Compute Reserve within the Department of Energy; authorizes acquisition of
  government DGX SuperPOD-class computing systems; establishes academic AI
  compute grants; and creates a National AI Research Cloud accessible to
  qualified researchers at American universities and national laboratories.

  TITLE III — RARE EARTH AND ADVANCED MATERIALS ($15 billion): Provides
  incentives for domestic rare earth permanent magnet production; supports
  permanent magnet supply chain development; funds domestic processing
  facilities for gallium, germanium, and graphite; and expands the National
  Defense Stockpile for critical minerals.

  TITLE IV — ADVANCED PACKAGING ($20 billion): Establishes incentives for
  domestic advanced packaging facilities targeting CoWoS, EMIB, Foveros,
  and hybrid bonding technologies; sets a target of ten or more advanced
  packaging facilities operational in the United States by 2035.

  TITLE V — SEMICONDUCTOR WORKFORCE ($10 billion): Funds university
  microelectronics education programs; reforms H-1B visa processing for
  semiconductor-sector talent; establishes veteran retraining programs;
  and expands community college semiconductor technician certification.

  TITLE VI — EXPORT CONTROLS AND TECHNOLOGY SECURITY: Expands Bureau of
  Industry and Security (BIS) entity list authorities; codifies AI chip
  export control frameworks; strengthens semiconductor manufacturing
  equipment restrictions; and expands Committee on Foreign Investment in
  the United States (CFIUS) jurisdiction over critical technology sectors.

  TITLE VII — ALLIED SEMICONDUCTOR COOPERATION: Formalizes a United States-
  European Union-Japan-Korea semiconductor alliance framework; establishes
  joint research and development programs; mandates global supply chain
  mapping; and facilitates mutual investment.

  TITLE VIII — ENERGY FOR SEMICONDUCTOR MANUFACTURING ($15 billion):
  Guarantees reliable 24/7 power for fabrication facilities at competitive
  industrial rates; provides incentives for nuclear and Small Modular Reactor
  co-location with semiconductor clusters; and funds transmission corridor
  buildout for designated semiconductor manufacturing corridors.

  Additional appropriations of $20 billion are authorized for foundational
  research and development across all program areas.

IV. ECONOMIC PROJECTIONS

  The Department of Commerce estimates that CTSCRA implementation will:

  1. Increase the United States share of global advanced semiconductor
     manufacturing from approximately 12 percent to no less than 30 percent
     by 2035;

  2. Generate approximately 500,000 direct and indirect domestic jobs in
     semiconductor manufacturing, equipment, materials, and related sectors;

  3. Contribute approximately $500 billion in cumulative GDP growth over
     the ten-year authorization period;

  4. Secure the domestic artificial intelligence industry supply chain,
     which is projected to represent a multi-trillion dollar global market
     by 2035; and

  5. Reduce strategic dependency on potentially adversarial nations for
     critical technology inputs.

V. NATIONAL SECURITY IMPERATIVE

  The CTSCRA finds that the current concentration of critical technology
  supply chains in geographically concentrated and politically vulnerable
  regions constitutes a clear and present danger to the national security
  of the United States. The uninterrupted availability of advanced
  semiconductors, rare earth materials, and associated technologies is
  essential to the economic prosperity, military readiness, and technological
  competitiveness of the United States. This legislation represents an
  operationalization of the consensus view across the Intelligence Community,
  the Department of Defense, and the Department of Commerce that supply chain
  resilience in critical technologies must be treated with the same strategic
  urgency as conventional defense procurement.
