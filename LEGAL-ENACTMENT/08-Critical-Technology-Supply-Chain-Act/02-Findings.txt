SECTION 001. CONGRESSIONAL FINDINGS.

  The Congress finds the following:

    (1) DECLINE OF UNITED STATES SEMICONDUCTOR MANUFACTURING. — The United
        States share of global semiconductor manufacturing capacity has
        declined from approximately 37 percent in 1990 to approximately
        12 percent in 2024, a structural shift of profound economic and
        national security consequence.

    (2) OFFSHORE CONCENTRATION OF ADVANCED CHIP PRODUCTION. — Approximately
        92 percent of the world's most advanced semiconductor chips,
        fabricated at process nodes below 7 nanometers, are manufactured
        on the island of Taiwan, a territory the People's Republic of
        China has consistently claimed as part of its sovereign territory
        and over which it has not renounced the use of force.

    (3) TAIWAN STRAIT GEOPOLITICAL RISK. — The Department of Defense Annual
        Report on Military and Security Developments Involving the People's
        Republic of China has consistently identified the Taiwan Strait as
        a potential flashpoint for military conflict. A disruption of
        semiconductor manufacturing in Taiwan, whether by military action,
        blockade, natural disaster, or other contingency, would sever
        access to advanced chips powering the entirety of the United States
        defense industrial base, financial infrastructure, artificial
        intelligence systems, and commercial technology sectors.

    (4) SEMICONDUCTOR SHORTAGE CASCADE EFFECTS. — The global semiconductor
        shortage of 2020-2023, triggered by COVID-19 pandemic supply-demand
        imbalances, caused an estimated $240 billion in lost United States
        gross domestic product in calendar year 2021 and forced production
        shutdowns across the automotive, consumer electronics, medical
        device, and defense manufacturing sectors.

    (5) ARTIFICIAL INTELLIGENCE NATIONAL SECURITY IMPERATIVE. — Advanced
        AI accelerator chips, including Graphics Processing Units (GPUs)
        and purpose-built AI processors, constitute the foundational
        infrastructure for modern artificial intelligence systems. The
        Department of Defense, Intelligence Community, and United States
        technology industry rely on a single supplier—Nvidia Corporation—
        for in excess of 80 percent of AI training accelerator hardware,
        which hardware is manufactured by a single foundry, Taiwan
        Semiconductor Manufacturing Company (TSMC), creating a concentrated
        supply chain vulnerability of extraordinary national security
        significance.

    (6) CHINESE DOMINANCE IN RARE EARTH ELEMENTS. — The People's Republic
        of China controls approximately 60 percent of the global mining
        production of rare earth elements and approximately 90 percent of
        the global manufacturing of rare earth permanent magnets, which
        are essential components in defense systems, electric vehicles,
        wind turbines, consumer electronics, and precision-guided munitions.

    (7) CHINESE EXPORT CONTROL LEVERAGE. — The People's Republic of China
        has demonstrated willingness to weaponize its rare earth supply
        chain dominance through formal export controls, including the
        restrictions imposed on gallium and germanium exports in 2023 and
        the subsequent expansion of controls on graphite and antimony
        exports, creating immediate supply disruptions for United States
         semiconductor, defense, and clean energy manufacturing.

    (8) ADVANCED PACKAGING CONCENTRATION. — Over 90 percent of global
        advanced semiconductor packaging capacity—including Chip-on-Wafer-
        on-Substrate (CoWoS) technology essential for artificial intelligence
        accelerator chips—is concentrated in Asia, primarily in Taiwan and
        South Korea, creating a critical chokepoint between semiconductor
        fabrication and finished product delivery.

    (9) SEMICONDUCTOR EQUIPMENT DEPENDENCY. — The global semiconductor
        manufacturing equipment market is dominated by five corporations—
        Applied Materials, ASML, Lam Research, Tokyo Electron, and KLA
        Corporation—which together account for approximately 70 percent of
        global equipment revenue. ASML, a Netherlands-based corporation,
        holds a monopoly on extreme ultraviolet (EUV) lithography equipment
        essential for manufacturing advanced logic chips below 7 nanometers.

    (10) CHINESE SEMICONDUCTOR INDUSTRIAL POLICY. — The People's Republic
         of China has invested in excess of $150 billion through its National
         Integrated Circuit Industry Investment Fund and associated state-
         directed initiatives to build an indigenous semiconductor
         manufacturing capability, with the explicit goal of semiconductor
         self-sufficiency and technological decoupling from United States
         and allied suppliers.

    (11) ECONOMIC COERCION AND TECHNOLOGY DENIAL. — The People's Republic
         of China has employed a range of economic coercion measures,
         including restrictions on exports of critical minerals and
         materials, procurement preferences favoring domestic semiconductor
         products, intellectual property transfer requirements as conditions
         of market access, and state-sponsored industrial espionage targeting
         semiconductor technology, with the Department of Justice reporting
         that over 80 percent of all economic espionage cases filed involve
         Chinese actors seeking United States trade secrets.

    (12) DEFENSE INDUSTRIAL BASE VULNERABILITY. — The Department of Defense
         has identified over 300 semiconductor-dependent defense systems,
         including the F-35 Joint Strike Fighter, Aegis Combat System, Patriot
         missile defense system, and nuclear command, control, and
         communications infrastructure, that rely on advanced semiconductors
         manufactured predominantly in Taiwan and subject to supply
         disruption from a Taiwan Strait contingency.

    (13) COMMERCIAL INNOVATION AT RISK. — The United States semiconductor
         design industry, including Nvidia, AMD, Qualcomm, Broadcom, Apple,
         Google, Microsoft, and Amazon, generated combined revenue exceeding
         $500 billion in 2025 and invested over $80 billion in research and
         development, yet these United States-headquartered companies depend
         almost entirely on foreign foundries for manufacturing, creating a
         fundamental disconnect between design leadership and manufacturing
         capability that threatens long-term innovation leadership.

    (14) PRIVATE SECTOR INVESTMENT RESPONSE. — The CHIPS and Science Act of
         2022 has catalyzed over $450 billion in private sector semiconductor
         manufacturing investment announcements in the United States, and
         continuing Federal partnership is essential to ensure these projects
         reach commercial operation, expand in scope, and attract a
         supporting ecosystem of equipment, materials, and packaging
         suppliers.

    (15) WORKFORCE IMPERATIVE. — The Semiconductor Industry Association
         projects a need for 115,000 additional semiconductor workers by
         2030, yet current science and engineering graduation rates will meet
         only approximately 40 percent of projected demand, and more than
         55 percent of graduate students in United States electrical
         engineering programs are international students, making visa policy
         a direct determinant of semiconductor workforce availability.
