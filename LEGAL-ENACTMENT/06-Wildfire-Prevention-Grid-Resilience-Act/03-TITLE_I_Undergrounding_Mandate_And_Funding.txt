             TITLE I — UNDERGROUNDING OF HIGH-FIRE-THREAT
                           ELECTRIC LINES

================================================================================

SEC. 101. NATIONAL UNDERGROUNDING PROGRAM.

    (a) ESTABLISHMENT. — There is established within the Department of
    Energy a National Undergrounding Program ("the Program") to provide
    federal cost-sharing for the undergrounding of electric distribution
    and sub-transmission lines located within high-fire-threat districts
    as designated by the relevant state regulatory authority, or, in the
    absence of such designation, by the U.S. Forest Service Wildfire
    Hazard Potential Index.

    (b) PROGRAM TARGET. — The Program shall target the undergrounding of
    not fewer than 10,000 miles of overhead electric lines over the ten-
    year authorization period of this Act (FY2027–FY2036), at an average
    federal cost-share rate of not less than 60 percent and a program
    average cost of approximately $3 million per mile, adjusted annually
    for inflation based on the Producer Price Index for construction
    materials.

    (c) PROGRAM ADMINISTRATION. — The Secretary of Energy shall:
        (1) Promulgate regulations implementing the Program within 180
            days of enactment, including eligibility criteria, application
            procedures, cost-share formulas, and compliance requirements;
        (2) Establish a competitive grant process with semi-annual
            application windows;
        (3) Prioritize applications based on:
            (A) Fire risk severity of the proposed undergrounding
                corridor, as measured by the Wildfire Hazard Potential
                Index and state fire threat maps;
            (B) Population density and critical facility concentration
                within the service area;
            (C) Historical ignition frequency on the lines proposed
                for undergrounding;
            (D) The applicant utility's demonstrated commitment to
                complementary wildfire mitigation measures;
            (E) Geographic diversity to ensure equitable distribution
                of Program funds across eligible regions;
        (4) Provide technical assistance to applicant utilities, with
            particular emphasis on small and rural electric cooperatives
            and municipal utilities that may lack in-house undergrounding
            engineering capabilities.

    (d) PG&E UNDERGROUNDING MODEL. — The Program shall recognize and
    incorporate lessons from PG&E's undergrounding program, which has
    successfully completed 1,241 miles of undergrounding in high-fire-
    threat districts since 2021. The Secretary shall:
        (1) Document and disseminate PG&E's undergrounding engineering
            standards, construction methodologies, and cost-management
            practices as a reference model for Program participants;
        (2) Require participating utilities to adopt, at minimum, the
            following undergrounding design standards derived from PG&E's
            operational experience:
            (A) Direct-burial or conduit-encased cable rated for not
                less than 50-year service life in high-fire environments;
            (B) Pad-mounted or submersible transformers with fire-
                resistant enclosures;
            (C) Fault indicators and remote monitoring capability on
                all undergrounded circuit segments;
            (D) Coordination with telecommunications providers for
                joint-trench deployment where feasible.

SEC. 102. FEDERAL COST-SHARE FORMULA.

    (a) BASE RATE. — The federal cost-share for undergrounding projects
    shall be at a base rate of 60 percent of eligible project costs, with
    the remaining 40 percent provided by the participating utility and its
    state cost-share partner.

    (b) ENHANCED RATE FOR HIGH-RISK CORRIDORS. — Projects located within
    Tier 3 High Fire Threat Districts (as designated by the CPUC Fire-
    Threat Map or equivalent state designation) or within 0.5 miles of a
    wildland-urban interface area with a population density exceeding 500
    persons per square mile shall be eligible for an enhanced federal
    cost-share rate of 75 percent.

    (c) ENHANCED RATE FOR SMALL AND RURAL UTILITIES. — Electric
    cooperatives, municipal utilities, and public utility districts
    serving fewer than 100,000 customer accounts shall be eligible for an
    enhanced federal cost-share rate of 80 percent.

    (d) STATE PARTICIPATION. — States shall be encouraged to contribute
    a minimum of 10 percent of project costs through state-appropriated
    funds, state infrastructure bank loans, or approved ratepayer
    securitization mechanisms. States that fail to provide matching funds
    shall not be penalized, but the utility applicant shall be responsible
    for the non-federal share.

    (e) ELIGIBLE COSTS. — Eligible project costs shall include:
        (1) Engineering and design;
        (2) Trenching, conduit installation, cable pulling, and
            restoration;
        (3) Transformer and switchgear procurement and installation;
        (4) Environmental review and permitting compliance;
        (5) Traffic control and community impact mitigation;
        (6) Telecommunications coordination and joint-trench costs;
        (7) Project management and administration, not to exceed 10%
            of total project costs.

SEC. 103. EXPEDITED ENVIRONMENTAL REVIEW.

    (a) NEPA CATEGORICAL EXCLUSION. — Undergrounding projects funded
    under this Title shall be eligible for a categorical exclusion under
    the National Environmental Policy Act (42 U.S.C. 4321 et seq.) where
    the undergrounding is conducted within existing road rights-of-way,
    utility easements, or previously disturbed corridors, provided that
    the project does not:
        (1) Cross or encroach upon designated critical habitat for
            species listed under the Endangered Species Act;
        (2) Disturb archaeological or cultural resources eligible for
            listing on the National Register of Historic Places;
        (3) Require new permanent access roads in previously
            undisturbed areas.

    (b) CEQA COORDINATION. — For projects in California, the Secretary
    shall negotiate a Memorandum of Understanding with the California
    Natural Resources Agency to establish coordinated NEPA-CEQA review
    procedures that satisfy both federal and state environmental review
    requirements within a combined timeline not exceeding 180 days from
    the date of a complete application.

    (c) PERMIT COORDINATION. — The Secretary shall designate a single
    federal point of contact for each Program project to coordinate all
    required federal permits, including Clean Water Act Section 404
    permits, Endangered Species Act Section 7 consultations, and National
    Historic Preservation Act Section 106 reviews.

SEC. 104. ELIGIBLE UTILITIES AND PRIORITIZATION.

    (a) ELIGIBLE ENTITIES. — The following entities shall be eligible to
    apply for Program funding:
        (1) Investor-owned electric utilities;
        (2) Electric cooperatives organized under the Rural
            Electrification Act;
        (3) Municipal electric utilities;
        (4) Public utility districts;
        (5) Tribal utility authorities.

    (b) PRIORITIZATION CRITERIA. — In allocating Program funds, the
    Secretary shall apply the following prioritization weighting:
        (1) Wildfire risk severity index — 35 percent;
        (2) Population and critical facility exposure — 25 percent;
        (3) Historical ignition frequency on proposed corridors — 20
            percent;
        (4) Geographic and utility-type diversity — 10 percent;
        (5) Demonstrated co-investment from state and local sources —
            10 percent.

SEC. 105. REPORTING AND ACCOUNTABILITY.

    (a) ANNUAL REPORTING. — Each Program participant shall submit to the
    Secretary an annual report, not later than January 31 of each year,
    detailing:
        (1) Miles of line undergrounded during the preceding calendar
            year;
        (2) Total project costs, itemized by cost category;
        (3) Any schedule deviations exceeding 90 days;
        (4) Performance metrics including post-undergrounding ignition
            frequency on the relevant circuit segment.

    (b) INDEPENDENT AUDIT. — The Government Accountability Office (GAO)
    shall conduct an independent audit of the Program every three years
    and report its findings to the Committee on Energy and Commerce of
    the House of Representatives and the Committee on Energy and Natural
    Resources of the Senate.

    (c) CLAWBACK PROVISION. — The Secretary may recapture up to 100
    percent of Program funds disbursed to any participant that fails to
    achieve substantial completion of its funded project within 36 months
    of the grant award date, absent a showing of good cause for delay.
