    (b) INDIRECT AND INDUCED EMPLOYMENT. — Using a multiplier of 1.8,
    total employment impact including indirect (supply chain) and
    induced (worker spending) effects is estimated at approximately
    900,000 job-years.

    (c) WORKFORCE TRAINING. — The Act authorizes $3 billion (within
    TITLE VI) specifically for workforce development and training
    programs, including community college partnerships, registered
    apprenticeship programs, and veteran transition programs in
    utility vegetation management, grid construction, and energy
    storage installation.

    (d) DOMESTIC MANUFACTURING. — Buy America provisions in Titles
    II, III, V, and VII are projected to support an estimated 50,000
    domestic manufacturing jobs in the production of composite
    utility poles, covered conductor, battery storage systems, DCD
    devices, weather stations, and related grid-hardening equipment.

SECTION 2. WILDFIRE COST AVOIDANCE.

    (a) BASELINE WILDFIRE COSTS. — The United States currently
    experiences annual wildfire-related economic costs estimated
    at $20 billion to $50 billion, comprising:
        (1) Direct federal, state, and local fire suppression
            expenditures exceeding $5 billion annually;
        (2) Insured property losses averaging $10 billion to $15
            billion per year (2017–2025 average);
        (3) Uninsured losses, business interruption, health
            impacts from smoke exposure, watershed degradation,
            and ecosystem service losses estimated at $5 billion
            to $30 billion annually.

    (b) COST AVOIDANCE PROJECTION. — The comprehensive wildfire
    prevention measures authorized by this Act are projected to
    reduce utility-caused wildfire ignitions by an estimated 80
    to 95 percent in treated areas, based on PG&E's demonstrated
    results:

        (1) PG&E achieved a reduction from an average of
            approximately 1,500 ignitions per year (2015–2017
            baseline) to zero major utility-caused wildfires for
            three consecutive years (2023, 2024, 2025) through
            systematic implementation of undergrounding, DCD,
            enhanced vegetation management, weather monitoring,
            and PSPS protocols.

        (2) Extrapolating PG&E's outcomes to all high-fire-threat
            utility territories nationally yields an estimated
            reduction of 200 to 400 major utility-caused wildfires
            over the twenty-year evaluation horizon.

        (3) Using a conservative avoided-cost estimate of $1.5
            billion per avoided major wildfire (based on the
            average cost of the 2017–2021 California wildfire
            events attributed to utility infrastructure), total
            avoided costs over 20 years are projected at $300
            billion.

    (c) NET BENEFIT. — Deducting the $150 billion federal
    authorization from the projected $300 billion in avoided
    wildfire costs yields a net benefit to the United States
    economy of approximately $150 billion over 20 years, a
    benefit-cost ratio of approximately 2:1 using conservative
    assumptions and excluding co-benefits.

    (d) CO-BENEFITS NOT QUANTIFIED. — Additional economic
    benefits not quantified in the above analysis include:
        (1) Reduced greenhouse gas emissions from avoided
            wildfire combustion, with avoided carbon emissions
            conservatively valued at $20 to $40 per metric ton
            CO2 equivalent under federal social cost of carbon
            guidance;
        (2) Avoided public health costs from reduced wildfire
            smoke exposure, which affects an estimated 30
            million Americans in high-fire-risk regions;
        (3) Improved electric reliability for residential,
            commercial, and industrial customers, including
            avoided outage costs;
        (4) Preservation of watershed function and reduced
            water treatment costs;
        (5) Avoided liability and bankruptcy costs for
            investor-owned utilities and their ratepayers.

SECTION 3. DATA CENTER ECONOMIC PROTECTION.

    (a) The United States is experiencing a 4.6 GW pipeline of
    new data center load seeking interconnection, concentrated
    in regions overlapping with High Fire Threat Districts. This
    load represents approximately $500 billion in capital
    investment at risk from wildfire-related grid disruptions.

    (b) Title VIII of this Act protects this investment by:
        (1) Establishing minimum grid resilience standards for
            data center interconnections;
        (2) Mandating behind-meter storage to ensure continuity
            of critical digital infrastructure operations;
        (3) Reducing the probability of extended data center
            outages caused by wildfire-related transmission
            disruptions.

    (c) The economic value of avoided data center downtime,
    conservatively estimated at $5,000 to $9,000 per minute for
    a large hyperscale facility, reinforces the benefit-cost
    justification for the Title VIII provisions.

SECTION 4. RATEPAYER IMPACT.

    (a) Without the federal cost-sharing provided by this Act,
    the full cost of wildfire prevention infrastructure would
    be borne by utility ratepayers, with estimated residential
    rate increases of 8 to 15 percent in high-fire-risk utility
    territories (based on PG&E's $73 billion five-year capital
    plan and California Public Utilities Commission rate
    proceedings).

    (b) The 60 to 80 percent federal cost-share provided by this
    Act reduces the ratepayer burden proportionally, preserving
    electricity affordability while achieving fire-prevention
    outcomes at the scale and pace required.

    (c) The $300 billion in avoided wildfire costs over 20 years
    translates to avoided insurance premium increases, avoided
    property value depreciation, and avoided business disruption
    costs that would otherwise fall disproportionately on
    residents and businesses in wildfire-prone communities.

SECTION 6. CONCLUSION.

    The Wildfire Prevention and Grid Resilience Act of 2026
    represents an investment of $150 billion projected to return
    $300 billion in avoided wildfire costs over twenty years,
    create 500,000 jobs, protect $500 billion in data center
    capital investment, and preserve electricity affordability
    for millions of American households and businesses in
    wildfire-prone regions.
