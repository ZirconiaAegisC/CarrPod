                       SECTION 2. FINDINGS AND PURPOSES

================================================================================

(a) FINDINGS. — The Congress finds the following:

    (1) WILDFIRE CRISIS SCOPE. — Wildfires in the United States cause
    between $20 billion and $50 billion in annual economic damages,
    encompassing:
        (A) Direct fire suppression costs exceeding $4 billion annually
            for federal agencies alone;
        (B) Insured property losses averaging $10 billion to $15 billion
            per year since 2017;
        (C) Uninsured and indirect losses, including business
            interruption, health impacts from smoke exposure affecting
            an estimated 30 million Americans annually, watershed
            contamination, and ecosystem service degradation, estimated
            at $10 billion to $20 billion annually;
        (D) Carbon emissions from wildfire combustion exceeding 100
            million metric tons of CO2 equivalent in extreme fire years.

    (2) UTILITY INFRASTRUCTURE AS IGNITION SOURCE. — Electric utility
    infrastructure has been identified as the ignition source for
    multiple catastrophic wildfires. In California, seven of the twenty
    most destructive wildfires since 1991 involved utility equipment.
    The 2018 Camp Fire, ignited by Pacific Gas and Electric Company
    (PG&E) transmission equipment, resulted in 85 fatalities, the
    destruction of 18,804 structures, and total damages estimated at
    $16.5 billion. PG&E's resulting bankruptcy—the largest utility
    bankruptcy in United States history—demonstrates the existential
    financial risk that wildfire liability poses to investor-owned
    utilities and, by extension, to ratepayers and energy markets.

    (3) PG&E TRANSFORMATION AS NATIONAL MODEL. — Since emerging from
    bankruptcy in 2020, PG&E has executed the most comprehensive
    wildfire prevention program of any electric utility in North
    America. Operating with $24.9 billion in operating revenue (FY2025)
    and executing a $73 billion five-year capital investment plan,
    PG&E has demonstrated the following measurable outcomes:
        (A) 1,241 miles of distribution and transmission lines
            undergrounded in high-fire-threat districts since 2021;
        (B) Three consecutive calendar years (2023, 2024, 2025) with
            zero major utility-caused wildfires, a result unprecedented
            in the company's history;
        (C) Deployment of 2,000 Downed Conductor Detection (DCD) devices
            on high-fire-risk circuits, which have prevented 45
            confirmed ignitions through automatic circuit de-
            energization upon detection of a downed or faulted
            conductor;
        (D) Installation and operation of 1,627 weather stations and
            706 high-definition cameras equipped with automated
            detection capabilities, providing real-time situational
            awareness across the service territory;
        (E) Public Safety Power Shutoff (PSPS) program achieving 95%
            risk reduction compared to pre-2020 operational protocols;
        (F) Enhanced Powerline Safety Settings (EPSS) achieving 69%
            reduction in ignition frequency on enabled circuits.

    (4) CALIFORNIA AND PACIFIC NORTHWEST RISK CONCENTRATION. — The
    Western United States, particularly California, Oregon, and
    Washington, contains the nation's highest concentration of
    wildfire-vulnerable electric infrastructure. California's High Fire
    Threat District (HFTD), as designated by the California Public
    Utilities Commission (CPUC), encompasses approximately 44% of the
    state's land area and includes 25.5 million acres of wildland-
    urban interface. Oregon and Washington have experienced a five-fold
    increase in large fire frequency since 1990, with electric utility
    infrastructure implicated in multiple catastrophic events, including
    the 2020 Labor Day fires that burned over 1 million acres across
    both states.

    (5) GRID INFRASTRUCTURE AGING AND CLIMATE EXPOSURE. — The average
    age of distribution infrastructure in high-fire-risk regions exceeds
    40 years, with significant portions dating to the post-World War II
    era. Climate change has extended the fire season by approximately
    78 days since 1970, with mean temperatures in the Western United
    States increasing by 2.0 degrees Fahrenheit over the same period.
    The compounding effect of aging infrastructure, increased
    temperatures, prolonged drought conditions, and expanding wildland-
    urban interface development has created a systemic risk that
    individual utility ratepayers cannot reasonably be expected to
    bear through electricity rates alone.

    (6) DATA CENTER LOAD GROWTH AND GRID STRAIN. — The United States is
    experiencing unprecedented growth in data center electricity demand,
    with a projected 4.6 GW pipeline of new data center load seeking
    interconnection in high-fire-risk regions of the Western United
    States. This represents approximately $500 billion in capital
    investment at risk. Ensuring wildfire-resilient electric service to
    critical digital infrastructure is an imperative of national
    economic security and technological competitiveness.

    (7) INADEQUACY OF STATE-LEVEL RESPONSES. — While California, through
    the CPUC's wildfire mitigation proceedings (including the Wildfire
    Mitigation Plan process established by SB 901 of 2018), has created
    the most advanced state-level wildfire regulatory framework in the
    nation, the scope and cost of comprehensive wildfire prevention
    infrastructure exceeds the financial capacity of individual states
    and their ratepayers. A federal partnership is essential to achieve
    wildfire prevention at the scale and pace required by the escalating
    risk environment.

    (8) TECHNOLOGICAL READINESS. — Technologies essential to
    comprehensive wildfire prevention—including underground cable
    systems rated for high-fire environments, DCD devices capable of
    sub-cycle fault detection and automatic circuit interruption,
    weather stations with integrated AI detection capability, HD camera
    networks with automated smoke and flame recognition, composite
    utility poles with fire resistance ratings exceeding 60 minutes,
    covered conductor systems with arc-suppression capability, grid-
    forming battery inverters, and utility-scale lithium-iron-phosphate
    storage systems—have reached commercial maturity and are currently
    deployed at scale within PG&E and other leading utilities. The
    technological foundation for the programs authorized by this Act
    is established and proven.

(b) PURPOSES. — The purposes of this Act are:

    (1) To establish a coordinated federal-state-utility partnership
        for comprehensive wildfire prevention and grid resilience.

    (2) To provide federal cost-sharing for the undergrounding of
        10,000 miles of high-fire-threat electric lines.

    (3) To mandate and fund the deployment of DCD technology on all
        high-fire-threat distribution and sub-transmission circuits.

    (4) To create a national network of weather stations and HD
        cameras providing real-time wildfire situational awareness.

    (5) To establish national standards for Public Safety Power
        Shutoffs with protections for medically vulnerable populations.

    (6) To fund community microgrids, utility-scale storage, and
        solar-plus-storage systems for critical facility resilience.

    (7) To enhance vegetation management through advanced technology
        and expanded workforce training.

    (8) To harden transmission infrastructure with fire-resistant
        materials and structural upgrades.

    (9) To ensure grid reliability for data centers and critical
        digital infrastructure in wildfire-prone regions.

    (10) To create approximately 500,000 jobs and avoid an estimated
         $300 billion in wildfire-related costs over 20 years.
