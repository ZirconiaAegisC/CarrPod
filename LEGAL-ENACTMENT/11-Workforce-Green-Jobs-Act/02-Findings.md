# 119TH CONGRESS — WORKFORCE DEVELOPMENT AND GREEN JOBS ACT OF 2026
# DOCUMENT 02: CONGRESSIONAL FINDINGS

---

## CONGRESSIONAL FINDINGS

### PURSUANT TO THE WORKFORCE DEVELOPMENT AND GREEN JOBS ACT OF 2026

---

**The Congress finds the following:**

### PART A — WORKFORCE SHORTAGE IN CLEAN ENERGY

**Finding 1.** The Department of Energy, in its 2024 United States Energy and Employment Report (USEER), documented that employers across every clean energy technology category reported difficulty hiring qualified workers, with the most acute shortages reported by firms in solar installation (89 percent reported difficulty), wind turbine service and maintenance (83 percent), battery and energy storage manufacturing (78 percent), and electric grid modernization (76 percent).

**Finding 2.** The National Association of Manufacturers (NAM), in its 2025 Manufacturing Workforce Survey, reported that 77 percent of manufacturers identified attracting and retaining a quality workforce as their primary business challenge, and that the manufacturing skills gap could result in 2.1 million unfilled jobs by 2030.

**Finding 3.** The Semiconductor Industry Association (SIA), in its 2024 workforce assessment, projected that the CHIPS and Science Act of 2022 will create approximately 115,000 new semiconductor industry jobs by 2030, of which an estimated 67,000 positions — including 39,000 technician roles and 28,000 engineer roles — risk going unfilled without significant expansion of workforce training pipelines.

### PART B — APPRENTICESHIP EFFECTIVENESS AND UNMET POTENTIAL

**Finding 4.** The Department of Labor, through its Registered Apprenticeship Partners Information Database System (RAPIDS), documented that individuals who complete registered apprenticeship programs earn an average starting wage of $77,000 per year and receive an estimated $240,000 in additional lifetime earnings compared to non-apprentice peers, representing a 38 percent earnings premium.

**Finding 5.** Registered apprenticeship participation in the United States stood at approximately 600,000 active apprentices in 2024, representing 0.3 percent of the total labor force — substantially below peer nations including Germany (2.8 percent), Switzerland (3.5 percent), and Australia (3.8 percent) — indicating significant unmet potential for apprenticeship-based workforce development.

**Finding 6.** The Inflation Reduction Act of 2022 (Public Law 117-169) established prevailing wage and apprenticeship requirements for enhanced clean energy tax credits, creating structural demand for registered apprenticeship programs that the current training system lacks capacity to meet, according to the Treasury Department and Internal Revenue Service guidance implementing sections 45, 48, 45Q, and 45V.

### PART C — VETERAN AND MILITARY TRANSITION OPPORTUNITY

**Finding 7.** The Department of Defense Separation and Transition Report (2024) documented that approximately 200,000 service members separate from active duty annually, of whom 65 percent express interest in technical or skilled trades careers. More than 40 percent of separating service members already possess skills directly transferable to clean energy occupations, including electrical systems, mechanical maintenance, heavy equipment operation, construction project management, and safety compliance.

**Finding 8.** The Department of Veterans Affairs reported that the veteran unemployment rate for post-9/11 veterans was 8.5 percent in 2023, more than 2 percentage points above the national average, despite veterans possessing above-average technical training, discipline, and leadership skills highly valued by clean energy employers.

**Finding 9.** The DOD SkillBridge program, which provides active-duty service members with civilian work experience during their final 180 days of service, has partnered with fewer than 50 clean energy employers as of 2025, representing less than 2 percent of available SkillBridge opportunities, indicating a substantial gap in clean energy industry participation in military transition programming.

### PART D — FOSSIL FUEL COMMUNITY TRANSITION IMPERATIVE

**Finding 10.** The Department of Energy, in its 2024 report on energy transition communities, identified that approximately 1.7 million American workers are employed in coal mining, coal-fired power generation, oil and gas extraction, and petroleum refining. The Bureau of Labor Statistics projects that coal mining employment will decline by 25 percent and coal power plant employment by 35 percent between 2025 and 2035, independent of any additional policy measures.

**Finding 11.** The Interagency Working Group on Coal and Power Plant Communities and Economic Revitalization identified 25 geographic priority regions encompassing 232 counties and 37 Tribal areas that are most affected by the decline of fossil fuel industries and that have been historically underserved by federal economic development programs.

**Finding 12.** Analysis by the Brookings Institution and Massachusetts Institute of Technology has documented that fossil fuel workers in extraction industries possess skills, including heavy equipment operation, welding and metal fabrication, electrical systems maintenance, process control and monitoring, and safety and permitting compliance, that map to clean energy occupations with an average of only 6 to 18 months of additional training, compared to 2 to 4 years for workers entering from unrelated fields.

### PART E — EDUCATIONAL PIPELINE INSUFFICIENCY

**Finding 13.** The National Center for Education Statistics reported in 2024 that only 2 percent of United States high school students participated in Career Technical Education (CTE) programs related to energy, environment, or advanced manufacturing, and that 68 percent of high schools offer no courses specifically addressing clean energy technologies, climate science, or sustainable engineering.

**Finding 14.** The Association for Career and Technical Education (ACTE) documented that 92 percent of school districts report difficulty hiring qualified CTE instructors in technical fields, due to the wage differential between industry employment and teaching, and that the average age of CTE instructors in energy-related fields exceeds 55 years.

**Finding 15.** The American Association of Community Colleges reported in 2025 that only 18 percent of the nation's 936 public community colleges offer credential programs in clean energy technologies, and that the average age of laboratory and workshop equipment in manufacturing and energy programs exceeds 20 years, representing a significant infrastructure deficit relative to industry training needs for modern clean energy technologies.

### PART F — NUCLEAR AND FUSION WORKFORCE GAP

**Finding 16.** The Nuclear Energy Institute (NEI) documented in 2025 that the domestic nuclear energy workforce stands at approximately 100,000 workers, but that projected retirements, the expansion of the existing fleet through license extensions, and new advanced reactor deployments will require an additional 75,000 nuclear workers by 2035.

**Finding 17.** The National Academies of Sciences, Engineering, and Medicine, in its 2024 report on the fusion energy workforce, identified that fewer than 500 doctoral-level fusion scientists and engineers graduate annually in the United States, and that achieving commercial fusion energy by 2035 will require a fusion workforce of not fewer than 10,000 trained professionals across science, engineering, technology, operations, and regulatory disciplines.

### PART G — NATIONAL SERVICE AND CORPS MODEL

**Finding 18.** The Civilian Conservation Corps (CCC) of 1933 through 1942 employed more than 3,000,000 young men, planted 3 billion trees, built 800 parks and 47,000 bridges, and installed 8,000 miles of telephone and power lines — demonstrating that large-scale national service programs can simultaneously achieve infrastructure, conservation, and workforce development objectives.

**Finding 19.** AmeriCorps and the Corporation for National and Community Service documented that for every federal dollar invested in national service programs, an estimated $3.90 in economic benefits are generated through increased earnings, reduced social service costs, and community infrastructure improvements.

### PART H — TRIBAL ENERGY SOVEREIGNTY AND WORKFORCE NEEDS

**Finding 20.** The Department of Energy Office of Indian Energy Policy and Programs reported in 2024 that 16,800 Tribal homes lacked access to electricity and that Tribal communities pay an average of 68 percent more for electricity than the national average. The DOE further found that the 574 federally recognized Tribes control lands with an estimated 6.5 percent of United States utility-scale renewable energy technical potential, yet Tribal members face an unemployment rate approximately three times the national average.

**Finding 21.** The Bureau of Indian Education and the American Indian Higher Education Consortium reported that the nation's 35 Tribal Colleges and Universities (TCUs) enroll approximately 130,000 students, but only 4 TCUs offer certificate or degree programs in clean energy or energy-related trades, and average TCU laboratory and workshop equipment is more than 25 years old.

---

*These findings are incorporated by reference into Section 2 of the Workforce Development and Green Jobs Act of 2026 and shall inform the interpretation and implementation of all provisions of this Act.*
