================================================================================
                       ECONOMIC IMPACT ASSESSMENT
                        COASTAL AND PORT INFRASTRUCTURE
                        PROTECTION ACT OF 2026
================================================================================

SECTION 1. MACROECONOMIC PROJECTIONS.

  (a) GROSS DOMESTIC PRODUCT. — The Council of Economic Advisers, in
      consultation with the Department of the Treasury and the Office of
      Management and Budget, projects that the $100 billion authorized by
      the Coastal and Port Infrastructure Protection Act of 2026 will
      generate cumulative gross domestic product contributions of
      approximately $150 billion over the ten-year authorization period,
      representing a fiscal multiplier of approximately 1.5.

  (b) EMPLOYMENT IMPACTS. — Direct, indirect, and induced employment
      effects are projected to generate:

    (1) TITLE I (Sea Wall and Coastal Armoring): .............. 75,000 jobs
        Heavy civil construction, concrete and steel manufacturing,
        engineering and design, environmental monitoring;

    (2) TITLE II (Port Electrification): ...................... 60,000 jobs
        Electrical infrastructure construction, zero-emission equipment
        manufacturing, microgrid and battery installation;

    (3) TITLE III (Harbor Deepening): ......................... 40,000 jobs
        Dredging operations, beneficial use placement, navigation
        system upgrades, Corps of Engineers fleet operations;

    (4) TITLE IV (Coastal Ecosystem Restoration): ............. 30,000 jobs
        Wetland construction and restoration, marine biology and
        ecology, conservation land management;

    (5) TITLES V-VIII (Resilience, Offshore Wind, Maritime
        Decarbonization, Flood Insurance): .................... 45,000 jobs
        Port operations and logistics, alternative fuel production,
        vessel construction, flood mapping and insurance;

    (6) TOTAL PROJECTED EMPLOYMENT: .......................... 250,000 jobs

  (c) WAGE ANALYSIS. — The weighted average wage for jobs created under
      this Act is projected at $35.80 per hour, approximately 25 percent
      higher than the national median wage, reflecting the specialized
      and skilled nature of coastal construction, maritime operations,
      and port infrastructure work.

SECTION 2. SECTORAL AND REGIONAL ECONOMIC ANALYSIS.

  (a) COASTAL CONSTRUCTION AND MANUFACTURING. — The concrete, steel,
      heavy equipment, dredging, electrical infrastructure, and
      specialized maritime manufacturing sectors are projected to
      experience annual revenue growth of 10-15 percent above baseline
      forecasts, supporting expansion of domestic manufacturing capacity
      for coastal protection and maritime infrastructure products.

  (b) GULF COAST ECONOMIC BENEFITS. — Texas, Louisiana, Mississippi,
      Alabama, and Florida are projected to realize:

    (1) Avoided economic disruption valued at $8 billion annually from
        hurricane and storm-surge-related losses, business interruption,
        and emergency response costs;

    (2) Port throughput capacity increases of 30-50 percent at major
        Gulf container and energy ports, generating an estimated
        $12 billion annually in incremental trade-related economic
        activity;

    (3) Ecosystem restoration investments generating approximately
        $3 billion annually in fisheries, tourism, and recreation
        economic activity.

  (c) ATLANTIC COAST ECONOMIC BENEFITS. — The Northeast Megaregion from
      Boston to Norfolk and the Southeast from Charleston to Miami
      are projected to realize aggregate benefits of $50 billion over
      ten years from:

    (1) Protection of $300 billion in coastal real estate and
        infrastructure value;
    (2) Harbor deepening enabling capture of post-Panamax vessel
        traffic and associated logistics industry growth;
    (3) Offshore wind port infrastructure catalyzing Atlantic coast
        wind energy development valued at $100 billion.

  (d) PACIFIC COAST ECONOMIC BENEFITS. — California, Oregon, and
      Washington are projected to realize:

    (1) Enhanced port competitiveness at Los Angeles-Long Beach,
        Oakland, Seattle-Tacoma, and Portland;
    (2) Green shipping corridor network generating first-mover
        advantage in maritime decarbonization;
    (3) Kelp forest and wetland restoration supporting commercial
        fisheries valued at $2 billion annually.

  (e) GREAT LAKES REGIONAL BENEFITS. — Great Lakes ports handling
      iron ore, coal, grain, and manufactured goods are projected
      to receive approximately 15 percent of harbor deepening and
      port electrification investments, preserving critical shipping
      infrastructure in an aging system.

SECTION 3. SUPPLY CHAIN AND TRADE ECONOMIC ANALYSIS.

  (a) PORT CONGESTION COST REDUCTION. — Harbor deepening and port
      modernization investments are projected to:

    (1) Reduce average vessel wait times at major container ports
        from 3.5 days to 1.0 day or less;

    (2) Increase annual container throughput capacity by 40 percent
        at deepened ports;

    (3) Reduce supply chain congestion costs cumulatively by at
        least $50 billion over the ten-year authorization period;

    (4) Increase United States agricultural export competitiveness
        through reduced shipping costs and faster vessel turnaround.

  (b) ENERGY SECURITY. — Investment in port infrastructure supporting
      liquefied natural gas, crude oil, petroleum product, and future
      low-carbon fuel exports is projected to:

    (1) Support United States energy exports valued at $200 billion
        annually by 2036;

    (2) Enable port infrastructure for green ammonia and methanol
        exports, positioning United States ports as hubs of the
        global maritime energy transition;

    (3) Reduce vulnerability of energy export infrastructure to
        storm surge and sea level rise.

SECTION 4. PUBLIC HEALTH AND ENVIRONMENTAL BENEFITS.

  (a) PORT EMISSIONS REDUCTION. — Electrification of port operations
      and deployment of zero-emission cargo handling equipment is
      projected to:

    (1) Reduce annual emissions of nitrogen oxides by 25,000 tons,
        particulate matter (PM2.5) by 1,500 tons, and sulfur oxides
        by 12,000 tons by 2036;

    (2) Avoid approximately 3,500 premature deaths and 4,000 asthma
        exacerbations annually in port-adjacent communities;

    (3) Generate public health cost savings of approximately $25
        billion annually through reduced healthcare costs, avoided
        lost work and school days, and reduced mortality.

  (b) COASTAL FLOOD DAMAGE AVOIDANCE. — Sea wall, coastal armoring,
      and ecosystem restoration investments are projected to avoid:

    (1) $30 billion annually in direct property damage from storm
        surge and coastal flooding;

    (2) $5 billion annually in business interruption costs;

    (3) Emergency response and disaster recovery costs of $3 billion
        annually.

  (c) BLUE CARBON AND CLIMATE BENEFITS. — Coastal ecosystem restoration
      is projected to sequester 50 million metric tons of CO2 equivalent
      annually by 2036, valued at $10 billion annually at a social cost
      of carbon of $200 per metric ton.

SECTION 5. RETURN ON INVESTMENT ANALYSIS.

  (a) COST-BENEFIT RATIO. — The overall social return on investment for
      CPIPA programs is projected as:

    (1) TITLE I (Sea Wall and Armoring): Benefit-cost ratio 4:1,
        based on avoided flood damage, property value protection,
        and business continuity;

    (2) TITLE II (Port Electrification): Benefit-cost ratio 3:1,
        based on public health improvements, emissions reductions,
        and operational cost savings;

    (3) TITLE III (Harbor Deepening): Benefit-cost ratio 5:1,
        based on transportation cost savings, supply chain
        efficiency, and trade competitiveness;

    (4) TITLE IV (Coastal Ecosystem Restoration): Benefit-cost
        ratio 3:1, based on storm protection, fishery habitat,
        carbon sequestration, and recreation;

    (5) Aggregate return on investment: approximately $370 billion
        over 10 years, representing an overall benefit-cost ratio
        of 3.7 to 1.

  (b) COMPARISON WITH DEFERRED INVESTMENT. — The Congressional Budget
      Office estimates that deferring investments authorized by this
      Act for 10 years would increase total costs by approximately
      50 percent as sea levels continue rising, storms intensify,
      port infrastructure deteriorates further, and construction
      costs escalate.

SECTION 6. FISCAL IMPACT ESTIMATES.

  (a) DIRECT SPENDING. — The Congressional Budget Office estimates that
      this Act will increase direct spending by $100 billion over the
      period of fiscal years 2027-2036, distributed as follows:

    FY2027: $7 billion
    FY2028: $8 billion
    FY2029: $10 billion
    FY2030-$2036: $75 billion (average $10.7 billion per year)

  (b) REVENUE EFFECTS. — This Act is projected to increase Federal
      revenues by approximately $25 billion over the authorization
      period through:

    (1) Increased payroll and income tax receipts from employment;
    (2) Increased corporate tax receipts from manufacturing,
        construction, and port activity;
    (3) Harbor Maintenance Tax receipts on increased import volumes
        facilitated by port modernization.

  (c) STATE AND LOCAL FISCAL EFFECTS. — States and local governments
      are projected to realize:

    (1) Increased tax revenues of approximately $20 billion;
    (2) Reduced disaster response and recovery costs of approximately
        $5 billion annually;
    (3) Increased property tax base in communities protected from
        flooding and sea level rise.

  (d) LONG-TERM FISCAL OUTLOOK. — Over a 30-year horizon, CPIPA is
      projected to be strongly net-positive on a Federal fiscal basis
      due to avoided disaster recovery costs, increased trade-related
      revenues, public health savings, and economic growth effects
      that substantially exceed the initial Federal investment.
