================================================================================
                              EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
                        COASTAL AND PORT INFRASTRUCTURE
                        PROTECTION ACT OF 2026
================================================================================

I. PURPOSE AND SCOPE

  The Coastal and Port Infrastructure Protection Act of 2026 (CPIPA)
  constitutes a comprehensive legislative framework authorizing $100 billion
  in Federal investment over a ten-year period to protect the nation's
  coastal communities, modernize port infrastructure, accelerate maritime
  decarbonization, and build long-term resilience against sea level rise and
  coastal flooding. CPIPA addresses the urgent economic, environmental, and
  national security imperatives arising from accelerating sea level rise,
  aging port infrastructure, increasing storm intensity, and the critical
  need for greenhouse gas emissions reductions in the maritime sector.

II. STRATEGIC CONTEXT

  Approximately 40 percent of the United States population — more than 130
  million Americans — lives in coastal shoreline counties, which account for
  less than 10 percent of the nation's land area. These coastal communities
  generate approximately $9.5 trillion in goods and services annually,
  representing roughly 45 percent of the nation's gross domestic product.
  Coastal counties are growing at a rate of approximately 15 percent per
  decade, with population density in coastal areas five times the national
  average.

  Global mean sea level has risen approximately 10 to 12 inches since 1880,
  with approximately 4 inches of that rise occurring since 1993. The National
  Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration (NOAA) projects an additional 10 to
  12 inches of sea level rise along United States coastlines by 2050, equal
  to the total rise observed over the entire preceding century. The United
  States coastline extends approximately 95,000 miles, encompassing diverse
  geographic settings from the rocky shores of New England to the barrier
  islands of the Gulf Coast to the bluffs of the Pacific Northwest.

  An estimated $1 trillion in coastal property value is at risk of chronic
  flooding by 2050 under intermediate sea level rise scenarios, with more
  than 300,000 homes in the contiguous United States projected to experience
  chronic inundation by 2045. The Federal Emergency Management Agency
  estimates that flood damage to coastal properties already exceeds
  $20 billion annually, a figure projected to double by 2050 absent
  significant adaptation investment.

  United States seaports handle approximately 99 percent of overseas trade
  by volume and 65 percent by value, supporting over 30 million jobs and
  generating $5.4 trillion in annual economic activity. The supply chain
  disruptions of 2021-2023, during which port congestion costs exceeded
  $20 billion, exposed the fragility of port infrastructure and the critical
  need for modernization, expansion, and resilience investment. The average
  age of major United States port infrastructure exceeds 50 years, and many
  ports lack the depth, width, and shore-side infrastructure to accommodate
  the new generation of ultra-large container vessels exceeding 24,000 TEU
  capacity.

  The maritime sector accounts for approximately 3 percent of global
  greenhouse gas emissions, and the International Maritime Organization has
  adopted a strategy targeting net-zero emissions by or around 2050. The
  United States must invest urgently in alternative maritime fuel
  infrastructure, vessel electrification, and green shipping corridor
  development to maintain competitiveness, meet international commitments,
  and lead the global maritime energy transition.

III. KEY PROVISIONS AND FUNDING ALLOCATION

  The CPIPA authorizes $100 billion across eight titles:

  TITLE I — SEA WALL AND COASTAL ARMORING ($30 billion): Funds comprehensive
  coastal vulnerability assessment; sea wall, levee, and flood barrier
  construction in high-risk urban areas; living shoreline and hybrid coastal
  protection projects; and beach nourishment and dune restoration programs.

  TITLE II — PORT ELECTRIFICATION ($25 billion): Funds shore power and cold
  ironing infrastructure at major ports; zero-emission cargo handling
  equipment deployment; port microgrids and renewable energy generation;
  drayage truck electrification; and green port certification.

  TITLE III — HARBOR DEEPENING ($20 billion): Prioritizes harbor deepening
  to accommodate ultra-large container vessels; funds navigation channel
  widening; supports beneficial use of dredged material for coastal
  restoration; and modernizes the Army Corps of Engineers dredging fleet.

  TITLE IV — COASTAL ECOSYSTEM RESTORATION ($15 billion): Funds wetland
  restoration; oyster reef and shellfish habitat restoration; seagrass
  and kelp forest restoration; coastal land conservation; and blue carbon
  ecosystem preservation to sequester atmospheric carbon.

  TITLE V — PORT RESILIENCE HUBS: Designates strategic ports as resilience
  hubs with investments in storm surge protection, emergency power systems,
  redundant supply chain infrastructure, and cybersecurity hardening.

  TITLE VI — OFFSHORE WIND PORT INFRASTRUCTURE: Funds development of
  specialized ports for offshore wind turbine staging, assembly, and
  deployment; submarine cable landing; and workforce training.

  TITLE VII — MARITIME DECARBONIZATION: Funds alternative maritime fuel
  infrastructure including green methanol, ammonia, and hydrogen; vessel
  electrification and hybridization; green shipping corridors; and zero-
  emission vessel research and development.

  TITLE VIII — FLOOD INSURANCE REFORM: Reforms the National Flood Insurance
  Program to reflect climate risk accurately; funds Risk Rating 3.0
  implementation with affordability provisions; updates coastal flood
  mapping; and establishes managed retreat and relocation assistance
  for communities facing unavoidable inundation.

  An additional $10 billion is allocated for program administration,
  oversight, and harbor maintenance trust fund reform.

IV. ECONOMIC PROJECTIONS

  1. Protect approximately $500 billion in coastal property value from
     chronic flooding and storm surge by 2050 through Title I investments.

  2. Create approximately 250,000 direct and indirect domestic jobs in
     coastal construction, port operations, maritime manufacturing, and
     ecosystem restoration.

  3. Generate approximately $150 billion in cumulative GDP contributions
     over the ten-year authorization period.

  4. Reduce port-related greenhouse gas emissions by 60 percent from 2025
     levels by 2036 through electrification and alternative fuel deployment.

  5. Reduce port congestion costs by at least $50 billion over ten years
     through harbor deepening and modernization.

V. NATIONAL SECURITY AND ECONOMIC IMPERATIVE

  CPIPA finds that the vulnerability of United States coastal communities
  and port infrastructure to accelerating sea level rise, intensifying
  storms, and supply chain disruptions constitutes a material threat to
  national security, economic prosperity, and community well-being. The
  uninterrupted operation of United States seaports is essential to the
  national economy, military logistics, energy security, and international
  trade competitiveness. This legislation represents the operationalization
  of the consensus view across the Department of Defense, the Department
  of Homeland Security, the Department of Transportation, and NOAA that
  coastal and port infrastructure investment must be treated with strategic
  urgency commensurate with its economic and security importance.
