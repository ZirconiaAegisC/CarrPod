                         EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
                              OF THE
     BRIDGE AND TRANSPORTATION INFRASTRUCTURE MODERNIZATION ACT OF 2026

================================================================================

I.  OVERVIEW AND LEGISLATIVE PURPOSE

    The Bridge and Transportation Infrastructure Modernization Act of
    2026 (BTIMA) establishes the most comprehensive federal bridge
    preservation and modernization program in United States history.
    The Act authorizes $200 billion in appropriations over a ten-year
    period (Fiscal Years 2027 through 2036) to address the nation's
    deteriorating bridge infrastructure through a coordinated program
    of condition assessment, advanced corrosion protection, seismic
    hardening, digital monitoring, research, and workforce development.

    The United States currently faces a bridge infrastructure crisis
    of extraordinary scope and urgency. The American Society of Civil
    Engineers (ASCE) 2025 Report Card for America's Infrastructure
    assigns the nation's bridges a grade of "C+", finding that
    approximately 46,000 bridges are structurally deficient and that
    one in three bridges has exceeded its 50-year design service life
    without major rehabilitation. The corrosion-related cost to the
    U.S. economy exceeds $13.6 billion annually for direct bridge
    maintenance alone (NACE International IMPACT Study).

    BTIMA addresses this crisis through eight integrated Titles
    spanning assessment, preservation, seismic safety, digital
    modernization, research, and workforce development. The Act
    leverages proven technologies—including Norwegian thermal spray
    zinc duplex coating systems with a demonstrated 60-year service
    record on 62 bridges, impressed current cathodic protection
    successfully deployed on 15 Oregon DOT bridges, and structural
    health monitoring systems—to extend bridge service life, reduce
    lifecycle costs, and protect public safety.

III. KEY MANDATES AND REQUIREMENTS

    A.  Federal Bridge Fleet Assessment (Title I). Requires the
        Federal Highway Administration (FHWA), in coordination with
        State departments of transportation, to complete a mandatory
        condition inventory of all bridges on the National Bridge
        Inventory within 24 months of enactment. Establishes a
        standardized National Infrastructure Performance Rating
        (NIPR) system and mandates a two-year inspection cycle for
        all bridges rated "Poor" or below.

        Lead Agency: Federal Highway Administration
        Implementing Entities: State DOTs, MPOs
        Key Authority: 23 U.S.C. 144 (amended)

    B.  Advanced Coating Mandate (Title II). Requires thermal spray
        zinc (TSZ) duplex coating systems on all steel bridge
        elements receiving federal funding for new construction or
        major rehabilitation. TSZ duplex systems have demonstrated
        60–100 year service life compared to 15–25 years for
        conventional paint systems, as documented in NCHRP Report
        398 and AASHTO Duplex Coating Guidelines (2026).

        Lead Agency: Federal Highway Administration
        Standards Body: AASHTO, NACE/AMPP, SSPC
        Key Authority: 23 U.S.C. 144, 23 U.S.C. 119 (amended)

    C.  Seismic Retrofit Acceleration (Title III). Mandates
        seismic vulnerability assessments every five years for all
        bridges in Seismic Design Categories C, D, E, and F.
        Establishes a $50 billion National Seismic Retrofit Priority
        Fund informed by case studies including the San Mateo-Hayward
        Bridge (Bay Area Toll Authority) and Caltrans seismic
        retrofit program.

        Lead Agency: Federal Highway Administration
        Technical Partner: U.S. Geological Survey
        Key Authority: 23 U.S.C. 120, 23 U.S.C. 144 (amended)

    D.  Cathodic Protection for Concrete (Title IV). Mandates
        impressed current cathodic protection (ICCP) on all new and
        rehabilitated concrete bridge substructures and decks in
        corrosive environments. The Oregon DOT model—protecting 15
        bridges at a cost of $150 million versus an estimated $1
        billion in replacement costs—establishes the lifecycle
        cost-effectiveness of this approach.

        Lead Agency: Federal Highway Administration
        Technical Partner: NACE/AMPP
        Key Authority: 23 U.S.C. 119, 23 U.S.C. 144 (amended)

    E.  Digital Twin and Structural Monitoring (Title V). Requires
        Bridge Information Modeling (BrIM) deliverables on all
        federally funded bridge projects exceeding $20 million.
        Mandates structural health monitoring sensor deployment on
        all bridges rated "Poor" or in Seismic Design Categories D
        and above. Establishes a secure National Bridge Digital
        Twin Data Platform for interagency data sharing.

        Lead Agency: Federal Highway Administration
        Technical Partner: National Institute of Standards and Technology
        Key Authority: 23 U.S.C. 503, 23 U.S.C. 144 (amended)

    F.  Corrosion Research (Title VI). Authorizes $5 billion for
        competitive research grants in advanced coating technologies,
        graphene-enhanced protective systems, self-healing coating
        development, and predictive corrosion analytics. Establishes
        a National Bridge Corrosion Research Center.

        Lead Agency: Federal Highway Administration, Turner-Fairbank
                     Highway Research Center
        Research Partners: NACE/AMPP, AASHTO, NCHRP, University
                          Transportation Centers

    G.  Workforce Development (Title VII). Establishes a National
        Bridge Inspector Training Pipeline, a NACE/AMPP-certified
        coatings applicator program, and community college and
        apprenticeship grants to address the critical shortage of
        qualified bridge inspection and corrosion protection
        professionals.

        Lead Agency: Department of Transportation
        Training Partners: NACE/AMPP, SSPC, Community Colleges

IV. ECONOMIC IMPACT AND BENEFIT-COST ANALYSIS

    The Act is projected to:

    (a) Support approximately 4,000,000 direct and indirect jobs
        over the ten-year authorization period;
    (b) Generate approximately $500 billion in cumulative GDP impact
        through direct construction spending, supply chain activity,
        and induced economic effects;
    (c) Achieve a benefit-cost ratio of approximately 5:1, based on
        avoided bridge failures, reduced traffic disruption, extended
        service life, and lower lifecycle maintenance costs;
    (d) Reduce the number of structurally deficient bridges by an
        estimated 75 percent within the authorization period;
    (e) Extend the average service life of rehabilitated bridges by
        40 to 75 years relative to current practice.

V.  IMPLEMENTATION GOVERNANCE

    The Secretary of Transportation shall designate a BTIMA Program
    Director within FHWA no later than 90 days after enactment. The
    Program Director shall establish a BTIMA Interagency Coordinating
    Council including representatives from FHWA, NIST, USGS, the
    Army Corps of Engineers, and the Department of Labor. The
    Government Accountability Office shall conduct biennial audits
    of program expenditures and performance metrics.
