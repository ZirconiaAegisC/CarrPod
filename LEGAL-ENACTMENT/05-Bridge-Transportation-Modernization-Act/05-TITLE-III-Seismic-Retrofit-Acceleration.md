              TITLE III — SEISMIC RETROFIT ACCELERATION

================================================================================

SECTION 301. MANDATORY SEISMIC VULNERABILITY ASSESSMENTS.

    (a) FIVE-YEAR ASSESSMENT CYCLE. — Not later than 3 years after
    the date of enactment, and every 5 years thereafter, each State
    department of transportation shall complete a seismic
    vulnerability assessment of every bridge within its jurisdiction
    that is located in Seismic Design Categories C, D, E, or F as
    defined by AASHTO LRFD Bridge Design Specifications, 10th
    Edition (2024).

    (b) ASSESSMENT METHODOLOGY. — Each seismic vulnerability
    assessment shall:

        (1) Determine the seismic demand at the bridge site using
        the most current USGS National Seismic Hazard Model ground
        motion data for a 975-year return period event (5 percent
        probability of exceedance in 50 years) and, for bridges
        in Seismic Design Categories D, E, or F, a 2,475-year
        return period event (2 percent probability of exceedance
        in 50 years);

        (2) Compute the seismic capacity of the bridge substructure,
        including columns, pier caps, foundations, abutments, and
        bearings, using nonlinear static pushover analysis or
        nonlinear time-history analysis as appropriate for the
        bridge type and seismic hazard level;

        (3) Calculate the displacement capacity-to-demand ratio
        for each column and pier in both the longitudinal and
        transverse directions;

        (4) Identify all details not conforming to current AASHTO
        seismic detailing requirements, including inadequate column
        transverse reinforcement, insufficient confinement at
        plastic hinge zones, non-ductile connection details,
        inadequate seat widths, and liquefaction-susceptible
        foundations;

        (5) Classify each bridge into one of the following seismic
        vulnerability categories:

            (A) Category S-1: Adequate — displacement capacity
            exceeds demand by a factor of 1.5 or greater in all
            directions for both the 975-year and 2,475-year events;
            no retrofitting required.

            (B) Category S-2: Moderate Risk — displacement capacity
            exceeds demand for the 975-year event but is insufficient
            for the 2,475-year event; retrofit recommended within
            10 years.

            (C) Category S-3: High Risk — displacement capacity is
            insufficient for the 975-year event; retrofit required
            within 5 years.

            (D) Category S-4: Critical Risk — displacement capacity
            is less than 50 percent of demand for the 975-year
            event; retrofit required within 3 years; interim load
            restrictions or closure may be warranted; and

        (6) Estimate the cost of required seismic retrofit measures
        to achieve Category S-1 status for Categories S-2, S-3,
        and S-4.

    (c) QUALIFIED ENGINEERS. — Seismic vulnerability assessments
    shall be performed under the responsible charge of a licensed
    professional engineer with demonstrated experience in seismic
    analysis and design of bridges, including at least 5 years of
    experience in performance-based seismic design.

    (d) DATA SUBMISSION. — Assessment results shall be submitted
    to the Secretary and incorporated into the National Bridge
    Condition Inventory and NIPR database established under
    Title I of this Act.

SECTION 302. NATIONAL SEISMIC RETROFIT PRIORITY FUND.

    (a) ESTABLISHMENT. — There is established in the Highway Trust
    Fund a National Seismic Retrofit Priority Fund (hereinafter
    the "Seismic Fund").

    (b) FUNDING. — From amounts authorized under section 801 of
    this Act, $50,000,000,000 shall be deposited into the Seismic
    Fund for the period of fiscal years 2027 through 2036, to
    remain available until expended.

    (c) ALLOCATION PRIORITY. — The Secretary shall allocate funds
    from the Seismic Fund to States based on:

        (1) The number of bridges classified as Category S-3 or
        S-4 within the State, weighted at 50 percent;

        (2) The aggregate estimated retrofit cost for Category S-3
        and S-4 bridges within the State, weighted at 30 percent;

        (3) The seismic hazard level (weighted average PGA at the
        2,475-year return period for bridge sites within the State),
        weighted at 10 percent; and

        (4) The traffic criticality of vulnerable bridges (sum of
        average daily traffic multiplied by detour length), weighted
        at 10 percent.

    (d) FEDERAL SHARE. — The Federal share of the cost of seismic
    retrofit projects funded from the Seismic Fund shall be:

        (1) 90 percent for bridges classified as Category S-3 or
        S-4;

        (2) 80 percent for bridges classified as Category S-2; and

        (3) 50 percent for voluntary retrofit of Category S-1
        bridges to enhance resilience beyond minimum standards.

    (e) ELIGIBLE COSTS. — Eligible costs include seismic analysis,
    design, construction, construction engineering and inspection,
    materials testing, and project-related costs of traffic
    control and public communication.

SECTION 303. SEISMIC DESIGN CRITERIA FOR NEW BRIDGE CONSTRUCTION.

    (a) MINIMUM STANDARD. — All new bridges receiving Federal-aid
    highway funds and located in Seismic Design Categories C, D,
    E, or F shall be designed to meet the performance objectives
    specified in AASHTO Guide Specifications for LRFD Seismic
    Bridge Design, 3rd Edition (2023), or a successor standard
    approved by the Secretary.

    (b) POST-EARTHQUAKE SERVICEABILITY. — Bridges classified as
    "critical" or "essential" for emergency response and recovery
    under 23 CFR 667 shall, in addition to the requirements of
    subsection (a), be designed to remain open to emergency
    vehicle traffic following the 975-year return period design
    earthquake and shall be repairable with minimal disruption
    following the 2,475-year design earthquake.

    (c) LIQUEFACTION AND LATERAL SPREADING. — Bridge foundations
    in Seismic Design Categories D, E, and F shall be designed
    to resist liquefaction and lateral spreading effects for the
    2,475-year return period event, using site-specific geotechnical
    investigation and advanced analytical methods (effective stress
    or nonlinear deformation analysis).

    (d) SEISMIC ISOLATION AND ENERGY DISSIPATION. — The Secretary
    shall encourage the evaluation and use of seismic isolation
    bearings, fluid viscous dampers, buckling-restrained braces,
    and other seismic protective systems on all new and retrofitted
    bridges where such systems are shown, through lifecycle cost
    analysis, to reduce total project cost or improve post-earthquake
    serviceability.

SECTION 304. HIGH-SEISMIC-ZONE BRIDGE ACCELERATED PROGRAM.

    (a) HIGH-PRIORITY CORRIDORS. — The Secretary shall establish
    a High-Seismic-Zone Bridge Accelerated Program to expedite
    seismic retrofit of bridges on critical transportation
    corridors in California, Alaska, Washington, Oregon, Nevada,
    Utah, Hawaii, Puerto Rico, the New Madrid Seismic Zone
    (Arkansas, Illinois, Indiana, Kentucky, Mississippi, Missouri,
    Tennessee), and the Charleston Seismic Zone (South Carolina).

    (b) ACCELERATED DELIVERY. — The Secretary is authorized to
    use all available project delivery methods to accelerate
    seismic retrofit projects under this section, including
    design-build, construction manager/general contractor (CM/GC),
    and indefinite delivery/indefinite quantity (IDIQ) contracts.

    (c) INTERSTATE COORDINATION. — The Secretary shall facilitate
    coordination among States sharing seismically vulnerable
    interstate bridges to ensure consistent retrofit prioritization
    and minimize interstate commerce disruption during construction.

SECTION 305. CASE STUDY INTEGRATION AND BEST PRACTICES.

    (a) CALIFORNIA CASE STUDY. — The Secretary shall document
    and disseminate lessons learned from the Caltrans Seismic
    Retrofit Program, including:

        (1) Retrofit strategies and details developed for the
        San Francisco-Oakland Bay Bridge, the San Mateo-Hayward
        Bridge, and other major toll crossings;

        (2) Column jacketing techniques (steel, concrete, and
        fiber-reinforced polymer);

        (3) Foundation strengthening and liquefaction mitigation
        methods;

        (4) Hinge seat widening and cable restrainer systems; and

        (5) Cost data, construction sequencing strategies, and
        traffic management during multi-year retrofit projects.

    (b) BATA CASE STUDY. — The Secretary shall specifically
    document the Bay Area Toll Authority's seismic retrofit
    experience, including the $2,300,000,000 Capital Improvement
    Program spanning 10 toll bridge structures, the $163,000,000
    San Mateo-Hayward substructure rehabilitation, and the
    integration of seismic retrofit with corrosion protection
    and structural monitoring.

    (c) INTERNATIONAL BEST PRACTICES. — The Secretary shall
    evaluate and report to Congress on international best
    practices in bridge seismic design and retrofit, including
    practices from Japan, New Zealand, Chile, Italy, and Turkey,
    with recommendations for adoption in United States practice
    where beneficial.

    (d) RETROFIT MANUAL. — Within 2 years of enactment, the
    Secretary, in consultation with AASHTO, shall publish and
    maintain a National Bridge Seismic Retrofit Manual providing
    standardized methods, details, and cost estimation tools for
    common bridge types and seismic vulnerability conditions.
