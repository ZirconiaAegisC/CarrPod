SECTION 3. FINDINGS AND PURPOSES.

================================================================================

    (a) FINDINGS. — The Congress finds the following:

        (1) The American Society of Civil Engineers (ASCE), in its
        2025 Report Card for America's Infrastructure, assigned the
        nation's bridges a grade of "C+", indicating that the
        Nation's 617,000 bridges are, on average, in mediocre
        condition and require urgent attention.

        (2) As of 2025, approximately 46,000 bridges in the United
        States are classified as structurally deficient, meaning
        that one or more key structural elements—deck, superstructure,
        or substructure—are in "Poor" or worse condition under the
        National Bridge Inventory rating system.

        (3) One in three bridges on the National Bridge Inventory
        has exceeded its original 50-year design service life without
        undergoing major rehabilitation or replacement, according to
        FHWA data submitted to Congress in 2025.

        (4) The San Mateo-Hayward Bridge, a critical artery in the
        San Francisco Bay Area carrying over 93,000 vehicles daily,
        has been assigned a "Poor" substructure condition rating by
        the Bay Area Toll Authority (BATA), with an estimated
        rehabilitation cost of $163,000,000 for substructure repairs
        alone as documented in BATA's Fiscal Year 2025 Capital
        Improvement Program.

        (5) The Bay Area Toll Authority's current 10-year Capital
        Improvement Program totals $2,300,000,000 for 10 toll bridge
        structures, reflecting the accelerating cost of deferred
        maintenance on aging bridge infrastructure in seismically
        active regions.

        (6) The Richmond-San Rafael Bridge, spanning San Francisco
        Bay and carrying Interstate 580 traffic, requires an estimated
        $68,000,000 for repainting of corroded steel elements, a cost
        that could have been largely avoided through the application
        of a durable duplex coating system at the time of original
        construction or earlier rehabilitation.

        (7) Corrosion of steel bridge elements imposes an estimated
        direct annual cost of $13,600,000,000 on the United States
        economy, according to the NACE International IMPACT study
        on corrosion costs and preventive strategies, with indirect
        costs—including traffic delays, detours, and commercial
        vehicle operating costs—estimated at two to three times
        the direct cost.

        (8) Conventional liquid-applied paint coating systems on
        steel bridges typically require recoating every 15 to 25
        years, whereas thermal spray zinc (TSZ) duplex coating
        systems have demonstrated a service life of 60 to 100 years
        in marine and deicing-salt environments, as documented in
        NCHRP Report 398, "Durability of Thermal Spray Coatings
        for Corrosion Protection of Steel Bridges."

        (9) The Norwegian Public Roads Administration has deployed
        thermal spray zinc duplex coating systems on 62 bridges
        over a 60-year period, achieving a documented service life
        in excess of 60 years with minimal maintenance intervention,
        providing a validated international benchmark for coating
        performance in cold-climate marine environments comparable
        to those in the northeastern and northwestern United States.

        (10) Thermal spray zinc duplex coatings, when properly
        applied to abrasive blast-cleaned steel (SSPC-SP 10 near-white
        metal blast), provide both barrier protection and galvanic
        cathodic protection to the underlying steel substrate, a
        dual mechanism that conventional paint systems cannot provide.

        (11) The AASHTO Duplex Coating Guidelines (2026 Edition)
        provide detailed specifications for thermal spray zinc
        application on bridge steel, including surface preparation
        standards, minimum zinc thickness requirements (8–12 mils),
        sealer application procedures, and quality control protocols.

        (12) The FHWA report HIF-22-004, "Corrosion Protection of
        Steel Bridges," identifies duplex coating systems as the
        preferred long-term corrosion protection strategy for steel
        bridges in aggressive environments and recommends their use
        on all new and rehabilitated bridge steel where a 75-year
        design life is specified.

        (13) Impressed current cathodic protection (ICCP) systems
        have been successfully deployed by the Oregon Department of
        Transportation on 15 concrete bridges, protecting substructure
        and deck elements from chloride-induced corrosion at a total
        cost of approximately $150,000,000, compared to an estimated
        replacement cost exceeding $1,000,000,000 for the same
        structures, yielding a benefit-cost ratio exceeding 6.5:1.

        (14) Chloride-induced corrosion of reinforcing steel in
        concrete bridge decks and substructures is the predominant
        cause of concrete bridge deterioration in the United States,
        particularly in northern states where deicing salts are
        applied and in coastal environments subject to marine salt
        spray.

        (15) The United States Geological Survey's National Seismic
        Hazard Model identifies significant seismic risk in 42
        States, with approximately 35 percent of the National Bridge
        Inventory located in Seismic Design Categories C, D, E, or
        F where significant seismic demands may be imposed.

        (16) The Caltrans Seismic Retrofit Program, initiated after
        the 1989 Loma Prieta earthquake, has successfully retrofitted
        or replaced over 2,200 State-owned bridges at a cost of
        approximately $15,000,000,000, demonstrating the feasibility
        and life-safety benefits of systematic seismic hardening
        programs.

        (17) Bridge Information Modeling (BrIM) and structural health
        monitoring technologies, including fiber optic strain sensors,
        accelerometers, corrosion potential sensors, and acoustic
        emission monitoring, have matured to the point of commercial
        viability and can provide real-time structural condition data
        that significantly enhances inspection accuracy and enables
        predictive, condition-based maintenance.

        (18) The United States faces a critical shortage of qualified
        bridge inspectors certified by the National Highway Institute
        and of coatings applicators certified by NACE/AMPP or SSPC,
        with an estimated 30 percent of the current inspection
        workforce eligible for retirement within the next five years.

        (19) The Federal Highway Administration estimates a national
        bridge rehabilitation backlog exceeding $125,000,000,000,
        a figure that continues to grow as bridges age faster than
        available funding can address their deterioration.

        (20) The Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act (Public Law
        117-58) provided significant funding for bridge rehabilitation
        through the Bridge Formula Program and Bridge Investment
        Program, but did not establish mandatory performance standards
        for corrosion protection, seismic evaluation, or digital
        monitoring that would ensure the long-term durability of
        funded improvements.

        (21) The economic cost of a major bridge failure—including
        loss of life, emergency response, traffic disruption, supply
        chain interruption, and replacement cost—far exceeds the
        incremental cost of preventive measures such as advanced
        coatings, cathodic protection, and structural monitoring.

        (22) The Federal Government has a compelling interest in
        ensuring the safety, resilience, and longevity of the
        Nation's bridge infrastructure, which is essential to
        interstate commerce, national defense mobility, emergency
        evacuation, and the daily movement of persons and goods.

    (b) PURPOSES. — The purposes of this Act are:

        (1) To establish a comprehensive national program for the
        systematic assessment, preservation, and modernization of
        the United States bridge infrastructure;

        (2) To mandate the use of advanced corrosion protection
        technologies—including thermal spray zinc duplex coatings
        and impressed current cathodic protection—that have
        demonstrated multi-decade service life in domestic and
        international applications;

        (3) To accelerate seismic evaluation and retrofit of bridges
        in moderate to high seismic hazard zones;

        (4) To deploy digital twin and structural health monitoring
        technologies that enable predictive, condition-based
        maintenance and reduce lifecycle costs;

        (5) To invest in corrosion research and innovation that will
        develop the next generation of bridge protection technologies;

        (6) To build a skilled workforce of bridge inspectors,
        coatings applicators, and structural monitoring technicians
        capable of executing the programs authorized by this Act; and

        (7) To reduce the number of structurally deficient bridges
        in the United States by at least 75 percent within ten years
        of enactment.
