              SECTION 902. ECONOMIC IMPACT ANALYSIS

================================================================================

SECTION 902. ECONOMIC IMPACT REPORTING.

    (a) FINDINGS. — The Congress finds, based on the economic
    impact analysis prepared by the Congressional Budget Office,
    the FHWA, and independent economic research organizations,
    that the programs authorized under this Act are expected to
    generate substantial economic benefits to the United States.

    (b) EMPLOYMENT IMPACT. —

        (1) DIRECT EMPLOYMENT. — The programs authorized under
        this Act are projected to support approximately
        2,000,000 direct job-years in engineering, construction,
        manufacturing, inspection, and related fields over the
        10-year authorization period. Direct employment includes:

            (A) Construction trades: ironworkers, laborers,
            carpenters, equipment operators, cement masons,
            painters and coatings applicators;

            (B) Engineering and design: structural engineers,
            corrosion engineers, geotechnical engineers,
            electrical engineers, and BIM/CAD technicians;

            (C) Inspection and testing: bridge inspectors,
            coatings inspectors, nondestructive testing
            technicians, and materials testing personnel;

            (D) Manufacturing: thermal spray equipment, zinc
            wire, anode materials, sensors, data acquisition
            hardware, and coating materials; and

            (E) Program management: project managers, schedulers,
            cost estimators, and administrative personnel.

        (2) INDIRECT AND INDUCED EMPLOYMENT. — Supply chain and
        induced economic effects are projected to support an
        additional 2,000,000 indirect job-years, for a total
        employment impact of approximately 4,000,000 job-years.

        (3) WORKFORCE MULTIPLIER. — Using the Department of
        Commerce RIMS II input-output model, each $1,000,000,000
        in bridge infrastructure investment is estimated to
        support approximately 13,000 direct and indirect jobs
        for one year. Applied to the $200,000,000,000 authorization,
        the total job-year estimate is approximately 2,600,000,
        conservatively adjusted to 4,000,000 inclusive of induced
        effects and program efficiencies.

    (c) GDP IMPACT. —

        (1) DIRECT SPENDING. — The $200,000,000,000 in authorized
        appropriations will generate an estimated $200,000,000,000
        in direct economic output.

        (2) MULTIPLIER EFFECTS. — Using a GDP multiplier of 2.5
        for infrastructure spending, as estimated by the
        Congressional Budget Office for transportation
        infrastructure investment, the total GDP impact of the
        programs authorized under this Act is projected to be
        approximately $500,000,000,000 over the 10-year
        authorization period and the subsequent economic activity
        generated by improved infrastructure productivity.

        (3) PRODUCTIVITY GAINS. — Reduced traffic congestion from
        avoided bridge closures, reduced detour lengths from
        improved bridge condition, and reduced commercial vehicle
        operating costs from improved load ratings and reduced
        load restrictions are projected to generate ongoing annual
        productivity gains of approximately $5,000,000,000 to
        $8,000,000,000 per year beginning in FY2031 and continuing
        indefinitely.

    (d) BENEFIT-COST ANALYSIS. —

        (1) METHODOLOGY. — The benefit-cost analysis presented
        here follows the methodology prescribed by OMB Circular
        A-94 (Guidelines and Discount Rates for Benefit-Cost
        Analysis of Federal Programs) and OMB Circular A-4
        (Regulatory Analysis), using a 3 percent real discount
        rate and a 30-year analysis period for lifecycle benefits.

        (2) QUANTIFIED BENEFITS. — The following benefits have
        been quantified in the analysis:

            (A) AVOIDED BRIDGE FAILURE COSTS. — Based on the
            ASCE estimate of 46,000 structurally deficient bridges,
            a projected failure rate of 0.1 percent per year without
            intervention, and an average failure cost of
            $50,000,000 (including emergency response, loss of life,
            reconstruction, detour costs, and economic disruption),
            the programs authorized under this Act are projected to
            avoid bridge failures with a total present value of
            approximately $40,000,000,000.

            (B) EXTENDED SERVICE LIFE. — By extending the average
            service life of rehabilitated bridges by 40–75 years
            through advanced coatings, cathodic protection, and
            proactive maintenance informed by SHM data, the programs
            are projected to defer replacement costs with a present
            value of approximately $300,000,000,000.

            (C) REDUCED MAINTENANCE COSTS. — The adoption of TSZ
            duplex coatings (60–100 year life) in place of
            conventional paint systems (15–25 year life) is
            projected to reduce recurring maintenance coating
            costs by approximately $25,000,000,000 in present value
            terms over the analysis period.

            (D) REDUCED TRAFFIC DISRUPTION. — Avoided lane closures
            for maintenance coating, avoided load postings, and
            reduced emergency bridge closures are projected to
            generate user cost savings with a present value of
            approximately $120,000,000,000, based on FHWA
            work zone road user cost methodology.

            (E) SEISMIC RESILIENCE BENEFITS. — The seismic retrofit
            program is projected to reduce expected earthquake
            losses (structural damage, casualties, and economic
            disruption) by approximately $60,000,000,000 in present
            value terms for bridges in high-seismic zones.

        (3) TOTAL BENEFIT-COST RATIO. — Incorporating the
        quantified benefits listed above, together with additional
        benefits not fully monetized (ecosystem services from
        reduced construction material consumption, improved
        national security mobility, and enhanced quality of life),
        the programs authorized under this Act are estimated to
        achieve a benefit-cost ratio of approximately 5:1,
        meaning that each dollar of Federal investment is
        projected to generate approximately five dollars in
        economic benefits.

    (e) REGIONAL DISTRIBUTION OF BENEFITS. —

        (1) RURAL STATES. — Rural States with high bridge
        counts relative to population are expected to receive
        disproportionate benefits from the inspection,
        rehabilitation, and replacement programs, as their bridge
        networks are typically older and less well-maintained
        than those in urbanized States.

        (2) SEISMIC ZONES. — States in the Western U.S., Alaska,
        Hawaii, and the New Madrid and Charleston Seismic Zones
        will receive concentrated benefits from the seismic
        retrofit and monitoring programs.

        (3) COASTAL AND NORTHERN STATES. — States with extensive
        marine coastlines, tidal waterways, and high deicing
        salt usage will receive concentrated benefits from the
        advanced coating and cathodic protection programs.

        (4) MANUFACTURING REGIONS. — States with existing zinc
        production, thermal spray equipment manufacturing, sensor
        manufacturing, and coating material production capacity
        are expected to benefit from increased manufacturing
        demand under the Act.

    (f) ANNUAL REPORTING. — The Secretary shall include in the
    annual report to Congress required under section 104(c) an
    updated economic impact analysis reflecting actual
    expenditures, employment data, and observed bridge condition
    improvements relative to the projections in this section.
