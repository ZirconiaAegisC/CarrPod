And when we examine the worst-case scenario for hospital electrical infrastructure, we find a gap between what NFPA 99 requires and what a major geomagnetic disturbance could deliver.

NFPA 99, the Health Care Facilities Code, is the foundation of hospital electrical safety in the United States. It specifies the requirements for essential electrical systems — the emergency power supply systems, the equipment branches, the life safety and critical branches — that keep hospitals functioning when utility power fails. NFPA 99 requires on-site fuel storage for generator operation, typically sized for 24 to 96 hours of runtime depending on the facility classification and local authority having jurisdiction. This is adequate for the outage durations associated with hurricanes, ice storms, and earthquakes — the familiar hazards that hospital emergency planning has been built around for decades.

But a GMD event is fundamentally different. A Carrington-class geomagnetic storm would not damage hospital generators — those are on-site, disconnected from the grid, and inherently immune to geomagnetic induction. What it would damage are the large power transformers in the substations that serve the hospital, and those transformers have replacement lead times measured in months, not hours or days. Even if a hospital has a full week of generator fuel on site, that fuel will be exhausted long before the grid transformer is replaced. And resupply of diesel fuel in a region-wide, months-long grid-down scenario is, to put it mildly, not something that current hospital emergency plans address with any specificity.

Our SHIELD framework — Strategic Hardening Integration for Extended-Lead-time Defense — addresses this gap directly. For the healthcare sector, SHIELD recommends: (1) extending on-site fuel storage to a minimum of three weeks of continuous operation, with contracted priority resupply agreements beyond that; (2) pre-qualifying mobile generator connections at hospital electrical switchgear, so that rented or FEMA-provided generators can be rapidly deployed and connected without field engineering; (3) establishing regional hospital consortium agreements for shared mobile transformer assets, recognizing that no single hospital can justify a spare 50 MVA transformer but a consortium of ten hospitals can; and (4) incorporating GMD-specific operational procedures into hospital emergency operations plans, including load-shedding protocols that preserve life-safety and critical care circuits while shedding non-essential loads to extend fuel endurance.
