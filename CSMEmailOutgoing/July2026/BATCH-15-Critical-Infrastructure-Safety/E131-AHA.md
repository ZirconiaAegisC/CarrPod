Let me be specific about what a Carrington-class GMD event would mean for AHA member hospitals. The event itself would not physically damage hospital buildings or directly harm patients. What it would do is destroy or damage a significant fraction of the large power transformers in the regional transmission grid — the 345 kV and 500 kV transformer banks that step down transmission voltage to distribution levels. These transformers are not stock items. They are custom-engineered, built to order, with manufacturing lead times of 12 to 24 months under normal conditions and significantly longer when dozens or hundreds of units are damaged simultaneously across a continent. Hospital emergency generators would operate until their on-site fuel is exhausted — typically 48 to 96 hours. After that, hospitals would be unable to provide anything beyond basic first aid. Patients on ventilators, dialysis machines, infusion pumps, and cardiac monitors — the very patients that hospitals exist to serve — would be at immediate risk. The evacuation challenge would be unprecedented: you cannot transfer ICU patients to the hospital across town because the hospital across town is in the same situation.

Our SHIELD framework — Strategic Hardening Integration for Extended-Lead-time Defense — is designed to provide AHA and its member hospitals with a systematic approach to GMD resilience. The key elements that are directly relevant to hospital system-wide planning include:

1. **Extended fuel autonomy:** Moving from the current 48-96 hour standard to a minimum of three weeks of on-site diesel storage, with pre-negotiated priority resupply contracts.
2. **Regional mobile generator cooperatives:** Establishing pre-positioned, shared mobile generator assets across hospital systems within a region, with standardized connection interfaces and deployment protocols.
3. **Critical care triage and transfer protocols:** Developing GMD-specific patient management protocols that identify which patients can be safely discharged, which can be transferred to facilities with extended fuel autonomy, and which require continuous power for survival.
4. **Supply chain resilience for extended outages:** Ensuring that pharmaceutical cold chains, medical gas supplies, and sterile processing capabilities have contingency plans that extend to the multi-month timeframe.
