Jason Brodsky at Aegis Cipher Space, reaching out from 221B Baker Street — because emergency physicians, like detectives, deal in differential diagnoses: systematically considering the possible causes of a presenting condition, eliminating those that do not fit the evidence, and acting on the diagnosis that remains. When we apply that same systematic approach to diagnosing the vulnerabilities in America's emergency medical infrastructure, GMD emerges as a condition that the healthcare system has not yet diagnosed — and undiagnosed conditions, as every emergency physician knows, are the ones that kill patients.

The American College of Emergency Physicians represents the clinical leaders who will be on the front lines of any disaster response. ACEP's members run the emergency departments that serve as the entry point for disaster victims, the triage centers that sort the injured from the merely displaced, and the critical care safety net that catches patients when the rest of the healthcare system is overwhelmed. ACEP has developed excellent guidance for mass casualty incidents, chemical and biological exposures, and natural disaster response. But the clinical leadership for extended-outage scenarios — events where the hospital itself becomes resource-constrained not for hours but for weeks or months — has not been fully developed. A GMD event is precisely that scenario.

Consider the clinical reality of a hospital operating on generator power that will be exhausted in 72 hours, with no prospect of grid restoration for months. The emergency department becomes the de facto decision center for a cascade of impossible choices. Which ventilator patients can be manually bagged, and for how long? At what point does a dialysis-dependent patient become triaged to palliative care because dialysis capacity has been exhausted? How do you maintain cold chain integrity for insulin, for blood products, for vaccines, when refrigeration fails and resupply is impossible? How do you manage a trauma patient who arrives after a car accident — because accidents do not stop during disasters — when your CT scanner has no power and your operating rooms are dark? These are not theoretical questions. They are clinical decisions that emergency physicians would face, and for which current disaster medicine protocols provide inadequate preparation because they assume that definitive care capability returns when the immediate event passes. For a GMD event, it does not.

Our SHIELD framework — Strategic Hardening Integration for Extended-Lead-time Defense — includes a clinical planning dimension that I believe ACEP is uniquely qualified to lead:

1. **Extended outage clinical practice guidelines:** Developing evidence-based protocols for managing critical care patients when diagnostic and therapeutic capabilities are progressively degraded over an extended timeline.
2. **Resource triage ethics framework:** Establishing ethical decision-making frameworks for resource allocation in extended-outage scenarios, grounded in the principles of emergency medicine ethics that ACEP has articulated.
3. **Emergency department power autonomy:** Advocating for dedicated, extended-duration emergency department power systems that can maintain core ED functions — triage, stabilization, basic diagnostics — even when the main hospital generator fuel is exhausted.
4. **Regional patient distribution protocols:** Developing inter-facility transfer and patient distribution protocols for extended-outage scenarios that account for differential resource availability across a region.
