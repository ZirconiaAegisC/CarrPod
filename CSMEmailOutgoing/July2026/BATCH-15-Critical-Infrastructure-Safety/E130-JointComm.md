Jason Brodsky at Aegis Cipher Space, writing from 221B Baker Street — an address that understands the critical importance of standards and the equally critical importance of regularly re-examining whether those standards remain adequate for the threat environment they are meant to address. That is the question I am respectfully posing to the Joint Commission today with respect to hospital emergency power requirements.

The Joint Commission's emergency management standards (EM.02.02.01 through EM.02.02.09 for hospitals) require accredited facilities to conduct hazard vulnerability analyses and to develop emergency operations plans that address the identified hazards. These standards have driven significant improvements in hospital preparedness for the hazards that are familiar and well-understood: hurricanes, earthquakes, active shooter incidents, infectious disease outbreaks. But I have reviewed the current Joint Commission standards, the associated elements of performance, and the survey process guidance, and I find no explicit consideration of hazards whose impact duration is measured in months rather than hours or days — and that is precisely the threat profile of a major geomagnetic disturbance.

Consider what a Carrington-class GMD event would mean for a Joint Commission-accredited hospital. The facility's on-site emergency generators would start and operate normally — the generators themselves are unaffected by GIC. The hospital would meet the Joint Commission's requirement for emergency power availability. For approximately 48 to 96 hours, depending on fuel storage. After that, the hospital would be out of compliance with every standard that depends on electrical power: life support systems cease, refrigeration for medications and blood products fails, electronic health records become inaccessible, laboratory diagnostics stop, imaging equipment goes dark, and the facility transitions from a hospital to a holding area. The patients who can be evacuated will be — but to where? The neighboring hospitals are in the same situation. The grid transformer that serves the facility is damaged, and its replacement is 18 months out. The Joint Commission's standards, as currently written, do not account for this scenario. They assume that grid power returns within the fuel endurance window. For a GMD event, that assumption fails.

Our SHIELD framework — Strategic Hardening Integration for Extended-Lead-time Defense — proposes that the Joint Commission consider three specific enhancements to its emergency management standards:

1. **Extended-duration hazard vulnerability assessment:** Require accredited hospitals to specifically evaluate hazards whose impact duration exceeds 30 days, including GMD, and to document the facility's sustainability plan beyond generator fuel exhaustion.
2. **Regional coordination requirement:** Incorporate into the emergency operations plan standard a requirement for documented mutual aid agreements that address extended grid-down scenarios — not just patient evacuation, but coordinated mobile generator and transformer sharing across hospital systems.
3. **Fuel supply continuity:** Require that emergency operations plans address the logistics of fuel resupply during region-wide, extended-duration grid outages, including pre-negotiated priority supply contracts with fuel distributors.
