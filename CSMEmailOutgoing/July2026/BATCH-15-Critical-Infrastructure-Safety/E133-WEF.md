WEF represents the full spectrum of water quality professionals — the wastewater treatment plant operators, the collection system engineers, the stormwater managers, the laboratory scientists, and the regulatory compliance specialists who ensure that what goes down the drain does not come back up as a public health crisis. Your members operate treatment plants that process hundreds of millions of gallons per day, pumping stations distributed across thousands of miles of collection systems, and advanced treatment processes — membrane bioreactors, ultraviolet disinfection, anaerobic digestion — that are entirely dependent on continuous electrical power. When that power is interrupted for an extended period, the consequences cascade rapidly: untreated sewage overflows into waterways, combined sewer systems discharge into rivers and lakes, drinking water sources become contaminated, and the public health achievements of a century of wastewater treatment are reversed in a matter of days.

The GMD threat to wastewater infrastructure is particularly acute because wastewater systems combine three characteristics that amplify vulnerability: (1) they are electrically intensive, with large pump motors, blowers for aeration basins, and increasingly sophisticated control systems that cannot operate without power; (2) they are geographically distributed, with collection systems that follow gravity flow paths across entire watersheds, meaning that pumping stations are scattered across the very long-conductor geometries that maximize GIC induction; and (3) they have limited on-site backup power, with most wastewater treatment plants having either no backup generation or generator capacity sufficient only for essential processes and for limited duration. A major GMD event that disables grid transformers across a region would simultaneously take multiple wastewater facilities offline, and the public health and environmental consequences would mount with each passing day.

Our SHIELD framework — Strategic Hardening Integration for Extended-Lead-time Defense — includes wastewater-specific applications that I believe WEF's membership will find directly relevant:

1. **Treatment plant power resilience:** Prioritizing GIC hardening for the transformers serving major wastewater treatment plants, and establishing extended-duration backup power capability (minimum three weeks) for facilities that discharge to sensitive receiving waters.
2. **Collection system continuity:** Identifying the critical pumping stations in the collection system whose failure would cause sanitary sewer overflows, and ensuring those stations have either GIC-hardened grid connections or extended-duration backup power.
3. **Biosolids management during extended outages:** Developing operational protocols for managing the biological treatment processes during extended power interruptions, including controlled shutdown and restart procedures for activated sludge systems and anaerobic digesters.
4. **Laboratory and monitoring resilience:** Ensuring that water quality monitoring capability can be maintained or rapidly restored following an extended outage, so that public health decisions about water use can be based on data rather than assumptions.
