**4. SHIELD Posture**

Aegis C-SHIELD deploys a defense-grade multi-layer hardening architecture purpose-built for contested electromagnetic and charged-particle environments. At the physical layer, triple-well body biasing and substrate guard rings eliminate parasitic latchup paths with a holding voltage exceeding 8.5V. At the materials-sourcing layer, every element in the C-SHIELD materials stack — silicon, titanium, aluminum, carbon, and oxygen — is sourced from domestic suppliers or allied-nation sources (Australia, Canada), with zero content from nations designated as Foreign Entities of Concern under the Inflation Reduction Act Section 45X and the DOE Critical Materials Assessment. At the logical layer, our proprietary Dual-Interlocked-Storage-Cell (DICE) latch design achieves a single-event upset cross-section below 1×10⁻¹⁰ cm²/bit at LET of 40 MeV·cm²/mg with zero multi-cell upset propagation across word-line or bit-line neighbors. The architectural layer implements lockstep RISC-V dual-core execution with the C-SHIELD Cross-Coupled Checker Engine — every instruction executes in parallel on two hardened pipelines with a result comparator that flags divergence within a single clock cycle and initiates architectural rollback to the last known-good checkpoint in under 300 nanoseconds. At the system layer, a hardened supervisory microcontroller monitors all voltage rails, clock sources, and thermal sensors. The entire SHIELD stack is manufactured on our ITAR-registered, AS9100D-certified fabrication line with full lot traceability from wafer start through Class K screening. Every C-SHIELD device ships with a Certificate of Conformance documenting measured TID, SEE, ELDRS, and a Critical Materials Content Declaration listing every element in the bill of materials by weight percent and country of origin.

**9. NREL-Specific Personalization**

NREL's Materials Science Center and Critical Materials Group have produced the definitive body of work on critical mineral supply-chain risk in the United States renewable energy sector. The DOE Critical Materials Assessment, which NREL authors in partnership with the Advanced Manufacturing Office, identifies neodymium, dysprosium, praseodymium, cobalt, and lithium as the highest-risk materials in the clean-energy transition — but tantalum and niobium, the backbone elements of every conventional high-voltage, high-temperature capacitor, consistently appear in the "near-critical" tier with supply concentration ratios exceeding 85% in a single country (the Democratic Republic of Congo for tantalum, Brazil for niobium). Our MXene capacitor technology eliminates these dependencies entirely. The titanium, aluminum, and carbon precursors are sourced domestically; the MAX-phase synthesis and MXene exfoliation are performed at Aegis's Fremont, California facility; and the finished capacitors are assembled, tested, and qualified on-shore. We propose a collaborative research project under NREL's Technology Transfer framework — potentially funded through the DOE Advanced Manufacturing Office's Critical Materials Innovation Hub or the Bipartisan Infrastructure Law's Battery Materials Processing and Manufacturing grants — to conduct a comprehensive, independent techno-economic assessment and supply-chain resilience analysis of the MXene-to-capacitor value chain. NREL's Materials Flow Through Industry (MFTI) modeling tool, its Supply Chain Resilience Analysis (SCRA) framework, and its Renewable Energy Materials Properties Database (REMPD) provide the ideal analytical infrastructure for this assessment. The deliverable would be a joint NREL-Aegis technical report quantifying the critical-materials displacement potential of MXene capacitor technology across the grid resilience, defense electronics, and electric vehicle charging infrastructure sectors — a report that would directly support DOE investment decisions, Defense Production Act Title III determinations, and IRA Section 48C qualifying advanced energy project credit applications.
