In an EMP event, the threat vectors are: (1) conducted transients on the high-voltage DC bus coupling from the traction battery cables (which are, functionally, multi-meter-long antennas connected directly to the inverter's DC-link capacitors and IGBT modules); (2) radiated E-field coupling into the gate-drive circuits (15V gate-emitter threshold, gate-oxide breakdown at 20-30V — a 50 kV/m E1 pulse induces far more than that into unshielded gate-drive PCB traces); (3) CAN bus transceivers failing from common-mode voltage transients exceeding the ±12V to ±40V common-mode range, causing loss of communication between VCU, inverter, BMS, and charger; (4) resolver excitation and feedback signal disruption from conducted and radiated transients, causing the inverter to lose rotor position and trigger an overcurrent fault — which at 500A DC bus current may be a destructive fault, not just a limp-mode event.

The hardening solutions we have built: (a) MXene EMI films at 45 μm thickness applied as conformal coating to inverter control board, BMS cell-monitoring boards, DC-DC converter control board, and VCU circuit boards — providing 20-40 dB of additional protection against radiated and conducted transients, with negligible thermal impact (MXene thermal conductivity is approximately 50 W/mK, comparable to alumina); (b) Aegis-C composite shielding at SE 148-165 dB installed as enclosure-level shielding around the inverter housing, the BMS enclosure, and the VCU enclosure — consisting of panels that can be cut to size with standard tools and mounted with adhesive standoffs; (c) Basalt-fiber composite battery-box enclosures — replacing aluminum and steel enclosures that act as GIC coupling surfaces — providing inherently dielectric structural protection for the traction battery while maintaining equivalent mechanical protection and thermal management capability; (d) Ferrite common-mode chokes on the CAN bus, DC bus voltage-sense wiring, resolver signal cable, and all sensor leads entering the power-electronics enclosures — attenuating conducted transients at the point of entry before they reach semiconductor junctions.

All materials tested against MIL-STD-461G RS103 (radiated susceptibility) and CS114/CS115/CS116 (conducted susceptibility). **[PARAGRAPH 4 — KEY FACT 1: The Traction Inverter — the Most Vulnerable and Most Critical Component]**

The traction inverter is simultaneously the most EMP-vulnerable and most safety-critical component in an EV conversion. It switches 300-400V DC at hundreds of amps, controls the motor torque that moves the vehicle, and contains power-semiconductor modules (IGBT or SiC MOSFET) whose gate-drive circuits are directly exposed to any voltage transient on the DC bus. The typical IGBT gate-drive circuit operates with a positive gate voltage of +15V (turn-on) and negative gate voltage of -8V to -15V (turn-off), with gate-oxide breakdown occurring at approximately ±20-30V. A conducted transient on the DC bus — from EMP coupling into the traction battery cables — can appear at the IGBT collector and capacitively couple across the gate-collector capacitance (Miller capacitance) directly into the gate-drive circuit. If the gate voltage exceeds the breakdown threshold, the IGBT fails short-circuit — and a short-circuited IGBT in a 400V, 500A traction inverter draws fault current limited only by the battery's internal resistance, which for a lithium-ion pack is typically 1-5 milliohms. The result is thermal runaway at the IGBT module level within milliseconds — and potentially at the battery-cell level if the DC contactor fails to open. This is the worst-case failure mode for an EV powertrain, and it is triggered by exactly the kind of transient that an EMP event produces. MXene conformal coating on the gate-drive circuit board, combined with a ferrite common-mode choke on the gate-drive signal path (between the inverter control board and the IGBT gate terminals), provides the protection layer that prevents gate-oxide breakdown from conducted transients. The choke is specified to present high impedance (greater than 500 Ω) at the typical EMP transient frequency range (100 kHz to 100 MHz) while passing the gate-drive switching waveform (typically 5-20 kHz for IGBT, 50-100 kHz for SiC) with negligible attenuation. Aegis-C shielding around the inverter enclosure provides the additional layer of radiated-field protection — 148-165 dB of attenuation, reducing a 50 kV/m E1 field to millivolts per meter at the inverter control electronics. Both protection layers are documented with component specifications, wiring diagrams, and installation photographs in the CSMGeneralOutreach compendium.

**[PARAGRAPH 5 — KEY FACT 2: The BMS — Distributed, Daisy-Chained, and Highly Vulnerable]**

The BMS in a typical EV conversion consists of 8-12 cell-monitoring boards (each monitoring 8-12 series cells via LTC6811 or similar ICs), connected in a daisy-chain configuration using the isoSPI protocol (transformer-isolated differential signaling at 1 Mbps over twisted-pair wiring). The isoSPI isolation transformers provide galvanic isolation between each cell-monitoring board and the central BMS controller — but that isolation is rated for 1,500-5,000 VDC steady-state, which is not adequate for the nanosecond-rise-time transients (dV/dt in excess of 10^10 V/s) characteristic of an E1 EMP pulse. The distributed nature of the BMS — with cell-monitoring boards located at different physical positions within the battery enclosure, connected by wiring that runs the full length of the pack — makes it particularly vulnerable to differential-mode transients between boards at different potentials during the EMP event. A single failed cell-monitoring board takes the entire BMS offline — which means no cell-voltage monitoring, no temperature monitoring, no state-of-charge estimation, and no ability to open the DC contactors in a fault condition. That is a battery fire waiting to happen. The hardening response: MXene conformal coating on each cell-monitoring board; ferrite common-mode chokes on each isoSPI daisy-chain segment; and Aegis-C shielding around the BMS master controller enclosure. For the central BMS controller, the additional hardening of a basalt-fiber composite enclosure replaces the typical aluminum project box, eliminating the enclosure itself as a coupling pathway.
