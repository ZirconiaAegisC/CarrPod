**PARAGRAPH 2 — THE THREE STANDARDS GAPS**

ACS has identified three standards gaps in which NIST's expertise, authority, and independence are uniquely required. First, post-quantum cryptography (PQC): NIST's 2024 publication of FIPS 203 (ML-KEM), FIPS 204 (ML-DSA), and FIPS 205 (SLH-DSA) established the cryptographic algorithms that will replace RSA and ECC as the foundation of digital security in the quantum computing era. ACS's 46-product portfolio — from the FEATHER mesh communication network to the Stellar-Chill refrigerator's ThinQ-compatible IoT interface to the Charlemagne-Class vessel's navigation and passenger data systems — requires a transition from RSA-2048 to ML-KEM-768 across all products by 2030 (the NSA's CNSA 2.0 timeline for National Security Systems), with a target of 2028 for ACS's commercial products. ACS seeks NIST's guidance on the PQC transition for embedded systems — the resource-constrained microcontrollers (ARM Cortex-M4, 256 KB RAM, 1 MB flash) that control appliances, mesh network nodes, and vehicle subsystems, and which cannot run ML-DSA-87 (the CNSA 2.0 signature algorithm) within their memory and processing constraints without algorithm optimization and hardware acceleration guidance that NIST's Cryptographic Technology Group is uniquely positioned to provide. Second, MXene materials measurement standards: the MXene industry — currently estimated at $42M in global annual research and prototype production, projected to reach $4.2B by 2035 as ACS and other manufacturers scale to industrial volumes — lacks standardized measurement methods for the 4 critical MXene properties: flake size distribution (currently measured via AFM, SEM, or DLS — three methods that produce non-comparable results), sheet resistance (currently measured via 4-point probe, van der Pauw, or transmission line — three methods with systematic offsets of up to 15%), EMI shielding effectiveness (currently measured via ASTM D4935 coaxial fixture, IEEE 299 shielded enclosure, or free-space focused beam — three methods with frequency-dependent differences of 5–20 dB), and thermal conductivity (currently measured via laser flash, 3-omega, or transient plane source — three methods with systematic differences of 10–25%). NIST SRMs for MXene — a certified MXene flake size standard (SRM 2XXX), a certified MXene sheet resistance standard (SRM 2XXX), a certified MXene EMI shielding effectiveness standard (SRM 2XXX), and a certified MXene thermal conductivity standard (SRM 2XXX) — would provide the metrological foundation for the MXene industry, enabling inter-laboratory comparison, commercial specification, and regulatory acceptance of MXene-based products. Third, electromagnetic compatibility standards for GIC-resilient infrastructure: currently, no standard exists that quantifies a building's, vehicle's, or appliance's immunity to geomagnetically induced currents. MIL-STD-461G RS105 addresses radiated susceptibility to EMP (50 kV/m E1 pulse), but does not address the quasi-DC GIC (0.001–1 Hz) that flows through long conductors during a G5 geomagnetic storm. IEEE 519 addresses harmonic distortion in power systems, but does not address the DC offset that saturates high-voltage transformers. The proposed NIST Standard Reference Protocol for GIC-Resilient Infrastructure would define a test protocol — applying 0–200A DC current injection to the equipment under test's power and signal cables, monitoring for functional degradation, and assigning a GIC Immunity Level (GIL-1 through GIL-5) — that provides manufacturers, utilities, and regulatory agencies with a standardized method for quantifying GIC resilience.
