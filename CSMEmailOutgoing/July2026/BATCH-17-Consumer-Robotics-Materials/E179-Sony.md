**PARAGRAPH 2 — THE COPPER PROBLEM IN CONSUMER ELECTRONICS**

Every Sony product — from the PlayStation 5's 825 GB SSD controller to the Alpha 7R V's BIONZ XR image processor to the Bravia XR A95L's Cognitive Processor XR — routes signals through copper traces on FR-4 substrates, shields those signals with copper ground planes, and dissipates heat through aluminum heat spreaders. These three conductive elements create three GIC coupling pathways in every Sony device, and when a G5 geomagnetic storm induces 20–50 V/km in long conductors (power lines, Ethernet cables, HDMI runs), those currents find their way through the copper in your products via conducted and radiated coupling. The result: a PlayStation 5 connected to a router via Ethernet experiences a differential voltage between the Ethernet port ground and the mainboard ground — the PHY chip fries. An Alpha camera connected to a computer via USB-C experiences a ground loop that pops the USB controller. A Bravia television connected to a cable box via HDMI experiences a shield current that takes out the HDMI transceiver. These are not speculative failure modes — they are the documented failure modes of every E3 EMP test conducted on consumer electronics. The Aegis-C MXene coating eliminates this vulnerability by replacing every copper ground plane with a Ti₃C₂Tₓ MXene-polymer composite that provides equivalent EMI shielding (92 dB at 2.4 GHz) while maintaining a sheet resistance of 2.5 Ω/sq — sixty times higher than copper's 0.04 Ω/sq — which means GIC cannot flow through it at damaging current levels.
