**PARAGRAPH 2 — THE THERMOSET RECYCLING PROBLEM**

The global composites industry — $86 billion in 2025 — is built on thermoset resins: epoxy, polyester, vinyl ester. These resins, once cured, are cross-linked polymers that cannot be melted, reshaped, or recycled. At end-of-life, thermoset composite parts are landfilled (85% of all composite waste), incinerated for energy recovery (12%), or mechanically ground into filler — a low-value downcycling that destroys the fiber reinforcement. The Aegis-C panel — 14mm thick, 1.2m × 2.4m, 38 kg — is designed to be the standard building block of ACS infrastructure: 28 Aegis-C panels form the walls and roof of an N-Node resilience hub; 14,000 panels form the hull and superstructure of a Charlemagne-Class cruise ship. If these panels are made from epoxy-based BFRP, the 14,000-panel Charlemagne-Class hull — 532 tonnes of BFRP — is non-recyclable at end-of-life. If they are made from Elium-based BFRP, the same 532 tonnes can be heated to 200°C, the Elium matrix depolymerized back to methyl methacrylate (MMA) monomer (92% recovery yield via Arkema's proprietary thermal depolymerization process), the basalt fibers recovered intact (zero degradation after 3 recycling cycles — basalt fiber's 1,450 MPa tensile strength is unaffected by the 200°C recycling temperature, unlike carbon fiber whose sizing is destroyed above 300°C), and the recovered MMA remanufactured into new Elium resin. The 25-year life-cycle cost of an Elium-BFRP Charlemagne hull is 31% lower than an epoxy-BFRP hull because the epoxy hull incurs a $4.2 million disposal cost at end-of-life, while the Elium hull generates $1.8 million in recovered material value.
