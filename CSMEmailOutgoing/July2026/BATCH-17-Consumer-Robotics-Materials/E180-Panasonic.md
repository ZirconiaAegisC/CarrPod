**PARAGRAPH 2 — THE THERMAL INTERFACE BOTTLENECK**

Panasonic's PGS — introduced in 2008 and refined over 16 years — solved a critical problem in consumer electronics: how to spread heat from a 5 mm × 5 mm processor die across a 50 mm × 50 mm area fast enough to keep the junction temperature below the 105°C limit. PGS does this brilliantly in-plane (1,950 W/m·K — better than copper at 401 W/m·K). But PGS's through-plane thermal conductivity — the direction from the chip surface down into the heat sink — is only 5.7 W/m·K, a factor of 340× lower than its in-plane value. This anisotropy is inherent to graphite: the covalent bonds in the graphene plane conduct heat at phonon velocities near 22 km/s, but the van der Waals bonds between planes conduct heat at phonon velocities below 1 km/s. The result is a material that spreads heat beautifully across a surface but forms a thermal bottleneck between the chip and the heat sink — the exact location where thermal resistance matters most. MXene (Ti₃C₂Tₓ) solves this bottleneck with a fundamentally different phonon transport mechanism: the MAX phase precursor's layered carbide structure provides strong covalent bonding in-plane (delivering 520 W/m·K), while the MXene's surface terminations (-O, -OH, -F) provide phonon bridges between layers — delivering 45 W/m·K through-plane, a 7.9× improvement over PGS. In a 5 mm × 5 mm chip hotspot at 15W dissipation, the junction-to-case thermal resistance drops from 2.1 K/W (PGS) to 0.82 K/W (MXene TIM), allowing either 62% higher power density at the same junction temperature or 14°C lower junction temperature at the same power — extending chip life by approximately 2.5× per the Arrhenius reliability model.
