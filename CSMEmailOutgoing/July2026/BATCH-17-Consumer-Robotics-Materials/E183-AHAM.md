**PARAGRAPH 2 — THE STANDARDS GAP**

AHAM's current appliance performance standards — AHAM HRF-1 for refrigerators, AHAM MWO-1 for microwave ovens, AHAM ER-1 for electric ranges — assume that the appliance being tested operates on the same thermodynamic and electromagnetic principles that have defined home appliances since the 1920s: vapor-compression refrigeration (a compressor, a condenser, an expansion valve, and an evaporator), magnetron-based microwave cooking (a high-voltage transformer, a magnetron tube, and a waveguide), and resistive heating (a nichrome element that converts electricity to heat via Joule heating). A dielectric appliance — operating on electroocaloric cooling, solid-state RF cooking, or dielectric polymer heating — does not fit these standards, because the underlying physics are different, the efficiency metrics are different, and the failure modes are different. An electroocaloric refrigerator has no refrigerant to measure for Energy Star compliance. A solid-state microwave has no magnetron output power to measure for AHAM MWO-1. A dielectric oven has no heating element wattage to measure for AHAM ER-1. The standards do not exist because the product category does not exist — and the product category needs the standards to exist before it can achieve broad consumer and regulatory acceptance. AHAM is the organization that creates those standards. ACS is asking AHAM to create them for dielectric appliances.

**PARAGRAPH 3 — BAKER STREET PRECISE SPECS**

Baker Street, let's get precise on the three standards ACS proposes AHAM develop. Standard 1 — AHAM DR-1 (Dielectric Refrigerator Performance): test protocol measuring cooling capacity (watts of heat removed at 25°C ambient), energy factor (liters of refrigerated volume per watt of input power, analogous to AHAM HRF-1), temperature recovery time after door opening (minutes to return to 4°C after a 30-second full-door opening at 25°C ambient), and no-load power consumption at 25°C, 32°C, and 43°C ambient. The key differentiator from HRF-1: DR-1 measures electroocaloric module efficiency (COP) rather than compressor Energy Efficiency Ratio (EER), because there is no compressor. Standard 2 — AHAM DM-1 (Dielectric Microwave Performance): test protocol measuring cooking power delivery (watts of RF energy absorbed by a 1,000g water load, analogous to AHAM MWO-1 but measured via calorimetry rather than magnetron output), cooking uniformity (standard deviation of temperature across a 1,000g water load after 2 minutes of heating with beam steering enabled and disabled, measured via an 8-point thermocouple array), and standby power consumption. The key differentiator from MWO-1: DM-1 measures phased-array beam-forming performance — the ability of the solid-state emitter to direct cooking energy to specific regions of the cavity — a capability that magnetron-based microwaves do not possess and therefore no existing standard measures. Standard 3 — AHAM DO-1 (Dielectric Oven Performance): test protocol measuring heating time to 175°C from 25°C ambient, temperature uniformity across the cooking volume (±°C at 10 measurement points at 175°C setpoint), and power consumption per baking cycle (kWh to bake a standardized 1 kg loaf of white bread from room temperature to 98°C internal temperature at 175°C oven setpoint). The key differentiator from ER-1: DO-1 measures dielectric polymer heating element efficiency (joules of heat delivered to the cooking volume per joule of electrical input) rather than resistive element wattage.

ACS proposes initiating the process now, in 2026, so that when the first consumer-grade dielectric appliances reach the market in 2028, the standards are ready, the certification program is operational, and the AHAM seal on a Stellar-Chill refrigerator means something specific and verifiable to the consumer who is considering whether to pay a premium for a refrigerator that can survive a Carrington Event.

**PARAGRAPH 5 — THE PROPOSAL**

ACS proposes an AHAM-AEGIS Dielectric Appliance Standards Working Group: Phase Alpha — formation of the working group with ACS as the initiating member, open to all AHAM member manufacturers (Samsung, LG, Whirlpool, GE Appliances, Electrolux, Bosch, Sub-Zero, etc.), with ACS providing prototype hardware for round-robin testing at member laboratories, a proposed draft of AHAM DR-1, DM-1, and DO-1 for working group review, and funding for the ANSI standards development process administrative costs, $75,000 contribution from ACS to AHAM's standards development fund. Phase Bravo — 12-month standards development cycle including 4 working group meetings (2 virtual, 2 in-person at AHAM's Washington, DC, offices), round-robin testing at 3 independent laboratories (UL, Intertek, CSA Group), public comment period (60 days), ANSI canvass review, and publication, estimated $340,000 total. Phase Charlie — AHAM certification program launch for dielectric appliances, with ACS as the first manufacturer to submit products for certification, certification marks (AHAM Certified Dielectric Refrigerator, AHAM Certified Dielectric Microwave, AHAM Certified Dielectric Oven), and integration into the existing AHAM Verifide directory on aham.org, estimated $120,000 first-year certification program setup cost. ACS commits to being the first manufacturer through the door and funding the standards process to ensure it happens on a timeline compatible with product launch.
