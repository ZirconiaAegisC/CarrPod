**[PARAGRAPH 4 — KEY FACT 1: Transformer GIC vulnerability — the continuity-site supply chain problem]**

A large power transformer (LPT) is the single most vulnerable element in the power grid during a G5 geomagnetic storm. GIC enters the transformer through the grounded neutral connection, saturates the magnetic core, generates harmonic currents that cause rapid internal heating, and can destroy the transformer through thermal damage to winding insulation — a failure mode that cannot be repaired in the field and requires full replacement. The United States has approximately 2,000 LPTs, with approximately 300-400 at risk of damage or destruction during a Carrington-class event. Replacement lead time: 12-24 months, because LPTs are custom-built, not off-the-shelf. A continuity site connected to a substation whose LPT is destroyed has no grid power for up to two years — regardless of how well the continuity site itself is hardened. Backup generators can run for days. They cannot run for months. The solution is GIC blocking at the transformer level: Aegis-C shielding on transformer control electronics, neutral-blocking capacitor banks to prevent GIC coupling, and substation-level EMP hardening for protection relays and SCADA systems.
