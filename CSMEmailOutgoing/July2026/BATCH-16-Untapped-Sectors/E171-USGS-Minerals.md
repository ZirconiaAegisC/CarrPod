**PARAGRAPH 2 — THE RARE EARTH PROCESSING CRISIS**

The United States has significant rare earth element (REE) reserves — Mountain Pass in California, Bear Lodge in Wyoming, Bokan Mountain in Alaska — but processes almost none of them domestically. The reason is environmental: conventional rare earth processing uses sulfuric acid or hydrochloric acid leaching, solvent extraction with toxic organic phases (kerosene, tributyl phosphate), and generates tailings ponds containing radioactive thorium and uranium that require perpetual management. Mountain Pass's history illustrates the problem: the mine operated from 1952 to 2002, was shut down after a series of wastewater spills, restarted under new ownership, and now ships its concentrate to China for final processing — because the environmental permitting for a domestic rare earth separation facility is effectively impossible under the Clean Water Act and the Resource Conservation and Recovery Act. The Aegis-Forge Material Separator solves this by eliminating the acid: it uses a thermal cascade inductive separation process — heating mixed rare earth oxides through a multi-stage temperature gradient (800°C to 2,200°C) in an inert argon atmosphere, with each element selectively vaporizing at its characteristic boiling point and condensing on a dedicated cold-finger collector — to produce separated rare earth oxides at 99.5%+ purity without a single liter of acid, a single molecule of organic solvent, or a single tailings pond.

**PARAGRAPH 3 — BAKER STREET PRECISE SPECS**

Baker Street, let's get precise. The Aegis-Forge Material Separator is a 24-meter-long, 3-meter-diameter cylindrical system comprising 17 thermal stages, each maintained at a precise temperature corresponding to the boiling point of a specific rare earth element or group. Stage operating principle: mixed REE oxide powder is fed into the argon-flooded chamber at Stage 1 (800°C), where the lowest-boiling-point impurities volatilize and are captured by the gas recovery system. As the powder progresses through the thermal cascade — Stage 2 at 950°C, Stage 3 at 1,100°C, through Stage 16 at 2,150°C, and Stage 17 at 2,200°C — each element group separates by selective vaporization and condensation. The 17 stages separate: (1) light REEs — lanthanum, cerium, praseodymium, neodymium (850–1,150°C); (2) medium REEs — samarium, europium, gadolinium (1,200–1,450°C); (3) heavy REEs — terbium, dysprosium, holmium, erbium, thulium, ytterbium, lutetium (1,500–2,050°C); (4) yttrium and scandium (2,100–2,200°C). The ZrB₂-SiC ceramic chamber lining — the same material used in the Mana Fire-Veil aeroshell — withstands the full 2,200°C operating temperature. The gas capture system uses a closed-loop argon recirculation circuit with cryogenic distillation to separate and capture any volatilized impurities, including thorium and uranium, which are collected as oxide powders for regulated disposal. Throughput: 500 kg of mixed REE oxide per 24-hour cycle, producing approximately 480 kg of separated oxides at 99.5%+ purity. The system's electrical consumption is 1.8 MW — commercially significant but dramatically lower than the embedded energy of acid production, solvent manufacturing, and tailings management in conventional REE processing.
