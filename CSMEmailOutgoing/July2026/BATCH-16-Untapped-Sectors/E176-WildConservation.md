**PARAGRAPH 2 — THE CONSERVATION INFRASTRUCTURE PARADOX**

Wildlife conservation depends on infrastructure — roads for ranger patrols, fencing to delineate protected areas and manage human-wildlife conflict, equipment caches in fire-prone landscapes — but conventional infrastructure often damages the very ecosystems it's meant to protect. Steel fencing fragments habitat: ungulates become entangled, predators dig under, and the galvanized coating leaches zinc into soil. Asphalt patrol roads create impermeable barriers for amphibians and small mammals, generate stormwater runoff that erodes adjacent habitat, and require fuel-intensive maintenance in remote locations. Fire management in conservation areas relies on controlled burns and firebreaks — but when wildfire escapes containment (as it increasingly does in a warming climate), the conservation area's infrastructure, staff, and equipment have no hardened survival architecture. The three products presented here are designed as conservation infrastructure that functions ecologically — not infrastructure imposed on ecosystems, but infrastructure integrated into them.
