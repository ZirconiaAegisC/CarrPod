**PARAGRAPH 2 — THE PARK ROAD DILEMMA**

The National Park Service maintains 5,500 miles of paved roads and 8,400 miles of unpaved roads — infrastructure that provides visitor access but fragments habitat, creates stormwater runoff that erodes trails and contaminates streams, requires continuous maintenance in freeze-thaw and flood-prone environments, and is extraordinarily expensive to build and repair in remote locations where asphalt plants are hundreds of miles away and concrete trucks cannot reach trailhead construction sites. The Going-to-the-Sun Road in Glacier National Park required $27 million per mile for its most recent rehabilitation. The Blue Ridge Parkway's 469 miles consume $20 million annually in maintenance. Park roads are simultaneously the most essential and most environmentally destructive infrastructure in the National Park System. The Bio-Path road system addresses both dimensions: an access surface that preserves 85% of the ground's natural permeability — eliminating stormwater runoff, preserving soil microbiome connectivity, and allowing the small-animal and insect movement that asphalt and concrete roads block — while providing a 1.5 metric ton axle load rating, enough for NPS maintenance vehicles, emergency vehicles, and visitor shuttle buses.

**PARAGRAPH 3 — BAKER STREET PRECISE SPECS**

Baker Street, let's get precise for the NPS sustainable design context. The Bio-Path road tile is a 600mm × 600mm × 48mm interlocking hexagonal BFRP composite panel with a 42mm structural foam core and 3mm BFRP facesheets on both top and bottom surfaces. The hexagonal interlocking geometry — 6 attachment points per tile via cam-lock connectors — creates a continuous load-distributing surface that transfers vehicle weight laterally across adjacent tiles, preventing the rutting and edge-cracking that plague conventional modular paving systems. The 85% permeability is achieved through a pattern of 19mm diameter drainage channels through the tile thickness, spaced at 45mm centers in a triangular grid — large enough to prevent clogging from pine needles and sediment, small enough to prevent a 6mm heel from catching. The tile's open area is 32%, and the subsurface retains natural soil structure (no gravel base, no geotextile, no compaction), meaning the soil microbiome, mycorrhizal networks, and small-invertebrate habitat beneath the road remains intact and connected. Axle load rating: 1.5 metric tons — sufficient for a fully loaded NPS Ford F-550 maintenance truck, a Type I ambulance, or a 25-passenger shuttle bus. Individual tile replacement: 90 seconds — lift the damaged tile (cam-lock release), drop in a replacement, re-engage the connectors, done. Compare to asphalt: a 1m² pothole repair requires a hot patch crew, a asphalt truck, a roller, and 4 hours minimum — and cannot be performed in remote backcountry settings where the Bio-Path's hand-carried tile replacement is routine. Integrated FEATHER mesh conduits: every 5th tile (spacing of 3 meters) contains a 12mm diameter conduit for FEATHER mesh node installation, enabling the road itself to become the backbone of a park-wide wireless mesh network providing visitor safety communications, trail condition monitoring, and wildlife tracking data.

**PARAGRAPH 4 — SHIELD CALM POSTURE**

SHIELD does not panic. SHIELD does not claim that Bio-Path roads replace every paved road in the National Park System. SHIELD claims they replace the paved roads that shouldn't be paved — the trailhead access roads, the campground loops, the scenic overlook parking areas, the maintenance access tracks — where asphalt is environmentally destructive, visually intrusive, and maintenance-intensive, and where a permeable, wildlife-friendly, hand-maintainable surface better serves both the NPS mission of "conservation and enjoyment" and the practical reality of remote infrastructure maintenance. The NPS Sustainable Design Guidelines (2016) call for low-impact development, stormwater retention, and materials with low embodied energy. Bio-Path meets all three — and adds the ability to remove every tile and restore the site to its natural condition with zero permanent ground alteration, which is not possible with asphalt, concrete, or gravel.

**PARAGRAPH 5 — THE PROPOSAL**

ACS proposes an NPS-AEGIS Bio-Path pilot deployment: Phase Alpha — installation of 500 linear meters of Bio-Path road at one NPS unit representing the most challenging deployment conditions (options: Denali National Park for extreme freeze-thaw, Everglades National Park for wet/humid conditions, or Yosemite National Park for high-traffic visitor use), with NPS monitoring of permeability maintenance, freeze-thaw durability, wildlife crossing observation, and maintenance effort compared to adjacent conventional paved or unpaved sections, 12-month evaluation, $225,000 including all materials and installation. Phase Bravo — NPS Sustainable Design evaluation report with recommendations for Bio-Path specification in NPS trailhead, campground, and maintenance road applications, published through the NPS Denver Service Center, $75,000 for NPS evaluation staff time. Phase Charlie — NPS adoption of Bio-Path as an approved road surface in the NPS Standard Specifications for Construction of Roads, Trails, and Bridges, enabling individual park units to specify Bio-Path in construction contracts, with ACS as a qualified supplier.

**PARAGRAPH 7 — NPS-SPECIFIC PERSONALIZATION**

National Park Service, your agency manages the most beloved landscapes in America with a deferred maintenance backlog of $22 billion — $12 billion of which is transportation infrastructure. The Bio-Path system addresses the maintenance backlog at the root: it doesn't require annual crack sealing or pothole patching because individual tiles are replaced in 90 seconds by a single ranger with a mallet and a spare tile. It doesn't require stormwater culverts and detention basins because the road surface itself is 85% permeable. It doesn't fragment the habitat or block animal migration corridors because small animals, amphibians, and insects can pass through the drainage channels and the subsurface soil remains biologically connected. And when the road is no longer needed — when a campground is decommissioned, when a trailhead is relocated, when a scenic overlook is restored to natural condition — every Bio-Path tile can be lifted, palletized, and redeployed elsewhere, leaving zero asphalt, zero concrete, and zero permanent ground alteration. That's not just sustainable design. That's the NPS mission — conservation and enjoyment — expressed as infrastructure.
