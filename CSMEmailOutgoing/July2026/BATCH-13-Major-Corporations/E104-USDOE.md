**PARAGRAPH 2 — THE THREAT INTRODUCED**

The United States bulk electric system comprises approximately 60,000 miles of extra-high-voltage (EHV) transmission lines operating at 345 kV, 500 kV, and 765 kV, connected through roughly 2,100 major substations. The transformers at these substations — particularly the high-voltage autotransformers that provide the backbone of the Eastern, Western, and ERCOT interconnections — are the most vulnerable assets in the American grid during a geomagnetic disturbance. The U.S. Geological Survey's magnetotelluric survey has identified regions of high telluric conductivity that concentrate GIC energy: the Appalachian Piedmont, the Upper Midwest's igneous shield formations, and the Pacific Northwest's Columbia River basalts. A Carrington-class CME striking these regions would induce sufficient neutral DC current to saturate and destroy 300–400 EHV transformers simultaneously. The United States currently maintains a strategic transformer reserve of approximately 110 units, with 18–24 month manufacturing lead times for new units. The math is unforgiving. Estimated loss prevented by a national AEGIS deployment: $5 billion in transformer replacement costs, plus an estimated $2–4 trillion in economic damage from prolonged multi-state blackouts — the NIST-estimated cost of a 6-month regional grid outage.

**PARAGRAPH 3 — BAKER STREET PRECISE SPECS**

The ACS AEGIS National Grid Protection System is a standardized, utility-grade GIC neutral blocking architecture designed for deployment at every EHV substation in the NERC bulk electric system. The AEGIS-N unit is a 5.6-millifarad MXene solid-state capacitor bank rated for 500 kV and 765 kV application, providing 0.8-millisecond DC injection response at the transformer neutral-to-ground connection, interrupting up to 20 kA of neutral DC current. The MXene-silicon carbide composite capacitor material withstands 1,800°C thermal loading, maintains 99.97% capacitive retention over a 30-year service life, and requires zero external cooling — enabling installation in existing substation control houses without HVAC modifications. Each AEGIS-N unit integrates with existing utility protection relays (SEL, GE Multilin, ABB) via IEC 61850 GOOSE messaging and reports GIC telemetry to both the utility SCADA system and the NERC Situational Awareness for FERC, NERC and the Regional Entities (SAFNR) platform. The system is manufactured in California at ACS's Fremont facility, with all components sourced from domestic suppliers in compliance with Build America, Buy America Act requirements applicable to IIJA-funded projects. **PARAGRAPH 5 — THE PROPOSAL**

Phase I (Years 1–2): High-Risk Region Priority Deployment — AEGIS-N installation at 500 substations on USGS-identified high-GIC-risk geology (Appalachian Piedmont, Upper Midwest, Columbia Plateau), covering the Eastern Interconnection's critical 500 kV backbone, $1.2 billion. Phase II (Years 3–5): System-Wide EHV Coverage — AEGIS-N deployment at all remaining 345 kV, 500 kV, and 765 kV substations in the continental United States, covering all three interconnections, $2.4 billion. Phase III (Years 6–8): Regional Coverage Extension — AEGIS-Compact deployment at critical 230 kV and 115 kV substations serving designated critical infrastructure (military bases, hospitals, emergency operations centers, major urban load centers), $1.1 billion. Phase IV (Years 9–10): Continuous monitoring, capacitor refresh cycle, GIC protocol development, and training for utility operations personnel through the National Renewable Energy Laboratory (NREL) and the DOE National Laboratories, $300 million.
