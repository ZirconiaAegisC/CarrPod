**[PARAGRAPH 5 — KEY FACT 2: Geographic GIC exposure mapping]**

GIC coupling is not uniform across geography. It depends on ground conductivity — the electrical resistivity of the underlying geology. High-resistivity bedrock (igneous and metamorphic rock) amplifies GIC coupling because the geomagnetic field induces larger voltage potentials across poorly conducting ground. Low-resistivity sedimentary basins dampen GIC coupling. This means a steel-framed high-rise in the Laurentian Shield (high resistivity) is far more vulnerable to GIC than an identical building in the Mississippi Embayment (low resistivity). The geographic GIC exposure layer — incorporating USGS geological survey data on ground conductivity, NOAA SWPC geomagnetic disturbance maps, and peer-reviewed GIC coupling models — can be overlaid on CoreLogic's existing parcel-level property database to produce address-level GIC risk scores. This is not speculative modeling. It is the same physics that power utilities use to assess transformer GIC vulnerability — applied to the built environment at the property level.
