**[PARAGRAPH 2 — BAKER STREET ADJUSTER]**

The electromagnetic shielding problem is fundamentally a geometry problem combined with a materials problem. At the physics level, shielding effectiveness (SE) in decibels is determined by three mechanisms: reflection loss (R), absorption loss (A), and multiple-reflection correction (B) — SE = R + A + B. The geometry of the shielding enclosure — its shape, seam design, aperture placement, and ventilation-hole pattern — determines how effectively the enclosure prevents electromagnetic energy from reaching the protected electronics inside. The materials — Aegis-C composites (SE 148-165 dB across 1 kHz to 10 GHz), MXene films (20-40 dB at 45 μm), conductive filament for 3D printing (SE 20-50 dB depending on fill percentage and geometry), and basalt-fiber composites (inherently dielectric, for non-conductive structural components) — determine the upper bound of what the geometry can achieve. For the Maker community, the challenge is to design and fabricate shielding enclosures — using 3D printers, laser cutters, CNC routers, and hand tools — that achieve the maximum shielding effectiveness at the minimum cost, weight, and complexity. We propose three specific challenge categories: (1) 3D-Printed Lattice Shielding — design a 3D-printable lattice structure, using conductive filament, that achieves SE >30 dB at 1 GHz while maintaining >50% open area for ventilation; (2) DIY Electronics Enclosure — design and fabricate a completely DIY-built electronics enclosure using MXene spray coating on any substrate material, achieving SE >20 dB from 1 kHz to 6 GHz, with a bill of materials under $50; (3) Aegis-C Composite Integration — design an enclosure that integrates Aegis-C composite panels into a consumer-electronics-sized form factor, with documented assembly instructions and a materials cost estimate for volume production. Winners determined by measured SE in a calibrated TEM cell at the Maker Faire event.

**[PARAGRAPH 4 — KEY FACT 1: Conductive 3D-Printing Filament and Lattice Optimization]**

Conductive PLA and PETG filaments — typically carbon-black or graphene-infused — provide volume resistivity in the range of 0.5-15 Ω·cm, which is sufficient for EMI shielding at frequencies above approximately 100 MHz. The key variables for 3D-printed shielding are: infill percentage (higher = better conductivity = better SE, but heavier and slower to print), wall thickness (the absorption-loss term is directly proportional to material thickness), and lattice geometry (a gyroid infill pattern provides superior SE-to-weight ratio compared to rectilinear or honeycomb because its continuous-surface topology minimizes seam leakage). The challenge for Maker Faire entrants would be to optimize these variables — infill pattern, infill percentage, wall count, layer height, print orientation — to maximize SE at minimum weight and print time. We will provide a baseline dataset: measured SE for gyroid, rectilinear, and honeycomb infill patterns at 20%, 50%, and 100% infill, for three different conductive filaments. Entrants improve on the baseline. The best designs are published open-source, with print profiles for PrusaSlicer, Cura, and Bambu Studio.

**[PARAGRAPH 5 — KEY FACT 2: MXene Spray Coating — the Maker-Friendly Nanomaterial]**

MXene (Ti₃C₂Tₓ) is stored as a colloidal suspension in water — it looks like black ink, can be applied with an airbrush or HVLP spray gun, and dries at room temperature in approximately 10 minutes. The application process requires no cleanroom, no vacuum chamber, no high-temperature curing — just a well-ventilated workspace and standard personal protective equipment (nitrile gloves, safety glasses, N95 respirator). For the maker community, MXene is the EMI-shielding equivalent of spray paint: mask off the areas you don't want coated, spray a thin even layer, let it dry, and you have added 20-40 dB of shielding to whatever surface you sprayed. It adheres to PLA, ABS, PETG, resin prints, fiberglass, wood, cardboard, and metal. It is flexible after drying — it won't crack when the substrate flexes. For the Maker Faire challenge, MXene spray coating enables category 2 (DIY electronics enclosure) — entrants choose a substrate material, design an enclosure, spray-coat it with MXene, and test the result. The material is provided as part of the challenge kit. The only limit is the entrant's imagination.
