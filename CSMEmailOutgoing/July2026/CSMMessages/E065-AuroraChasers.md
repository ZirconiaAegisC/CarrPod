**[PARAGRAPH 2 — BAKER STREET ADJUSTER]**

The aurora is the visible manifestation of a geomagnetic storm: energetic electrons and protons from the solar wind, guided by Earth's magnetic field lines, collide with atmospheric oxygen (producing green at 100-250 km altitude and red above 250 km) and nitrogen (producing blue and purple at lower altitudes). The Kp index — the planetary geomagnetic activity index on a scale of 0 to 9 — quantifies the intensity of the disturbance: Kp1-3 is quiet, Kp4 is unsettled, Kp5 is a G1 minor storm, Kp6 is G2 moderate, Kp7 is G3 strong, Kp8 is G4 severe, and Kp9 is G5 extreme. The May 2024 Gannon Storm reached Kp9 — the first time since 2003 — and produced aurora visible as far south as Florida, Mexico, and Cuba. For the aurora-chasing community, a Kp8 or Kp9 event is the holy grail — the event you spend years waiting for, the event that produces photographs you will keep for a lifetime. For the power grid, a Kp8 or Kp9 event is a threat — geomagnetically induced currents saturate transformer cores, generate harmonics, cause reactive power loss, and, in a Carrington-class event, cause permanent thermal damage with 12-month replacement lead times. The same event that produces the most beautiful aurora you will ever photograph is the event that could collapse the electrical infrastructure of an entire continent. The aurora chasers see the storm. The grid operators manage the consequences. And now — with the Schumann Resonance monitoring network and the LoRa mesh and the Aegis-C shielding and the MXene films — we can connect the observation to the protection.

**[PARAGRAPH 4 — KEY FACT 1: The Aurora Chaser as Citizen Scientist — Real-Time Ground Truth]**

The aurora-chasing community already functions as a distributed ground-truth network. When a CME arrives, NOAA SWPC issues a G-scale watch based on DSCOVR and ACE satellite data — but the satellite data only tells you what the solar wind conditions are at L1. It does not tell you where the aurora is actually visible at ground level, what color it is, how bright it is, or how rapidly it is changing — all of which are indicators of the storm's intensity and structure. Aurora chasers, posting real-time photographs and reports to Twitter, Facebook groups, SpaceWeatherLive, and aurora-alert apps, provide that ground truth. During the May 2024 Gannon Storm, aurora chasers in Florida, Cuba, Mexico, and the Bahamas provided the first confirmation that the storm had reached historically low latitudes — confirmation that arrived before the official scientific data was processed. That is citizen science operating at its highest level. We propose formalizing this capability: an Aurora Chaser Data Network that provides standardized, timestamped, geotagged aurora observations — photograph, location, time, estimated Kp-equivalent based on visual observation criteria, and any equipment or infrastructure anomalies noted (power flickers, GPS glitches, radio interference) — to a database that NOAA SWPC and grid operators can access in real time. The LoRa mesh nodes provide the communications backhaul for observations in areas without cellular coverage. The Schumann Resonance monitoring stations provide the quantitative electromagnetic data that complements the visual observations.

**[PARAGRAPH 5 — KEY FACT 2: The Kp Index and GIC Correlation — What Aurora Chasers Already Know]**

Experienced aurora chasers can estimate the Kp index by visual observation: at Kp3-4, the aurora is a faint green glow on the northern horizon at mid-latitudes; at Kp5-6, distinct green arcs and rays are visible overhead at typical aurora-viewing latitudes; at Kp7, red and purple colors appear and the aurora extends significantly southward; at Kp8, the aurora fills half the sky with rapid motion and vivid color; at Kp9, the entire sky is alight, the aurora reaches tropical latitudes, and — critically — the geomagnetically induced currents at the Earth's surface reach levels that threaten power-grid stability. The correlation between visual aurora intensity and GIC magnitude is not perfect — GIC depends on local ground conductivity, power-line orientation, and transformer design, not just storm intensity — but it is strong enough that aurora chaser reports of "Kp8+ aurora visible from latitude 30N" are a valid, actionable indicator that GIC threat levels are high. We want to quantify this correlation and make it operationally useful. The Aurora Chaser Data Network, combined with the Schumann Resonance monitoring stations, would provide the data needed to refine the GIC-prediction models that grid operators use to decide when to implement mitigation protocols.

**[PARAGRAPH 6 — KEY FACT 3: The Schumann Resonance Station — a $2,000 Aurora Chaser Science Upgrade]**

The Schumann Resonance monitoring station is designed to be deployed and operated by citizen scientists — including aurora chasers — with basic electronics skills. The station consists of: (1) a magnetic-loop antenna (1 meter diameter, 100 turns of enameled copper wire, mounted on a PVC frame); (2) a low-noise preamplifier (INA849 instrumentation amplifier, gain 1000, 0.1 Hz to 100 Hz bandwidth); (3) a 24-bit ADC (ADS1256, 30 kSPS sampling rate); (4) a Raspberry Pi 4 for data acquisition, FFT processing, and network connectivity; and (5) a solar-charged battery power system for field deployment. Total component cost: approximately $2,000. Assembly time: approximately 8 hours for a person with basic soldering and electronics skills. The station measures the magnetic field in the ELF band (3-100 Hz), detects the Schumann Resonance frequencies and amplitudes, and transmits the data via the LoRa mesh or internet to a central database. During a CME impact, the station detects the frequency shifts and amplitude changes that provide 4-6 hours of advance warning before the peak of the geomagnetic storm. For an aurora chaser, this station provides two things: (1) a quantitative measure of the electromagnetic environment during the aurora they are photographing, adding scientific depth to their observations; and (2) the knowledge that their station is contributing directly to an early-warning network that protects the power grid from the same storm that is producing the aurora they are photographing.
