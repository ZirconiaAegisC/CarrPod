## THREAT PROFILE: MIDDLE EAST

### Solar Storm Impact on Infrastructure

The Middle East is conventionally considered "low geomagnetic latitude" and therefore "low GIC risk" — a comfortable assumption that is dangerously outdated. Three factors have converged to revise this assessment: (1) the May 2024 Gannon Storm produced measurable GIC effects at latitudes as low as 20°N, encompassing the entire Arabian Peninsula, Levant, and Mesopotamia; (2) the region's massive investment in ultra-long-distance HVDC and HVAC transmission corridors — particularly the GCC Interconnection Authority grid, Saudi Arabia's 380 kV backbone, and the Iran-Iraq-Turkey interconnectors — has created kilometer-scale conductor networks that act as efficient GIC antennas regardless of latitude; and (3) the region's simultaneous concentration of desalination plants, submarine cable landing stations, and oil/gas SCADA systems creates a cascading critical-infrastructure vulnerability profile unlike any other region on Earth.

### Grid Vulnerability

The GCC Interconnection Authority grid — linking Saudi Arabia, UAE, Qatar, Bahrain, Kuwait, and Oman — is one of the most ambitious grid integration projects in the world. Its 400 kV backbone running along the Arabian Gulf coast is a multi-thousand-kilometer conductor network that, during a G5 storm, would experience induced currents simultaneously at multiple nodes. Saudi Arabia's 380 kV national grid spans 13 of the country's 14 provinces, with long east-west and north-south transmission corridors crossing varied terrain types. The Kingdom's NEOM and Red Sea Project developments represent massive new grid construction in areas where ground resistivity (desert igneous formations) may amplify GIC coupling. Iran's national grid, while less interconnected with the GCC, has its own vulnerability in the north-south corridors connecting Caspian-region generation to southern load centers. Iraq's grid, still recovering from decades of conflict damage, operates with transformers already stressed beyond design parameters — GIC-induced half-cycle saturation on an already degraded transformer is a failure, not a margin exceedance. Israel's grid is compact, modern, and well-instrumented, but its interconnection with Jordan and potentially the wider Eastern Mediterranean grid creates a cascade pathway. Lebanon's grid is in a state of near-collapse under normal conditions; a GMD event would likely be the terminal stressor for equipment already operating beyond its service life. Syria and Yemen have grid infrastructure severely damaged by conflict — the question is not whether transformers would survive a G5 event, but whether any transformers remain to be affected.

### Maritime Dependency

This is the region's most acute infrastructure vulnerability. The Strait of Hormuz — through which approximately 21% of global petroleum liquids consumption transits — depends on GPS/GNSS for vessel navigation, collision avoidance, and port operations at Fujairah, Jebel Ali, Ras Tanura, and other critical energy terminals. GNSS disruption during a G5 storm would halt tanker traffic through Hormuz within hours. The Bab el-Mandeb and Suez Canal represent additional chokepoints. Simultaneously, every major city on the Arabian Gulf coast depends on desalination for 90-100% of its potable water. Desalination plants require continuous power. A GMD-induced grid disturbance that forces desalination plants offline — even temporarily — creates a water crisis measured in hours, not days. The region's concentration of submarine cable landing stations (Fujairah, Jeddah, Alexandria, Haifa, Doha, Muscat) forms the internet backbone for the entire Middle East, East Africa, and South Asia. Grid power loss at these landing stations during a G5 event would sever an enormous fraction of global intercontinental internet traffic.

### Specific Regional Considerations

- **Desert ground resistivity**: Arid desert terrain in Saudi Arabia, UAE, Oman, and Jordan exhibits high ground resistivity, which increases the magnitude of telluric electric fields during geomagnetic disturbances — the same effect observed in the Canadian Shield. Desert terrain amplifies GIC risk, it does not reduce it.
- **Oil and gas SCADA exposure**: The region's oil and gas infrastructure — from wellhead to export terminal — operates on SCADA systems linked by long-distance copper communication cables and satellite telemetry. Both are susceptible to interference during a GMD event. Pipeline cathodic protection systems, in particular, are directly affected by telluric currents.
- **Conflict-damaged infrastructure overlay**: Syria, Iraq, Yemen, and Lebanon have grid infrastructure already operating below design specifications due to conflict damage. A GMD event in these countries would compound infrastructure failure with humanitarian crisis.
- **Water-electricity interdependence**: The desalination-grid dependency loop means that any grid disturbance is simultaneously a water security event. This is unique to the Gulf states and represents a national security vulnerability that conventional grid hardening frameworks — designed for North America and Europe — do not adequately model.
