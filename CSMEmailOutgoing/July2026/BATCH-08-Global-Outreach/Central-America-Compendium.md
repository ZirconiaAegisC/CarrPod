## Regional Threat Profile: Central America

### Solar Storm Impact on Infrastructure

Central America (7°N–18°N) lies at **low geomagnetic latitudes** where the direct threat from GICs induced by the auroral electrojet is minimal under moderate storm conditions. However, Central America faces a **distinct and under-appreciated space-weather threat profile** centred on equatorial ionospheric phenomena and indirect grid vulnerability:

- **Equatorial electrojet (EEJ)**: Central America lies directly beneath the equatorial electrojet — an intense eastward current flowing in the ionospheric E-region at ~105 km altitude near the magnetic equator. During geomagnetic storms, the EEJ intensifies dramatically and can produce "equatorial GICs" that, while lower in magnitude than auroral-zone GICs, affect long transmission corridors in a region with no history of designing for GIC tolerance.
- **Ionospheric scintillation**: The equatorial anomaly region produces severe ionospheric scintillation (rapid fluctuations in GNSS signal amplitude and phase) that routinely disrupts GPS, Galileo, and GLONASS. Central America — particularly the Pacific coastal zones — experiences some of the most intense scintillation environments on Earth.
- **SIEPAC interconnection vulnerability**: The Central American Electrical Interconnection System (SIEPAC) is a 1,790 km, 230 kV transmission line connecting Guatemala → El Salvador → Honduras → Nicaragua → Costa Rica → Panama. It is the region's single most critical infrastructure asset. A GIC event affecting SIEPAC would cascade across six national grids simultaneously.
- **Volcanic arc geoconductivity**: Central America's Pacific volcanic chain creates strong ground-conductivity contrasts (conductive volcanic deposits interspersed with resistive igneous rock) that can focus telluric currents into specific transmission corridors.
- **Hurricane compound risk**: Central America's hurricane vulnerability (notably Hurricanes Eta/Iota 2020) means that space-weather GNSS disruption during hurricane season directly degrades disaster response — search and rescue, logistics coordination, and damage assessment all depend on satellite navigation.

### Maritime & Canal Infrastructure

- **Panama Canal**: The single most critical infrastructure asset in Central America. Canal operations include:
  - Vessel Traffic Management System (VTMS) — GNSS-dependent
  - Lock gate operations — electric motor-driven; grid loss = manual operation, throughput drops 80%
  - Tug and pilot navigation — GPS-dependent
  - AIS (Automatic Identification System) — GNSS-dependent collision avoidance
  - Meteorological monitoring — ionospheric scintillation degrades satellite data feeds
  - A 24-hour Canal closure costs global trade an estimated $5–10 billion.

- **Pacific Coast shipping corridor**: The Pacific coast of Central America is a major maritime transit zone connecting North and South American ports to the Panama Canal and trans-Pacific routes. GNSS disruption affects the ports of:
  - Puerto Quetzal (Guatemala) — largest Pacific port in Central America
  - Acajutla (El Salvador)
  - Puerto Cortés (Honduras — Caribbean) — largest Caribbean port in Central America
  - Puerto Limón/Moín (Costa Rica — Caribbean)
  - Corinto (Nicaragua — Pacific)
  - Balboa and Cristóbal (Panama — Canal anchor ports)

- **Caribbean basin shipping**: The Western Caribbean is a hurricane-transit zone for cruise ships, tankers, and container vessels. Combined GNSS loss and hurricane conditions = severe maritime safety risk.

### Satellite & Telecommunications

- Central American countries rely on satellite backhaul for rural telecommunications (VSAT terminals across the C-band and Ku-band spectrum). Ionospheric scintillation disrupts satellite links during storm periods.
- Aeronautical navigation: Central America's major airports (Panama Tocumen — "Hub of the Americas", San José, Guatemala City, San Salvador) handle significant international connecting traffic; GNSS-based approach procedures are increasingly adopted and vulnerable.
