## THREAT PROFILE: SOUTH ASIA

### Solar Storm Impact on Infrastructure

South Asia's infrastructure profile is defined by a stark asymmetry: India operates the world's third-largest power grid, interconnected nationally through a complex network of 765 kV and 400 kV transmission corridors — while simultaneously, several neighboring countries have among the least hardened grids on the subcontinent. This asymmetry means that a Carrington-level GMD event would produce two distinct threat profiles within the same geographic region: (1) for India, the primary concern is cascading transformer failure across a deeply interconnected national grid; (2) for most neighboring countries, the concern is total communications and grid isolation, with minimal domestic restoration capacity. Recent research from the Indian Institute of Geomagnetism (IIG) confirms that the equatorial electrojet effect produces measurable GIC at Indian geomagnetic latitudes, particularly along the north-south transmission corridors in northern India, Pakistan, Nepal, and Bangladesh.

### Grid Vulnerability

India's national grid — operated by Power Grid Corporation of India Limited (PGCIL) — is a synchronous all-India network with five regional grids now unified into one. This integration, completed in 2013, was a landmark engineering achievement. It also means that a GIC event originating in the northern grid corridor (Punjab, Haryana, Himachal Pradesh) propagates transformer saturation events across the entire national network within seconds. India's 765 kV backbone — the highest voltage in commercial operation in South Asia — provides the longest, most conductive GIC antennas on the subcontinent. The 2012 grid collapse (affecting 620 million people) was caused by non-GMD factors but demonstrated the cascading failure potential of the interconnected Indian grid.

Pakistan's grid is less interconnected but critically dependent on north-south transmission corridors from hydropower generation in the north to load centers in Karachi and the Indus Valley. These long corridors align with geomagnetic field orientation that maximizes GIC coupling. Bangladesh's rapidly expanding grid — with new cross-border interconnections to India — faces similar coupling risk along its Padma-Jamuna river crossing corridors. Nepal and Bhutan's mountainous grid topologies involve long transmission lines through high-altitude terrain where GIC coupling is enhanced by resistive ground conditions. Afghanistan's grid is severely fragmented, but the transmission corridors serving Kabul and Kandahar face GIC risk compounded by conflict-related infrastructure damage. Sri Lanka, as an island nation, avoids the continental cascade threat but faces complete isolation risk if undersea cable connections to India are simultaneously affected by a GMD event and grid collapse at the Indian landing stations. Maldives, almost entirely dependent on diesel generation and undersea cables, would face rapid telecom and navigation isolation.

### Maritime Dependency

South Asia's maritime trade corridor through the Arabian Sea and Bay of Bengal is among the busiest in the world. The ports of Colombo, Mumbai, Karachi, Chittagong, and Chennai handle critical energy imports, food supplies, and container traffic. GPS/GNSS disruption would halt port operations simultaneously with vessel navigation. For Bangladesh and Pakistan, which import significant percentages of their energy through maritime channels, simultaneous port disruption and grid instability would compound into an immediate supply-chain crisis. The Maldives, entirely dependent on maritime supply chains, faces existential vulnerability: if undersea cables lose power amplification at landing stations and GNSS navigation is degraded, the country's connection to global communications and logistics is severed.

### Specific Regional Considerations

- **High-altitude terrain effects**: The Himalayan transmission corridors through Nepal, Bhutan, and northern India traverse resistive rock formations that increase telluric current magnitudes compared to conductive sedimentary basins. The same geology that makes these regions ideal for hydropower makes them GIC amplifiers.
- **Monsoon season overlap**: South Asia's monsoon season creates saturated soil conditions that alter ground conductivity, potentially changing GIC coupling patterns during a storm. A severe GMD event during monsoon flooding would complicate restoration logistics enormously.
- **Population density**: Any grid outage in the Indo-Gangetic plain affects populations measured in hundreds of millions. Transformer replacement lead times of 8-12 months, combined with the sheer number of transformers in the Indian grid, make rapid post-event restoration a civilizational-scale logistics challenge.
