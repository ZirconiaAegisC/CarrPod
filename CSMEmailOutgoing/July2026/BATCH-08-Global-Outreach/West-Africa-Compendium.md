## REGIONAL THREAT PROFILE — WEST AFRICA

### Solar Storm Impact on Infrastructure

West Africa — from the Sahara's southern edge through the Sahel to the Gulf of Guinea rainforest belt — represents the most demographically concentrated and economically diverse region in sub-Saharan Africa. Sixteen nations containing over 400 million people, connected by the West African Power Pool (WAPP) — a regional grid interconnection project that, while transformative for energy access, introduces precisely the kind of long-conductor, multi-jurisdictional topology that amplifies geomagnetic risk. The region's grid faces a uniquely West African compound-threat profile: GIC vulnerability on long-distance transmission lines crossing the Sahelian and coastal sedimentary basins, rapid urbanization without corresponding grid hardening, extreme weather events (coastal erosion, Harmattan dust loading, seasonal flooding) that compound grid stress, and — critically — a maritime dependency through the Gulf of Guinea that handles the majority of the region's oil, gas, mineral, and agricultural exports.

Solar Cycle 25 has officially peaked at SSN ~137 — approximately 19% above the 2019 NOAA SWPC forecast. The May 2024 Gannon Storm (G5/Kp9) demonstrated that a near-miss CME produces $500-565M in GPS losses. For West Africa — where GPS/GNSS disruption affects not just precision agriculture but also offshore oil platform positioning, maritime navigation through the Gulf of Guinea, and the mobile-money infrastructure that underpins the region's financial inclusion — the economic exposure is structurally different and potentially more severe than in OECD economies.

### Grid Vulnerability

- **Nigeria**: The Transmission Company of Nigeria (TCN) operates sub-Saharan Africa's largest national grid, serving over 200 million people, with generation heavily concentrated in the southern gas fields and long 330 kV transmission corridors to the northern states. The grid's chronic voltage instability and frequency excursions mean that GIC-induced reactive power demand could trigger cascading collapse along corridors that already operate near their stability limits. The Lagos mega-city concentration, the Niger Delta oil and gas electrification, and the Dangote Refinery complex represent single-point-of-failure nodes of continental economic significance.
- **Ghana**: The Ghana Grid Company (GRIDCo) and Volta River Authority operate the Akosombo Dam — one of the world's largest hydroelectric installations — and an increasingly sophisticated grid serving as the WAPP eastern anchor. Ghana's growing data center sector, Tema port electrification, and the West African Gas Pipeline infrastructure create concentrated GIC exposure nodes.
- **Cote d'Ivoire**: CIE/Compagnie Ivoirienne d'Électricité operates the region's most reliable grid, exporting power to Ghana, Burkina Faso, Mali, and Liberia. The Abidjan economic hub, offshore oil and gas platforms, and the port of Abidjan — West Africa's second-largest — are all grid-dependent with zero published GIC hardening.
- **Senegal**: SENELEC operates a grid that anchors the western WAPP, with growing wind and solar integration, the Dakar-Diamniadio corridor, and the new Blaise Diagne International Airport and port complex. Senegal's position as a regional aviation hub makes GNSS disruption a direct threat to air traffic management.
- **Mali, Burkina Faso, Niger**: Sahelian nations with long, exposed transmission corridors across resistive geology. Grid dependence for water pumping, humanitarian logistics, and uranium/gold mining electrification creates a compound humanitarian-energy vulnerability during prolonged grid outage.
- **Guinea**: Electricité de Guinée (EDG) operates the grid serving the world's largest bauxite reserves and the Simandou iron ore project. Mining electrification — conveyor belts, processing plants, rail transport — is entirely grid-dependent.
- **Liberia, Sierra Leone**: Post-conflict grids undergoing reconstruction and expansion, with the Mount Coffee Hydropower Plant (Liberia) and Bumbuna Hydro (Sierra Leone) as critical nodes. Grid fragility amplifies GIC impact.
- **Benin, Togo**: The Communauté Électrique du Bénin (CEB) operates a binational grid that serves both countries and exports to neighbors. The Lomé port complex and Cotonou port are regional maritime gateways.
- **Cape Verde**: Island grid, fully dependent on diesel generation and growing wind/solar, with zero interconnection backup. Desalination plants — on which the entire population depends for fresh water — are grid-powered.
- **Mauritania**: SOMELEC operates a Saharan-coastal grid with iron ore mining electrification powering one of Africa's longest mineral railways. Extreme heat converts any grid failure into a health emergency.
- **Gambia**: NAWEC operates a small, import-dependent grid centered on the Greater Banjul Area, with the Gambia River estuary presenting unique GIC-geology coupling.

### Maritime Dependency

The Gulf of Guinea is one of the world's most strategically important maritime regions — handling the majority of West and Central Africa's oil exports, mineral shipments, and container traffic. Ports from Dakar to Lagos are fully electrified, with container cranes, automated cargo handling, vessel traffic services, and navigation aids all dependent on uninterrupted grid power and GNSS signals. A G5 event that degrades GPS/GNSS across the Gulf of Guinea would simultaneously disrupt offshore oil platform dynamic positioning, tanker navigation, port operations, and the AIS (Automatic Identification System) on which maritime security — including anti-piracy operations — depends. Aegis-C shielding (SE 148-165 dB) is directly applicable to every electronic system in this maritime ecosystem.
