## Regional Threat Profile: North America

### Solar Storm Impact on Infrastructure

North America is the **best-documented but least-mitigated** space-weather risk zone on Earth. The March 1989 Hydro-Québec blackout — caused by a G5 geomagnetic storm that collapsed the entire Québec grid in 92 seconds — remains the canonical warning. The region's unique combination of the Canadian Shield's resistive bedrock, continent-spanning high-voltage corridors, and the largest synchronous grid on the continent (the Eastern Interconnection) creates **systemic, non-diversifiable GIC risk**.

### Grid-Specific Vulnerabilities

| Infrastructure Layer | Risk Level | Notes |
|---------------------|------------|-------|
| Canadian Grid (Provincial Systems) | **EXTREME** | Hydro-Québec, Hydro One (Ontario), BC Hydro, Manitoba Hydro all traverse the Canadian Shield; HQ has the world's most advanced GIC monitoring programme (SILK, SAGE) but remains vulnerable to >1989-class events |
| US Eastern Interconnection | **SEVERE** | Long E-W transmission corridors from Great Plains to Eastern Seaboard; reactive power absorption during GIC events can trigger voltage collapse |
| US Western Interconnection (WECC) | **HIGH** | Pacific DC Intertie (PDCI) and extensive long-distance transmission; resistive terrain in Basin-and-Range province |
| ERCOT (Texas) | **MODERATE** | Lower geomagnetic latitude but near-islanded grid with limited external support in blackout scenario |
| Mexican Grid (CFE/CENACE) | **MODERATE-HIGH** | Rapidly modernising grid; limited GIC operational experience; interconnections to US (WECC and ERCOT) and Central America (SIEPAC) |
| Caribbean Island Grids | **EXTREME** | Isolated, diesel-dependent island grids; no mutual support; recovery after blackout can take weeks; hurricane compound risk |
| Substation Transformers | **EXTREME** | US has ~2,000+ EHV transformers (≥345 kV); replacement lead time 12–24 months for large units; a coordinated multi-transformer loss is a national-security scenario (NERC GMD Task Force, 2012) |
| Nuclear Power Plants | **HIGH** | 93 operating reactors in the US, 19 in Canada, 2 in Mexico; NRC/FERC Order requires GMD planning but implementation varies by plant |

### Geophysical Amplification Factors

- **Canadian Shield**: The largest exposure of Precambrian igneous/metamorphic bedrock on Earth (resistivity >10,000 Ω·m) covers most of Québec, Ontario, Manitoba, Saskatchewan, and the Northwest Territories. It provides essentially no alternative conductive path — GICs flow entirely through the transmission grid.
- **East Coast Anomaly**: The contrast between conductive Atlantic seawater and resistive inland bedrock creates a coastal enhancement zone from Newfoundland to Florida where GICs concentrate in coastal substations.
- **Mid-Continent conductivity structures**: Buried conductive sedimentary basins (Williston, Michigan, Illinois, Appalachian) create subsurface pathways that focus telluric currents into specific transmission corridors.
- **US-Mexico border region**: Variable soil conductivity and extensive cross-border interconnections create complex GIC flow patterns not captured in national-level models.

### Maritime & Coastal Infrastructure Impact

- **Atlantic Seaboard**: GNSS-dependent maritime traffic from the Gulf of St. Lawrence to the Gulf of Mexico (including the St. Lawrence Seaway — a critical US-Canada trade artery); major ports (Halifax, Montréal, New York/New Jersey, Norfolk, Savannah, Charleston, Miami, Houston, New Orleans, Veracruz).
- **Pacific Coast**: Ports of Vancouver, Seattle-Tacoma, Oakland, Los Angeles/Long Beach, Manzanillo, Lázaro Cárdenas.
- **Caribbean Basin**: Hurricane-prone region where space-weather GNSS disruption compounds natural-disaster response capabilities; island nations depend on maritime resupply.
- **Arctic (Canada/US-Alaska)**: Northwest Passage, DEW Line radar stations, Alaska Radar System, Yukon energy infrastructure — extreme GIC exposure above 60°N.
- **Transatlantic submarine cables**: Cable landing stations in Canada (Halifax, St. John's), US (New York, New Jersey, Virginia Beach, Miami) and Bermuda face GIC-induced voltage surges on cable repeaters.

### Existing US Regulatory Framework (Context)

- **NERC Reliability Standard TPL-007-4**: Requires Transmission Planners and Planning Coordinators to conduct GMD Vulnerability Assessments for ≥200 kV. However, implementation deadlines extend to 2028+ for some entities.
- **FERC Order 830**: Mandated GMD planning but did not require operational GIC monitoring or real-time mitigation.
- **Presidential Executive Order 13865 (2019)**: Coordinating National Resilience to Electromagnetic Pulses — covers both EMP and GMD but has limited operational enforcement.
- **PROSWIFT Act (2020)**: Promoting Research and Observations of Space Weather to Improve the Forecasting of Tomorrow — authorises but does not fully fund operational space-weather monitoring expansion.
