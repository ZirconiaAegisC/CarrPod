## THREAT PROFILE: CENTRAL ASIA

### Solar Storm Impact on Infrastructure

Central Asia occupies a uniquely dangerous geomagnetic position: all five republics sit between approximately 35°N and 55°N latitude — directly within the Northern Hemisphere auroral oval expansion zone during a G5 storm. This region experiences GIC magnitudes comparable to Scandinavia and Canada under equivalent storm conditions, but operates with a fraction of the hardening investment. The Soviet-era Central Asian Unified Power System (CAPS), built largely between the 1960s and 1980s, was designed without any consideration of geomagnetic disturbance. Transmission corridors stretching thousands of kilometers across the Kazakh steppe, the Kyrgyz and Tajik highlands, and the Turkmen and Uzbek deserts are among the longest uninterrupted conductors on the Eurasian landmass — and therefore among the most efficient GIC antennas anywhere on Earth.

### Grid Vulnerability

The CAPS grid, which connects all five republics, was designed as a single synchronized system centered on the massive Toktogul hydropower cascade in Kyrgyzstan. Kazakhstan's north-south transmission corridors — running from the Russian border to the southern industrial cities — span over 1,500 km of sparsely populated terrain with minimal intermediate substations. This topology maximizes GIC induction: long, uninterrupted conductors become kilometer-scale antennas for geomagnetic field fluctuations. Kazakhstan's Ekibastuz and other coal-fired generation centers feed the grid through transformer banks that date from the Soviet era, many exceeding 40 years of service with insulation already thermally aged beyond modern replacement margins. Kyrgyzstan and Tajikistan's mountain-grid corridors traverse resistive bedrock formations that amplify telluric currents. The Nurek and Toktogul hydroelectric complexes are not just generation assets — they are regional grid stability anchors. If a G5 event saturates the transformers at either complex, voltage instability propagates across all five republics. Turkmenistan's grid is heavily concentrated along the Caspian coastal corridor and the Karakum Canal — both long linear transmission paths in conductive sedimentary basins that couple GIC efficiently. Uzbekistan's denser urban grid around Tashkent, Samarkand, and the Fergana Valley offers more segmentation potential but remains interconnected through the CAPS to the wider regional network.

### Maritime Dependency — The Caspian Exception

Central Asia is not typically thought of as "maritime," but the Caspian Sea plays an analogous role: it houses critical energy export infrastructure (Kashagan, Tengiz pipelines), trans-Caspian fiber optic cables, and port operations at Aktau (Kazakhstan) and Turkmenbashi (Turkmenistan). GPS/GNSS disruption would affect Caspian shipping, offshore platform operations, and pipeline monitoring telemetry. For Kazakhstan in particular, the Tengiz and Kashagan oil fields represent a substantial fraction of national revenue — any disruption to their satellite-linked control and monitoring systems during a GMD event would have immediate fiscal consequences.

### Specific Regional Considerations

- **Soviet-era equipment age**: The average transformer in the Central Asian grid is older than the national independence of any of the five republics. Insulation degradation from thermal cycling means GIC-induced half-cycle saturation hits a transformer that is already operating near its thermal margin — not a transformer with generous design headroom.
- **Winter extremes**: Central Asian winters produce sustained temperatures below -30°C in northern Kazakhstan and Kyrgyzstan. Heating load during these periods already stresses the transmission system. A G5 event during a January cold snap would compound transformer saturation with maximum load — the worst-case scenario for thermal failure.
- **Water-energy nexus**: The Central Asian grid is fundamentally hydropower-dependent in the upstream republics (Kyrgyzstan, Tajikistan) and fossil-fuel dependent in the downstream republics (Kazakhstan, Uzbekistan, Turkmenistan). This creates a seasonal tension in grid operations that limits the operational flexibility needed to manage GMD-induced voltage instability.
- **Telecom isolation**: Central Asia's terrestrial fiber optic backbone largely follows the same transmission corridors as the power grid. A GIC event that affects power infrastructure would simultaneously affect telecom amplification stations along the same routes.
