## THREAT PROFILE: OCEANIA / PACIFIC

### Solar Storm Impact on Infrastructure

The Pacific region presents the most extreme asymmetry in GMD vulnerability on the planet. Australia and New Zealand operate sophisticated, well-instrumented national grids at mid-southern latitudes where geomagnetic disturbance is a recognized and studied threat. The Pacific Island states — from Kiribati to Tuvalu — operate small, isolated, diesel-dependent grids, often on single atolls, where the GMD threat is less about transformer saturation and more about complete isolation: loss of GPS/GNSS navigation, loss of satellite communications, loss of undersea cable connectivity, and loss of the maritime supply chain on which these nations depend for food, fuel, and medicine. The equatorial Pacific states sit geographically within the equatorial electrojet zone, where ionospheric current intensification during geomagnetic storms can produce unexpected ground-level effects.

Australia's experience during the 1989 Quebec-style GMD event was fortunately limited — Australia's geomagnetic latitude (ranging from roughly 30°S to 45°S in the populated southern states) places the southern and eastern grid in the region where GIC effects have been documented globally. Geoscience Australia and the Bureau of Meteorology's Space Weather Services operate world-class monitoring capability. New Zealand's South Island grid reaches approximately 47°S — comparable to the southern equivalent of central France — and its long, narrow island topology creates unique GIC coupling patterns across the Cook Strait HVDC interconnector. New Zealand's 2016 Kaikoura earthquake demonstrated cascading infrastructure failure in a geographically constrained environment; a GMD-compounded seismic event is a recognized scenario in New Zealand's national risk register.

### Grid Vulnerability

**Australia**: The National Electricity Market (NEM) grid is the longest interconnected power system in the world by geographic span — stretching from Port Douglas in northern Queensland to Port Augusta in South Australia, with Tasmania connected via the Basslink HVDC interconnector. This extreme geographic extent, combined with east-west transmission corridors crossing varied geological formations, creates differential GIC coupling patterns that Australia's grid operators have only recently begun modeling at national scale. The South Australia-Victoria interconnectors traverse resistive terrain that amplifies GIC. Tasmania's Basslink HVDC is a single-point interconnector: GIC at the converter stations would isolate Tasmania's grid during the event.

**New Zealand**: The national grid, operated by Transpower, features a unique vulnerability in the HVDC link crossing Cook Strait between Benmore (South Island) and Haywards (North Island). This interconnector is both a critical stability asset and a GIC concentration point. The South Island's alpine transmission corridors through resistive schist terrain maximize telluric current induction. The North Island's volcanic plateau introduces ground conductivity variability that complicates GIC modeling.

**Pacific Island States**: For the small island developing states (SIDS) of the Pacific, the threat is not grid cascade — it is grid isolation compounded by communications isolation. Most Pacific Island nations depend on diesel generation with limited backup, undersea fiber optic cables for internet connectivity, satellite communications for inter-island coordination, and GPS/GNSS for inter-island shipping and fishing fleet navigation. A G5 storm that simultaneously degrades GPS accuracy, disrupts satellite communications, and causes power fluctuations at undersea cable landing stations would effectively sever these nations from the global communications and logistics grid. For low-lying atoll nations (Kiribati, Tuvalu, Marshall Islands, Maldives-equivalent Pacific states), the compounding effect of a GMD event during cyclone season or king tide flooding is an existential scenario.

### Maritime Dependency — The Defining Vulnerability

Oceania is the most maritime-dependent region on Earth, full stop. Pacific Island nations have exclusive economic zones (EEZs) that are orders of magnitude larger than their land area. Kiribati's EEZ spans 3.5 million square kilometers. Tuvalu's spans 900,000. These nations depend on GPS/GNSS for: (1) inter-island shipping and supply delivery, (2) fishing fleet navigation (often the primary economic activity), (3) maritime boundary enforcement and illegal fishing monitoring, (4) search and rescue coordination, (5) tsunami and cyclone early warning systems. GNSS degradation during a G5 storm would disable all of these functions simultaneously. Australia's iron ore and LNG export terminals at Port Hedland, Dampier, and Gladstone depend on GPS for vessel berthing and channel navigation. The Torres Strait, Bass Strait, and Tasman Sea are commercial shipping routes where GNSS failure would halt transit immediately. Undersea cable connectivity — the Southern Cross Cable, Australia-Japan Cable, Hawaiki Cable, and numerous Pacific island spurs — depends on powered amplification at landing stations. Grid disturbance at these stations severs the physical layer of Pacific internet connectivity. For nations where the undersea cable is the only high-bandwidth connection to the world (virtually every Pacific Island state), this is communications blackout.

### Specific Regional Considerations

- **Auroral visibility at low southern latitudes**: During a G5 event, aurora would be visible as far north as Brisbane and potentially further north toward the Coral Sea — a visual indicator that GIC threat in southern Australia, Tasmania, and New Zealand is at maximum.
- **Southern Hemisphere data gap**: The vast majority of GMD modeling and empirical data has been collected in the Northern Hemisphere. The Southern Hemisphere's smaller landmass and different geomagnetic field geometry mean that models extrapolated from northern data may not accurately predict southern GIC magnitudes. This is a recognized research gap at Geoscience Australia.
- **Cyclone-GMD compound risk**: The South Pacific cyclone season (November-April) overlaps with the solar maximum period. A G5 event during an active cyclone — disabling early warning systems, satellite tracking, and emergency communications — is a compound catastrophe scenario specific to this region.
- **Sea-level rise overlay**: For atoll nations, every infrastructure vulnerability is compounded by sea-level rise and coastal inundation. Substation equipment at near-sea-level locations is exposed to both GMD electrical stress and saltwater corrosion — a dual degradation pathway that accelerates equipment aging.
