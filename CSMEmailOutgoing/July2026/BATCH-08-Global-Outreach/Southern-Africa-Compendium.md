### Solar Storm Impact on Infrastructure

Southern Africa is the only region in the CSM Global Outreach campaign that contains a G20 economy operating the most industrialized, most interconnected, and most mid-latitude-exposed power grid on the African continent. South Africa's Eskom grid — with over 30,000 km of transmission lines, the continent's only commercial nuclear power station (Koeberg), and extensive HVDC and HVAC corridors crossing some of the most resistive geology on Earth (the Kalahari Desert and Namib formations) — represents not just a national infrastructure asset but a continental systemic risk node. When Eskom experiences a major disturbance, the entire Southern African Power Pool (SAPP) feels it. When a G5 geomagnetic storm induces GIC into Eskom's transmission network, the cascade would propagate through Botswana, Namibia, Eswatini, and Lesotho within seconds — these countries import a substantial fraction of their electricity from South Africa and have minimal islanding capability.

### Grid Vulnerability

- **South Africa**: Eskom Holdings SOC Ltd operates Africa's most complex and heavily loaded grid. Koeberg Nuclear Power Station (1.94 GW) — the continent's only nuclear plant — is located on the coast near Cape Town at a geomagnetic latitude (approximately 34°S) that places it squarely within the expanded auroral electrojet footprint during G4-G5 events. Nuclear plant safety systems, reactor protection circuits, and emergency cooling pumps are all electronic and all GIC-vulnerable — a G5 event that disrupts Koeberg's control systems is not just a power outage, it is a nuclear safety incident. The Cahora Bassa-Apollo HVDC line (importing 1.5 GW from Mozambique) and the extensive 400 kV and 765 kV transmission network — including lines crossing the Karoo semi-desert and the Drakensberg escarpment on highly resistive geology — are textbook GIC coupling paths. Eskom's well-documented generation capacity constraints and load-shedding history mean the grid already operates near stability limits; GIC-induced reactive power demand could trigger cascading collapse on corridors that Eskom engineers already monitor with concern. The Gauteng economic concentration (Johannesburg-Pretoria), the Western Cape, the Mpumalanga coal-fired generation cluster, and the Northern Cape solar/wind renewables integration zones represent concentrated single-point-of-failure nodes.
- **Botswana**: Botswana Power Corporation (BPC) operates a grid heavily dependent on South African imports via the SAPP, with the Morupule coal-fired power station and growing solar PV as domestic generation. The Kalahari Desert transmission corridors — crossing some of the driest, most resistive geology on Earth — create ideal GIC coupling conditions. Botswana's diamond mining electrification (Jwaneng, Orapa) and the growing Gaborone economic zone are grid-dependent loads of national economic significance.
- **Namibia**: NamPower operates a grid characterized by extremely long transmission corridors — some of the longest per-capita in the world — crossing the Namib Desert on highly resistive geology. The Ruacana hydroelectric station (on the Angola border), the Kudu gas-to-power project, and extensive uranium mining electrification (Rössing, Husab) represent concentrated GIC exposure. The Walvis Bay port expansion and the Lüderitz maritime zone depend on uninterrupted grid power. Namibia's unique 220 kV DC interconnector with South Africa and its growing solar/wind renewable integration add harmonic complexity during GIC events.
- **Eswatini**: Eswatini Electricity Company (EEC) operates a grid almost entirely dependent on South African imports, with limited domestic hydro and biomass generation. Grid fragility and import dependency mean that a GIC event affecting Eskom would cascade into Eswatini within seconds, with zero domestic islanding capability.
- **Lesotho**: Lesotho Electricity Company (LEC) operates a grid dependent on the Lesotho Highlands Water Project hydroelectric generation (Muela Hydropower Station, 72 MW) and South African imports. The high-altitude (above 1,500 m), mountainous terrain creates unique GIC-ground-coupling conditions that have never been modeled. Lesotho's position as a water exporter to South Africa (via the LHWP tunnel system) means that grid outage affecting water pumping would have cross-border humanitarian consequences within days.

### Maritime Dependency

South Africa's maritime infrastructure is the most developed in Africa. The ports of Durban (Africa's busiest container port), Cape Town, Port Elizabeth (Gqeberha), Ngqura (deep-water container terminal), Richards Bay (coal export), and Saldanha Bay (iron ore export) are fully electrified, GNSS-dependent nodes in the global shipping network. The Cape of Good Hope sea route — around which approximately 30% of global maritime trade transits — is monitored by South African maritime radar, VTS, and navigation infrastructure that is entirely electronic and entirely GIC-vulnerable. A G5 event that degrades GPS/GNSS around the Cape would simultaneously disrupt vessel navigation, port operations, and the maritime domain awareness systems that monitor one of the world's most strategically important shipping chokepoints.

Namibia's Walvis Bay port — the primary maritime gateway for landlocked Botswana, Zambia, and Zimbabwe — and the Lüderitz port, supporting the offshore diamond mining and fishing industries, add coastal GIC exposure on Atlantic-facing infrastructure.
