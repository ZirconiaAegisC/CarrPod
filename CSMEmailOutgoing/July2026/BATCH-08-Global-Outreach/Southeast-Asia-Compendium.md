## THREAT PROFILE: SOUTHEAST ASIA

### Solar Storm Impact on Infrastructure

Southeast Asia sits at lower geomagnetic latitudes than East Asia, which historically has meant lower GIC magnitudes per kilometer of transmission line. However, this conventional assumption is being revised by three developments: (1) the equatorial electrojet effect produces enhanced ionospheric currents during geomagnetic storms that can couple to ground-level infrastructure in unexpected ways; (2) the region's rapid grid interconnectivity — the ASEAN Power Grid initiative linking Laos, Thailand, Malaysia, and Singapore — means that a disturbance at one node propagates across national boundaries; and (3) the May 2024 Gannon Storm demonstrated that GIC effects at mid-to-low latitudes are not theoretical — measurable transformer stress was recorded as far south as 20°N, which includes northern Thailand, Laos, Vietnam, and Myanmar.

### Grid Vulnerability

The ASEAN grid is in the most rapid expansion phase of any region globally. Vietnam alone added over 4,500 MW of solar capacity between 2019-2021, much of it interconnected through transmission corridors designed before the latest GMD modeling. Indonesia's archipelagic grid structure — thousands of islands served by localized generation — provides some segmentation advantage but creates a hardening logistics nightmare: every island's transformers represent a separate hardening target. The Philippines' typhoon-vulnerable grid already experiences frequent brownouts; a compound typhoon-plus-GMD event is a scenario that Philippine disaster planners should model. Thailand's well-developed EGAT grid has moderate GIC exposure in northern provinces. Singapore's grid is among the most modern and densely instrumented in the world, but its total dependence on undersea power interconnectors and gas pipeline imports creates an indirect GMD vulnerability. Laos, Cambodia, and Myanmar have rapidly expanding but minimally hardened transmission networks, with Myanmar's ongoing civil conflict adding the complicating factor of damaged infrastructure that may already be operating below design specifications.

### Maritime Dependency

This is the defining vulnerability for Southeast Asia. The region is archipelagic and maritime by definition. The Malacca Strait — through which 25% of global seaborne trade transits — is wholly dependent on GPS/GNSS for navigation, automated collision avoidance, and port approach. The Singapore Strait, Sunda Strait, Lombok Strait, and Makassar Strait are similarly critical chokepoints. GNSS disruption during a G5 storm would halt commercial shipping through these passages within hours. The economic effect on ASEAN economies, which collectively depend on maritime trade for 40-60% of GDP, would be immediate and cascading. Undersea cable landing stations in Singapore, Malaysia, Indonesia, Philippines, and Vietnam form the region's internet backbone — simultaneous grid-power loss at multiple landing stations would sever intra-ASEAN and global connectivity. The region's fishing fleets — among the world's largest — operate with minimal satellite backup and would lose navigation and communication simultaneously.

### Specific Regional Considerations

- **Equatorial electrojet**: The ionospheric current system near the magnetic equator intensifies during geomagnetic storms. Countries near 0-10°N — Indonesia, southern Thailand, Malaysia, Singapore — may experience enhanced telluric currents not captured by high-latitude GMD models designed for North America and Europe.
- **Coastal inundation overlap**: Much of ASEAN's coastal infrastructure — ports, power plants, substations — is at or near sea level. A solar event disabling early warning systems during typhoon season or monsoon flooding creates a compound-crisis scenario requiring layered contingency planning.
- **Data center concentration**: Singapore and increasingly Malaysia and Indonesia host the region's data center clusters. These facilities require continuous power conditioning — a GMD-induced harmonic distortion event could cascade through UPS systems not designed for the waveform distortion profile of a geomagnetic storm.
