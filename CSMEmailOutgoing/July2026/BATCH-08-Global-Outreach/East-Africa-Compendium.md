## REGIONAL THREAT PROFILE — EAST AFRICA

### Solar Storm Impact on Infrastructure

East Africa — eighteen nations spanning the Horn of Africa, the Great Lakes, the Indian Ocean islands, and the Southern African extension states — presents the most geoelectrically complex and economically diverse regional profile in the CSM Global Outreach campaign. The East African Rift System (EARS), one of the planet's most active tectonic zones, creates a subsurface geology of faulted, fractured, and highly variable conductivity structures that can dramatically amplify and localize geomagnetically induced currents in ways that are poorly understood and completely unmodeled for GIC risk. When a G5 geomagnetic storm induces telluric currents across this fractured geology, the resulting GIC coupling into power lines, pipelines, railways, and telecommunications infrastructure can be concentrated at conductivity boundaries in ways that uniform-geology models cannot predict.

Solar Cycle 25 has officially peaked at SSN ~137 — approximately 19% above the 2019 NOAA SWPC forecast. The May 2024 Gannon Storm (G5/Kp9) caused $500-565M in U.S. agricultural GPS losses. For East Africa — where precision agriculture is expanding rapidly in Kenya, Ethiopia, and Tanzania; where mobile-money infrastructure (M-Pesa and equivalents) underpins financial inclusion for over 400 million people; where the Indian Ocean maritime corridor carries the majority of the region's imports and exports; and where multiple island states have zero grid interconnection — the compound vulnerability is structurally different and more severe than in any other region in this campaign.

### Grid Vulnerability

- **Ethiopia**: Ethiopian Electric Power (EEP) and Ethiopian Electric Utility (EEU) operate Africa's second-largest grid, anchored by the Grand Ethiopian Renaissance Dam (GERD, 5.15 GW), the Gibe cascade, and extensive 400 kV and 500 kV transmission lines — including the Ethiopia-Kenya and Ethiopia-Sudan interconnectors. GERD's location on the Blue Nile, combined with the Ethiopian Highlands' complex geology, places one of Africa's most critical generation assets on some of the most GIC-amplifying terrain on the continent. A GERD transformer failure during a geomagnetic storm would affect not just Ethiopia but Sudan and Egypt downstream.
- **Kenya**: Kenya Power and Lighting Company (KPLC) and Kenya Electricity Transmission Company (KETRACO) operate East Africa's most sophisticated grid, with extensive geothermal generation (Olkaria complex, ~1 GW), the Lake Turkana Wind Power project (310 MW), growing solar PV, and a rapidly expanding data center sector. Kenya is the EAPP anchor. The Nairobi-Mombasa corridor, the Lamu Port-South Sudan-Ethiopia Transport (LAPSSET) corridor electrification, and Kenya's position as the regional ICT hub make GIC preparedness a national economic security issue.
- **Tanzania**: Tanzania Electric Supply Company (TANESCO) operates a grid anchored by the Julius Nyerere Hydropower Station (2.1 GW) on the Rufiji River, the largest dam in East Africa, with the new Tanzania-Zambia interconnector and growing natural gas generation. The Dar es Salaam port and the new Standard Gauge Railway electrification add concentrated GIC exposure.
- **Uganda**: Uganda Electricity Transmission Company (UETCL) and generation assets on the Nile (Karuma 600 MW, Isimba 183 MW, Bujagali 250 MW) make Uganda hydro-dependent with exports to Kenya, Tanzania, Rwanda, and DR Congo. The Kampala economic concentration creates a single-point-of-failure load center.
- **Rwanda**: Rwanda Energy Group (REG) operates Africa's most rapidly modernizing grid, with the Kigali Innovation City and growing ICT/data center sector. Rwanda's small grid size means that even a moderate GIC event could produce disproportionate systemic effects.
- **Burundi**: REGIDESO operates a small, hydro-dependent grid with the Jiji-Murembwe and Rusumo Falls (shared with Rwanda/Tanzania) hydro projects as critical nodes. Grid fragility amplifies GIC impact.
- **South Sudan**: South Sudan Electricity Corporation operates a nascent grid centered on Juba, heavily dependent on diesel generation and the under-construction Fula Rapids hydro project. Grid fragility in a humanitarian context makes GIC hardening a resilience multiplier.
- **Somalia**: Benadir Electric Company and private operators serve a fragmented grid in Mogadishu and regional capitals, with the Somali energy sector undergoing its first post-conflict reconstruction. Mobile-money infrastructure is the de facto financial system. GNSS disruption would be economically catastrophic.
- **Djibouti**: Électricité de Djibouti operates a grid heavily dependent on the Ethiopia-Djibouti interconnector, with the Doraleh Container Terminal and Djibouti port — one of Africa's busiest maritime gateways — as the primary electrical load. A failure of the Ethiopia interconnector during a geomagnetic storm would leave Djibouti without backup.
- **Eritrea**: Eritrean Electric Authority operates a small, isolated grid with limited interconnection. Grid fragility amplifies any GIC event.
- **Zambia**: ZESCO operates a hydro-dependent grid anchored by the Kariba Dam and the Zambezi River cascade, with the Copperbelt mining electrification as the primary load center. Drought + GIC compound threat. SAPP membership creates cross-border cascade risk.
- **Zimbabwe**: Zimbabwe Electricity Supply Authority (ZESA) and the Zimbabwe Power Company operate a grid anchored by Kariba South and Hwange Thermal, with the Harare-Bulawayo corridor and mining electrification (platinum, gold, chrome) as critical loads.
- **Malawi**: Electricity Supply Corporation of Malawi (ESCOM) operates a hydro-dependent grid on the Shire River cascade, with the Mozambique-Malawi interconnector as the primary import path. Lake Malawi's conductivity anomalies may create unique GIC coupling.
- **Mozambique**: Electricidade de Moçambique (EDM) operates the Cahora Bassa hydroelectric complex (2.075 GW) on the Zambezi River, with the Cahora Bassa-Apollo HVDC line to South Africa representing one of Africa's most critical single transmission corridors. Mozambique's offshore natural gas platforms (Rovuma Basin) add GNSS vulnerability.
- **Madagascar**: Jirama operates an isolated island grid with hydro and thermal generation, zero interconnection backup, and extensive vanilla, mining, and textile electrification. Island-grid GIC vulnerability has never been modeled.
- **Mauritius**: Central Electricity Board (CEB) operates a sophisticated island grid with bagasse, solar, and thermal generation. Zero interconnection. Financial services and ICT sectors are the primary electrical loads.
- **Seychelles**: Public Utilities Corporation (PUC) operates an isolated island grid on Mahé and inner islands. Diesel-dependent. Tourism, fisheries, and financial services electrification. Zero interconnection.
- **Comoros**: Société Nationale de l'Électricité des Comores operates isolated grids on Grande Comore, Anjouan, and Mohéli. Diesel-dependent. No interconnection.

### Maritime Dependency

The Indian Ocean is East Africa's economic artery. The ports of Mombasa, Dar es Salaam, Djibouti, Maputo, Beira, Nacala, Port Louis (Mauritius), Port Victoria (Seychelles), Toamasina (Madagascar), and Berbera handle the region's entire import/export volume. These ports are fully electrified, GNSS-dependent, and operationally vulnerable to GPS/GNSS degradation during geomagnetic storms. The Mozambique Channel and the Western Indian Ocean shipping lanes carry a substantial portion of global oil tanker traffic. Aegis-C shielding (SE 148-165 dB) is directly applicable to port VTS consoles, shipboard navigation suites, offshore platform electronics, and the coastal radar installations that monitor this strategic maritime space.

The island states — Madagascar, Mauritius, Seychelles, Comoros — have zero grid interconnection. When a geomagnetic storm hits, there is no neighboring grid to borrow from. This makes proactive GIC hardening on island grids one of the highest-value interventions per dollar invested anywhere in the campaign.
