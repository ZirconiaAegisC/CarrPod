## THREAT PROFILE: EAST ASIA

### Solar Storm Impact on Infrastructure

East Asia hosts the world's largest concentration of high-voltage long-distance power transmission corridors. China's State Grid operates the planet's most extensive EHV/UHV network — over 1.1 million km of transmission lines, much of it running east-west across varied geomagnetic latitudes. A G5/Carrington-class geomagnetic storm would induce geomagnetically induced currents (GIC) throughout these corridors, with particular vulnerability in northern China (Heilongjiang, Inner Mongolia), Hokkaido and northern Honshu in Japan, and the Korean peninsula. Transformer saturation from half-cycle geomagnetic disturbances would cascade through interconnected grids. Japan's islanded grid topology offers partial segmentation advantage but does not eliminate the common-mode threat to substation control electronics.

### Grid Vulnerability

China's ultra-high-voltage DC (UHVDC) lines — the Changji-Guquan ±1100 kV system and equivalents — present a unique concern: DC systems are fundamentally immune to GIC on the DC side, but their converter stations and AC interconnections remain vulnerable. Japan's post-Fukushima grid fragmentation created a frequency-separated east/west network with limited intertie capacity, which constrains cascading failure but complicates black-start restoration. South Korea's dense, highly interconnected KEPCO grid presents rapid cascade risk. North Korea's grid is among the least hardened on Earth — aging Soviet-era infrastructure with zero GMD-specific mitigation. Mongolia's long north-south transmission corridors serving mining operations traverse high-latitude terrain exceptionally prone to GIC. Taiwan's islanded grid is less susceptible to continental-scale cascades but fully exposed to transformer-level GIC and substation control disruption.

### Maritime Dependency

East Asia contains six of the world's ten busiest container ports (Shanghai, Singapore-adjacent transshipment notwithstanding, Ningbo-Zhoushan, Shenzhen, Busan, Qingdao, and Tokyo-Yokohama). GPS/GNSS disruption during a severe solar event would cripple precision navigation, automated container handling, and AIS vessel tracking across the entire region. Japan's maritime SLOC dependence for energy imports approaches 99%. The Malacca, Taiwan, and Tsushima straits are chokepoints where GNSS failure would halt transit within hours. If undersea cable landing stations lose grid power simultaneously with satellite communication degradation, East Asia's connectivity to global financial and internet infrastructure would be severed.

### Specific Regional Considerations

- **Seismic overlap**: Japan, Taiwan, and the Korean peninsula are seismically active. A compound event — earthquake damaging grid infrastructure followed by solar storm disabling restoration equipment — is a low-probability, catastrophic-consequence scenario.
- **Winter peak loading**: Northern China, Korea, and Mongolia experience winter heating loads that stress transformer thermal margins, reducing headroom for GIC-induced half-cycle saturation.
- **Auroral visibility zone**: During G5 events, aurora becomes visible as far south as Taiwan and Hong Kong — a visual indicator that GIC threat is acute even at traditionally "low-risk" latitudes.
