## REGIONAL THREAT PROFILE — CENTRAL AFRICA

### Solar Storm Impact on Infrastructure

Central Africa occupies a distinctive position in the global geomagnetic risk landscape — equatorial latitudes where the direct auroral electrojet threat is lower than at mid-latitudes, but where the secondary effects of a Carrington-class event would be disproportionately severe due to infrastructure concentration, economic dependency on electrified extraction, and the near-total absence of GIC modeling or hardening anywhere in the region. The equatorial electrojet itself — a narrow, intense current system flowing in the ionosphere directly above the magnetic equator — is amplified during geomagnetic storms and can induce surface currents through a different physical mechanism than the high-latitude electrojet. This equatorial coupling is poorly studied but physically real, and it flows directly above Gabon, Congo, DR Congo, and northern Angola.

Solar Cycle 25 has officially peaked at SSN ~137 — approximately 19% above the 2019 NOAA SWPC forecast. The May 2024 Gannon Storm (G5/Kp9) demonstrated that the economic cost of GPS/GNSS disruption alone reaches into the hundreds of millions of dollars. For Central Africa — where mobile-money infrastructure, mining logistics, and offshore oil platform positioning are all GNSS-dependent — the cascade would be immediate and severe.

### Grid Vulnerability

- **DR Congo**: Société Nationale d'Électricité (SNEL) operates the grid serving sub-Saharan Africa's largest country, anchored by the Inga hydroelectric complex on the Congo River — a site with the potential to generate 40 GW, making it the largest hydroelectric site on Earth. The Inga-Kolwezi transmission corridor (1,700+ km of HVDC and HVAC lines) is one of the longest single transmission paths in the world, crossing the Congo Basin on highly variable geology. This is a textbook GIC antenna. The Grand Inga expansion, if built, would be the largest power plant on Earth — and it is being designed, as of 2026, with zero published consideration of geomagnetic hardening. The Katanga copper-cobalt mining belt electrification is entirely grid-dependent.
- **Angola**: Rede Nacional de Transporte de Electricidade (RNT) operates a post-conflict grid undergoing rapid expansion, with long transmission corridors connecting the northern hydroelectric dams (Capanda, Laúca, Cambambe) to the southern consumption centers and the capital Luanda. Angola's offshore oil platforms — the economic engine of the country — use GPS-dependent dynamic positioning and subsea electronics systems that have never been tested against GIC or GNSS disruption scenarios.
- **Cameroon**: ENEO (formerly AES-SONEL) and the Southern Interconnected Grid serve the economic triangle of Douala-Yaoundé-Bafoussam, with hydroelectric generation from the Sanaga River system. The Chad-Cameroon oil pipeline electrification, the Kribi deep-water port, and the Lom Pangar Dam represent concentrated infrastructure nodes.
- **Congo (Republic)**: Énergie Electrique du Congo (E2C) operates a grid heavily dependent on the Pointe-Noire-Brazzaville corridor, with offshore oil platforms and the Pointe-Noire port as critical electrical loads. The country is both a significant oil producer and transit hub for Central African mineral exports.
- **Gabon**: Société d'Énergie et d'Eau du Gabon (SEEG) operates a grid concentrated on the Libreville-Port-Gentil axis, with the country's oil-dependent economy making grid reliability a direct fiscal stability issue. Libreville sits directly under the equatorial electrojet.
- **Equatorial Guinea**: SEGESA operates an island and mainland grid serving the Malabo (Bioko) and Bata (continental) zones, with LNG and methanol plant electrification representing some of the most concentrated industrial electrical loads per capita in Africa. Offshore gas platforms add GNSS vulnerability.
- **Chad**: Société Nationale d'Électricité (SNE) operates a small, isolated grid centered on N'Djamena, with the Djermaya solar plant and the Chad-Cameroon oil pipeline pump stations as critical nodes. The Sahelian transmission environment on resistive geology increases GIC coupling.
- **Central African Republic**: ENERCA operates a severely capacity-constrained grid centered on Bangui, with the Boali hydroelectric system as the primary generation source. Grid fragility amplifies any GIC event into a total system collapse.
- **Sao Tome and Principe**: EMAE operates an isolated island grid with diesel generation and growing solar. Zero interconnection. Desalination and fisheries electrification are existential infrastructure.

### Maritime Dependency

The Gulf of Guinea maritime corridor is even more critical for Central Africa than for West Africa — the region's oil economies (Angola, Congo, Gabon, Equatorial Guinea) are entirely dependent on offshore platform operations, tanker loading, and pipeline pump-station electrification. Ports at Luanda, Pointe-Noire, Douala, Libreville, Malabo, and Port-Gentil are fully electrified nodes in the global energy supply chain. The Congo River itself — navigable for thousands of kilometers — depends on GPS/GNSS for barge navigation, lock operations, and port coordination. Aegis-C shielding is directly applicable to every electronic system in this maritime and riverine ecosystem.
