**Document Identifier:** ACS-RVN-FIN-002
**Date:** July 2026

## 1. Cost Structure Overview

Rivian's financial profile is characterized by high fixed-cost intensity associated with vertically integrated vehicle manufacturing, a growing but still sub-scale production base relative to those fixed costs, and significant forward investment in R2 platform development and Georgia facility construction. The path to sustained profitability depends on achieving production scale sufficient to absorb fixed costs, realizing design-to-cost reductions on the R2 platform, and developing high-margin ancillary revenue streams.

The transition to positive consolidated gross profit in Q1 2026 — $119 million at a 9% margin — represents a critical financial inflection point. On a vehicle gross profit basis (excluding regulatory credit revenue and other non-vehicle items), the quarterly result may reflect continued modest negative margins, but the trajectory toward the company's stated goal of positive vehicle gross profit is demonstrably progressing.

### 2.3 R2 Cost Advantage vs. R1

The R2 platform is designed with a fundamentally lower cost structure than the R1 platform. Key cost reduction vectors include:

- **Reduced Component Count:** The R2 platform uses an estimated 30–40% fewer unique parts than the R1, driving down bill-of-materials cost, assembly labor, and supply chain complexity
- **Simplified Manufacturing:** Design-for-manufacturing (DFM) optimizations reduce body shop robot count, weld count, and assembly station count
- **Lower Material Content:** The smaller vehicle footprint reduces raw material consumption (steel, aluminum, battery cells, interior materials) proportionally
- **Shared Electrical Architecture:** The Volkswagen Group JV enables amortization of electronics and software development cost across higher combined volumes
- **Targeted Supplier Cost-Downs:** Rivian has engaged suppliers on cost-reduction roadmaps tied to R2 volume commitments, leveraging the higher projected volumes versus R1

## 3. Operating Expense Structure

### 3.1 Research and Development

R&D expenditure covers vehicle platform engineering (R2, R3), autonomy software development (LDM, Universal Hands-Free), electrical architecture (Volkswagen JV), and advanced manufacturing process development. As a percentage of revenue, R&D has declined from >50% in FY2023 to an estimated 15–25% in FY2026 as revenue scales, though absolute R&D spending continues to grow.

### 3.2 Selling, General and Administrative (SG&A)

SG&A includes retail operations, marketing, corporate overhead, IT systems, legal and regulatory, and insurance. Rivian's D2C model results in lower selling costs per vehicle relative to OEMs supporting dealer networks, but higher corporate overhead as a percentage of revenue during the scaling phase.

### 3.3 EBITDA Trajectory

| Period | Adjusted EBITDA | % of Revenue |
|--------|----------------|--------------|
| FY2023 | $(5.82)B | (131%) |
| FY2024 | $(3.5–4.0)B (est.) | (74–85%) |
| FY2025 | $(2.5–3.0)B (est.) | (46–56%) |
| FY2026 (Guidance) | $(1.80)–$(2.10)B | (28–33%) |

The EBITDA loss trajectory is narrowing consistently as revenue scales and gross margin improves. At the midpoint of FY2026 guidance, the company loses approximately $28,000–33,000 per vehicle delivered, down from an estimated $60,000+ in FY2024 and over $100,000 in FY2023.

## 4. Path to Breakeven

### 4.1 Volume-Driven Fixed-Cost Absorption

The primary path to profitability is volume-driven absorption of fixed manufacturing, R&D, and SG&A costs. At the mid-point of FY2026 guidance (67,500 deliveries), Rivian remains below the estimated breakeven volume threshold. Sensitivity analysis suggests:

- **Breakeven Volume (Vehicle Gross Profit):** ~60,000–70,000 annualized units, potentially achievable by late FY2026 or FY2027
- **Breakeven Volume (Operating Income):** ~120,000–150,000 annualized units, requiring the Georgia plant to reach meaningful production
- **Breakeven Volume (Free Cash Flow Positive):** ~150,000–180,000 annualized units (including ongoing capital expenditures)

### 4.2 Margin Expansion Levers

Beyond pure volume scaling, the path to sustained profitability involves:
- R2 cost structure realization at target levels (estimated $12,000–20,000 per-unit gross margin opportunity versus R1)
- R1 bill-of-materials cost reduction through engineering changes, supplier renegotiation, and volume discounts
- Manufacturing overhead absorption as the Normal and Georgia plants approach utilization targets
- Ancillary revenue growth (software subscriptions, charging revenue, technology licensing)
- Operating expense leverage as revenue growth outpaces R&D and SG&A growth

### 4.3 Timeline to Operating Profitability

Based on current trajectory and forward guidance, a plausible timeline to key profitability milestones:

| Milestone | Estimated Timing | Key Conditions |
|-----------|-----------------|----------------|
| Positive Vehicle Gross Profit | Late FY2026 / FY2027 | R2 ramp sustained; R1 costs controlled |
| Positive Operating Income (Quarterly) | FY2028 | Georgia initial production + Normal at scale |
| Positive Operating Income (Annual) | FY2029–2030 | 200,000+ annual deliveries; Georgia approaching utilization target |
| Positive Free Cash Flow | FY2030+ | CapEx normalizes from Georgia peak; working capital efficiency |

## 5. Cash Consumption and Runway

Rivian held $4.83 billion in cash and equivalents as of mid-2026. At the FY2026 adjusted EBITDA guidance midpoint ($(1.95)B) and capex midpoint ($2.00B), the implied annual cash consumption is approximately $4.0 billion before financing activities. This burn rate, if sustained, implies approximately 12–14 months of cash runway without additional capital.

However, the DOE ATVM loan (first draw early 2027) and the Uber investment ($250 million second tranche, late 2026) provide significant non-equity capital inflows that extend runway substantially. The company may also access additional capital through convertible debt, equity offerings, or further strategic partnerships if required to bridge to sustained positive free cash flow.
