## 2. DOE ATVM Loan Facility

### 2.1 Program Overview

The Advanced Technology Vehicles Manufacturing (ATVM) Loan Program, administered by the U.S. Department of Energy's Loan Programs Office, provides direct loans to eligible manufacturers of advanced technology vehicles and qualifying components. The program was established under Section 136 of the Energy Independence and Security Act of 2007 and has previously supported Tesla (repaid in 2013), Ford, Nissan, and other domestic clean-vehicle manufacturing projects.

### 2.2 Rivian ATVM Loan Terms

| Parameter | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Total Loan Commitment | $4.5 billion |
| Principal | $4,006 million |
| Capitalized Interest | $494 million |
| Purpose | Construction and equipment for Georgia manufacturing facility |

| Repayment Structure | Long-term amortization following construction draw period |
| Collateral | Georgia facility assets, certain corporate guarantees |
| Conditions Precedent | Final DOE environmental review, construction progress milestones, financial covenants |

### 2.3 Financial Impact

The ATVM loan provides several structural advantages:

- **Non-Dilutive Capital:** Unlike equity or convertible debt, the ATVM loan does not dilute existing shareholders, preserving equity value for current investors
- **Below-Market Rate:** As a government program loan, the interest rate is below what Rivian could access in the commercial debt market given its credit profile (unrated or sub-investment grade)
- **Capitalized Interest:** The $494 million capitalized interest component allows Rivian to defer cash interest payments until the facility generates revenue, reducing near-term cash burn
- **Project-Specific Ring-Fencing:** The loan is tied to the Georgia facility rather than the corporate entity, potentially insulating the project from broader corporate credit concerns
- **Validation Signal:** DOE due diligence — spanning technical feasibility, market demand, financial modeling, and environmental review — provides a third-party imprimatur that can favorably influence commercial lender and investor perceptions

### 2.4 Risks and Considerations

- **Drawdown Conditions:** First draw is contingent on meeting conditions precedent, including environmental approvals and construction progress verification. Any delays in meeting these conditions would delay capital availability.
- **Repayment Obligation:** Unlike equity, the loan must be repaid regardless of Georgia facility financial performance, creating a fixed charge on future cash flows.
- **Political Risk:** The ATVM program's existence and funding are subject to political dynamics. A change in administration or Congressional priorities could affect the program, though committed loans are contractually binding.
- **Covenant Compliance:** The loan agreement will include financial and operational covenants; a breach could trigger acceleration or cross-default provisions.

## 3. Strategic Partner Capital

### 3.1 Uber Investment Structure

The $1.25 billion total Uber commitment is structured in tranches:
- $300 million initial investment (Q2 2026) — likely in the form of an equity investment, convertible note, or prepayment against future vehicle deliveries
- $250 million second tranche (late 2026) — contingent on achieving mutually agreed milestones (likely technology, production, or regulatory milestones)
- Remaining $700 million — deployed in subsequent tranches tied to fleet deployment milestones through 2030

The Uber commitment provides strategic validation beyond its monetary value: a major technology platform company is investing in and committing to deploy Rivian's autonomous vehicle technology at scale, signaling confidence in the autonomy roadmap.

### 3.2 Volkswagen Group Joint Venture

The Volkswagen Group JV, with a total commitment exceeding $5 billion, is structured as a combination of:
- Direct equity investment in Rivian
- Technology access and licensing payments for shared electrical architecture
- Joint venture capitalization to fund co-development activities
- Potential manufacturing or supply chain cost-sharing arrangements

The staggered nature of the VW commitment provides a multi-year funding stream that reduces Rivian's reliance on public market capital raises.

## 5. Capital Market Access and Financing Strategy

### 5.1 Historical Capital Raising

Rivian has accessed capital through multiple channels since inception:
- Private venture funding (pre-IPO): ~$10.5 billion raised from investors including Amazon, Ford, T. Rowe Price, and others
- November 2021 IPO: ~$13.7 billion raised at a valuation of approximately $66.5 billion (largest US IPO since 2014 at the time)
- March 2023: $1.3 billion convertible note offering
- 2024–2025: Additional convertible note and equity-linked offerings
- 2026: Uber investment; VW JV capitalization

### 5.2 Future Financing Considerations

Rivian's capital requirements through FY2028–2030 will be driven primarily by Georgia facility construction completion, robotaxi fleet deployment capital, and working capital for scaled production. The company may access additional capital through:
- Further ATVM loan draws (Georgia facility completion)
- Additional strategic partner investments or prepayments
- Convertible note or corporate bond offerings (if credit profile improves)
- Secondary equity offerings (if market conditions and valuation support)
- Asset-backed financing for Uber robotaxi fleet vehicles
- DOE or other government grant programs (Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act, Inflation Reduction Act provisions)

## 6. Credit Profile Assessment

While Rivian is not publicly rated by major credit rating agencies, an indicative assessment suggests a sub-investment grade credit profile (B/B+ equivalent) characterized by:
- **Strengths:** Strong cash position, improving gross margins, diverse capital access channels, strategic partner backing (Amazon, VW, Uber), government loan support (DOE ATVM), valuable brand and technology platform
- **Weaknesses:** Negative operating cash flow, negative EBITDA, high capital expenditure requirements, unproven mass-market product (R2), competitive market dynamics, reliance on government incentives and programs

Credit profile improvement over the next 2–3 years will depend primarily on the pace of gross margin improvement, the R2 production ramp trajectory, and the demonstrated ability to access non-dilutive capital (DOE loan draws, partner payments) to reduce equity market dependency.

## 7. Key Financial Risks

- **Liquidity Timing Mismatch:** If ATVM loan draws, Uber tranche payments, or VW JV payments are delayed, Rivian may face a liquidity gap requiring accelerated equity market access at potentially unfavorable terms.
- **Capital Market Dependency:** The company remains dependent on external capital to fund operations and growth. A deterioration in EV market sentiment, rising interest rates, or company-specific execution issues could impair capital market access.
- **Covenant Risk:** As Rivian adds secured debt (ATVM loan) and potentially other borrowings, financial and operational covenants will constrain financial flexibility.
- **Dilution Risk:** If equity market access is required for additional capital, existing shareholders face dilution at a share price that may not reflect the company's long-term intrinsic value.
- **Concentration Risk:** A significant portion of committed capital is sourced from a small number of counterparties (DOE, Uber, VW), creating concentration risk across both the asset and liability sides of the balance sheet.
