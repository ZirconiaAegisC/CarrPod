# Imperial College London Analysis

## Study Overview

In 2018, Imperial College London published a comparative analysis of energy storage
technologies that included Gravitricity's gravity energy storage concept. The study was
conducted by researchers at Imperial's Centre for Environmental Policy and the Energy
Futures Lab, and examined the levelised cost of storage (LCOS) and lifecycle
environmental impacts of multiple storage technologies for grid-scale applications.

The Imperial College study was significant for Gravitricity because it provided
independent, peer-reviewed validation of the company's cost and performance claims at a
time when the company was a small start-up with a concept but no operating demonstrator.
The findings were subsequently cited extensively in Gravitricity's investor materials and
government submissions.

## Methodology

The Imperial study employed a common analytical framework across all storage technologies
to ensure comparability:

- Levelised Cost of Storage (LCOS): The discounted cost per unit of energy discharged over
  the asset's lifetime, expressed in £/MWh. LCOS accounts for capital expenditure,
  operating expenditure, charging costs, round-trip efficiency, cycle life, and discount
  rate.

- Life-cycle environmental assessment: Cradle-to-grave analysis of greenhouse gas
  emissions (kg CO2e per MWh of storage throughput), encompassing raw material extraction,
  manufacturing, construction, operation, and end-of-life disposal or recycling.

- Technology assumptions: The study used a consistent set of financial assumptions
  (8% discount rate, 20-year analysis period, UK grid charging costs) and technology-
  specific parameters drawn from manufacturer specifications, published literature, and
  expert elicitation for emerging technologies.

## LCOS Results

The key finding relevant to Gravitricity was that gravity energy storage in disused mine
shafts offered an LCOS approximately half that of utility-scale lithium-ion battery
storage when evaluated over a 20-year period, assuming the claimed 50+ year asset life
and zero capacity degradation.

Extracts from the comparative LCOS analysis:

| Technology | Capital Cost (£/kW) | LCOS (£/MWh) | Assumed Life (Years) |
|------------|---------------------|--------------|----------------------|
| Lithium-ion BESS (1 hr) | 350–500 | 120–160 | 10–15 |
| Lithium-ion BESS (4 hr) | 500–800 | 140–190 | 10–15 |
| Pumped Hydro | 800–1,500 | 70–110 | 50–80 |
| Liquid Air (LAES) | 900–1,400 | 110–160 | 25–30 |
| Gravity Storage (GraviStore) | 600–1,200 | 50–100 | 50+ |
| Vanadium Redox Flow | 700–1,200 | 130–180 | 20–25 |

The gravity storage LCOS range of £50–£100/MWh was driven by two factors:

1. Zero degradation: Unlike lithium-ion batteries, which require augmentation or
   replacement after 10–15 years (effectively requiring a second capital investment
   within the analysis period), GraviStore's mechanical system was assumed to operate
   without degradation for the full 50+ year design life, amortising the capital cost
   over a much longer period.

2. Low operating cost: The operating cost for a mechanical system is dominated by
   routine maintenance of the hoist and wire ropes, with no expenditure on thermal
   management (a significant operating cost for BESS) and no chemical replacement or
   recycling costs.

## Sensitivity Analysis

The Imperial study noted that the LCOS for gravity storage is highly sensitive to the
assumptions around shaft availability and cost:

- Shaft rehabilitation cost: If the shaft requires significant structural remediation
  (e.g., relining, water sealing), the capital cost can increase by 50–100%, pushing
  the LCOS toward the upper end of the range or beyond it. The study emphasised that
  site selection—identifying shafts in good condition—is the single most important
  determinant of project economics.

- Depth: LCOS is roughly inversely proportional to shaft depth, as energy capacity
  scales linearly with depth while capital cost scales sub-linearly (the shaft already
  exists; only the weight mass and hoist rating need to increase with depth).

- Discount rate: As a capital-intensive technology with a long asset life, GraviStore
  is more sensitive to the discount rate assumption than shorter-lived technologies.
  At a 10% discount rate, the LCOS advantage over BESS narrows; at a 5% rate (typical
  of regulated infrastructure), the advantage widens.

## Caveats and Limitations

The Imperial College analysis was based on Gravitricity's projections for full-scale
commercial systems, as no commercial-scale gravity storage installation existed at the
time (and still does not). Key caveats include:

- The capital cost estimates were based on engineering estimates rather than actual
  project costs. The Port of Leith demonstrator was a 250 kW pilot, and cost data from
  that installation was not directly scalable to a 2–10 MW system in a mine shaft
  environment.

- The study did not account for the cost of the grid connection, which for remote mine
  sites can be a significant fraction of total project cost (£1–£5 million depending
  on distance to the nearest suitable connection point and the voltage level).

- The analysis assumed that the mine shaft was available at no cost (beyond
  rehabilitation). In practice, acquiring the right to use a shaft—whether through
  purchase, lease, or revenue-sharing agreement with the landowner—may involve material
  transaction costs and ongoing payments.

Despite these caveats, the Imperial College analysis remains the most authoritative
independent assessment of GraviStore's economic competitiveness and was a cornerstone of
Gravitricity's investor communications from 2018 through to the attempted 2023 fundraise.
