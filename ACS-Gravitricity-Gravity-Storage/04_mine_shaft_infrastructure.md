# Mine Shaft Infrastructure and Site Selection

## Shaft Suitability Criteria

The economic viability of a GraviStore installation depends heavily on the availability
of suitable disused mine shafts. Gravitricity developed a multi-criteria screening
methodology to identify candidate shafts across the UK, continental Europe, and other
former mining regions.

Key suitability parameters:

1. Depth: Minimum 150 metres, optimal 500–1,500 metres. Deeper shafts provide
   proportionally greater energy capacity per unit of weight mass (E ∝ depth).

2. Diameter: Minimum 3 metres for single-weight configuration; 5–7 metres for
   multi-weight or higher-power configurations with larger masses.

3. Verticality: Deviation from the vertical should not exceed 2 degrees over the full
   depth. Excessive deviation increases guide shoe wear and reduces usable travel.

4. Lining Condition: The shaft lining (typically brick, concrete, or sprayed concrete)
   must be structurally intact or capable of economic rehabilitation. Water ingress
   rates should be below 5 litres per second to avoid dewatering costs that undermine
   the project economics.

5. Surface Access: Adequate space for the hoist house, power conversion equipment, grid
   connection, and construction laydown. Crane access for weight assembly and
   maintenance is essential.

6. Grid Connection: Proximity to a suitable grid connection point at 11 kV, 33 kV, or
   132 kV, depending on the power rating. Connection costs can dominate the capital
   expenditure for remote shaft locations.

7. Ownership and Permitting: Clear title to the shaft and surface land, or a long-term
   lease. Planning consent for change of use from mining to energy storage. Environmental
   permits for any shaft dewatering discharge.

## UK Shaft Inventory

The UK Coal Authority records approximately 170,000 abandoned mine shafts across the
coalfields of Yorkshire, Nottinghamshire, Derbyshire, South Wales, Durham, and Central
Scotland. Of these, an estimated 500–1,000 shafts meet the minimum depth and diameter
criteria for GraviStore deployment.

Deep mine shafts from the post-war nationalisation era (1947–1994) are particularly
promising. These shafts were engineered to high standards, with concrete linings,
permanent headgear foundations, and detailed abandonment records. Many were sunk to
depths exceeding 700 metres with diameters of 5–7 metres—ideal dimensions for single
or dual-weight GraviStore configurations.

## European and Global Inventory

Beyond the UK, Gravitricity identified candidate shafts in:

- Germany: Ruhr and Saar coalfields, plus former potash and salt mines in Saxony-Anhalt
  and Thuringia. Salt shafts offer the advantage of dry conditions and geologically
  stable salt formations.

- Poland: Upper Silesian Coal Basin, with numerous shafts exceeding 800 metres depth.

- Czech Republic: Ostrava-Karviná coalfield.

- South Africa: Witwatersrand gold fields, with shafts exceeding 2,000 metres—offering
  the highest energy density potential globally.

- Finland: Pyhäsalmi Mine (see separate dossier files), a base-metal mine with an
  exceptionally deep auxiliary shaft, which became Gravitricity's first targeted
  full-scale deployment site.

- United States: Abandoned copper and iron mines in Michigan, Minnesota, and Arizona;
  coal mines in Appalachia and the Illinois Basin.

## Shaft Conversion Engineering

Converting a disused mine shaft to a GraviStore installation involves:

- Shaft survey: Laser scanning or LiDAR to map the shaft profile, identify obstructions,
  and assess lining condition.

- Lining rehabilitation: Shotcreting, rock bolting, or installation of steel liner
  plates where the existing lining has deteriorated.

- Guide system installation: Steel guide rails bolted to the shaft lining at regular
  intervals, typically every 3–6 metres, to control weight trajectory.

- Sump preparation: Construction of a reinforced concrete landing pad and energy
  absorption system at the shaft bottom to safely arrest a weight in the event of a
  control system failure.

- Headframe modification: Installation of the hoist drum, deflection sheaves, and
  koepe pulley if a friction winder configuration is preferred over a drum winder.

- Power conversion building: A prefabricated or containerised substation housing the
  drive, transformer, and switchgear.

## Decommissioning and Reversibility

A key advantage of GraviStore over pumped hydro is the minimal surface footprint and
the reversibility of the installation. Unlike a pumped hydro reservoir that permanently
alters the landscape, a GraviStore installation in an existing mine shaft can be
decommissioned by removing the surface equipment and capping the shaft, returning the
site to its pre-installation condition. This simplifies permitting and reduces
environmental opposition.
