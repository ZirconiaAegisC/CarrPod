# Ofgem Strategic Innovation Fund Project

## Funding Background

In 2023, Gravitricity joined a consortium led by SGN (Scottish Gas Networks, formerly
Scotia Gas Networks) that successfully applied for funding from the Ofgem Strategic
Innovation Fund (SIF). The SIF is a programme administered by Ofgem in partnership with
UK Research and Innovation (UKRI), established under the RIIO-2 price control framework
to support projects that accelerate the transition to a net-zero energy system.

The consortium was awarded approximately £500,000 for a feasibility study examining the
potential of lined rock shaft hydrogen storage as a component of the UK's future gas and
hydrogen infrastructure. The project was known as "H2FlexiStore" after Gravitricity's
trademark for the technology.

## Consortium Members

The consortium comprised six organisations:

1. SGN (Lead Partner): One of the UK's four gas distribution network operators, serving
   5.9 million customers in Scotland and southern England. SGN's interest in hydrogen
   storage derives from its responsibility to maintain security of gas supply in a
   future scenario where natural gas is progressively replaced by hydrogen.

2. Gravitricity Ltd: Technology developer and IP holder for the H2FlexiStore lined rock
   shaft concept. Gravitricity's role was to provide the geotechnical engineering design
   for the shaft and liner system, drawing on its experience with mine shaft
   rehabilitation for the GraviStore gravity storage product.

3. Guidehouse: A global consultancy providing techno-economic modelling of the hydrogen
   storage business case, including market sizing, revenue forecasting, and regulatory
   pathway analysis.

4. University of Edinburgh: School of Engineering and School of GeoSciences, providing
   rock mechanics modelling (finite element analysis of the lined shaft under cyclic
   pressure loading) and hydrogen-rock interaction studies (potential for hydrogen
   reaction with rock minerals, microbial hydrogen consumption, and leakage pathways
   through fracture networks).

5. Energy Reform: A specialist energy consultancy providing regulatory and policy analysis,
   including assessment of the UK's hydrogen storage permitting regime and its alignment
   with the existing gas storage regulatory framework (principally the Planning Act 2008
   regime for nationally significant infrastructure projects).

6. Premzero: A sustainability consultancy providing life-cycle assessment of the
   environmental impacts of H2FlexiStore, including embodied carbon in shaft construction,
   operational emissions, and end-of-life decommissioning.

## Work Packages

The feasibility study was organised into five work packages:

WP1 — Geotechnical Design: Shaft sizing optimisation, liner material selection, pressure
containment analysis. Edinburgh University performed coupled thermo-hydro-mechanical
finite element modelling of the shaft-rock system under cyclic pressure loading (0–200
bar, 50 cycles per year for 30 years). The modelling confirmed that for competent granite
or gneiss (unconfined compressive strength >100 MPa), the rock mass remains within the
elastic regime under all load cases, and that the steel liner-grout-rock composite system
provides adequate containment with a safety factor exceeding 2.0 on ultimate tensile
strength.

WP2 — Hydrogen Compatibility: Assessment of liner material hydrogen embrittlement
susceptibility per ISO 11114-4. Testing of candidate steels (316L, 304L, and API X65
with hydrogen-resistant coating) in 200 bar hydrogen at 10–40°C. The study concluded
that austenitic stainless steels are suitable without special treatment; ferritic
pipeline steels require a hydrogen-resistant lining or coating.

WP3 — Economic Modelling: Levelised cost of storage calculation for a matrix of shaft
dimensions and geological settings. Guidehouse's model estimated a breakeven hydrogen
storage fee of £0.18–£0.32/kg H2 for the reference design, comparing favourably with
above-ground tube trailer storage (£0.50–£0.80/kg) but less favourably than salt cavern
storage (£0.05–£0.15/kg) where geology permits.

WP4 — Regulatory Pathway: Energy Reform produced a gap analysis identifying the absence
of a specific hydrogen storage licensing regime as a barrier to investment. The report
recommended that the UK government establish a regulated asset base (RAB) model or a
cap-and-floor mechanism for strategic hydrogen storage, analogous to the approach used
for electricity interconnectors.

WP5 — Life-Cycle Assessment: Premzero's cradle-to-grave analysis estimated that the
embodied carbon of a 500-metre H2FlexiStore shaft (construction phase, including steel
liner and cement grout) is approximately 2,000–3,500 tonnes CO2e, which is recovered
within 1–2 years of operation through the grid decarbonisation benefit of stored
hydrogen displacing natural gas-fired generation.

## Outcomes and Legacy

The SIF feasibility study was completed in mid-2024. The final report concluded that
H2FlexiStore is technically feasible and economically promising under a range of
scenarios, but that the absence of a hydrogen storage business model and the
uncertainty around the pace of UK hydrogen market development represent material
barriers to commercial deployment within the current decade.

The report remains the property of the consortium and represents a valuable reference
for any acquirer of Gravitricity's IP assets. The geotechnical modelling and hydrogen
compatibility testing data are directly applicable to any future lined rock shaft
hydrogen storage development, regardless of whether it uses the H2FlexiStore trademark.
