# H2FlexiStore Concept and Design

## Concept Origin

H2FlexiStore is Gravitricity's hydrogen storage technology, conceived as a complementary
system to the GraviStore gravity storage platform. Whereas GraviStore addresses
short-to-medium duration electricity storage (seconds to hours), H2FlexiStore targets
inter-seasonal energy storage at the gigawatt-hour scale using compressed gaseous
hydrogen stored in purpose-built underground lined rock shafts.

The concept emerged from Gravitricity's expertise in shaft engineering and geotechnical
design, recognising that the same competencies applied to gravity energy storage could
be repurposed for hydrogen containment. The technology was developed during the period
2022–2024, when it became apparent that the company's gravity storage business alone
could not attract the £40 million targeted in the 2023 fundraise.

## Storage Configuration

H2FlexiStore uses a vertical cylindrical rock shaft, mechanically excavated to a depth
of 300–800 metres with a diameter of 2–5 metres. The shaft is lined with a gas-tight
steel liner, with the annular space between the liner and the rock wall filled with
cement grout to transfer pressure loads to the surrounding rock mass.

The design operating pressure is 50–200 bar, with the surrounding rock providing the
primary pressure containment. This is the same principle used in lined rock cavern
(LRC) gas storage, a technology proven at commercial scale in Sweden (Skallen and
Stenlille sites) for natural gas storage since the early 2000s.

At 200 bar and ambient temperature (~10°C at depth), hydrogen has a density of
approximately 15 kg/m³. A shaft of 500 metres depth and 3 metres diameter provides a
volume of approximately 3,530 m³, capable of storing 53 tonnes of hydrogen—equivalent
to roughly 1.75 GWh of lower heating value energy.

A larger configuration—800 metres depth, 5 metres diameter, 200 bar—stores approximately
100 tonnes of hydrogen, representing 3.3 GWh of raw chemical energy before conversion
back to electricity.

## Comparison with Alternative Hydrogen Storage

| Technology | Pressure | Energy Density | Cost Range (£/kWh) |
|------------|----------|---------------|---------------------|
| H2FlexiStore LRS | 50–200 bar | ~15 kg/m³ (at 200 bar) | £1–5 |
| Above-ground tubes | 50–100 bar | ~7 kg/m³ | £20–40 |
| Salt cavern | 60–180 bar | ~12 kg/m³ | £0.5–2 |
| Depleted gas field | 30–100 bar | Variable | £0.3–1 |
| Liquid H2 (cryogenic) | 1–4 bar | 71 kg/m³ | £30–50 |

H2FlexiStore is not cost-competitive with salt cavern storage, which benefits from
solution-mining economics and self-healing salt behaviour. However, salt caverns are
geographically constrained to regions with suitable salt formations (in the UK:
Cheshire, Teesside, and parts of Yorkshire). H2FlexiStore's advantage is geological
flexibility—hard rock is far more widely distributed, and the technology can be sited
close to industrial hydrogen demand centres or wind/solar generation hubs.

## Electrolyser and Fuel Cell Integration

A complete H2FlexiStore installation includes:

- A proton exchange membrane (PEM) or alkaline electrolyser sized to absorb surplus
  renewable generation during periods of excess production (typically 10–100 MW input).

- Compression train: multi-stage reciprocating or centrifugal compressors to raise
  hydrogen from electrolyser delivery pressure (typically 30 bar) to storage pressure
  (up to 200 bar). Compression work is approximately 6–8% of the hydrogen energy content.

- Decompression and fuel cell (or hydrogen turbine) for reconversion to electricity.
  Round-trip efficiency (electricity → hydrogen → electricity) is approximately
  35–42% for PEM electrolysis and fuel cell, or up to 50% if waste heat is recovered
  for district heating or industrial use.

- Balance of plant: drying, purification, metering, and safety systems including hydrogen
  detection, ventilation, and flare stack.

## Safety Considerations

Hydrogen storage in lined rock shafts presents distinct safety challenges compared to
gravity weight storage. Hydrogen is flammable in air at concentrations between 4% and
75% by volume, has a low ignition energy (0.02 mJ), and can embrittle high-strength
steels through hydrogen-induced cracking.

Mitigations designed into H2FlexiStore include:

- Steel liner materials selected for hydrogen compatibility (e.g., austenitic stainless
  steels or hydrogen-resistant low-alloy steels per ASME B31.12).

- Continuous hydrogen detection in the annular space between the liner and the rock wall,
  with automatic shutdown and venting on detection.

- Inert gas purging of the annular space with nitrogen to eliminate oxygen and prevent
  the formation of a flammable mixture in the event of a leak.

- Depth-based safety: locating the storage volume 300+ metres below ground surface
  provides inherent protection against surface impact events and limits the consequence
  zone of any hypothetical release.

- Redundant pressure relief systems venting to a flare stack sized for the maximum
  credible release rate.

## Development Status

H2FlexiStore received £500,000 in funding from the Ofgem Strategic Innovation Fund (SIF)
as part of a consortium led by SGN (Scottish Gas Networks) and including Guidehouse,
Edinburgh University, Energy Reform, and Premzero. This funding supported a feasibility
study completed in 2024, but no physical prototype was constructed before Gravitricity's
liquidation in October 2025.
