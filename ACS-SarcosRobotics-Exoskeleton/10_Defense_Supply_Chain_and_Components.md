# DEFENSE SUPPLY CHAIN & PRECISION COMPONENTS

## THE CRITICALITY OF THE SUB-TIER SUPPLY CHAIN

The U.S. defense industrial base depends on a layered supply chain in which Tier 1 prime
contractors (Lockheed Martin, RTX, Boeing, Northrop Grumman, General Dynamics) integrate
systems from Tier 2 subsystem suppliers, who in turn rely on Tier 3 and Tier 4 manufacturers
for precision components, castings, forgings, and specialized materials. Crucis Companies
operates primarily at the Tier 3 level, supplying precision-machined components and
sub-assemblies to both Tier 2 integrators and directly to prime contractors on select
programs.

This position in the supply chain confers several structural advantages:

- **Barriers to Entry** — Qualification for a defense program of record typically requires
  18-36 months of first-article testing, process validation, and source inspection approval.
  Once qualified, suppliers rarely face direct competition for the life of a program due to
  the cost and schedule risk of requalification.

- **Program Longevity** — Major weapons systems remain in service for decades. The F-16 is
  in its fifth decade of production; the Tomahawk has been in continuous production since
  1983. Once qualified on these programs, Crucis has visibility into multi-year revenue
  streams with limited competitive threat.

- **Pricing Power** — Sole-source and limited-source positions on mature programs provide
  meaningful pricing power, particularly when production rates increase and the primes face
  schedule pressure.

## KEY PLATFORM EXPOSURE ANALYSIS

### F-35 Lightning II

The F-35 is the largest procurement program in DoD history, with the U.S. services planning
to acquire approximately 2,456 aircraft across the Air Force, Marine Corps, and Navy variants,
plus approximately 600 additional aircraft for international partners and Foreign Military
Sales customers. Annual production rates target 156 aircraft per year through the mid-2030s.

Crucis's F-35 content includes structural and systems components across all three variants
(F-35A CTOL, F-35B STOVL, F-35C carrier variant). The program's longevity and production
volume make it Crucis's most significant single-program exposure. Risk factors include the
ongoing Technology Refresh 3 (TR-3) delays, which have slowed production acceptance, and
congressional pressure on program costs.

### F-16 Fighting Falcon

The F-16 production line has experienced a resurgence driven by FMS demand, particularly
from Taiwan (66 aircraft), Bulgaria, and other partners. Lockheed Martin's Greenville, South
Carolina production line is producing at rates exceeding pre-2017 levels. Crucis supplies
structural and actuation components for the Block 70/72 configuration, the most advanced
F-16 variant.

### Tomahawk Cruise Missile

The Tomahawk program is experiencing a demand surge driven by U.S. Navy inventory
replenishment and expanded FMS interest following demonstrated effectiveness. RTX (Raytheon)
has increased annual production rates, with multi-year procurement contracts providing
visibility through the late 2020s. The Maritime Strike Tomahawk (MST) variant, providing
anti-ship capability, opens an additional demand vector from the Navy's Distributed Maritime
Operations concept.

### Harpoon Anti-Ship Missile

Harpoon demand has been amplified by transfers to Ukraine and Taiwan replenishment
requirements. Boeing's production line has benefited from the Navy's decision to continue
Harpoon procurement alongside the newer Naval Strike Missile (NSM), ensuring continued
production well into the 2030s. MKR's Harpoon components represent a stable, long-duration
revenue stream.

## SUPPLY CHAIN RISK MANAGEMENT

Crucis faces several supply chain risk factors:

1. **Single-Program Concentration** — While Crucis serves multiple programs, the revenue
   concentration in the top 3-4 programs presents risk if any single program experiences
   a production rate cut, as has historically occurred with F-35 and F-16.

2. **Raw Material Availability** — Aerospace-grade titanium, Inconel, and specialty
   stainless steels are subject to supply constraints, particularly given sanctions on
   Russian titanium (VSMPO-AVISMA historically supplied ~30% of global aerospace
   titanium). Crucis's material sourcing strategy and inventory management are critical
   operational factors.

3. **Labor Constraints** — Precision machining requires a skilled workforce that is in
   structural decline in the United States. The average age of a precision machinist in
   the U.S. exceeds 55 years. Crucis's ability to recruit and train the next generation
   of machinists is a long-term strategic consideration.

4. **Prime Contractor Consolidation** — As primes consolidate their own supply chains and
   increasingly insource critical manufacturing (e.g., Lockheed Martin's vertical
   integration strategy), Tier 3 suppliers face the risk of disintermediation. Crucis's
   defense against this is its specialized capabilities and multi-prime relationships,
   which make it more valuable as a shared supplier than as an insourced captive.
