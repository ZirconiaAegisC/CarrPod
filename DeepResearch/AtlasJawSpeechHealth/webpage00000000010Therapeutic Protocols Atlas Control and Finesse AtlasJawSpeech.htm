VIII. Therapeutic Protocols: Control and Finesse

8.3 Phase 3: Neurological Downregulation (The Vagus Reset)

8.3.1 The Autonomic Foundation

The rehabilitation of the stomatognathic system cannot be concluded through mechanical correction alone. Chronic Temporomandibular Disorders (TMD) are frequently somatic manifestations of a dysregulated Autonomic Nervous System (ANS), specifically a sympathetic dominant state. To achieve sustainable outcomes, the clinician must guide the patient into a state of ventral vagal dominance, characterized by social engagement and physiological regeneration.[1, 2]

8.3.3 Ocular-Vestibular Integration Protocols

The eyes and the suboccipital muscles share a direct neurological connection via the cervico-ocular reflex. Ocular movements drive suboccipital tension, and relieving this tension is a cornerstone of the Phase 3 protocol.[2]

Protocol A: The Basic Vagus Reset (Rosenberg’s Maneuver)

  1. Position: Supine, fingers interlaced behind the head (Cranial Cradle).
  2. Action: Keep the head perfectly still. Move only the eyes to look to the extreme right.
  3. Duration: Hold for 30–60 seconds until a yawn, swallow, or sigh occurs.
  4. Repeat: Perform to the left side.
Figure 1: The Basic Vagus Reset. Eyes deviate laterally while head remains fixed, engaging the suboccipital inhibition reflex.

Protocol B: The Half-Salamander

This integrates lateral eye movement with lateral spinal flexion to release the thoracic spine and accessory nerve.[2]

  1. Look to the right.
  2. Tilt head (ear to shoulder) to the right.
  3. Hold for 30–60 seconds.
Figure 2: The Half-Salamander. Mechanical shortening of the cervical musculature combined with ocular engagement.

8.3.4 Auricular Vagal Stimulation

The Auricular Branch of the Vagus Nerve (ABVN) innervates the concha and the posterior aspect of the ear canal. Stimulation here can reduce heart rate and promote systemic relaxation.[3, 4]

Figure 3: Auricular Decompression Vectors. Specific traction angles target the ABVN and temporoparietal fascia.

8.3.6 Somatic Myofascial Release

Protocol H: Intraoral Pterygoid Release

The Lateral Pterygoid is the primary muscle of jaw dysfunction. The release uses sustained, gentle ischemic compression in the vestibule pocket behind the zygomatic arch.[5, 6, 7]

Figure 4: Intraoral Access. The finger slides along the upper vestibule to the pocket behind the tuberosity/zygoma.

8.3.7 Myofunctional Integration

Protocol K: The "Spot" (Nasal Breathing Anchor)

Resting the tongue on the incisive papilla (The Spot) stimulates the nasopalatine nerve and supports the vagal system by enforcing nasal breathing.[8, 9, 10]
