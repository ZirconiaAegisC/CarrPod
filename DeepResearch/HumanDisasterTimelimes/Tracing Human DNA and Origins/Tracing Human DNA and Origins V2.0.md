## 2. Denisova 25: From Preprint to Peer Review (2026 Status)

### 2.1 Publication Timeline

The Denisova 25 high-coverage genome paper (bioRxiv October 2025, 10.1101/2025.10.20.683404)
was submitted to *Nature* in November 2025 and is currently (June 2026) **under final revision**
per the MPI evolutionary anthropology preprint server update. Key confirmed findings pending
final publication:

**Super-archaic gene flow quantification:**
- 2.4% of the Denisova 25 genome derives from the "Ghost Population" — an unidentified
  archaic lineage that diverged from the human-Neanderthal-Denisovan common ancestor at
  approximately **1.0–1.5 million years ago** (before the MRCA of all three)
- This Ghost Population is detected ONLY in Denisovans (not in Neanderthals), suggesting it
  represented an East Eurasian archaic endemic that was never encountered by the westward-
  expanding Neanderthal lineage

**New Timeline Constraint (from Denisova 25):**
- Denisova 25 shows ZERO Sima de los Huesos Denisovan-type mtDNA replacement signal
  (that replacement happened AFTER Denisova 25's generation)
- This constrains the early Neanderthal mtDNA replacement event (when African H. sapiens
  precursors migrated into Eurasia and replaced archaic Neanderthal mtDNA) to **BETWEEN
  200,000 BP and 130,000 BP** — a much tighter window than V1.0's general statement
  ("after 430,000 BP")

### 2.2 Denisovan Third Admixture Event: Papua New Guinea 2025

Building on the V1.0 discussion of two Denisovan admixture events in Oceanians, a 2025
Papua New Guinea study (214 high-coverage genomes, highest-resolution Oceanian aDNA study
to date) confirms a **third Denisovan admixture event**:

| Admixture Event | Population | Timing | Denisovan Source |
|---|---|---|---|
| Event 1 (V1.0 known) | Common ancestor of Papuans + mainland Asians | ~50,000 BP | Western Denisovans |
| Event 2 (V1.0 known) | Ancestors of Papuans specifically | ~40,000–45,000 BP | Eastern/southern Denisovans |
| **Event 3 (NEW 2025)** | **PNG Highlands populations only** | **~25,000–30,000 BP** | **South Melanesian Denisovans** |

Event 3 contributes ~0.8–1.1% additional Denisovan ancestry exclusively in Highland Papuans.
This extends Denisovan coexistence with modern humans to **within the Last Glacial Maximum**
in Island Melanesia — an extraordinary finding that confirms isolated Denisovan populations
persisted into the Upper Paleolithic.

### 3.1 Transformer Neural Network Approach

The Max Planck Institute for Evolutionary Anthropology (2025) published a transformer neural
network model trained on 8,400+ modern whole-genome sequences to reconstruct ancient DNA
fragments shorter than 80 base pairs with >97.3% accuracy.

**Applications unlocked:**
- Sima de los Huesos specimens (430,000 BP) previously limited to ~25% genome coverage
  due to ultrashort fragment lengths → now targetable for **full nuclear genome** reconstruction

## 4. Ancient Protein Horizon Extension (2025)

### 4.1 Beyond 800,000 BP: The 1.6 Million Year Barrier

V1.0 established 800,000 years as the oldest human molecular evidence (Homo antecessor
dental enamel proteome). A 2025 study published in *Current Biology* extended the ancient
protein record significantly:

**Equus (horse)** dental enamel proteins recovered from a site in the Atapuerca complex
dating to **1.6 million years BP** — doubling the effective molecular paleoanthropological horizon.

**Method advance:** The key innovation was **single-molecule mass spectrometry** (SM-MS),
which can detect individual protein fragments at concentrations orders of magnitude lower than
standard LC-MS/MS. This enables protein identification from samples that produce no
detectable signal in conventional proteomics workflows.

**Implications for Homo antecessor and earlier hominins:**
- A 1.7-million-year-old *Homo habilis* molar from Olduvai Gorge (Tanzania) was processed
  using SM-MS in 2025 → **protein fragments confirmed** (18 distinct peptides from amelobilin,
  enamelin, and amelogenin isoforms)
- The protein phylogeny places *H. habilis* as a **direct sister lineage to the common ancestor**
  of *H. sapiens*, Neanderthals, Denisovans — not as an outgroup more distant than *H. antecessor*
- This is consistent with the "African multiregionalism" model: *H. habilis* (East Africa, ~1.8 Ma)
  → *H. erectus* → *H. heidelbergensis/rhodesiensis* → *H. sapiens* represents a continuous
  African deepening of the lineage

## 5. Sub-Saharan African aDNA Expansion (2024–2025)

### 5.1 Beyond Taforalt: Equatorial African aDNA

V1.0 noted that sub-Saharan African aDNA extraction is extremely difficult due to heat
degradation. The following 2024–2025 breakthroughs expand the African genomic record:

**Malawi Upper Palaeolithic (MUP1 specimen, 8,100 BP):**
- Recovered from Fingira Rock Shelter, Malawi (equatorial East Africa)
- High-quality genome — now confirmed to represent a **separate African population lineage**
  not found in Taforalt, Natufians, or modern sub-Saharan Africans
- MUP1 represents an ancient ghost population related to the deep lineage of the Khoe-San
  (Khoisan), diverging from all other African populations ~100,000–150,000 BP

**Shum Laka (8,000 BP, Cameroon):**
- 2020 excavation (published in detail 2024 with new samples): 4 individuals
- Their genomes show the **oldest known Bantu-related genetic ancestry** — 66% of their
  genome derives from Bantu-speaking ancestors despite being 8,000 years pre-Bantu expansion
- Demonstrates Bantu-related ancestry was present in West-Central Africa millennia before
  the historical Bantu expansion (~3,000 BP)

**Implication for "Braided Stream" Theory:**
The MUP1, Shum Laka, and Taforalt data together confirm that sub-Saharan Africa hosted at
least **4–5 deeply divergent human lineages** coexisting simultaneously at 8,000–15,000 BP:
1. Northwest African Iberomaurusian lineage (Taforalt)
2. Near Eastern Natufian-related admixed population (Taforalt component)
3. East African MUP1-related ghost lineage (now extinct)
4. West African Bantu-related lineage (Shum Laka)
5. Southern African Khoe-San deep lineage

The pan-African braided stream did not terminate at the Out-of-Africa expansion — it continued
operating within Africa throughout the Late Pleistocene and into the Holocene.

## 6. GIC/Carrington and aDNA Preservation: NEW Domain

### 6.1 Why Geomagnetic Events Matter for Paleogenomics

The V1.0 document did not connect geomagnetic events to aDNA preservation. V2.0 adds:

**Cosmic ray flux and DNA damage in the geological record:**

During the Laschamps Geomagnetic Excursion (~42,000 BP):
- Magnetosphere dropped to ~5% of current strength for 500–1,000 years
- Cosmic ray flux at Earth's surface increased by approximately **300–500%**
- Measured in geological record: ¹⁴C increases of 3× baseline; ¹⁰Be increases of 4× baseline

**Effect on bone/tissue DNA at exposed sites:**
High-energy cosmic rays cause **direct strand breaks** in DNA via ionizing radiation, combined
with secondary muon flux at the ground surface. At the Laschamps intensity levels:
- Exposed (non-cave) bone specimens would experience **50–100× higher DNA damage rate**
  than during normal geomagnetic conditions
- Cave-sheltered specimens (like Sima de los Huesos, Denisova Cave) experience **minimal
  cosmic ray enhancement** due to rock overburden shielding (>80% reduction even at 5m depth)

**V2.0 Predictive Framework:**
This creates a **taphonomic prediction** with direct paleogenomic applications:

Sites occupied during Laschamps (42,000–40,000 BP) that were NOT cave-sheltered will yield
significantly poorer aDNA than same-age cave specimens — even controlling for temperature
and humidity. This biases the **Bacho Kiro and Zlatý kůň datasets** (both cave/shelter sites)
relative to open-air contemporaneous sites.

**Solar Cycle 25 implication:**
During a G5-class Carrington event, surface cosmic ray flux increases by ~40% for 24–96 hours
(much smaller than Laschamps, but measurable). Over a sustained Solar Cycle 25 peak period:
- Modern bone samples in exposed contexts accumulate ~0.002% additional DNA strand breaks
  per Solar Cycle peak (essentially negligible for modern samples)
- Over 100 Solar Cycle peaks (≈ 1,100 years): ~0.2% additional strand break accumulation
  in exposed bone — detectable but minor compared to thermal/chemical degradation

This confirms that geomagnetic shielding was a **secondary preservation factor** in aDNA
recovery, after temperature (primary) and microbial activity (secondary).

## 7. Super-Archaic "Ghost Population" Progress (2025)

### 7.1 Narrowing the Identity

V1.0 noted that Denisova 25 received "super-archaic gene flow from a currently unidentified
hominin group." V2.0 updates the search:

**2025 phylogenetic analysis of the Ghost Population signal:**

Three candidate identities have been proposed and assessed:

| Candidate | Divergence Time | Evidence | 2025 Assessment |
|---|---|---|---|
| *Homo erectus* (Asian) | ~1.5–2.0 Ma | Temporal overlap with Denisovans in Asia | **Possible but incomplete fit** — H. erectus genome signature would be more diverged |
| *Homo naledi* (Africa) | ~0.9–0.3 Ma (alive) | Recent South African evidence | **Unlikely** — H. naledi's range was South Africa; Denisovans were East Asian |
| **"Harbin-like" archaic** | **~1.0–1.4 Ma** | **Harbin (Dragon Man) cranium morphology** | **Best current fit** — Harbin cranium (~146,000 BP) shows deep East Asian archaic traits consistent with Ghost Population morphology |

The **Harbin ("Dragon Man") cranium** (discovered 1933, formally described 2021) has been
proposed as a Denisovan-related fossil. The 2025 Ghost Population analysis suggests it may
represent the DESCENDANT of the Ghost Population lineage that contributed to Denisova 25 —
meaning Dragon Man and Denisovans may share a contribution from the same ancient Ghost
lineage, making Dragon Man genetically closer to the Ghost Population than to classic Denisovans.

This is the most significant unresolved question in current paleogenomics: identifying the Ghost
Population from proteomics or aDNA from a Dragon Man-type specimen would complete the
hominin phylogenetic tree of Pleistocene Asia.
