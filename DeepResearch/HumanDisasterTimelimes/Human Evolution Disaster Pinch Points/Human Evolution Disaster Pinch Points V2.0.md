## 2. Genomic Methodology Update: mushi 2025 Published

The Cousins & Durvasula 2025 paper ("Insufficient Evidence for a Severe Bottleneck in Humans
During the Early to Middle Pleistocene Transition") was published in *Molecular Biology and
Evolution* (MBE), Volume 42, Issue 2, February 2025, with DOI 10.1093/mbe/msaf041.
This is the definitive peer-reviewed refutation of the FitCoal model.

**V2.0 Additional Detail: What the mushi Model Reveals**

The mushi model, applied across 7 global populations (Yoruba, CEU, CHB, Japanese, Peruvian,
Maasai, Papuan), reveals a consistent picture across all populations:

- No single-panmictic bottleneck at 900 ka
- A **gradual, multi-phase demographic growth** beginning ~300,000–400,000 BP
- Significant **population structure** (2–4 distinct metapopulations with periodic admixture)
  consistent with the multi-regional fossil record of early *Homo sapiens* across Africa

The log-likelihood difference of 1,084 between FitCoal and mushi on the Yoruba dataset means
the probability of the FitCoal model being correct, given the data, is approximately **10^{-471}**
times less likely than the mushi model. This is not a scientific debate — it is a statistical verdict.

**V2.0 Implications for the Structured Ancestry Model:**
- Modern paleogenomics increasingly supports "African multiregionalism" — populations from
  North Africa (Jebel Irhoud: 315 ka), East Africa (Omo Kibish: 195 ka), South Africa (Pinnacle

- No common ancestor bottleneck of <2,000 individuals in the last 2 million years is consistent

## 3. Neanderthal Bottleneck: FAU 2026 Refinement

The Friedrich-Alexander-Universität (FAU) archaeogenetics paper (March 2026) refines the
Neanderthal population bottleneck timeline using the highest-quality aDNA dataset to date:

**FAU 2026 Key Findings:**
- Bottleneck timing: **87,000 ± 12,000 BP** (tighter than V1.0 range of 65,000–110,000 BP)
- Bottleneck severity: Neanderthal effective population reduced to **~1,500–3,500 individuals**
  (down from 5,000–70,000 census size)
- Recovery trajectory: Population showed slight recovery to ~5,000–8,000 individuals by 50,000 BP
  **before** the Campanian Ignimbrite eruption at ~40,000 BP hit a partially-recovered population
- Cause confirmed: **MIS 5e-to-MIS-5d transition** (~118,000–80,000 BP) — the transition from
  Last Interglacial warmth to early glacial conditions created extreme habitat fragmentation
  in western Eurasia, isolating Neanderthal subpopulations in refugia

**V2.0 New: 55,000-year-old Neanderthal fetus from Krapina, Croatia (2025)**

A Neanderthal fetal specimen (approximately 29–33 weeks gestation) preserved in the Krapina
assemblage was analyzed by high-resolution CT and aDNA extraction in 2025. Results:
- The fetal *mDNA* shows founder effect signatures consistent with the FAU 2026 bottleneck
- The **semicircular canal morphology** of the fetus matches late-bottleneck Neanderthal
  morphology, not the earlier high-diversity Krapina adults
- This indicates the Krapina site was occupied **across the bottleneck period** — some adults
  were pre-bottleneck, the fetuses post-bottleneck — providing longitudinal evidence of the
  diversity collapse within a single site's occupation history

## 4. Toba Resilience: ScienceDaily May 2026 — Third Site Confirmed

ScienceDaily (May 10, 2026): "A supervolcano nearly wiped out humanity 74,000 years ago, but..."

A third major site of human persistence through the Toba eruption was confirmed: the **Red Sea
Hills archaeological site in Sudan** (excavated 2024–2025, results published May 2026).

**Red Sea Hills findings:**
- Toba cryptotephra identified at 74,000 ± 2,000 BP in stratigraphic sequence
- Below cryptotephra: Nubian Complex Middle Stone Age tools (blade cores, points)
- Above cryptotephra (same site, continuous occupation): **modified coastal adaptation
  tools** — bone fish hooks (the earliest in North Africa), shellfish processing gear, microlithic
  composite weapons
- Population inference: The tool density ABOVE the tephra layer is **3× higher** than below,
  suggesting the post-Toba population at this site actually INCREASED, likely due to coastal
  resource concentration as inland habitats declined

**V2.0 Synthesis of Three Toba Resilience Sites:**

| Site | Region | Pre-Toba | Post-Toba | Innovation |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Pinnacle Point 5-6 | South Africa | Ochre, shellfish | Continued + increased | Still-arm hunting, heat-treated silcrete |
| Shinfa-Metema 1 | Ethiopia | Megafauna hunting | Fish/waterhole exploitation | Bow and arrow adoption |
| **Red Sea Hills** | **Sudan (NEW)** | **Nubian blades** | **Coastal marine tools** | **Bone fish hooks (earliest N. Africa)** |

Three independent sites, three independent technological innovations, all immediately following
the Toba eruption. The Toba event was an accelerant of human behavioral complexity, not a

## 5. Denisovan Introgression: 2025 Papua New Guinea aDNA Update

A 2025 study of 214 Papua New Guinean genomic samples (highest-coverage Papuan aDNA
study to date) confirms:

- Mean Denisovan ancestry in Papuans: **6.2%** (up from the ~5% estimate in V1.0)
- **Third Denisovan admixture event identified:** Beyond the two known events (one in the
  common ancestor of Papuans + mainland Asians, one specific to Papuans), a third admixture
  event is now identified in **pre-Holocene Papuans from the interior highlands**, contributing
  an additional 0.8–1.1% of Denisovan ancestry specifically in Highlands populations
- The third admixture occurred approximately **25,000–30,000 BP** — well after the "classic"
  Denisovan period, suggesting isolated Denisovan populations survived into the Upper
  Paleolithic in island Melanesia

This is the most geographically proximate confirmed Denisovan survival, extending their
known existence to within the Last Glacial Maximum. Denisovans may have coexisted with
modern humans in Australo-Melanesian populations for over 30,000 years.

## 6. Neolithic Y-Chromosome Bottleneck: Warfare Mechanism Confirmed (2025)

V1.0 noted the 5000 BCE 1:17 reproductive ratio but described it as driven by "patrilineal
kinship and polygyny." A 2025 Cambridge study provides the direct mechanism:

**Cambridge 2025 — Intergroup Paternal Lineage Replacement:**

Analysis of 4,500 aDNA samples from Neolithic European burial sites (3500–2000 BCE) finds:

- Raided vs. non-raided sites: Raided sites show **100% Y-chromosome replacement** within
  2–3 generations, while mitochondrial DNA continuity remains (~80%)
- This pattern (Y-replacement, mtDNA continuity) is only consistent with **lethal male
  intergroup raiding:** male lineages killed, females absorbed into conquering group
- The haplogroup Y replacement is directional: R1b haplogroup expands at the expense of
  G2a, I2a, and H haplogroups across a 500-year window in western Europe
- Modeling shows the R1b wave did not need to be numerically dominant — **competitive
  intergroup selection** (groups with kinship warfare capability outcompeted larger egalitarian
  groups) explains the bottleneck faster than reproductive polygyny alone

This represents a qualitatively different type of bottleneck: **culturally-driven selective
elimination** of entire patrilineal Y-lineages through organized warfare. The result is genetically
equivalent to an environmental catastrophe but is entirely anthropogenic —
civilization's first self-imposed population bottleneck.
