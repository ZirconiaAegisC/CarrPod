## 1. The Laschamps Excursion — 42,000 BP

The Laschamps Event (named after the Laschamps lava flows in the Massif Central, France,
where it was first identified) was the most extreme geomagnetic excursion of the last
100,000 years.

**Key parameters:**
- **Date:** 42,200-41,500 BP (radiocarbon calibrated, confirmed by ¹⁰Be and ³⁶Cl
  cosmogenic isotope records from GISP2 Greenland ice core)
- **Field strength:** Dropped to approximately **5-6% of current intensity** at minimum,
  compared with ~10-20% for typical Holocene excursions
- **Duration:** ~700-800 years (rapid onset within ~250 years, recovery over ~500 years)
- **Polarity reversal:** NOT a full reversal (poles did not permanently switch), but
  the field exhibited a brief, extreme excursion with multipolar structure

## 2. Environmental and Biological Consequences

The Laschamps Event is associated with significant global environmental changes:

**Cosmic ray flooding:** With magnetosphere shielding reduced by ~95%, galactic cosmic
ray (GCR) flux at Earth's surface increased dramatically:
- ¹⁴C production spike confirmed in tree ring and ice core records
- ¹⁰Be spike in ice cores (GISP2, EPICA Dome C)
- Estimated effective radiation dose increase at ground level: **2-3 times** normal
  background, equivalent to approximately 6-8 mSv/year

**Ozone depletion:** Increased GCR flux produced nitric oxide (NO) in the stratosphere,
catalyzing ozone destruction:
- Estimated ozone column reduction: 10-15% globally, up to 30% at mid-latitudes
- Increased surface UV-B radiation with documented biological impacts

**Climate effects:** The Laschamps coincided with a period of **mega-drought** in
Australia (documented by lake sediment and speleothem records) and the extinction of
multiple Australian megafaunal species. The role of the geomagnetic excursion vs.
human arrival and climate change in these extinctions remains debated.

**Auroral displays:** The aurora borealis would have been visible at the equator during
Laschamps minimum, creating the celestial phenomena that Brodsky V2.0 identifies as
the "signs in the sky" encoding preserved in the SKL and related traditions.

## 4. The Australian Archaeological Record

Australian Aboriginal oral traditions record the rising of sea levels after the last
glacial maximum (~18,000-7,000 BP) with documented geographic accuracy (Nunn and Reid,
2016). If oral traditions can preserve 10,000+ year geological memories, the SKL's
encoding of geomagnetic excursion events falls within the demonstrated memory capacity
of human oral cultures.

**Nunn, P.D., Reid, N.J.** "Aboriginal Memories of Inundation of the Australian Coast
Dating from More than 7,000 Years Ago." *Australian Geographer*, 2016. — 21 independent
Aboriginal oral traditions from around the Australian coastline accurately describe
sea level rises documented by geological evidence, confirming that oral traditions can
preserve accurate environmental observations across millennia.
