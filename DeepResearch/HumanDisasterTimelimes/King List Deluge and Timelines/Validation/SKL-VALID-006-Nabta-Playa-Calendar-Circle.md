## 1. Nabta Playa Site Overview

Nabta Playa (22°32'N, 30°42'E, Nubian Desert, Egypt) is the oldest known astronomical
observatory, predating Stonehenge by at least 5,000 years.

- **Lead excavator:** Fred Wendorf, Southern Methodist University
- **Excavation period:** 1974–2001 (intermittent), with major findings reported 1998-2001
- **Key publication:** Wendorf, F., Schild, R., et al. "Megaliths and Neolithic Astronomy
  in Southern Egypt." *Nature*, Vol. 392 (1998), pp. 488-491
- **Site dating:** 15,000–10,000 BP (based on cattle bone collagen and hearth charcoal
  beneath megalith alignments, calibrated via radiocarbon dating)
- **Annual rainfall at occupation:** ~500 mm (currently receiving <5 mm annually) —
  confirming a radically different climate regime during the African Humid Period

## 2. The Calendar Circle — Precision Astronomical Alignment

Nabta Playa's central feature is a calendar circle of sandstone slabs:
- Six megalith alignments radiating from a central point
- A stone circle with four gateways aligned to cardinal directions
- **Astronomical alignments confirmed by archaeoastronomer J. McKim Malville**
  (University of Colorado): Sirius, Arcturus, Alpha Centauri, and Orion's Belt
- **Alignment date:** The stones are aligned to the positions of these stars as they
  appeared at **17,700 BP** (due to precession of the equinoxes — the stars have
  shifted 27° in azimuth in 17,700 years)

This alignment date of **17,700 BP** is significant: it predates the Younger Dryas
impact (12,800 BP) by approximately **5,000 years**. This means the Nabta Playa builders
were tracking stellar positions while the last Ice Age was still in progress —
establishing astronomical record-keeping as a human practice LONG before the emergence
of Sumerian or Egyptian civilization.
