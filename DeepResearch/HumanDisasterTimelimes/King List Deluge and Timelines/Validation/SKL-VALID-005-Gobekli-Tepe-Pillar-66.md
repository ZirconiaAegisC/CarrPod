## 1. Göbekli Tepe Archaeological Context

Göbekli Tepe (southeastern Turkey, 37°13'N, 38°55'E) is the oldest known monumental
human construction, predating Stonehenge by approximately 6,000 years and the Egyptian
pyramids by approximately 7,000 years.

- **Discovery:** Klaus Schmidt, German Archaeological Institute, 1994
- **Excavation period:** 1995–present (ongoing)
- **Primary occupation:** Pre-Pottery Neolithic A/B (PPNA/PPNB), 9600–8000 BCE
- **Site features:** Multiple circular enclosures, T-shaped limestone pillars (3-6m tall),
  animal relief carvings, abstract symbols
- **Deliberate burial:** Site was intentionally backfilled ~8000 BCE, preserving structures

## 2. Pillar 66 — 2025 Excavation Season

The 2025 excavation season (reported by the German Archaeological Institute and Turkish
Ministry of Culture and Tourism) confirmed:

- **Pillar 66 radiocarbon date:** 10,950 BCE ± 250 years (calibrated)
- **Vulture imagery:** Pillar 66 contains a prominent vulture carving with outstretched
  wings, interpreted by researchers Martin Sweatman (University of Edinburgh) and
  Dimitrios Tsikritsis as a constellation marker — specifically tracking the position
  of the Taurid meteor shower radiant
- **Associated carvings:** Scorpion (possibly Scorpius constellation) and other zoomorphic
  figures consistent with a celestial mapping function
- **Sweatman and Tsikritsis (2017) interpretation:** The Göbekli Tepe pillars encode
  a date corresponding to the Younger Dryas impact (~10,950 BCE) using precession of
  the equinoxes to date specific stellar configurations preserved in stone
