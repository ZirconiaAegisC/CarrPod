## PART ONE: THE DOCUMENT ITSELF

### 1.1 What the Sumerian King List Is

The Sumerian King List (SKL) is not a single document but a textual tradition preserved across multiple clay tablets and prisms, the most complete of which is the Weld-Blundell Prism (WB 444, Ashmolean Museum, Oxford), compiled during the Isin-Larsa period approximately 2100-1800 BCE. The text exists in at least 16 known exemplars from Nippur, Larsa, Kish, and other Mesopotamian sites.

It opens with a phrase that has puzzled scholars for over a century:

> *"After the kingship descended from heaven, the kingship was in Eridu."*

What follows is a list of kings, their cities, and the lengths of their reigns, divided into two distinct sections: eight (or in some versions, ten) antediluvian kings who ruled for a combined 241,200 years before a great flood, followed by post-diluvian dynasties whose reigns become progressively more plausible — still exaggerated, but trending toward historical durations.

The antediluvian section reads as follows (composite from multiple exemplars):

| King | City | Reign (years) |
|------|------|---------------|
| Alulim | Eridu | 28,800 |
| Alalgar | Eridu | 36,000 |
| En-men-lu-ana | Bad-tibira | 43,200 |
| En-men-gal-ana | Bad-tibira | 28,800 |
| Dumuzid the Shepherd | Bad-tibira | 36,000 |
| En-sipad-zid-ana | Larak | 28,800 |
| En-men-dur-ana | Sippar | 21,000 |
| Ubara-Tutu | Shuruppak | 18,600 |
| **Total** | | **241,200** |

After Ubara-Tutu, the text states simply: *"Then the Flood swept thereover."*

The post-diluvian section begins with the First Dynasty of Kish, whose kings reign for 1,200, 960, 900, and 1,560 years — still impossible as individual lifespans, but an order of magnitude more plausible than the antediluvian figures. By the time the text reaches the historically attested Early Dynastic period (approximately 2900-2350 BCE), the reign lengths approach realistic durations: 30, 15, 9, 8, 36, and 6 years for the kings of Uruk.

### 1.2 The Three-Tiered Chronological Problem

Any analysis of the SKL must confront three distinct problems, operating at different scales:

**Tier One — The Biological Problem:** No human being has ever lived for 28,800 years. The longest verified human lifespan is 122 years. The antediluvian reigns therefore cannot represent individual human lifetimes. This is the most basic recognition and the one from which all interpretation must begin.

**Tier Two — The Mathematical Problem:** The antediluvian reigns are all multiples of 3,600 — the Sumerian unit known as a SAR (also a "cosmic year" in later Babylonian astronomy, corresponding to one degree of precession of the equinoxes). Division by 3,600 yields more manageable figures: Alulim's 28,800 years becomes 8 SARs; Dumuzid's 36,000 years becomes 10 SARs; the total 241,200 becomes exactly 67 SARs. This sexagesimal structure is deliberate and systematic — not random exaggeration. The question is: what were the scribes counting?

**Tier Three — The Mythological Problem:** The SKL is clearly not a straightforward historical document. But neither is it pure fiction. It contains demonstrably historical kings in its later sections (Enmebaragesi of Kish, Gilgamesh of Uruk) whose existence has been confirmed by independent archaeological evidence. The transition from mythological to historical is gradual — the same document that claims Alulim reigned for 28,800 years also correctly records the sequence of Early Dynastic kings at Ur. The SKL is a hybrid document — part cosmological memory, part political propaganda, part genuine historical record.

These three tiers must be addressed simultaneously. An interpretation that explains the mathematics but dismisses the mythology is as incomplete as one that accepts the mythology but ignores the biology.
