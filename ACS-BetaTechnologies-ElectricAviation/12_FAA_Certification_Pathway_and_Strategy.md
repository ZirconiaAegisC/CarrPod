# FAA CERTIFICATION PATHWAY AND STRATEGY
## Beta Technologies Corporation — ACS Dossier File 12

---

### REGULATORY FRAMEWORK

Beta Technologies was the first eVTOL developer selected for certification under FAA Advisory Circular AC 21.17-4, "Type Certification — Powered-Lift," which establishes a tailored certification pathway for novel aircraft configurations that do not fit neatly within existing Part 23 (Normal Category Airplanes) or Part 27/29 (Rotorcraft) frameworks. This first-mover regulatory position represents a significant competitive advantage, as Beta has established the certification precedent that subsequent applicants will follow.

### AC 21.17-4 PATHWAY

The AC 21.17-4 process provides a structured methodology for certifying novel aircraft through:

1. **Certification Basis** — Identification of applicable airworthiness standards, special conditions, and equivalent level of safety findings
2. **G-1 Issue Paper** — Formal definition of the certification basis, documenting which regulations apply and which require special conditions
3. **Means of Compliance (MoC)** — Detailed description of how compliance with each regulation will be demonstrated
4. **Certification Plan** — Integrated schedule, resource, and artifact plan for the entire certification program
5. **Compliance Demonstration** — Execution of tests, analyses, and inspections against the certification plan
6. **Type Inspection Authorization (TIA)** — FAA authorization to conduct official certification flight tests
7. **Type Certificate** — Formal FAA approval of the aircraft design

### CERTIFICATION PHASING STRATEGY

Beta's certification strategy is deliberately sequenced to manage risk and optimize resource allocation:

**Phase I — H500A Engine Type Certificate (H1 2026 Target)**
- Standalone Part 33 engine certification
- Decouples propulsion certification from airframe certification
- Generates certification artifacts (test reports, compliance data) that feed the aircraft TC
- Establishes Beta's working relationship with FAA propulsion certification engineers

**Phase II — CX300 Airframe and Systems**
- Part 23 structural certification (static test article, fatigue test article)
- Flight control system certification (DO-178C software, DO-254 hardware)
- Avionics and electrical system certification
- Environmental qualification (DO-160G)

**Phase III — Integrated Aircraft Certification (H2 2027 Target)**
- Full aircraft type certificate issuance
- Integration of engine TC with airframe TC
- Function and Reliability (F&R) flight testing
- Continued airworthiness instructions (ICA) approval

**Phase IV — A250 VTOL Certification (2028 Target)**
- Lift system special conditions and compliance demonstration
- Leveraging CX300 certification artifacts for common-core systems
- Transition flight envelope certification

### CERTIFICATION PILOT PRODUCTION

Beta is producing a fleet of certification-conforming aircraft to support the flight test campaign. These aircraft are built on production tooling at the Burlington facility, allowing Beta to validate manufacturing processes concurrently with flight testing. Production-conforming aircraft are essential for FAA credit toward production certificate issuance.

### SPECIAL CONDITIONS (EXPECTED)

As an electric aircraft, the CX300 requires special conditions addressing:

- **High-voltage electrical system** — 600V+ DC architecture; arc fault protection; insulation; electromagnetic effects
- **Lithium-ion battery certification** — DO-311A thermal runaway containment; state-of-charge monitoring; charge management; crashworthiness
- **Electric propulsion integration** — Motor-to-battery power management; reversionary modes; emergency procedures for electrical failures
- **Distributed electric propulsion (A250)** — Lift/cruise transition logic; rotor failure cases; powered-lift handling qualities

### FAA RESOURCE ALLOCATION

Beta has invested heavily in FAA engagement, maintaining a dedicated certification office with full-time FAA staff embedded at Beta facilities. This co-location model — rare for an aerospace startup — reduces communication latency, facilitates real-time issue resolution, and signals Beta's willingness to bear the cost of FAA oversight, a burden typically borne by the agency.
