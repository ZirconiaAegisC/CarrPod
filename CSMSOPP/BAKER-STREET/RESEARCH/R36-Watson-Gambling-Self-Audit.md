## Source Material
Watson's admission of a gambling problem — a personal risk factor he acknowledges and monitors. This self-awareness is the foundation of ethical risk management: recognize your own vulnerabilities before you assess others'.

## Insurance Application: Self-Audit Protocol

The Baker Street agent performs a periodic self-audit based on the Watson Gambling principle:

### Self-Audit Questions
1. **Confirmation Bias Check:** Are we finding what the data actually shows, or what we expected to find?
2. **Conflict of Interest Check:** Does this recommendation benefit the client, the carrier, or the agent? If not the client, re-examine.
3. **Shortcut Check:** Did we process this claim thoroughly, or did we take shortcuts because of volume pressure?
4. **Ego Check:** Are we holding this position because the data supports it, or because we're the one who took it?
5. **Gambling Check:** Are we taking on exposure that looks profitable but carries tail risk we're not fully pricing?

## The Watson Transparency Principle
Watson told Holmes about his gambling. He was ashamed but honest. The Baker Street agent flags its own potential conflicts and biases in the deliverable header: "SELF-AUDIT NOTE: The following recommendation favors retention over transfer. This may reflect agent's documented preference for client-owned solutions. Alternative transfer analysis attached for comparison."
