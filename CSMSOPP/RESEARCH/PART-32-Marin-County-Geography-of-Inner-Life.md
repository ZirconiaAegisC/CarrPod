# PART 32 — Marin County: The Geography of the Inner Life

**Research Category:** Environment / Home / The Landscape That Held Him

Robin Williams lived in various places throughout his life — New York, San Francisco, Los Angeles — but Marin County, California, became his home in the deepest sense. He lived there for much of his adult life, raised his children there, cycled its hills, and died there. Marin sits just north of San Francisco, across the Golden Gate Bridge — one of the most beautiful counties in California, with redwood forests, rolling pasturelands, the rocky Pacific coastline at Point Reyes, and the San Francisco Bay to the east.

**The fit between person and place:** The specific geography of Marin seems calibrated for Williams' dual nature. The dramatic, trail-threaded natural landscape rewards physical challenge and offers genuine solitude. Adjacent to the dense cultural vitality of San Francisco, accessible via the Golden Gate. Mount Tamalpais — the dominant peak — offers cycling routes ranging from demanding to punishing. Williams was a Mount Tam regular. The mountain was not a backdrop to his life. It was a participant.

**The community:** Marin was a specific community in which Williams was a recognizable, locally-embedded figure. He was visible at local restaurants, at community events, at his children's school activities — not as a celebrity passing through but as a neighbor. Multiple accounts from Marin residents confirm: Williams remembered people's names, stopped to actually talk, was genuinely interested in the particular human standing in front of him. The same fundamental orientation that characterized his performance relationships characterized his daily-life relationships. Full attention. Genuine curiosity.

**Camp Winnarainbow:** Williams' connection to Wavy Gravy and Camp Winnarainbow — a circus performance camp for underprivileged children in nearby Mendocino County — expressed the same ethos as his philanthropy broadly: performance as joy, joy as a right for all children regardless of economic circumstances. He performed for campers and contributed to the camp's resources for years.

**Tiburon — the final address:** Williams' final home was in Tiburon, a small town on a peninsula in Marin County jutting into San Francisco Bay, with views of Angel Island and the distant San Francisco skyline. He died there on August 11, 2014. The landscape remains: the bay, the mountain, the redwoods. The place that held him holds the light differently without him in it.
