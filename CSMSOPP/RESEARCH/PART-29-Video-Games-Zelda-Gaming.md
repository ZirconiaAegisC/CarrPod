# PART 29 — Video Games: The Legend of Zelda and the Gamer Who Named His Daughter

**Research Category:** Personal Life / Popular Culture / Digital Worlds

Robin Williams' relationship with video games was deep, genuine, and documented. He was not a casual player — he was an enthusiast who maintained detailed current knowledge of gaming culture, discussed games with the specific vocabulary of someone who actually played them, and incorporated gaming references into his comedy in ways that rang true to gaming communities rather than sounding like a parent trying to connect.

His most documentable gaming passion was for *The Legend of Zelda* franchise — the Nintendo action-adventure series following Link on a quest to protect Princess Zelda. Williams loved the series enough to name his daughter Zelda Rae Williams — the first widely known person to name a child after a video game character.

**The Nintendo commercial (2012):** Robin and Zelda Williams appeared together in a Nintendo commercial promoting *Skyward Sword*. The chemistry is easy and warm — genuine, not performed. They are clearly people who have actually played this game together. The commercial is charming in itself, but it is also a document of a father and daughter who had a real shared enthusiasm and were comfortable enough in their relationship to be in a commercial without the seams showing.

**Gaming as worldbuilding:** Williams' attraction to narrative adventure games makes sense in his broader creative psychology. He had been a worldbuilder since the toy soldier days in Bloomfield Hills. Video games, at their best, are elaborate interactive worlds — systems of rule and possibility that reward investment with increasingly deep access to a constructed reality. For a mind structured as a generator of instantaneous imaginary worlds, video games offered something unusual: someone else had done the worldbuilding already, and he could inhabit the result.

**The Genie tribute — full circle:** When Williams died in 2014, Nintendo released a tribute video featuring Link and Zelda. Williams had played the Genie — an entity of PHENOMENAL COSMIC POWERS enormously helpful to a young man on a quest, who must be set free at the end. And he had spent years helping introduce a real-world Zelda to a real-world quest through a digital world he loved. The Genie and Link are not so different: both are helpers in other people's heroic journeys.

**For the module — worldbuilding as instruction:** The gaming enthusiasm is consistent with the fundamental orientation that ran through everything Williams did: genuine enthusiasm for constructed worlds, specific pleasure of inhabiting a framework of possibility and seeing what you can make of it within the rules. This is also what the Williams Heuristic does: operate within the constraints of a formal communication task and find, within those constraints, the maximum possible expression of humanity.
