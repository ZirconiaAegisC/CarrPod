# FACT TRANSFORMATION ENGINE — FROM DATA TO EXPERIENCE
## CSMSOPP000001 | MODULE-V2 | The Process of Making Information Human

---

> **CORE INSIGHT:** A fact is not automatically useful. A fact must be received
> to be useful, and reception requires context, comparison, and connection to
> what the recipient already knows. Williams never delivered a bare fact.
> He always delivered a fact already wearing its human clothes.
>
> The Fact Transformation Engine is the systematic process for taking raw data
> from a communication brief and converting each significant fact into the form
> that lands most effectively — with the least mental resistance and the most
> lasting memory encoding.

---

## SECTION 1: THE TRANSFORMATION SEQUENCE

Every significant fact passes through this sequence before appearing in
the Meta-Commentary. The raw data appears unchanged in the Core Deliverable.
The transformation ONLY appears in the Meta-Commentary.

```
STEP 1: RECEIVE THE FACT exactly as provided
STEP 2: CLASSIFY the fact by type (number / time / cost / specification / risk)
STEP 3: SELECT the appropriate transformation technique for this type
STEP 4: DRAFT the transformation
STEP 5: TEST the transformation against the four criteria:
         (a) More memorable than the raw fact?
         (b) Accurate — does it represent the real scale correctly?
         (c) Appropriate to the severity context?
         (d) Serves the communication rather than performing cleverness?
STEP 6: PLACE the transformation in the Meta-Commentary
STEP 7: CONFIRM the raw fact appears unchanged in the Core Deliverable
```

## SECTION 2: TRANSFORMATION BY FACT TYPE

### TYPE A — NUMERIC QUANTITIES

**Raw fact pattern:** "[Large number] [units]"
**Transformation target:** The number attached to something the reader has
experienced with their body

**The technique — Hierarchical Comparison:**
1. Find a reference point at the right scale for the audience
2. The reference point must be MORE vivid than the number
3. If possible, find TWO references — one at scale, one that creates absurdity

**Size number transformation examples:**

*"262 kilowatts dissipated in a 200kg engine block"*
→ "262 kilowatts — roughly the output of 262 household microwave ovens —
applied to something the size of your torso — for thirteen minutes.
Your engine block does not have a plan for this."

*"SE = 92 dB EMI shielding"*
→ "92 decibels of electromagnetic shielding. A normal conversation is 60dB.
A construction site is 85dB. This vehicle's EMI shielding is louder than a
construction site — except instead of noise, it's blocking electromagnetic
radiation. The vehicle is not listening to the storm. The storm is talking
to a wall."

*"LiFePO₄ rated to 50 kGy radiation hardness"*
→ "The battery is hardened to 50 kilogray of radiation exposure.
The International Space Station crews receive about 0.3 gray per year.
To overwhelm this battery with radiation you would need to subject it
to conditions that would be medically incompatible with the presence
of the passenger reviewing the quarterly projections."

### TYPE B — TIME SPANS

**Raw fact pattern:** "[Duration] [context]"
**Transformation target:** The duration mapped onto a lived experience

**The technique — Life Event Mapping:**
1. Find the life event or cultural event that matches the duration
2. Prefer shared cultural experiences (product cycles, media seasons, milestones)
3. For very long durations: use generational reference ("old enough to start school")
4. For very short durations: use comparisons to things that feel fast vs. slow

**Time transformation examples:**

*"T-52 minutes to tsunami impact"*
→ "52 minutes. That is the full runtime of any episode of The Bear, any episode
of Ted Lasso, the second half of any major sporting event. It is both enough time
and exactly not the amount of time that should be used watching any of those things."

*"13 minutes for engine block to reach melt temperature under G5 GIC"*
→ "13 minutes. The time needed to soft-boil an egg. The time
between most subway stops in New York. The time your car has before
it becomes a permanent sculptural feature of wherever it was parked."

*"18 months for transformer replacement"*
→ "18 months. Long enough that anyone pregnant today will have a walking child
before the grid comes back. Long enough for two holiday seasons, one complete
fiscal year, and a complete cycle of seasonal emotions about the outdoors."

*"Flash sintering at 1800°C"*
→ "The ZrB₂-SiC ceramic is flash-sintered at 1800 degrees Celsius.
That is hotter than the surface of Venus. Hotter than lava by a factor of two.
It is a temperature at which steel has been liquid for quite some time.
The ceramic is being made at a temperature at which steel is a memory.
That is the ceramic. That is what the vehicle's structure is made of."

### TYPE C — COST FIGURES

**Raw fact pattern:** "$[amount]"
**Transformation target:** The cost in terms of things people actually buy

**The technique — The Comparison Ladder:**
Start with the cost as stated, then build one or two comparisons that give it
human scale from two different angles — one that makes it seem reasonable,
one that makes it seem significant. Let the reader hold both simultaneously.

**Cost transformation examples:**

*"BFRP rebar at $55-75 per 6m bar"*
→ "$55-75 per six-meter structural rebar bar — comparable to a good restaurant
lunch, or three or four cups of bean-to-cup coffee, depending on your relationship
with artisanal beverages. For rebar that will not corrode, will not conduct GIC,
and will outlast the building it's in by decades if necessary.
The cost per year of service is approximately the price of a breath mint."

*"$496M Project Noah's Aegis program"*
→ "$496 million to harden all 93 Carnival ships against Carrington events.
That sounds large until you price it per passenger: the fleet carries roughly
200,000 passengers at any given moment. The per-passenger cost of the full
program is about $2,500 — roughly the price of a mid-tier cabin upgrade on
the very cruise you would be on when the solar storm arrives."

*"$9,500 COGS for Aegis-Ascension drone backpack"*
→ "$9,500 to manufacture a backpack that lifts 120 kilograms, flies 45 kilometers,
and navigates without GPS. For context: a professional-grade camera drone runs
$2,000-$8,000 and lifts a camera. This lifts a person. The price-to-lift-ratio
is, depending on how you value your continued ability to be airborne and inland
during a coastal emergency, extremely competitive."

### TYPE D — TECHNICAL SPECIFICATIONS

**Raw fact pattern:** "[Technical term] = [value] [unit]"
**Transformation target:** What does this specification MEAN in practice?
What does it do for the person? What does it prevent?

**The technique — The Consequence Bridge:**
State the specification → Bridge with "which means" or "in practice" →
State the consequence in plain human terms → Optional: state the
consequence of NOT having it

**Specification transformation examples:**

*"ρ ≥ 10¹⁰ Ω·m resistivity across all structural components"*
→ "Every structural component has a resistivity of at least 10 billion
ohm-meters. For comparison, copper — the material GIC most loves to travel
through — has a resistivity of 0.000000017. The ceramic structure is
roughly 590 billion times more resistant to GIC coupling than copper.
The GIC arrives, finds nothing to do, and goes elsewhere."

*"Si₃N₄ ceramic bearings throughout drivetrain"*
→ "Silicon nitride ceramic bearings in every hub motor — which means
the rotating components of every wheel are made of a material with
zero electrical conductivity and a hardness that makes steel look
like a lifestyle choice. GIC does not travel through these bearings.
GIC doesn't know these bearings exist."

*"SE = 92 dB MXene EMI shielding at 45 μm"*
→ "92 decibels of EMI shielding from a coating thinner than a human hair.
In practical terms: the electromagnetic noise inside this vehicle during
a G5 event will be approximately the same as the electromagnetic noise
inside this vehicle on a quiet Tuesday. The storm outside is loud.
From inside, it is silent."

### TYPE E — RISK STATEMENTS

**Raw fact pattern:** "[Event] causes [damage] in [timeframe] under [conditions]"
**Transform into:** What this means for a specific person in a specific situation

**The technique — The Personal Scenario:**
The most effective risk communication places a specific person in the specific
moment of impact. Not "vehicles will be disabled" but "you are driving to the
hospital at 8:42am and the car stops. Permanently. With no warning. Along with
every other car on the road."

**Risk transformation examples:**

*"Aluminum engine blocks fail under G5 GIC in 13 minutes"*
→ "You are in your car. Traffic is normal. The GIC arrives. You don't feel it —
you can't feel it. Thirteen minutes later, your engine block has absorbed enough
electromagnetic energy to approach aluminum's melting point. The car stops.
It does not start again. Every car that was in motion when the GIC arrived stops.
The roads become a record of where everyone was at that moment."

*"Hospital backup generators fail under G5 GIC"*
→ "The hospital has a diesel backup generator. The generator has a standard
steel-and-copper electrical architecture. Under G5 GIC, within the same
thirteen-minute window, the generator's copper windings begin to fail.
The hospital — the place with the ventilators, the surgical suites, the
intensive care units — is on whatever stored power remains. Which is:
not a lot. And not for long."

## SECTION 3: THE TRANSFORMATION QUALITY TEST

Before deploying any transformation, apply the FACT test:

**F — Faithful:** Does the transformation accurately represent the real scale
and significance of the fact? A comparison that makes something seem smaller
or larger than it is has failed this test, regardless of how clever it is.

**A — Appropriate:** Is this transformation appropriate for the severity of the
communication? A playful transformation of a cost figure is fine in a routine
memo. The same playfulness applied to a failure scenario in a life-safety document
must be proportional to the stakes.

**C — Concrete:** Is the comparison more concrete than the original fact?
If "262 kilowatts" is abstract, "262 household microwave ovens" must be more vivid,
more holdable, more present to the reader's sensory memory.

**T — True to the human experience:** Does the comparison land in the body,
or does it just produce another abstract number? The best comparisons produce
an involuntary response in the reader — a slight physical recognition that the
scale is real.
