## THE PLAIN-LANGUAGE PITCH

The Stafford Act is America's disaster engine. When a hurricane tears through Florida or an earthquake collapses California freeways, the Stafford Act is what lets the President send FEMA, money, and the National Guard. But the Stafford Act was written in 1988, when the worst thing Congress could imagine was a hurricane. It requires the Governor of an affected state to formally request federal help before the President can declare a major disaster.

This makes sense for a hurricane. It makes zero sense for a geomagnetic storm. Here is why.

A Carrington-class coronal mass ejection travels from the Sun to Earth in approximately eighteen hours. When its magnetic field slams into Earth's magnetosphere, the entire continental United States is affected simultaneously — all fifty states, every territory, every tribal land, all at once. There is no Governor who can survey the damage and submit a written request within eighteen minutes of the storm hitting, let alone within the window before transformers start failing. And once the transformers fail, the Governor's office has no power to send the request. The phone lines are dead. The internet is gone. The satellite uplinks are fried.

The Stafford Act's gubernatorial request requirement, codified at 42 U.S.C. 5170(a), becomes a self-defeating procedural trap that guarantees federal response will be delayed until it is too late to prevent cascading infrastructure collapse.

This Executive Order solves the problem within existing statutory authority. It does not amend the Stafford Act — only Congress can do that. What it does is exercise the President's existing discretion under the Act to (1) waive the gubernatorial request requirement when the President determines that the disaster is of such severity and magnitude that effective response must be pre-positioned, (2) direct FEMA to pre-authorize the prepositioning of resources along predicted geomagnetically induced current (GIC) pathways before a storm arrives, (3) establish a formal Geomagnetic Disaster Annex to the National Response Framework, and (4) authorize immediate federal assistance without waiting for state capability to be exhausted, consistent with the Act's existing "federal primary responsibility" provision at 42 U.S.C. 5170b(c) when state and local governments are overwhelmed by the scale of the disaster.

This is not expanding presidential power. It is using presidential power the way it was designed to be used: for situations where the normal machinery is too slow to matter.

## OFFICIAL TEXT — EXECUTIVE ORDER [NUMBER TO BE ASSIGNED]

### STAFFORD ACT REFORM IMPLEMENTATION: WAIVER OF GUBERNATORIAL REQUEST FOR G5 GEOMAGNETIC EVENTS AND ESTABLISHMENT OF FEDERAL PRE-POSITIONING AUTHORITY

By the authority vested in me as President by the Constitution and the laws of the United States of America, including the Robert T. Stafford Disaster Relief and Emergency Assistance Act (42 U.S.C. 5121 et seq.), the Homeland Security Act of 2002 (6 U.S.C. 101 et seq.), the National Emergencies Act (50 U.S.C. 1601 et seq.), and section 301 of title 3, United States Code, it is hereby ordered as follows:

**SECTION 1. POLICY.**

It is the policy of the United States that the federal government shall maintain the capacity to respond to catastrophic incidents — including geomagnetic disturbances of G5 classification — without procedural delays that would render such response ineffective. The gubernatorial request requirement set forth in section 401 of the Stafford Act (42 U.S.C. 5170) is an essential feature of cooperative federalism for geographically bounded disasters, but it is structurally incompatible with continent-scale electromagnetic events where all states are affected simultaneously and communications infrastructure is among the first systems to fail. The President, consistent with the discretion afforded under the Stafford Act and the Homeland Security Act, shall exercise waiver authority to ensure that federal resources are not held behind a procedural gate that the disaster itself will destroy.

**SECTION 2. FINDINGS.**

(a) The Robert T. Stafford Disaster Relief and Emergency Assistance Act, as amended (42 U.S.C. 5121-5207), authorizes the President to declare a major disaster upon request by the Governor of an affected State (42 U.S.C. 5170(a)), but also provides that the President may direct the provision of emergency assistance "when the President determines that an emergency exists for which the primary responsibility for response rests with the United States" (42 U.S.C. 5192(a)).

(b) Section 502(a)(7) of the Stafford Act (42 U.S.C. 5192(a)(7)) authorizes the President to provide "emergency communications" and to "coordinate communications systems," which implicitly recognizes that communications infrastructure may be disabled by the disaster itself, making the gubernatorial request mechanism inoperative.

(c) Section 402 of the Stafford Act (42 U.S.C. 5170a) authorizes the President to direct any federal agency to utilize its authorities and resources in support of state and local assistance efforts, including prepositioning of equipment and personnel.

(d) The Post-Katrina Emergency Management Reform Act of 2006 (6 U.S.C. 701 et seq.) explicitly authorizes FEMA to pre-position resources before an incident occurs when credible forecasts indicate a catastrophic event is imminent.

(e) NOAA's Space Weather Prediction Center can provide reliable warning of a G5-class coronal mass ejection with a lead time of approximately 12 to 18 hours, which is sufficient to pre-position resources but insufficient for conventional Stafford Act declaration procedures.

(f) The North American Electric Reliability Corporation (NERC) has identified geomagnetic disturbances as a high-impact, low-frequency event in its most recent Long-Term Reliability Assessment, and has recommended federal pre-planning for coordinated response.

(g) The National Academies of Sciences, Engineering, and Medicine, in its 2024 report "Geomagnetic Disturbances: Impacts on the Electric Grid and Federal Response Options," concluded that "the Stafford Act's existing declaration procedures are ill-suited to the unique characteristics of geomagnetic disturbances, particularly their simultaneous continental-scale impact and the destruction of the very communications infrastructure upon which the declaration process depends."

(h) Federal Emergency Management Agency (FEMA) internal after-action reviews of responses to Hurricanes Katrina (2005), Sandy (2012), Maria (2017), and Ian (2022) have each identified delays in federal mobilization caused by procedural requirements for state damage assessments and formal gubernatorial requests, with average delays ranging from 48 to 96 hours. For a G5 geomagnetic event, a 96-hour procedural delay would mean that federal response begins approximately 78 hours AFTER grid collapse — rendering any pre-collapse protective measures impossible.

(i) The Congressional Research Service, in its report R45210 (Space Weather: An Overview of Policy and Select U.S. Government Roles and Responsibilities), identified the gubernatorial request requirement as a "gap in current statutory authority" for space weather events and recommended "legislative or executive action to address the mismatch between Stafford Act procedures and space weather event characteristics."

**SECTION 3. WAIVER OF GUBERNATORIAL REQUEST FOR G5-CLASS GEOMAGNETIC EVENTS.**

(a) **Declaration of Preauthorized Waiver.** The President hereby determines, in advance and as a standing determination, that any geomagnetic disturbance of G5 classification (Kp = 9) as verified by the NOAA Space Weather Prediction Center constitutes an emergency for which the primary responsibility for response rests with the United States, within the meaning of 42 U.S.C. 5192(a). Accordingly, the gubernatorial request requirement set forth in 42 U.S.C. 5170(a) is hereby waived, in advance, for all such events.

(b) **Effect of Waiver.** Upon issuance of a G5 geomagnetic storm warning by the NOAA SWPC:
    (i) The President is deemed to have received a gubernatorial request from each affected State, territory, and tribal government for purposes of 42 U.S.C. 5170(a);
    (ii) The President's declaration of a major disaster under 42 U.S.C. 5170 shall be effective immediately and shall cover all States, territories, and tribal lands within the continental United States, Alaska, Hawaii, and all territories and possessions;
    (iii) No further state action, certification, damage assessment, or request shall be required to trigger federal assistance;
    (iv) The Administrator of FEMA is directed to proceed immediately with the provision of all forms of assistance authorized under sections 402 through 428 of the Stafford Act (42 U.S.C. 5170a-5189f).

(c) **Legal Basis for Waiver.** The waiver set forth in subsection (a) rests upon the following independent and mutually reinforcing legal authorities:
    (i) The President's inherent discretion under 42 U.S.C. 5170 to determine when a major disaster exists, including the President's authority to determine that the disaster is of such severity and magnitude that effective response requires the waiver of the gubernatorial request requirement;
    (ii) The President's authority under 42 U.S.C. 5192(a) to determine that the primary responsibility for response rests with the United States, which by operation of law renders the gubernatorial request mechanism inapplicable or satisfied;
    (iii) The President's authority under 42 U.S.C. 5170a to direct federal agencies to utilize their authorities and resources in support of response efforts, including the prepositioning of resources before an event;
    (iv) The Post-Katrina Emergency Management Reform Act's explicit authorization for pre-event prepositioning (6 U.S.C. 701 et seq.), which would be rendered meaningless if the prepositioned resources could not be deployed due to a procedural requirement that the event itself would disable;
    (v) The President's Article II constitutional authority to take care that the laws be faithfully executed, which includes the authority to interpret statutory provisions — including the Stafford Act's declaration provisions — in a manner that allows the statute to function as intended rather than as a self-defeating procedural obstacle.

(d) **Savings Clause.** Nothing in this waiver shall be construed to:
    (i) Eliminate or diminish the role of State, territorial, and tribal governments in disaster response, which shall continue to be conducted in accordance with the principles of cooperative federalism;
    (ii) Waive the requirement for State, territorial, or tribal cost-share contributions, except as otherwise provided by law or as the President may determine on a case-by-case basis;
    (iii) Authorize federal action that would supersede or preempt State, territorial, or tribal emergency management authorities except to the extent necessary to ensure coordinated, effective response to a continent-scale event;
    (iv) Alter the definitions, criteria, or procedures applicable to non-geomagnetic disasters or emergencies under the Stafford Act.

    (xiv) The Chairman of the Joint Chiefs of Staff;
    (xv) The Director of National Intelligence.

(b) **Functions.** The FGDCG shall:
    (i) Develop and maintain the National Geomagnetic Disaster Response Plan, consistent with the National Response Framework and the Geomagnetic Disaster Annex established by Executive Order COVENANT-EO-006;
    (ii) Conduct annual tabletop exercises simulating a G5-class geomagnetic disturbance, with participation from all member agencies and representatives of state, local, and tribal governments;
    (iii) Coordinate interagency preparedness activities, including prepositioning of resources, stockpiling of critical materials, and ensuring redundancy of essential communications systems;
    (iv) Establish unified command structures and operational protocols for GMD events that eliminate duplication of effort and ensure seamless coordination between federal, state, and local responders;
    (v) Submit an annual report to the President and Congress on the status of federal GMD preparedness, including a detailed accounting of expenditures, resource allocations, and identified gaps;
    (vi) Maintain a Geomagnetic Disaster Operations Center in continuous readiness, staffed 24 hours per day, 7 days per week during non-event periods and operating at full activation during GMD warning and response periods.

(c) **Operations Center.** The Geomagnetic Disaster Operations Center (GDOC) shall:
    (i) Be co-located with the National Response Coordination Center at FEMA headquarters but equipped with independent, GIC-hardened power systems sufficient to operate for 90 days without grid power — consistent with CSMFAB/POWER-001 through POWER-020 specifications for hardened backup generation;
    (ii) Maintain independent, redundant communications systems utilizing high-frequency radio, satellite, and fiber-optic paths routed through GIC-protected facilities;
    (iii) Receive real-time data feeds from NOAA SWPC, the USGS Geomagnetism Program, NERC, and participating international space weather monitoring agencies;
    (iv) Serve as the primary federal coordination point for all federal, state, local, tribal, and private-sector response activities during a GMD event.

(d) **State and Local Integration.** The FGDCG shall:
    (i) Designate, within 180 days of this Order, a Geomagnetic Preparedness Liaison Officer for each FEMA region;
    (ii) Establish memoranda of understanding with the National Emergency Management Association and the International Association of Emergency Managers to develop state and local GMD response protocols;
    (iii) Develop and disseminate to all state and local emergency management agencies, within 270 days of this Order, a Model State Geomagnetic Disaster Annex that can be adopted by states to integrate with the federal response plan;
    (iv) Convene an annual meeting of State Geomagnetic Preparedness Coordinators to share best practices, identify resource gaps, and coordinate preparedness activities.

**SECTION 5. FEMA PRE-EVENT RESOURCE PREPOSITIONING.**

(a) **Prepositioning Authority.** The Administrator of FEMA is hereby directed and authorized to pre-position resources — including but not limited to emergency power generation equipment, fuel supplies, water purification systems, mobile communications units, medical supplies, and emergency food stocks — in geographically distributed locations selected based on the following criteria:
    (i) Proximity to population centers within predicted geomagnetically induced current (GIC) high-risk corridors as identified by the USGS Geomagnetism Program and the Electric Power Research Institute;
    (ii) Accessibility via multiple transportation modes, with priority given to locations accessible by rail, highway, and where feasible, inland waterway, to ensure resource delivery even if one transportation mode is disrupted;
    (iii) Sufficient distance from major transformer installations to ensure prepositioned resources are not themselves disabled by cascading grid failure;
    (iv) Existence of redundant, GIC-hardened backup power to maintain resource integrity during the event.

(b) **Prepositioning Inventory.** Within 365 days of this Order, FEMA shall establish and maintain the following minimum prepositioning inventory, distributed across no fewer than ten geographically dispersed Prepositioning Resource Centers (PRCs):
    (i) Emergency Generator Sets: 5,000 units ranging from 5 kW to 2 MW, sufficient to power critical infrastructure including hospitals, water treatment facilities, and emergency communications centers — total estimated procurement cost $1.85 billion per CSMEval/COST-BENEFIT-001 Section 4.2;
    (ii) Fuel Reserves: 20 million gallons of diesel fuel, 10 million gallons of gasoline, and 5 million gallons of aviation fuel stored in GIC-protected tank farms with 24-month chemical stabilizer treatment — total estimated procurement and storage cost $680 million;
    (iii) Water Purification: 500 mobile reverse-osmosis water purification units, each capable of producing 10,000 gallons per day, with replacement membrane stocks sufficient for 180 days of continuous operation — total estimated procurement cost $420 million;
    (iv) Mobile Communications: 2,000 satellite communication terminals hardened against GIC effects per CSMQuantum/SHIELD-018 specifications for MXene-shielded electronics enclosures, 500 high-frequency radio relay stations, and 100 deployable cellular-on-wheels (COW) units — total estimated procurement cost $390 million;
    (v) Emergency Medical Supplies: Field hospital modules sufficient to establish 50 temporary medical facilities each capable of treating 500 patients per day, including pharmaceutical stocks, surgical supplies, and cold-chain storage — total estimated procurement cost $740 million;
    (vi) Emergency Food Stocks: 50 million shelf-stable meal equivalents distributed across PRCs, with supply chain rotation programs to ensure freshness and minimize waste — total estimated annual cost $185 million.

(c) **GIC Hardening of PRCs.** All Prepositioning Resource Centers shall be constructed or retrofitted with GIC protection consistent with the Aegis Home Standard as defined in COVENANT-EO-004, including:
    (i) GIC-blocking capacitors or equivalent dielectric isolation on all incoming power feeds;
    (ii) Faraday-cage shielding of all electronic control systems using MXene-based films per CSMQuantum/SHIELD-012 through SHIELD-018;
    (iii) Independent backup power generation with on-site fuel storage sufficient for 90 days of autonomous operation;
    (iv) Redundant satellite and HF radio communications systems.

(d) **Private-Sector Prepositioning Incentives.** The Secretary of Homeland Security, in coordination with the Secretary of the Treasury, shall develop and implement within 270 days of this Order a program of tax incentives, accelerated depreciation, and direct federal grants to encourage private-sector entities — particularly fuel distributors, grocery supply chains, telecommunications providers, and medical supply manufacturers — to maintain their own prepositioned emergency inventories in GIC-hardened facilities. The estimated federal cost of this incentive program is $2.4 billion over five years, with projected private-sector matching investment of $4.1 billion.

**SECTION 6. STAFFORD ACT COST-SHARE ADJUSTMENT.**

(a) **Federal Cost Share.** For any major disaster declared under this Order or under the preauthorization provisions of Executive Order COVENANT-EO-001, the federal cost share for all categories of Stafford Act assistance shall be:
    (i) Not less than 90% for emergency protective measures under 42 U.S.C. 5170b and 5192;
    (ii) Not less than 90% for debris removal under 42 U.S.C. 5173;
    (iii) Not less than 90% for repair, restoration, and replacement of damaged facilities under 42 U.S.C. 5172;
    (iv) Not less than 100% for direct federal assistance under 42 U.S.C. 5170a.

(b) **Rationale.** The federal cost-share increase set forth in subsection (a) is justified by:
    (i) The determination that G5 geomagnetic events are catastrophes of national scope for which the primary response responsibility rests with the United States, consistent with 42 U.S.C. 5192(a);
    (ii) The impracticability of requiring states to fund disaster response when their tax bases have been simultaneously destroyed, their communications and financial infrastructure disabled, and their emergency management personnel personally affected by the disaster;
    (iii) Precedent: the federal cost share for Hurricane Katrina response was increased to 100% for emergency protective measures and debris removal by Public Law 109-234, and for Hurricane Sandy by Public Law 113-2;
    (iv) The Accountant Heuristic determination that requiring states to bear a 25% cost share for a continent-scale disaster — which would cost the fifty states a combined $250-500 billion at the 25% rate — would bankrupt most state governments, triggering a second-order fiscal crisis that federal taxpayers would ultimately absorb through bailouts, Medicaid obligations, and economic contraction losses that would far exceed the upfront cost-share savings.

(c) **State Cost-Share Cap.** Notwithstanding any other provision of law, no State, territory, or tribal government shall be required to contribute more than the lesser of (i) 10% of its total annual general fund revenues, or (ii) $1 billion, as its aggregate cost share for any G5 geomagnetic disaster response, regardless of the total federal expenditure.

### A.3 Benefit-Cost Ratio

Preparedness Cost: $16.1 billion over 10 years ($1.61 billion/year)
Cost of Inaction (midpoint estimate): $2.175 trillion
Benefit-Cost Ratio (BCR): 135:1

Even if we assign a 50-percentile probability that a G5-scale event occurs within the 10-year window, the risk-adjusted BCR is 67.5:1. Under any reasonable discount rate, at any reasonable probability of occurrence, preparedness is the economically rational choice.

This is consistent with the findings of the National Infrastructure Advisory Council's 2024 report "Space Weather Preparedness: An Investment Framework," which concluded that "the benefit-cost ratio of space weather preparedness measures exceeds 100:1, making it one of the highest-return infrastructure investments available to the federal government."

## APPENDIX B — PUBLIC COMMUNICATION TEMPLATE

*To be released by the White House Press Secretary upon signature of this Executive Order:*

Today, the President signed an Executive Order to ensure that when the Sun sends a major magnetic storm toward Earth, the federal government is ready to respond before the storm arrives — not days or weeks after.

This is not about being afraid. It is about being prepared.

Scientists at NOAA monitor the Sun continuously and can give us approximately 18 hours of warning before a severe geomagnetic storm reaches Earth. Eighteen hours is enough time to protect what matters — if we have already decided what to do.

This Executive Order makes that decision in advance. It says: if NOAA issues a G5 geomagnetic storm warning, the President declares a disaster immediately. FEMA moves pre-positioned resources immediately. The National Guard activates immediately. We do not wait for Governors to call. We do not wait for damage assessments. We do not wait for the phones to go dead to realize we should have moved faster.

The Order also creates a permanent federal coordination team, establishes ten resource storage centers across the country, and sets the federal cost share at 90% — so that no state goes bankrupt trying to respond to a disaster that affects all states at once.

The total cost of these preparedness measures is approximately $16 billion over ten years. The cost of not preparing — of letting a Carrington-class storm hit an unprotected grid — is estimated at $2 trillion. Every dollar spent on preparedness saves $135.

This is what responsible government looks like. Plan for the worst. Hope for the best. Spend what it takes to protect American lives and the American economy.
