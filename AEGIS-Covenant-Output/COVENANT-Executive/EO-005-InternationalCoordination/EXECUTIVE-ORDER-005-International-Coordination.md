## THE PLAIN-LANGUAGE PITCH

A coronal mass ejection does not stop at the border. The magnetosphere does not check passports. When the Sun throws a magnetic tantrum, Canada, the United States, and Mexico all experience it simultaneously — because they share the same continent, the same interconnected electrical grid, and in most cases, the same geological formations that concentrate geomagnetically induced currents along particular paths.

This is not a problem any nation can solve alone. The North American grid is physically integrated. Power flows across the U.S.-Canada border at more than thirty interconnection points. A transformer failure in Manitoba pulls current from Minnesota. A substation collapse in Ontario affects New York. A blackout in Baja California cascades into Southern California.

If the United States hardens its grid but Canada does not, the unprotected Canadian transformers will fail during a G5 event, creating a blackout that cascades across the interconnected North American grid into the United States — destroying American transformers that were themselves hardened. Grid hardening is not additive; it is multiplicative. The protection is only as strong as the weakest link in the interconnected grid. Every unprotected transformer in Canada or Mexico is a threat vector to the American grid.

This Executive Order directs the State Department to lead a coordinated international effort in three dimensions: (1) bilateral agreements with Canada and Mexico for synchronized grid hardening and mutual assistance, (2) multilateral coordination through NATO, the United Nations, and the International Energy Agency, and (3) the establishment of an International Geomagnetic Preparedness Fund to help developing nations protect their grids, because America's supply chains depend on stable electrical infrastructure in countries that manufacture everything from semiconductors to pharmaceuticals.

## OFFICIAL TEXT — EXECUTIVE ORDER [NUMBER TO BE ASSIGNED]

### INTERNATIONAL GEOMAGNETIC DISASTER PREPAREDNESS COORDINATION

By the authority vested in me as President by the Constitution and the laws of the United States of America, including the Foreign Assistance Act of 1961 (22 U.S.C. 2151 et seq.), the State Department Basic Authorities Act of 1956 (22 U.S.C. 2651 et seq.), the National Emergencies Act (50 U.S.C. 1601 et seq.), and section 301 of title 3, United States Code, it is hereby ordered as follows:

**SECTION 1. POLICY.**

It is the policy of the United States to pursue coordinated international action to prepare for, mitigate, and respond to geomagnetic disturbances of sufficient severity to threaten critical infrastructure across national boundaries. The United States recognizes that (a) the North American electrical grid is a physically integrated system in which failure in one nation cascades into all connected nations; (b) global supply chains upon which the U.S. economy depends require electrical grid stability in manufacturing nations worldwide; (c) effective geomagnetic disaster response requires pre-negotiated international agreements for resource sharing, mutual assistance, and coordinated grid restoration; and (d) the United States has a strategic interest in ensuring that all nations — particularly close allies and trading partners — are prepared to withstand G5-class geomagnetic events.

**SECTION 2. FINDINGS.**

(a) The United States electrical grid is interconnected with the Canadian grid at more than 35 points and with the Mexican grid at several interconnection points in California and Texas. These interconnections create a single North American electrical system in which GIC-induced failures propagate across borders.

(b) The 1989 Hydro-Québec geomagnetic storm caused a complete collapse of the Hydro-Québec transmission system within 90 seconds of GIC onset, demonstrating that GMD events can cause near-instantaneous grid failure spanning entire regions. The 2003 Halloween solar storms caused transformer damage in South Africa and forced protective shutdowns across the Swedish and UK grids.

(c) NATO's Civil Emergency Planning (CEP) framework includes mechanisms for coordinated response to natural disasters affecting member states, including the Euro-Atlantic Disaster Response Coordination Centre (EADRCC).

(d) The United Nations Office for Disaster Risk Reduction (UNDRR) administers the Sendai Framework for Disaster Risk Reduction 2015-2030, which calls upon member states to strengthen disaster preparedness for effective response.

(e) The International Energy Agency's emergency response mechanisms, including strategic petroleum reserve coordination, provide a model for multinational coordination of strategic energy infrastructure protection.

(f) Existing bilateral defense agreements — including the NORAD Agreement with Canada and the U.S.-Mexico Border 2025 Program — provide legal frameworks that can be extended or adapted to cover geomagnetic disaster response coordination.

(g) No existing international treaty or convention specifically addresses the unique characteristics of geomagnetic disasters: simultaneous continent-scale impact, destruction of communications infrastructure, multi-year grid restoration timelines, and the need for pre-event hardening coordination across interconnected electrical grids.

If their grid fails, ours fails too — even if we've hardened every American transformer. Think of it like a chain: the chain is only as strong as its weakest link. Canada and Mexico are links in the North American electrical chain. We need to coordinate with them. We also need international agreements — NATO, the UN, the IEA — because no treaty currently exists that addresses the unique nature of solar storms. This Order starts that process.

**SECTION 3. BILATERAL AGREEMENTS — CANADA AND MEXICO.**

(a) **North American Geomagnetic Protection Treaty.** The Secretary of State is hereby directed to negotiate, within 180 days, a binding bilateral executive agreement with the Government of Canada and the Government of Mexico — to be known as the North American Geomagnetic Preparedness and Mutual Assistance Agreement — that shall provide for:

    (i) **Synchronized Grid Hardening.** Agreement to adopt a common GIC protection standard — equivalent to or compatible with the Aegis Home Standard — for all large power transformers (345 kV and above) and critical substations on the interconnected North American grid, with a target compliance date of 48 months from the date of the agreement;

    (ii) **Mutual Assistance Protocol.** Pre-authorization for the immediate deployment of grid restoration personnel, equipment, and materials across international borders upon declaration of a G5 geomagnetic storm warning by any of the three nations' designated space weather monitoring authorities — without waiting for customs clearance, visa processing, or other border formalities that would delay restoration by days or weeks;

    (iii) **Joint Strategic Transformer Reserve.** Agreement to coordinate the procurement, storage, and deployment of large power transformers so that the combined North American Strategic Transformer Reserve achieves geographic coverage adequate to restore grid backbone functionality across all three nations within 90 days of a G5 event;

    (iv) **Data Sharing.** Bilateral agreements with Natural Resources Canada and the Mexican Servicio Meteorológico Nacional for real-time sharing of geomagnetic monitoring data, grid status information, and vulnerability assessments;

    (v) **Coordinated Supply Chain.** Agreement to harmonize technical standards for GIC protection equipment — including capacitor specifications, shielding materials testing protocols, and installation certification requirements — to create a single North American market for GIC protection products, reducing costs through economies of scale;

    (vi) **Joint Exercises.** Agreement to conduct biennial joint tabletop and field exercises simulating a G5 geomagnetic event, with participation from the energy, defense, emergency management, and transportation agencies of all three nations.

(b) **U.S.-Canada Specific Provisions.** The bilateral agreement with Canada shall additionally address:

    (i) The unique vulnerabilities of the Hydro-Québec transmission system, which due to its location on the Canadian Shield — a region of high-resistivity igneous rock that concentrates GIC — is the most GIC-vulnerable major grid on the North American continent, as tragically demonstrated by the 1989 collapse;

    (ii) Joint hardening of the 35+ electrical interconnections between the U.S. and Canadian grids, prioritizing the Quebec-New England, Ontario-New York, Manitoba-Minnesota, and British Columbia-Washington interconnections;

    (iii) Extension of NORAD's mission scope or establishment of a parallel coordination structure for GMD monitoring and early warning, leveraging NORAD's existing infrastructure for continent-scale surveillance and communications;

    (iv) Coordination of GIC monitoring through the existing U.S. Geological Survey (USGS) and Natural Resources Canada magnetometer networks, with commitments to install additional monitoring stations in underserved regions of the Canadian Shield and the Pacific Northwest.

(c) **U.S.-Mexico Specific Provisions.** The bilateral agreement with Mexico shall additionally address:

    (i) Assessment and hardening of the electrical interconnections between the U.S. (California ISO and ERCOT) and Mexican (Baja California and Northeastern Mexico) grids;

    (ii) Technical assistance and capacity-building to support Mexico's adoption of GIC protection standards for its rapidly modernizing grid infrastructure;

    (iii) Joint assessment of GIC vulnerability in the Sonoran Desert corridor, where high-resistivity substrate and major transmission lines create elevated GIC risk.

(d) **Legal Authority.** The bilateral agreements negotiated under this Section shall be concluded as executive agreements pursuant to the President's Article II foreign affairs authority, consistent with the precedent of the U.S.-Canada Air Quality Agreement (1991), the U.S.-Mexico Border Environment Cooperation Agreement (1983), and the NORAD Agreement (1958, as renewed).

(e) **Fast-Track Customs and Immigration Protocol.** The Secretary of Homeland Security, acting through U.S. Customs and Border Protection and U.S. Citizenship and Immigration Services, shall, within 180 days, establish an expedited entry protocol for Canadian and Mexican grid restoration personnel, equipment, and materials that permits entry at designated border crossings within 2 hours of arrival during a declared GMD emergency, with pre-registration of qualified utility personnel and pre-clearance of standard restoration equipment manifests.

**SECTION 4. MULTILATERAL COORDINATION.**

(a) **NATO Civil Emergency Planning.** The Secretary of State shall, within 90 days, formally engage the North Atlantic Treaty Organization's Civil Emergency Planning (CEP) Committee to propose:

    (i) The addition of geomagnetic disturbance to NATO's list of recognized civil emergency scenarios addressed by the Euro-Atlantic Disaster Response Coordination Centre (EADRCC);

    (ii) The establishment of a NATO Geomagnetic Preparedness Working Group under the CEP Committee to develop common GIC protection standards, coordinate member-state hardening programs, and plan multinational grid restoration operations;

    (iii) Inclusion of GMD scenarios in NATO's annual Crisis Management Exercise (CMX) program;

    (iv) Exploration of the applicability of NATO Article 5 (collective defense) to a G5 geomagnetic event — specifically, whether a G5 CME that simultaneously disables the critical infrastructure of all NATO member states constitutes, in effect, a threat to the territorial integrity and security of the Alliance that merits collective response, even though the triggering event is natural rather than a conventional armed attack. The Secretary of State shall obtain a legal opinion from the NATO Office of Legal Affairs on this question and shall report findings to the President within 180 days.

(b) **International Energy Agency (IEA).** The Secretary of Energy shall, within 120 days, engage the IEA Governing Board to propose:

    (i) A multinational Strategic Transformer Reserve modeled on the IEA's existing framework for coordinated strategic petroleum reserves, under which participating nations agree to maintain minimum stocks of large power transformers and to release them to affected nations during a GMD emergency under pre-agreed allocation formulas;

    (ii) Integration of GMD scenarios into the IEA's emergency response exercises and its Emergency Response and Crisis Management framework;

    (iii) Development of common international standards for GIC protection equipment certification, enabling a global market for GIC-protection products and reducing costs through international competition and scale.

(c) **United Nations Office for Disaster Risk Reduction (UNDRR).** The Secretary of State shall, within 180 days, engage the UNDRR to:

    (i) Propose the inclusion of geomagnetic disaster preparedness in the successor framework to the Sendai Framework for Disaster Risk Reduction (post-2030);

    (ii) Advocate for the establishment of a UN Geomagnetic Disaster Risk Reduction Coordinator position within UNDRR;

    (iii) Support the development of an International Convention on Geomagnetic Disaster Coordination — as more fully described in COVENANT-UN/CONVENTION-001 — under the auspices of the UN General Assembly or a dedicated diplomatic conference.

(d) **World Meteorological Organization (WMO).** The Secretary of Commerce, acting through the Administrator of NOAA, shall engage the WMO to propose the expansion of the WMO's Space Programme to include global coordination of space weather monitoring and warning dissemination, with the goal of ensuring that every nation — regardless of its own monitoring capabilities — receives timely and accurate GMD warning information.

(e) **G7 and G20 Engagement.** The Secretary of State and the Secretary of the Treasury shall, as part of G7 and G20 ministerial engagements, raise geomagnetic disaster preparedness as a global financial stability issue, emphasizing:

    (i) That a G5 geomagnetic event causing simultaneous grid failure in multiple G20 nations could trigger a global financial crisis exceeding the 2008 financial crisis in severity, given the dependence of financial markets on electrical infrastructure;

    (ii) That the cost of global grid hardening — estimated at $200-400 billion for G20 nations collectively — is approximately 0.3-0.6% of G20 annual GDP, and approximately 0.05-0.1% of G20 GDP when amortized over 30 years;

    (iii) That GIC protection represents a uniquely cost-effective investment in global financial stability, with benefit-cost ratios exceeding 50:1 for virtually all nations.

The grid doesn't stop at borders. The storm doesn't stop at borders. Let's coordinate." This is all about taking existing international frameworks and expanding them to cover the thing they didn't anticipate in 1949 or 1974: the Sun.

**SECTION 5. INTERNATIONAL GEOMAGNETIC PREPAREDNESS FUND.**

(a) **Establishment.** The Secretary of the Treasury and the Secretary of State are hereby directed to develop a proposal, within 365 days, for the establishment of an International Geomagnetic Preparedness Fund (IGPF) under the administration of the World Bank or a dedicated multilateral financial institution.

(b) **Purpose.** The IGPF shall provide concessional financing — including grants, low-interest loans, and loan guarantees — to developing nations for:

    (i) GIC vulnerability assessments of their electrical grids;
    (ii) Procurement and installation of GIC protection equipment;
    (iii) Training of utility personnel in GMD preparedness and grid restoration;
    (iv) Integration into global space weather monitoring and warning networks.

(c) **U.S. Contribution.** The United States shall advocate for an initial IGPF capitalization of $10 billion, to which the United States shall contribute $2 billion over five years ($400 million per year), with the balance funded by other G7 and G20 nations, multilateral development banks, and private philanthropic contributions.

(d) **Rationale.** The U.S. contribution is justified by:

    (i) The strategic interest of the United States in ensuring that nations that are integral to American supply chains — particularly in Southeast Asia (semiconductors), South Asia (pharmaceuticals), and Latin America (food and raw materials) — maintain functioning electrical grids during and after GMD events;

    (ii) The national security interest in preventing GMD-induced economic collapse in developing nations from generating refugee flows, regional instability, and humanitarian crises that would require far more expensive U.S. military and humanitarian intervention;

    (iii) The economic interest of American GIC-protection equipment manufacturers in developing export markets, facilitated by IGPF-financed procurement that enables international deployment of American-made protection technology.

(e) **Accountant Heuristic Assessment.** The $2 billion U.S. contribution to the IGPF, compared to the estimated $50-200 billion cost of a single GMD-induced supply chain disruption to the U.S. economy, represents a benefit-cost ratio of 25:1 to 100:1. Even if only 25% of the Fund's financed hardening measures are effective, the risk-adjusted BCR remains above 6:1 — well within the range of cost-effective foreign assistance.

If those factories lose power for years, Americans lose access to semiconductors, medicines, food, and consumer goods. This is self-interested philanthropy. **SECTION 6. GLOBAL SPACE WEATHER MONITORING NETWORK.**

(a) The Secretary of Commerce, acting through the Administrator of NOAA, shall:

    (i) Propose, within 365 days and in coordination with the WMO and the International Space Environment Service (ISES), the establishment of a Global Geomagnetic Monitoring Network that integrates existing magnetometer arrays (including USGS, INTERMAGNET, CARISMA, and SuperMAG) into a unified, publicly accessible data platform;

    (ii) Advocate for the deployment of additional magnetometer stations in underserved regions — particularly Africa, South America, and Southeast Asia — where current monitoring coverage is sparse and where rapid industrialization is creating new GIC-vulnerable grid infrastructure;

    (iii) Fund, through NOAA's Space Weather Prediction Center, the development of open-source GIC modeling software that any nation can use to assess their grid's vulnerability without dependence on proprietary or export-controlled U.S. technology;

    (iv) Coordinate with the Department of Defense to ensure that U.S. military space weather monitoring assets — including those operated by the U.S. Space Force — contribute data to the civilian global monitoring network to the maximum extent consistent with national security.

(b) **Sun-Earth L1 Monitoring.** The Secretary of Commerce, in coordination with the Administrator of NASA and the Secretary of Defense, shall develop a plan for ensuring continuous, redundant solar wind monitoring at the Sun-Earth Lagrange Point L1 — the critical early-warning position approximately 1.5 million kilometers sunward of Earth — to replace the aging DSCOVR spacecraft and ensure that the United States and the world do not lose GMD early-warning capability due to a single-point failure in satellite coverage.

**SECTION 7. REPORTING AND ACCOUNTABILITY.**

(a) **Annual Report to Congress.** The Secretary of State shall submit to Congress, not later than January 31 of each year, a report detailing:

    (i) The status of bilateral negotiations with Canada and Mexico;
    (ii) The status of multilateral engagement through NATO, the IEA, the UN, and other bodies;
    (iii) The status of the IGPF proposal and pledging;
    (iv) An assessment of global GMD preparedness levels and identified gaps;
    (v) Recommendations for further congressional or executive action.

(b) **Congressional Consultation.** The Secretary of State shall consult with the Senate Foreign Relations Committee and the House Foreign Affairs Committee before concluding any binding bilateral or multilateral agreement under this Order.

**SECTION 8. SEVERABILITY AND GENERAL PROVISIONS.**

(a) If any provision of this Order is held invalid, the remainder shall not be affected.

(b) This Order shall be implemented consistent with applicable law and subject to the availability of appropriations.

(c) This Order is not intended to create any right or benefit enforceable at law.
