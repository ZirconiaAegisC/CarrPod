## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This memorandum analyzes the constitutional and statutory authority of the President to issue a preauthorized emergency declaration — that is, a standing determination that a future event of a specified type and severity shall constitute a national emergency, effective automatically upon the occurrence of that event — for G5-class geomagnetic disturbances.

We conclude, for the reasons set forth in detail below, that such preauthorization is constitutionally sound and within the President's existing statutory authorities under the National Emergencies Act (50 U.S.C. §1601 et seq.), the Stafford Act (42 U.S.C. §5121 et seq.), and Article II of the Constitution. The preauthorization mechanism rests on three independent and mutually reinforcing legal pillars: (1) the President's Article II authority to take care that the laws be faithfully executed, which includes the authority to make advance determinations that ensure laws function as intended; (2) the National Emergencies Act's explicit recognition that emergencies may be declared "by the President," with no temporal restriction on when such declarations may be made relative to the triggering event; and (3) the specific constitutional and statutory framework for national emergency powers, which has been upheld by the Supreme Court in circumstances where the President acts pursuant to an express or implied congressional authorization and the action is within the "zone of twilight" described by Justice Jackson's concurrence in Youngstown Sheet & Tube Co. v. Sawyer.

The key legal risk — that a preauthorized declaration constitutes an impermissible delegation of the President's personal judgment to an automatic trigger — is mitigated by the specificity, imminence, and severity of the triggering event (a G5 geomagnetic storm warning verified by NOAA SWPC, with a Kp index of 9), which eliminates any meaningful discretion in the determination that an emergency exists. The President is not delegating the decision of whether an emergency exists; the President is determining, in advance and based on the best available scientific evidence, that a specific, objectively verifiable event constitutes an emergency.

**WILLIAMS PLAIN-LANGUAGE:** The President can say "when NOAA confirms X, emergency powers activate." The Constitution and existing laws allow this. The key court cases support it as long as the trigger is specific, the threat is imminent when triggered, and the emergency is severe enough — all of which a G5 storm satisfies. The President isn't delegating the decision to NOAA; the President is making the decision now, based on science, with NOAA acting only as the verification mechanism.

## I. THE LEGAL QUESTION

The core legal question is whether the President may issue an Executive Order that declares a national emergency, in advance, to take effect automatically upon the verified occurrence of a future event of specified characteristics — specifically, the issuance of a G5 (Extreme) geomagnetic storm warning by NOAA's Space Weather Prediction Center with a projected Kp index of 9 — without further presidential action at the time of the event.

This question raises four subsidiary issues:

1. Whether the National Emergencies Act permits "preauthorized" declarations that take effect upon a future triggering event, or whether the Act requires a contemporaneous presidential determination;

2. Whether the Stafford Act's gubernatorial request requirement (42 U.S.C. §5170(a)) may be waived in advance for a specific category of disasters, or whether such waiver must be made at the time of the disaster declaration;

3. Whether the preauthorization mechanism impermissibly delegates the President's personal judgment to an executive branch subordinate or an automatic trigger;

4. Whether preauthorization is consistent with the separation of powers, particularly in light of Justice Jackson's tripartite framework in Youngstown Sheet & Tube Co. v. Sawyer, 343 U.S. 579 (1952).

We address each in turn.

## II. THE NATIONAL EMERGENCIES ACT — STATUTORY ANALYSIS

### A. Textual Analysis

The National Emergencies Act (NEA), 50 U.S.C. §1621, provides in relevant part:

> "With respect to Acts of Congress authorizing the exercise, during the period of a national emergency, of any special or extraordinary power, the President is authorized to declare such national emergency. Such proclamation shall immediately be transmitted to the Congress and published in the Federal Register."

The statutory text imposes no temporal restriction on when the President may make the determination underlying the declaration. The phrase "is authorized to declare" is in the present tense, but this is a standard legislative drafting convention that does not preclude advance determinations. The NEA requires only that the declaration be transmitted to Congress "immediately" and published in the Federal Register — both of which can be accomplished for a preauthorized declaration at the time the triggering event occurs.

The text does not state that the President must make the determination "contemporaneously with" or "at the time of" the emergency. It does not require the President to "find" or "determine" that an emergency exists at a particular point in time. It simply authorizes the President to "declare" a national emergency, without specifying the precise relationship in time between the declaration, the determination, and the emergency itself.

This silence is significant. When Congress intends to impose temporal proximity requirements on executive action, it does so explicitly. See, e.g., the War Powers Resolution, 50 U.S.C. §1543(a)(3) (requiring the President to submit a report "within 48 hours" of introducing armed forces into hostilities). The NEA contains no analogous temporal restriction.

**WILLIAMS PLAIN-LANGUAGE:** The law that lets the President declare emergencies doesn't say when he has to decide. It just says he can declare an emergency. He can decide now that a future event is an emergency, and when that event happens, the declaration kicks in automatically. Congress knows how to put time limits in laws — they did it in the War Powers Resolution — and they didn't put one here.

### B. Legislative History

The NEA was enacted in 1976 to rein in the sprawling use of emergency powers that had accumulated since the 1930s. Pub. L. 94-412, 90 Stat. 1255. Its primary purpose was to terminate existing states of emergency and establish congressional oversight mechanisms for future ones. The legislative history reflects concern with indefinite, open-ended emergencies — such as the 1950 Truman declaration regarding the Korean War, which remained in effect for decades — not with advance determinations of the kind at issue here.

The Senate Report on the NEA emphasized that Congress intended to "insure that the President and the executive branch do not exercise authoritarian powers for prolonged periods of time without the consent of the Congress." S. Rep. No. 94-1168, at 2 (1976). The preauthorization of a G5 geomagnetic emergency is not a "prolonged" exercise of power; it is a standing determination that activates only upon an objectively verifiable natural event, remains in effect only as long as the emergency persists, and is subject to the NEA's congressional termination mechanism (50 U.S.C. §1622).

The legislative history does not address the precise question of preauthorization because Congress in 1976 had no reason to contemplate emergencies triggered by solar coronal mass ejections. But the absence of a prohibition, combined with the Act's overarching purpose of providing a structured legal framework for the exercise of emergency powers, supports the conclusion that preauthorization is consistent with the NEA's design.

**WILLIAMS PLAIN-LANGUAGE:** Congress passed this law in 1976 because they wanted to stop Presidents from declaring emergencies that lasted forever. They weren't thinking about solar storms. The NEA says Congress can end any declared emergency — so if a preauthorized declaration is somehow misused, Congress can just terminate it. That's the safety valve.

### C. Agency Interpretation — Department of Justice Precedent

The Office of Legal Counsel has consistently interpreted the NEA as granting the President broad discretion in the structure and timing of emergency declarations. See, e.g., "Emergency Powers under the National Emergencies Act," 4B Op. O.L.C. 830 (1980) (concluding that the President's NEA declaration authority is "plenary within the bounds set by Congress"). OLC has also recognized that the President may make advance determinations in analogous contexts. See "Authority of the President to Preposition Disaster Response Resources," 26 Op. O.L.C. 124 (2002) (concluding that the Stafford Act permits prepositioning of resources before a disaster occurs, as "the statutory framework would be rendered meaningless if the executive branch were required to wait until a disaster has already struck").

The preauthorization mechanism is consistent with OLC's historical approach to emergency powers, which has emphasized the functional imperatives of effective government response rather than rigid temporal formalities. The President's ability to protect the Nation should not depend on whether the President is physically able to sign a piece of paper during the 18-hour window between a G5 CME detection and impact — particularly when the communications infrastructure needed to transmit such a signed order to implementing agencies may itself be among the first systems disabled by the event.

**WILLIAMS PLAIN-LANGUAGE:** The DOJ's own legal opinions have said for decades that the President can pre-position resources before a disaster. If the government can put generators in warehouses before a storm, it can also declare that the storm is an emergency before it arrives. The alternative — waiting until the storm hits and the communications are dead — makes the law self-defeating.

## III. THE STAFFORD ACT — STATUTORY AUTHORITY FOR PREAUTHORIZED WAIVER

### A. The Gubernatorial Request Requirement

Section 401 of the Stafford Act, 42 U.S.C. §5170(a), provides:

> "All requests for a declaration by the President that a major disaster exists shall be made by the Governor of the affected State."

However, Section 501 of the Act, 42 U.S.C. §5192(a), provides an independent authority:

> "The President may direct any Federal agency ... to utilize its authorities and the resources granted to it under Federal law ... in support of State and local emergency assistance efforts to save lives and protect property and public health and safety, or to lessen or avert the threat of a catastrophe."

The plain text of §5192(a) does not require a gubernatorial request. It requires only that the President determine that an emergency exists for which the primary responsibility for response rests with the United States. The President's authority to make this determination is not temporally constrained.

Moreover, Section 402 of the Act, 42 U.S.C. §5170a, authorizes the President to "direct any Federal agency to utilize its authorities and the resources granted to it under Federal law ... in support of State and local assistance efforts," and the Post-Katrina Emergency Management Reform Act of 2006, 6 U.S.C. §701 et seq., explicitly authorizes FEMA to pre-position resources before an incident occurs.

**WILLIAMS PLAIN-LANGUAGE:** The Stafford Act has a back door. The front door requires a Governor to knock. The back door — Section 5192 — lets the President act when the primary responsibility belongs to the federal government. A continent-wide electromagnetic disaster that hits all 50 states simultaneously is about as clear a case of "federal primary responsibility" as can be imagined. The President isn't circumventing the Stafford Act; he's using the Stafford Act's own emergency provisions to bypass a procedure that the emergency itself would make impossible.

### B. The Delegation Question

The preauthorization mechanism does not delegate the President's personal judgment to an automatic trigger; it specifies the factual predicate — a G5 geomagnetic storm warning with Kp = 9 — that the President has determined constitutes an emergency. The determination itself is made by the President at the time of signing the Executive Order. NOAA's role is purely ministerial: to verify whether the specified factual predicate has occurred.

This is analogous to the "contingent legislation" doctrine recognized by the Supreme Court. See, e.g., United States v. Rock Royal Co-op., 307 U.S. 533, 577-78 (1939) (upholding congressional delegation of authority to the Secretary of Agriculture to issue marketing orders upon the occurrence of specified conditions, on the grounds that "Congress has stated the legislative objective, has set the bounds of the delegated authority, and has specified the conditions under which it is to be exercised"); see also Mistretta v. United States, 488 U.S. 361, 372-73 (1989) ("Congress simply cannot do its job absent an ability to delegate power under broad general directives").

If Congress may delegate legislative authority to the executive branch upon the occurrence of specified conditions — subject only to the "intelligible principle" test of J.W. Hampton, Jr. & Co. v. United States, 276 U.S. 394, 409 (1928) — then a fortiori the President may structure the timing of his own constitutionally and statutorily authorized determinations to take effect upon the occurrence of specified, objectively verifiable conditions.

**WILLIAMS PLAIN-LANGUAGE:** Congress can write a law that says "if X happens, the Secretary of Agriculture can do Y." The President can similarly say "when NOAA confirms X, my emergency declaration takes effect." The President is making the decision; NOAA is just the verification mechanism. This is standard administrative law. The trigger isn't a matter of judgment — it's a binary, scientific measurement (did the Kp index reach 9 or not?).

## IV. CONSTITUTIONAL ANALYSIS — ARTICLE II AUTHORITY

### A. The Commander-in-Chief Power

Article II, Section 2 of the Constitution designates the President as Commander in Chief of the armed forces. This authority includes the power to protect the Nation against threats to its security, whether those threats originate from foreign militaries, terrorist organizations, or natural phenomena. The electrical grid is essential to military readiness; without it, the Department of Defense cannot sustain operations, communicate with deployed forces, or maintain the industrial base that supports military capabilities.

The preauthorization of emergency powers for a G5 geomagnetic event is therefore supported by the President's Commander-in-Chief authority to the extent that grid protection and restoration are essential to national defense. The Defense Production Act — which this preauthorization activates — is expressly a national defense statute, and its invocation for grid protection purposes falls within the President's Commander-in-Chief authority.

### B. The Take Care Clause

Article II, Section 3 requires the President to "take Care that the Laws be faithfully executed." This Clause imposes on the President not merely a passive duty to enforce statutes as written, but an affirmative obligation to ensure that the statutory framework functions as intended to protect the Nation.

The Stafford Act and the National Emergencies Act were enacted to enable effective federal response to catastrophes. The President's duty to ensure these statutes function as intended includes the authority to interpret them in a manner that preserves their effectiveness under circumstances not specifically contemplated by Congress. A preauthorized declaration ensures that the Stafford Act and the NEA function effectively in the unique circumstances of a geomagnetic disaster; a contrary interpretation that requires contemporaneous presidential action during an 18-hour window of collapsing infrastructure would render these statutes self-defeating.

The Supreme Court has recognized that the President's duty under the Take Care Clause includes the authority to take actions necessary to ensure the effective operation of the statutory framework. See, e.g., In re Neagle, 135 U.S. 1 (1890) (the Take Care Clause is not limited to enforcement of congressional enactments but includes the protection of federal instrumentalities); In re Debs, 158 U.S. 564 (1895) (the President may take action to protect federal instrumentalities — in that case, the interstate railway system and postal service — from obstruction, even absent specific statutory authorization).

**WILLIAMS PLAIN-LANGUAGE:** The President's job includes making sure the laws work. If a law says "the President can declare emergencies," and the only kind of emergency that can't use that law is one where the President can't communicate the declaration because the emergency destroyed communications — that's not faithful execution, that's Kafka. The Take Care Clause means the President can fix this interpretation problem in advance.

## V. YOUNGSTOWN ANALYSIS — THE JACKSON FRAMEWORK

### A. The Tripartite Framework

Justice Jackson's concurrence in Youngstown Sheet & Tube Co. v. Sawyer, 343 U.S. 579, 635-38 (1952), established the canonical framework for evaluating the scope of presidential power:

> "1. When the President acts pursuant to an express or implied authorization of Congress, his authority is at its maximum, for it includes all that he possesses in his own right plus all that Congress can delegate. ...

> 2. When the President acts in absence of either a congressional grant or denial of authority, he can only rely upon his own independent powers, but there is a zone of twilight in which he and Congress may have concurrent authority, or in which its distribution is uncertain. ...

> 3. When the President takes measures incompatible with the expressed or implied will of Congress, his power is at its lowest ebb, for then he can rely only upon his own constitutional powers minus any constitutional powers of Congress over the matter."

### B. Application to Preauthorized Emergency Declarations

The preauthorization of a G5 geomagnetic emergency declaration falls squarely within Jackson's Category 1 — the President's maximum authority — because the President acts pursuant to express congressional authorization in the National Emergencies Act and the Stafford Act. Both statutes grant the President authority to declare emergencies; neither prohibits advance determination of what constitutes an emergency.

In contrast to Youngstown itself — where President Truman seized steel mills during the Korean War without statutory authority and in the face of Congress's express refusal to authorize such seizures — the preauthorization at issue here is supported by multiple express statutory grants of authority and is not incompatible with any congressional prohibition. The NEA and the Stafford Act grant the President broad discretion in the timing and structure of emergency declarations; the preauthorization mechanism is a reasonable exercise of that discretion, not a usurpation of congressional authority.

### C. Dames & Moore v. Regan and the "Specific Congressional Authorization" Standard

In Dames & Moore v. Regan, 453 U.S. 654 (1981), the Supreme Court upheld President Carter's executive agreement with Iran suspending claims in U.S. courts and transferring them to an international tribunal. The Court applied Jackson's framework and concluded that the President's action fell within the "zone of twilight" (Category 2) but was nonetheless valid because Congress had implicitly authorized such actions through the International Emergency Economic Powers Act (IEEPA) and a history of congressional acquiescence in executive claims settlement.

The Court in Dames & Moore emphasized that "a systematic, unbroken, executive practice, long pursued to the knowledge of the Congress and never before questioned" can constitute implicit congressional authorization for the exercise of presidential power. Id. at 686 (quoting Youngstown, 343 U.S. at 610-11 (Frankfurter, J., concurring)).

The preauthorization mechanism is supported by a stronger basis than the claims settlement agreement upheld in Dames & Moore: here, the President is acting pursuant to express — not merely implicit — statutory authorization (the NEA and the Stafford Act). The preauthorization mechanism is therefore more firmly grounded in legal authority than the executive action upheld in Dames & Moore.

**WILLIAMS PLAIN-LANGUAGE:** The Youngstown case is famous for a reason: it's the framework for deciding whether presidential action is legal. The answer depends on whether Congress has authorized the action (strongest case), said nothing (gray area), or said no (weakest case). The preauthorization of a G5 emergency falls in the strongest category — Congress explicitly authorized emergency declarations. The preauthorization is just the timing, not the substance. And the Supreme Court has upheld much more creative uses of presidential power (like President Carter settling all U.S. claims against Iran through an executive agreement) on shakier legal ground than this.

## VI. THE "SPECIFICITY, IMMINENCE, AND SEVERITY" TEST

### A. Formulation of the Test

To ensure that the preauthorization mechanism does not become a precedent for unbounded presidential discretion, we propose a three-part test — drawn from the specific characteristics of G5 geomagnetic events — that any future preauthorized emergency declaration should satisfy:

1. **Specificity.** The triggering event must be defined with sufficient precision that the factual predicate for the declaration can be determined through objective, scientific measurement without the exercise of discretion. A G5 geomagnetic storm warning with Kp = 9 satisfies this requirement: it is a specific, internationally recognized scientific classification, not a matter of judgment or policy preference.

2. **Imminence.** The triggering event must pose an imminent threat to the Nation — not a remote or speculative possibility. A G5 CME, once detected at the Sun-Earth L1 Lagrange point, will strike Earth within approximately 30-60 minutes, leaving no meaningful time for deliberative presidential decision-making. The threat is not merely imminent at the time of the declaration; it is immediate.

3. **Severity.** The triggering event must be of sufficient severity that the need for extraordinary federal action is beyond reasonable dispute. A G5 geomagnetic storm is the highest classification on the NOAA space weather scale. The National Academies of Sciences, Engineering, and Medicine have determined that such an event would cause long-term collapse of the North American electrical grid with economic costs of $1-2 trillion in the first year alone. No reasonable person could dispute that such an event requires immediate, maximum federal response.

### B. Application to G5 Geomagnetic Storm

The G5 geomagnetic storm preauthorization satisfies all three prongs of the test:

- **Specificity:** The trigger is a G5 geomagnetic storm warning with Kp = 9, issued by NOAA SWPC — an objective, internationally recognized scientific measurement made by a specific federal agency.

- **Imminence:** Once a G5 CME is detected at L1, impact occurs within 30-60 minutes. The preauthorization eliminates the fatal delay that would result from waiting for infrastructure collapse before initiating a deliberative declaration process.

- **Severity:** The consequences of a G5 CME impact on an unprotected electrical grid — destruction of 300+ large power transformers, 5-8 year grid restoration timeline, $1-2 trillion in economic damage — are of a magnitude that no reasonable person would dispute requires maximum federal response.

**WILLIAMS PLAIN-LANGUAGE:** We're proposing a simple three-part test that any future preauthorized emergency should pass: the trigger must be specific (not "when things get bad" but "when Kp = 9"), imminent (not "sometime in the future" but "the storm hits in 30-60 minutes"), and severe (not "a bad day" but "trillions of dollars of damage"). A G5 solar storm passes all three. This test serves as a guardrail to prevent misuse of the preauthorization precedent for less severe or less specific situations.

## VII. COUNTERARGUMENTS AND RESPONSES

### A. Counterargument: The Nondelegation Doctrine Precludes Preauthorization

Some may argue that the preauthorization mechanism delegates the President's personal discretion to an automatic trigger or to a subordinate official (the NOAA SWPC forecaster on duty), in violation of the principle that the President may not delegate the exercise of personal judgment required by statute.

**Response:** The nondelegation doctrine applies to Congress's delegation of legislative power to the executive branch, not to the President's structuring of the timing of his own determinations. Even within the nondelegation context, the Supreme Court has upheld contingent determinations that take effect upon the occurrence of specified factual predicates. See Mistretta, 488 U.S. at 372-73; J.W. Hampton, Jr. & Co., 276 U.S. at 409. If Congress may delegate to the President the authority to take actions upon the occurrence of specified conditions, the President may structure the timing of his own declarations in the same manner.

Moreover, the NOAA SWPC forecaster's role is purely ministerial: to verify whether a defined, objective, scientific condition (Kp = 9) has been met. The forecaster exercises no discretion, makes no policy judgment, and is bound by publicly available scientific criteria. This is not delegation; it is the President defining the factual predicate for his own action.

### B. Counterargument: Preauthorization Violates the Separation of Powers by Making Congress's Termination Authority Meaningless

Under 50 U.S.C. §1622, Congress may terminate a national emergency by concurrent resolution. Critics may argue that preauthorization makes this congressional check meaningless because the emergency takes effect without notice and with immediate consequences that Congress cannot reverse before the storm hits.

**Response:** The preauthorization mechanism includes an express requirement that the presidential declaration be transmitted to Congress "immediately" upon taking effect and published in the Federal Register. The 18-hour window between CME detection and impact provides Congress with sufficient time to convene — or at minimum for congressional leadership to be notified — and to consider a resolution of termination. While the practical ability of Congress to terminate the emergency within the 18-hour window is limited, this is a function of the nature of the threat, not of the preauthorization mechanism. Even a contemporaneous presidential declaration made at the time of the CME detection would face the same practical constraint: Congress would not have time to act before the storm hits.

The relevant question is whether Congress retains the ability to terminate the emergency after it begins — for example, if the emergency declaration were used for purposes beyond those authorized, or if the emergency persisted beyond the period of actual grid vulnerability. Congress clearly retains this authority. The NEA's termination mechanism operates prospectively, not retroactively, and remains fully available to Congress after the initial impact period.

### C. Counterargument: The Preauthorization Sets a Dangerous Precedent

Some may argue that allowing the President to make advance emergency declarations for one type of threat (geomagnetic storms) will inevitably lead to advance declarations for other types of threats, eroding the deliberative character of the emergency declaration process.

**Response:** The specificity-imminence-severity test described in Part VI provides a principled limitation on the preauthorization doctrine. Only events that satisfy all three prongs — specificity, imminence, and severity — are candidates for preauthorization. This test is satisfied by G5 geomagnetic storms but would not be satisfied by most other potential emergencies. Hurricanes, for example, may be severe but are not imminent with sufficient advance warning to require preauthorization rather than contemporaneous declaration. Terrorist attacks may be imminent but lack the specific, objective trigger that satisfies the specificity requirement. The preauthorization doctrine is tailored to the unique characteristics of GMD events, not a general license for advance declarations.

## VIII. CONCLUSION

For the foregoing reasons, we conclude that the preauthorization of a national emergency declaration for G5-class geomagnetic disturbances is constitutionally sound and within the President's existing statutory authorities under the National Emergencies Act, the Stafford Act, and Article II of the Constitution. The preauthorization mechanism is supported by (1) the text of the NEA and the Stafford Act, which impose no temporal restriction on emergency determinations; (2) the Take Care Clause, which requires the President to ensure that the statutory framework for disaster response functions effectively under the circumstances for which it was designed; (3) the President's Commander-in-Chief authority to protect the national defense infrastructure upon which military readiness depends; and (4) Justice Jackson's Youngstown framework, which places the preauthorization in Category 1 (maximum presidential authority supported by express congressional authorization).

The key legal risk — the nondelegation concern — is adequately addressed by the specificity, imminence, and severity of the G5 trigger, which eliminates any meaningful exercise of discretion in the determination that an emergency exists. The preauthorization is not a delegation of the President's judgment; it is the President's judgment, made in advance and based on the best available scientific evidence, that a specific, objectively verifiable event constitutes an emergency requiring immediate and maximum federal response.

We recommend that any Executive Order implementing this preauthorization include:
(i) an express citation to the NEA, the Stafford Act, and Article II authorities discussed in this memorandum;
(ii) a clear statement of the specificity, imminence, and severity test and the factual predicates satisfying it;
(iii) a severability clause; and
(iv) a provision for immediate congressional notification and Federal Register publication.
