## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This memorandum analyzes the existing international legal frameworks applicable to cross-border geomagnetic disturbance (GMD) preparedness and response, identifies legal gaps, and recommends the negotiation of a new multilateral Convention on Geomagnetic Disaster Coordination. The analysis covers (1) collective defense frameworks (NATO Article 5), (2) international disaster response conventions (Tampere Convention, Customs Convention on Temporary Importation of Relief Goods), (3) public health frameworks (International Health Regulations), (4) the Sendai Framework for Disaster Risk Reduction, (5) bilateral defense agreements and their extension to GMD, (6) customary international law on humanitarian assistance, and (7) international telecommunications law.

Key findings: (a) existing international law provides a patchwork of partially applicable frameworks, none of which is specifically designed for the unique characteristics of GMD disasters — simultaneous continent-scale impact, destruction of communications infrastructure, multi-year recovery timelines, and dependence on pre-event hardening for effective mitigation; (b) the most promising existing frameworks for adaptation include the Tampere Convention (telecommunications disaster assistance), the NATO Civil Emergency Planning framework, and the IEA's coordinated emergency response mechanisms; (c) significant legal gaps exist in: (i) preauthorized cross-border deployment of grid restoration personnel and equipment, (ii) harmonized technical standards for GIC protection equipment, (iii) coordinated strategic reserves of critical grid components, (iv) mutual recognition of equipment certifications, and (v) allocation of liability for cross-border cascading grid failures; (d) a new Convention on Geomagnetic Disaster Coordination is the most effective mechanism for addressing these gaps comprehensively.

## I. THE CORE PROBLEM: GMD DOES NOT RECOGNIZE JURISDICTIONAL BOUNDARIES

The fundamental legal challenge for GMD preparedness and response is jurisdictional. Coronal mass ejections create continent-scale electromagnetic effects that do not respect national borders, and the North American electrical grid is physically integrated across the United States, Canada, and Mexico. A transformer failure in Manitoba causes cascading voltage instability in Minnesota. A blackout in Baja California propagates into Southern California. The electrical grid of the European continent is similarly integrated, with interconnection across 35 nations. The grid of East Asia links China, Japan, South Korea, and Russia.

No existing international legal instrument comprehensively addresses this jurisdictional mismatch. The current framework consists of: (1) general disaster response conventions that do not account for GMD-specific characteristics; (2) regional collective defense arrangements that were not designed for natural electromagnetic threats; (3) bilateral agreements of limited scope; and (4) customary international law principles that are insufficiently specific to guide operational coordination.

This memorandum proceeds from the premise that existing frameworks should be leveraged where applicable, but that a new, purpose-built international instrument is necessary to establish legally binding mechanisms for: (a) preauthorized cross-border deployment; (b) harmonized technical standards; (c) coordinated strategic reserves; (d) mutual recognition of certifications; (e) liability allocation; and (f) shared funding of preparedness measures.

**WILLIAMS PLAIN-LANGUAGE:** The Sun's storms hit whole continents at once. The electric grid crosses borders everywhere. But international law wasn't written for continent-scale electromagnetic disasters. We need new agreements that let countries deploy repair crews across borders instantly, share spare parts, and coordinate recovery — because no country can protect its own grid if its neighbors' grids are unprotected and interconnected.

## II. EXISTING TREATY FRAMEWORKS — ANALYSIS AND APPLICABILITY

### A. NATO Article 5 — Collective Defense

**Text:** "The Parties agree that an armed attack against one or more of them in Europe or North America shall be considered an attack against them all..."

**Analysis:** Article 5 of the North Atlantic Treaty, 63 Stat. 2241, T.I.A.S. No. 1964 (1949), is triggered by an "armed attack against one or more" of the Parties. A coronal mass ejection, as a natural phenomenon, does not constitute an "armed attack" within the ordinary meaning of the treaty. The drafters of the Washington Treaty were contemplating conventional military aggression, not natural electromagnetic phenomena of extraterrestrial origin.

However, two lines of analysis merit consideration:

1. **Functional equivalence.** If a CME produces effects that are functionally equivalent to the effects of an armed attack — simultaneous destruction of critical infrastructure across all member states, rendering them incapable of collective defense — the question arises whether the collective response mechanisms of NATO, including the Civil Emergency Planning framework and the Euro-Atlantic Disaster Response Coordination Centre (EADRCC), should be activated on a functional rather than formal basis. This argument is novel and has not been tested in any NATO forum. It would likely require a formal decision of the North Atlantic Council rather than a legal interpretation by the NATO Office of Legal Affairs.

2. **Article 4 consultation.** Article 4 of the Washington Treaty provides that "The Parties will consult together whenever, in the opinion of any of them, the territorial integrity, political independence or security of any of the Parties is threatened." Unlike Article 5, Article 4 is not limited to "armed attacks" and could be invoked in response to a G5 geomagnetic storm that threatens the territorial integrity (through infrastructure collapse) or security (through military readiness degradation) of member states. Article 4 consultation would trigger NATO's civil emergency planning mechanisms and could serve as the legal basis for coordinated NATO response to a GMD event, even if Article 5 is not formally invoked.

**Recommendation:** The United States should (a) seek a formal opinion from the NATO Office of Legal Affairs on the applicability of Article 4 to GMD events; (b) propose the inclusion of GMD scenarios in NATO's Civil Emergency Planning framework; (c) explore the functional equivalence argument through Track II diplomatic engagements with allied legal scholars before raising it formally at the North Atlantic Council; and (d) not rely on Article 5 as the primary legal basis for NATO GMD coordination, as its applicability is likely to be rejected by at least several NATO members, undermining confidence in the Alliance's legal predictability.

**WILLIAMS PLAIN-LANGUAGE:** Can NATO respond to a solar storm? Article 5 probably doesn't apply — a solar flare isn't an "armed attack." But Article 4, which triggers NATO consultation when any member feels threatened, probably does apply. And NATO already has disaster response mechanisms that don't require Article 5. The smart play is to use NATO's existing civil emergency planning frameworks, not try to stretch Article 5 to cover the Sun.

### B. International Health Regulations (IHR) — WHO

**Citation:** International Health Regulations (2005), 58 U.N.T.S. (adopted May 23, 2005, entered into force June 15, 2007)

**Analysis:** The IHR are designed for public health emergencies of international concern (PHEICs) — infectious disease outbreaks, biological events, chemical spills, and radiological incidents. A GMD event does not directly fall within the IHR's scope, as it is not a public health event per se but an infrastructure catastrophe with public health consequences.

However, the IHR provide a useful procedural model for GMD coordination: (a) the PHEIC declaration mechanism (Article 12), which allows the WHO Director-General to declare an emergency that triggers international coordination obligations; (b) the National IHR Focal Point system (Article 4), which provides a model for designated national points of contact for international coordination; (c) the requirement for member states to develop "minimum core public health capacities" (Annex 1), which could be adapted to minimum core GMD preparedness capacities.

**Recommendation:** The IHR model should be studied as a procedural template for GMD coordination, but the United States should not advocate for expanding the IHR to cover GMD — the WHO's mandate is public health, not electrical infrastructure resilience. A separate convention on GMD coordination, drawing on IHR procedural mechanisms, is preferable.

### C. Tampere Convention on the Provision of Telecommunication Resources for Disaster Mitigation and Relief Operations

**Citation:** Tampere Convention, 2296 U.N.T.S. 5 (adopted June 18, 1998, entered into force January 8, 2005)

**Analysis:** The Tampere Convention is the most directly applicable existing treaty to GMD response, as it specifically addresses telecommunications disaster assistance — including the deployment of telecommunications equipment and personnel across borders during disasters. Key provisions relevant to GMD response include:

- **Article 4(1):** States Parties shall reduce or remove regulatory barriers to the provision of telecommunication assistance, including restrictions on the import and export of telecommunications equipment.
- **Article 4(4):** States Parties shall facilitate the entry of telecommunication assistance personnel, including expedited visa processing.
- **Article 5:** States Parties shall, where possible, waive or reduce customs duties and other restrictions on the import of telecommunication equipment during disaster response.
- **Article 9(4):** States Parties may request telecommunication assistance from other States Parties or from non-State entities during disasters.

**GMD Applicability:** The Tampere Convention provides a legally binding framework for the cross-border deployment of satellite communication terminals, HF radio equipment, and telecommunications restoration personnel during a GMD event. Its provisions on expedited entry, customs waiver, and regulatory barrier reduction directly address the practical obstacles to international GMD response. However, the Convention is limited to telecommunications and does not cover grid restoration equipment, fuel, medical supplies, or other essential GMD response materials.

**Recommendation:** The United States should ensure that its Tampere Convention implementing legislation and regulations are current and include provisions for GMD-specific telecommunications response. The Convention's procedural mechanisms — expedited entry, customs waiver, regulatory barrier reduction — should serve as a model for the broader GMD coordination convention, extended from telecommunications to all GMD response categories.

**WILLIAMS PLAIN-LANGUAGE:** There's already a treaty that lets countries send satellite phones and radios across borders during disasters without waiting for customs. It's called the Tampere Convention. The problem is it only covers telecom equipment. We need the same thing for transformers, generators, fuel, and repair crews. But the template exists — we can extend it.

### D. Customs Convention on the Temporary Importation of Relief Goods

**Citation:** Convention on Temporary Admission (Istanbul Convention), Annex B.9 (relief consignments), U.N.T.S. (adopted June 26, 1990)

**Analysis:** The Istanbul Convention and its predecessor, the Customs Convention on the Temporary Importation of Relief Goods (often called the "Model Agreement"), provide mechanisms for duty-free, expedited temporary importation of disaster relief goods. These conventions have been widely adopted and provide a functioning legal framework that can be directly applied to GMD response equipment.

**GMD Applicability:** Grid restoration equipment — including mobile substations, transformers, generators, and specialized tools — imported on a temporary basis from allied nations during a GMD response would be eligible for duty-free temporary admission under the Istanbul Convention, provided that the equipment is re-exported within the Convention's time limits (typically 12 months, extendable). This is a critical existing legal framework that enables the rapid cross-border movement of response equipment without the delays of standard customs processing.

**Recommendation:** The United States should (a) confirm its implementation of the Istanbul Convention's relief consignment provisions; (b) negotiate bilateral MoUs with Canada and Mexico to extend the temporary importation period beyond the Convention's default limits specifically for GMD response equipment, recognizing that grid restoration may require more than 12 months; and (c) develop pre-approved customs manifests for standard GMD response equipment to further expedite processing.

## III. BILATERAL AND REGIONAL FRAMEWORKS

### A. U.S.-Canada NORAD Agreement

**Citation:** North American Aerospace Defense Command Agreement (1958, as renewed)

**Analysis:** NORAD provides a functioning, legally binding bilateral framework for continent-scale surveillance and coordinated response. The NORAD mission includes aerospace warning and aerospace control — functions that are directly applicable to GMD monitoring (detection and tracking of CMEs) and early warning dissemination. NORAD's command and control infrastructure, hardened communications systems, and integrated U.S.-Canadian personnel structure provide a proven model for bilateral GMD coordination.

**Extension to GMD:** The NORAD agreement could be extended — either through an amending protocol or a parallel agreement — to include GMD monitoring and warning as an explicit NORAD mission function. This would leverage existing infrastructure and personnel rather than creating a new bilateral institution. The operational concept would be: NORAD receives CME detection data from NOAA SWPC and Natural Resources Canada, processes it through its existing aerospace warning architecture, and disseminates coordinated U.S.-Canadian warnings to the North American electric utility sector. This is functionally analogous to NORAD's existing role in tracking and warning of airborne threats.

**Legal feasibility:** Extension of NORAD to include GMD monitoring would likely require an exchange of diplomatic notes between the U.S. and Canadian governments, or a protocol amending the existing NORAD agreement, rather than a new treaty. Either mechanism is within the President's Article II foreign affairs authority and would not require Senate advice and consent (the NORAD agreement itself is an executive agreement, not a treaty). Congressional consultation under the Case-Zablocki Act (1 U.S.C. §112b) would be required for any amendment.

**WILLIAMS PLAIN-LANGUAGE:** NORAD already watches the skies for missiles and bombers. We can add "watching the Sun for killer magnetic storms" to its mission. Same infrastructure, same bilateral command structure, same hardened communications. It's the most efficient way to coordinate U.S.-Canada GMD warning without building something new.

### B. U.S.-Mexico Border Cooperation Framework

The existing U.S.-Mexico border cooperation agreements — including the U.S.-Mexico Border 2025 Program and the bilateral emergency management coordination mechanisms established after Hurricane Katrina (when Mexico deployed military and civilian aid to the United States) — provide a foundation for GMD coordination. The key legal challenge is that existing border cooperation agreements focus on environmental protection and public health, not grid infrastructure resilience. New bilateral instruments will be necessary.

## IV. CUSTOMARY INTERNATIONAL LAW AND SOFT LAW FRAMEWORKS

### A. Customary International Law on Humanitarian Assistance

Customary international law imposes on states an obligation to "facilitate" the provision of humanitarian assistance during disasters, as recognized by the International Law Commission's Draft Articles on the Protection of Persons in the Event of Disasters (2016). Key principles applicable to GMD response include:

- **Duty to Cooperate:** Article 7 of the ILC Draft Articles provides that "States shall cooperate among themselves" in disaster response.
- **Consent Requirement:** Article 13 provides that external assistance requires the consent of the affected State. However, the ILC commentary notes that consent "shall not be withheld arbitrarily."
- **Facilitation of External Assistance:** Article 16 requires States to "take the necessary measures" to facilitate the prompt and effective provision of external assistance.

These customary principles apply to GMD events and establish a general obligation to facilitate cross-border humanitarian assistance, but they lack the specificity necessary for coordinated grid restoration operations. The governing principle — that assistance requires the consent of the affected State — could become a significant obstacle during a GMD event if an affected State is unable to communicate its consent (because its communications infrastructure has been destroyed by the GMD). This highlights the need for pre-event consent through bilateral or multilateral agreements.

### B. Sendai Framework for Disaster Risk Reduction

The Sendai Framework for Disaster Risk Reduction 2015-2030 (U.N. Doc. A/RES/69/283) provides a non-binding policy framework for international disaster risk reduction. While not legally binding, the Sendai Framework establishes norms for national disaster preparedness that inform the interpretation of customary international law and provide a political foundation for more specific legal instruments.

The Sendai Framework's "Priority 4: Enhancing disaster preparedness for effective response" explicitly calls upon states to "develop and strengthen disaster preparedness and response capacities" and to "promote regular disaster preparedness, response and recovery exercises." These norms support the creation of a legally binding GMD coordination convention that would operationalize the Sendai Framework's general aspirations in the specific context of geomagnetic disasters.

## V. PROPOSED INSTRUMENT: CONVENTION ON GEOMAGNETIC DISASTER COORDINATION

### A. Justification for a New Convention

No existing treaty or convention adequately addresses the unique legal challenges of GMD disaster coordination. The gaps identified in this memorandum cannot be filled through interpretation of existing instruments or through a patchwork of bilateral agreements. The problem is global in scope and requires a global legal framework.

### B. Core Provisions — Draft Outline

The proposed Convention would include the following core provisions:

**Article 1 — Definitions.** Define "geomagnetic disturbance," "G5-class event," "critical grid infrastructure," "grid restoration," "mutual assistance," and other key terms, drawing on NOAA SWPC and International Space Environment Service (ISES) technical definitions.

**Article 2 — General Obligation.** Establish a general obligation of States Parties to cooperate in GMD preparedness and response, to maintain minimum levels of GMD preparedness (consistent with national capabilities), and to facilitate the prompt provision of mutual assistance during GMD events.

**Article 3 — Preauthorized Cross-Border Deployment.** Require States Parties to establish preauthorized procedures for the cross-border deployment of grid restoration personnel, equipment, and materials during declared GMD emergencies, including: (a) expedited visa processing (target: 2 hours or less at designated border crossings); (b) temporary recognition of foreign professional credentials for grid restoration personnel; (c) duty-free temporary admission of restoration equipment under the Istanbul Convention framework; (d) freedom of movement for humanitarian and restoration convoys within affected territories.

**Article 4 — Harmonized Technical Standards.** Establish a process for the development of harmonized international standards for GIC protection equipment, transformer hardening, and GMD monitoring, to be administered by the International Electrotechnical Commission (IEC) in coordination with IEEE and NERC.

**Article 5 — Coordinated Strategic Reserves.** Establish a framework for the coordination of national strategic reserves of large power transformers and other critical grid components, modeled on the International Energy Program's strategic petroleum reserve coordination mechanism, including pre-agreed allocation formulas for the release of reserves during GMD events.

**Article 6 — Mutual Recognition of Certifications.** Require States Parties to mutually recognize equipment certifications for GIC protection equipment, so that a device certified in one State Party is accepted for deployment in all States Parties without redundant testing, reducing costs and accelerating deployment.

**Article 7 — Liability Allocation.** Establish a liability framework that: (a) provides immunity from civil liability under the receiving State's law for duly authorized foreign grid restoration personnel acting within the scope of their mission; (b) allocates liability for damage caused by foreign restoration equipment to the sending or receiving State based on a fault-based standard; (c) establishes a dispute resolution mechanism.

**Article 8 — Financing.** Establish an International Geomagnetic Preparedness Fund (IGPF) to provide concessional financing to developing nations for GMD preparedness activities, funded through assessed contributions based on a formula reflecting GDP and grid vulnerability.

**Article 9 — Information Sharing.** Require States Parties to share GMD monitoring data, grid vulnerability assessments, and restoration status information through a common platform administered by the WMO and the IEA.

**Article 10 — Institutional Mechanisms.** Establish a Conference of the Parties (COP), a Secretariat (to be hosted by the IEA or WMO), and a Scientific and Technical Advisory Body.

**Article 11 — Dispute Settlement.** Establish a mechanism for the peaceful settlement of disputes arising under the Convention, including good offices, mediation, and voluntary arbitration.

**Article 12 — Relationship to Other Instruments.** Clarify that the Convention supplements, and does not supersede, existing instruments including the Tampere Convention, the Istanbul Convention, and NATO civil emergency planning mechanisms.

### C. Negotiating Strategy

The United States should pursue the Convention through a stepwise approach:

**Phase 1 (Years 1-2):** Bilateral executive agreements with Canada and Mexico establishing the core mechanisms (preauthorized cross-border deployment, harmonized standards, coordinated reserves).

**Phase 2 (Years 2-3):** Expansion to key allies through executive agreements with the United Kingdom, Japan, South Korea, Germany, and Australia — leveraging existing defense and trade relationships.

**Phase 3 (Years 3-5):** Multilateral convention under UN auspices. Request the UN General Assembly to establish an Ad Hoc Committee on Geomagnetic Disaster Coordination, modeled on the process that produced the UN Convention on the Law of the Sea and the Arms Trade Treaty. Given the non-contentious nature of the subject matter (there is no constituency opposed to GMD preparedness), the negotiating timeline could be compressed relative to more politically charged treaty negotiations.

**WILLIAMS PLAIN-LANGUAGE:** We start with Canada and Mexico — our grid neighbors. Then we add our closest allies — UK, Japan, Germany, Australia, South Korea. Then we go to the UN and propose a global convention. Nobody opposes protecting the grid from the Sun. This is the easiest multilateral treaty negotiation in history — everyone benefits, nobody loses.

## VI. CONCLUSION

Existing international law provides a foundation but not a complete framework for cross-border GMD coordination. The Tampere Convention and Istanbul Convention provide useful procedural models; NATO Article 4 provides a consultation mechanism; and customary international law establishes general obligations of cooperation. However, the specific legal mechanisms necessary for effective GMD response — preauthorized cross-border deployment, harmonized technical standards, coordinated strategic reserves, mutual recognition of certifications, and liability allocation — require a new legal instrument.

The proposed Convention on Geomagnetic Disaster Coordination provides a comprehensive, legally binding framework that addresses all identified gaps and can be negotiated incrementally, beginning with bilateral agreements and expanding to a multilateral convention within five years.

The cost of not establishing this legal framework — measured in the months of additional grid restoration time attributable to customs delays, incompatible equipment standards, and uncoordinated reserve deployments — is estimated to be $100-300 billion in additional economic losses during a G5 event. The cost of negotiating the Convention: approximately $50 million in diplomatic, legal, and technical support costs over five years. The benefit-cost ratio: 2,000:1 to 6,000:1. This is among the highest-return diplomatic investments the United States can make.
