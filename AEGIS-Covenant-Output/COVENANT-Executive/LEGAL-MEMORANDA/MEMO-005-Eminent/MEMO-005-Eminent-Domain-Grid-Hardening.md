## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This memorandum analyzes the constitutional and statutory issues arising from the physical installation of geomagnetic disturbance (GMD) protection equipment on privately owned electrical infrastructure, the imposition of mandatory GMD hardening requirements on investor-owned utilities (IOUs), and the potential need for eminent domain to facilitate grid hardening. The Fifth Amendment to the United States Constitution provides that private property shall not be taken for public use without just compensation. The application of this principle to mandatory GMD hardening raises three categories of questions: (1) physical takings — does the mandatory installation of GIC-blocking capacitors on utility-owned transformers or customer premises constitute a physical occupation requiring compensation?; (2) regulatory takings — does the imposition of mandatory hardening requirements that reduce the economic value of utility property constitute a regulatory taking under the Penn Central test?; and (3) eminent domain — if voluntary compliance is insufficient, may the federal government exercise eminent domain to install GMD protection on privately owned critical grid infrastructure?

We conclude that: (a) mandatory hardening requirements imposed on investor-owned utilities are likely to survive Penn Central analysis and do not constitute compensable regulatory takings, provided that (i) cost recovery through rate-based amortization is authorized, (ii) the requirements are directly related to a legitimate public purpose (grid protection from GMD), and (iii) the economic impact does not deprive the utility of all economically beneficial use of its property; (b) physical installation of GMD protection equipment on customer-side premises (as distinct from utility-side infrastructure) may constitute a physical taking requiring just compensation, particularly where the equipment occupies space on private property; (c) voluntary compliance incentives — including accelerated depreciation, tax credits, and cost-sharing grants — are strongly preferred to eminent domain and are likely to achieve substantially the same result at lower constitutional risk and political cost; (d) if eminent domain is necessary for critical grid-hardening infrastructure, existing federal eminent domain authority under 40 U.S.C. §3113 (acquisition of real property for public buildings) and the Natural Gas Act (15 U.S.C. §717f(h)) model of eminent domain for critical energy infrastructure provides a statutory framework that can be adapted through new legislation.

**WILLIAMS PLAIN-LANGUAGE:** Can the government force private electric companies to install solar-storm protection equipment? Yes — with some important limits. There's a difference between telling a utility "you must harden your own transformers" (which is constitutional regulation, especially if you let them recover costs through rates) and telling a homeowner "we're putting a capacitor in your yard" (which may require paying compensation). The best approach is incentives, not force: tax breaks, accelerated depreciation, and cost-sharing grants that make hardening the smart business decision rather than a legal mandate. But if some utilities refuse, the government has tools — and this memo explains when and how to use them.

## I. THE PROPERTY RIGHTS LANDSCAPE IN ELECTRICAL GRID HARDENING

### A. Categories of Affected Property

GMD hardening requirements affect three categories of property, each with distinct constitutional implications:

1. **Utility-Owned Transmission and Distribution Infrastructure.** Transformers, substations, transmission towers, and distribution equipment owned by investor-owned utilities, municipal utilities, and rural electric cooperatives. These are business assets, not personal residences, and the owners are regulated public utilities with a statutory duty to provide reliable electric service.

2. **Customer-Side Equipment and Premises.** Electrical service entrances, meter sockets, disconnect switches, and the physical space on private property where GMD protection equipment (particularly whole-house GIC-blocking devices) would be installed. This encompasses both residential and commercial properties with distinct expectations of privacy and property rights.

3. **Rights-of-Way and Easements.** The land corridors through which transmission and distribution lines pass, many of which are held by utilities through easements or rights-of-way that may or may not permit the installation of additional equipment (such as GIC-blocking capacitors in substations).

### B. The Public Utility Distinction

The Supreme Court has long recognized that public utilities — because they hold themselves out to serve the public and operate under extensive state and federal regulation — have a diminished expectation of property rights relative to ordinary property owners. See, e.g., Munn v. Illinois, 94 U.S. 113 (1876) ("When, therefore, one devotes his property to a use in which the public has an interest, he, in effect, grants to the public an interest in that use, and must submit to be controlled by the public for the common good."); Duquesne Light Co. v. Barasch, 488 U.S. 299 (1989) (utilities have no constitutionally protected interest in any particular rate of return).

This diminished expectation is critical to the takings analysis. A regulatory requirement that a utility install GMD protection on its own transformers — with cost recovery through rates — is far less likely to constitute a compensable taking than a requirement that a private homeowner install similar equipment at their own expense.

## II. PHYSICAL TAKINGS ANALYSIS

### A. Per Se Physical Takings

The Supreme Court has held that a permanent physical occupation of private property — no matter how small — constitutes a per se taking requiring just compensation. Loretto v. Teleprompter Manhattan CATV Corp., 458 U.S. 419 (1982) (requiring a landlord to permit installation of cable television equipment on her building, occupying less than one cubic foot of space, was a compensable taking); see also Horne v. Department of Agriculture, 576 U.S. 350 (2015) (government requirement that raisin growers physically set aside a portion of their crop constituted a per se physical taking).

Under Loretto, if the federal government requires a private property owner to permit the physical installation of GMD protection equipment on their property — for example, a GIC-blocking device installed at the electrical service entrance of a private residence or commercial building — the requirement may constitute a per se physical taking requiring just compensation. The compensation would be measured by the fair market value of the space occupied by the equipment, plus any diminution in the property's overall value caused by the equipment's presence (aesthetic impact, noise from cooling fans, electromagnetic field concerns, etc.).

### B. Mitigation — Utility-Side Installation

The Loretto concern is largely avoidable. GMD protection can be installed almost entirely on utility-side infrastructure — at substations, on transmission towers, and at transformer locations — rather than on customer-side premises. GIC-blocking capacitors are most effective when installed at the neutral grounding point of transformers, which in virtually all cases is located at the utility's substation, not on customer property. MXene shielding is applied to the transformer housing itself — again, utility-side. CNT-doped dielectric insulation replaces the insulation inside the transformer — utility-side.

The only GMD protection measure that would require customer-side installation is a whole-house or whole-building GIC-blocking device at the electrical service entrance. This measure is technologically feasible but is a lower priority than transformer-level protection and can be implemented through voluntary incentive programs (tax credits for voluntary installation) rather than mandatory physical occupation. The Accountant Heuristic analysis (CSMEval/COST-BENEFIT-001 §6.3) demonstrates that transformer-level protection alone achieves approximately 85% of the total GMD protection benefit, with customer-side protection providing a marginal additional 15% benefit at significantly higher per-property cost.

**Recommendation:** Priority should be given to utility-side GMD protection, which avoids Loretto concerns entirely. Customer-side protection should be implemented through voluntary incentive programs (tax credits, rebates, accelerated depreciation) rather than mandatory installation requirements.

**WILLIAMS PLAIN-LANGUAGE:** The Supreme Court said the government can't make you put a cable box on your building without paying you — even if it's tiny. Same logic applies here: we can't make a homeowner install a capacitor in their yard without paying compensation. But here's the trick: most of the protection happens at the utility's own substations and transformers — not on private property. We can protect 85% of the grid without touching anyone's yard. For the remaining 15% at people's homes, we use incentives, not mandates.

## III. REGULATORY TAKINGS ANALYSIS — THE PENN CENTRAL FRAMEWORK

### A. The Penn Central Test

In Penn Central Transportation Co. v. New York City, 438 U.S. 104 (1978), the Supreme Court established a three-factor balancing test for determining whether a land-use regulation constitutes a compensable regulatory taking:

1. **Economic Impact of the Regulation on the Claimant.** The extent to which the regulation interferes with distinct investment-backed expectations. A regulation that merely reduces the profitability of a business does not constitute a taking; a regulation that denies all economically beneficial use of the property does (Lucas v. South Carolina Coastal Council, 505 U.S. 1003 (1992)).

2. **The Character of the Governmental Action.** A physical invasion is more likely to constitute a taking than a regulation that merely adjusts the benefits and burdens of economic life. Regulations that protect public health and safety are less likely to constitute takings.

3. **Interference with Investment-Backed Expectations.** The more a regulation interferes with the reasonable expectations of the property owner at the time of investment, the more likely it is to constitute a taking. If the regulation is foreseeable — particularly in a heavily regulated industry — the property owner has weaker expectations.

### B. Application to Utility GMD Hardening Mandates

**Factor 1 — Economic Impact.** A mandatory GMD hardening requirement imposed on investor-owned utilities will impose significant costs — estimated by CSMEval/COST-BENEFIT-003 at $20-40 billion industry-wide over 48 months. However, these costs do not deprive the utilities of all economically beneficial use of their property. The utilities continue to operate, collect revenue, and earn a regulated return. Moreover, cost recovery through rate-based amortization — which COVENANT-EO-001 §5(c) and COVENANT-EO-003 §5 authorize — substantially mitigates the economic impact by spreading costs over 30 years and allocating 75% of the capital cost to federal cost-sharing.

Under Duquesne Light Co. v. Barasch, 488 U.S. 299 (1989), utilities have no constitutional right to any particular rate of return or to avoid capital expenditures mandated by regulators in the public interest. The Supreme Court has consistently held that public utility regulation is a permissible exercise of the police power, and that the imposition of service obligations — even costly ones — is not a taking provided that the utility retains the ability to earn a just and reasonable return.

**Factor 2 — Character of the Governmental Action.** GMD hardening requirements are classic public health and safety regulation. They serve the same purposes as fire code requirements for commercial buildings, earthquake retrofitting mandates for hospitals, and flood protection requirements for infrastructure in flood plains — all of which have been uniformly upheld against takings challenges. The governmental interest in protecting the electrical grid against a natural electromagnetic threat that could cause trillions of dollars in damage and threaten millions of lives is among the most compelling governmental interests imaginable.

The Supreme Court has recognized that regulations protecting public health and safety are at the "heart of the state's police power" and are correspondingly less likely to constitute takings. See Keystone Bituminous Coal Ass'n v. DeBenedictis, 480 U.S. 470, 491 (1987) (upholding Pennsylvania's mine subsidence prevention law against a takings challenge, noting that the law served "important public interests" in environmental protection and public safety).

**Factor 3 — Investment-Backed Expectations.** Investor-owned utilities operate in one of the most heavily regulated industries in the United States. They are subject to rate regulation, reliability standards (including the NERC GMD reliability standards adopted under FERC Order 830), environmental requirements, and safety mandates. The imposition of additional reliability standards — including GMD hardening requirements — is entirely foreseeable to any utility investor. Under Connolly v. Pension Benefit Guaranty Corp., 475 U.S. 211 (1986), those who operate in a "heavily regulated industry" cannot reasonably expect that regulation will remain static. The electrical utility industry — subject to comprehensive federal and state regulation for nearly a century — is the paradigm case of a heavily regulated industry with diminished investment-backed expectations.

### C. Conclusion on Regulatory Takings

Mandatory GMD hardening requirements imposed on investor-owned utilities are unlikely to constitute compensable regulatory takings under the Penn Central framework, provided that: (i) the utility retains the ability to earn a just and reasonable return on its investment (including the hardening investment); (ii) cost recovery through rates is authorized; and (iii) the requirements are directly related to protecting grid reliability and public safety.

The legal risk is not zero — courts may view industry-wide costs of $20-40 billion as imposing an unusually severe economic impact even on regulated utilities. To mitigate this risk, the implementing legislation and regulations should include explicit cost-recovery mechanisms (rate-based amortization, federal cost-sharing, accelerated depreciation) and should be structured to phase in requirements over time, allowing utilities to plan capital expenditures rather than absorbing them all at once.

**WILLIAMS PLAIN-LANGUAGE:** Courts use a three-part test for whether a regulation amounts to a "taking" of private property. GMD hardening requirements pass the test: (1) they don't destroy the utility's business — just require an investment (and the utility gets to recover most of the cost through rates); (2) they're public safety regulations, which courts give more leeway to; (3) utilities are already heavily regulated, so they can't claim "I never expected the government to tell me what to do." As long as we let utilities charge customers for the cost over time and provide federal cost-sharing, the takings claim is weak.

## IV. EMINENT DOMAIN — CONSTITUTIONAL AND STATUTORY FRAMEWORK

### A. The Fifth Amendment Public Use Requirement

If voluntary compliance with GMD hardening requirements is insufficient — for example, if a recalcitrant utility refuses to install GIC protection, or if a private landowner refuses to permit access for transmission line hardening — the federal government may exercise eminent domain to acquire the property interests necessary for grid hardening. The Fifth Amendment requires that any such taking be for a "public use" and that "just compensation" be paid.

The public use requirement is easily satisfied: protecting the electrical grid against catastrophic failure is among the most compelling public uses imaginable, directly analogous to the public use of eminent domain for highways, airports, dams, and military installations, all of which have been upheld by the Supreme Court. See Kelo v. City of New London, 545 U.S. 469 (2005) (economic development constitutes a public use); Hawaii Housing Authority v. Midkiff, 467 U.S. 229 (1984) (eliminating oligopolistic land ownership constitutes a public use). If breaking up a land oligopoly in Hawaii is a public use, protecting the continent's electrical grid against destruction is a fortiori a public use.

### B. Existing Statutory Authority for Eminent Domain

The federal government possesses several existing eminent domain authorities that could be applied to grid hardening:

1. **40 U.S.C. §3113.** The Administrator of General Services may acquire real property — including by condemnation — for public buildings and related purposes. This authority is limited to GSA's property acquisition mission and would not extend to grid infrastructure hardening on private utility property.

2. **Natural Gas Act, 15 U.S.C. §717f(h).** FERC certificate holders for natural gas pipelines may exercise eminent domain to acquire necessary rights-of-way. This model — of conditioning a federal permit or certificate on the grant of eminent domain authority — could be extended to electric transmission infrastructure hardening. A statutory amendment to the Federal Power Act (16 U.S.C. §824 et seq.) could authorize FERC to grant certificates of public convenience and necessity for GMD hardening projects, with attendant eminent domain authority.

3. **16 U.S.C. §814 (Federal Power Act §21).** FPA licensees may exercise eminent domain for hydroelectric projects. This model could be extended to GMD protection.

4. **40 U.S.C. §3114.** The Secretary of a military department may acquire real property for military purposes. This authority could support military installation GMD hardening under COVENANT-EO-004 but would not extend to civilian grid infrastructure.

The existing eminent domain authorities are insufficient to support a comprehensive grid hardening program. New legislation — specifically, a statutory grant of eminent domain authority to FERC or the Secretary of Energy for GMD protection purposes — is necessary. The COVENANT-BILL-004 (Critical Infrastructure Hardening Act) should include such a provision.

### C. Just Compensation Calculation

If eminent domain is exercised, just compensation is measured by the fair market value of the property taken at the time of the taking. For GMD hardening, the property taken would typically be:

- **Easements for substation expansion** to accommodate GIC-blocking capacitor installations. Compensation: the fair market value of the easement, typically measured as a percentage of the fee simple value of the affected land.

- **Temporary construction easements** for installation, testing, and maintenance. Compensation: the rental value of the land for the period of the easement.

- **Permanent equipment footprints.** Compensation: the proportionate value of the land occupied by the equipment.

The total just compensation cost for eminent domain acquisition necessary to facilitate grid hardening is estimated by CSMEval/COST-BENEFIT-003 §7.2 at $1.5-3.0 billion, assuming eminent domain is necessary for approximately 15% of substation hardening projects (voluntary compliance and negotiated easements will achieve the remaining 85%). This cost is within the $47.5 billion total hardening budget and represents approximately 3-6% of total hardening costs — consistent with the typical eminent domain cost ratio for infrastructure projects of comparable scale (e.g., the interstate highway system, for which land acquisition costs represented approximately 5% of total construction costs).

**WILLIAMS PLAIN-LANGUAGE:** If a utility refuses to let us protect its own transformers — or if a landowner refuses to let us access a substation for hardening — we can take the land (or easement) and pay fair market value. This is what the government did to build the interstate highways. It's what pipeline companies do under the Natural Gas Act. We'd be using it for a purpose that's arguably more important than a highway: preventing a continent-wide blackout that kills millions. The total cost of land acquisition is estimated at $1.5-3 billion — a small fraction of the total hardening budget.

## V. LEGISLATIVE ALTERNATIVES TO EMINENT DOMAIN

Eminent domain is legally available but politically costly. The Accountant Heuristic and the El Segundo Heuristic both counsel that voluntary compliance incentives — which achieve the same result without the legal challenges, political opposition, and public relations cost of eminent domain — should be maximized before eminent domain is deployed. We recommend the following alternative mechanisms, in descending order of preference:

### A. Tier 1 — Voluntary Incentive Programs (Preferred)

1. **Accelerated Depreciation.** Amend the Internal Revenue Code (26 U.S.C. §168) to classify GMD protection equipment as 5-year property eligible for accelerated depreciation (Modified Accelerated Cost Recovery System — MACRS), reducing the after-tax cost of hardening by approximately 25-30%.

2. **Investment Tax Credit (ITC).** Establish a 30% investment tax credit for GMD protection equipment installed on utility infrastructure, modeled on the solar ITC (26 U.S.C. §48). The ITC reduces the upfront cost of hardening by 30% and is refundable (i.e., available to utilities that may have limited tax liability).

3. **Federal Cost-Sharing Grants.** The 75% federal / 25% utility cost-share model established by COVENANT-EO-001 §5(c) and COVENANT-EO-003. This reduces the utility's out-of-pocket cost to 25% of the capital expenditure, with the remaining 75% funded by federal grant.

4. **Rate-Based Amortization with Rider.** Authorize FERC and state public utility commissions to allow utilities to recover GMD hardening costs through a dedicated rate rider, amortized over 30 years, enabling utilities to finance hardening without absorbing the full capital cost in a single rate case.

5. **Green Bond / Resilience Bond Financing.** Authorize the issuance of tax-exempt "grid resilience bonds" for GMD hardening projects, reducing the cost of debt financing for utilities by 1-2 percentage points.

### B. Tier 2 — Conditional Incentives (If Tier 1 Insufficient)

1. **Priority Queue for Hardened Utilities.** FERC and RTOs/ISOs could grant priority interconnection queue positions to transmission projects that include GMD hardening, creating a competitive advantage for voluntary compliance.

2. **Favorable Rate of Return on Hardening Investment.** State PUCs could authorize a higher rate of return on equity for GMD hardening investments than for general rate base investments, providing an explicit financial incentive for voluntary compliance.

3. **Federal Reliability Compliance Credit.** FERC could grant compliance credit toward existing NERC reliability standards for utilities that install GMD protection, reducing their overall compliance burden.

### C. Tier 3 — Mandates with Opt-Out (Last Resort Before Eminent Domain)

If voluntary incentives are insufficient, the federal government could impose mandatory GMD hardening requirements with an opt-out provision: a utility that demonstrates, to the satisfaction of FERC or the Secretary of Energy, that it has adequate alternative GMD protection (such as islanding capability, rapid transformer replacement capacity, or equivalent measures) may opt out of the hardening mandate. This provides regulatory flexibility while maintaining the default requirement.

### D. Eminent Domain

Only if Tier 1 (incentives), Tier 2 (conditional incentives), and Tier 3 (mandates with opt-out) are insufficient to achieve the grid hardening necessary to protect national security should eminent domain be deployed. Even then, eminent domain should be limited to the specific recalcitrant entities whose noncompliance threatens grid-wide protection, not used as a general enforcement mechanism.

**WILLIAMS PLAIN-LANGUAGE:** The smart approach is: (1) make it financially irresistible — tax breaks, cost-sharing, low-interest bonds — so utilities WANT to harden their transformers; (2) if some still won't, make hardening mandatory but offer alternatives (like rapid spare transformer deployment capability); (3) only as a last resort, for utilities whose refusal threatens the whole grid, use eminent domain. This is the "all carrot, only stick when necessary" approach. It's cheaper, faster, and less legally risky than trying to force everyone through eminent domain from the start.

## VI. STATE-LEVEL TAKINGS ISSUES

Many of the takings issues associated with GMD hardening will arise at the state level, where public utility commissions regulate utility property and state constitutions often provide property rights protections that exceed the federal Fifth Amendment baseline. State PUCs have broad authority to mandate infrastructure investments and to authorize cost recovery, and state courts apply both federal and state takings jurisprudence.

The federal government should support state PUCs through: (a) model legislation that provides a clear state-level statutory framework for GMD hardening mandates, including cost-recovery mechanisms; (b) technical assistance from DOE and FERC to state PUCs in evaluating GMD hardening proposals; (c) federal preemption of state laws that impede GMD hardening, under the Supremacy Clause, to the extent such laws are inconsistent with federal GMD protection policy.

---

## VII. CONCLUSION

The imposition of mandatory GMD hardening requirements on investor-owned utilities is constitutional and is unlikely to constitute a compensable regulatory taking under the Penn Central framework, provided that the regulatory scheme includes cost-recovery mechanisms and is phased in over a reasonable period. The physical installation of GMD protection equipment on customer-side premises raises Loretto concerns that are best addressed through voluntary incentive programs rather than mandatory installation requirements. Eminent domain is constitutionally available but is a tool of last resort; voluntary compliance incentives, conditional mandates, and rate-based cost recovery should be exhausted before eminent domain is exercised.

The total compensation liability for eminent domain, if necessary, is estimated at $1.5-3.0 billion — less than 6% of the total grid hardening budget of $47.5 billion. The cost of voluntary incentives (accelerated depreciation, ITC, cost-sharing grants) is estimated at $8-12 billion over 10 years — substantially less than the cost of litigation and delay that would result from over-reliance on eminent domain.

The El Segundo principle applies: calm, measured implementation of a well-designed regulatory framework — emphasizing voluntary compliance, cost recovery, and phased implementation — will achieve grid hardening objectives at lower cost and with fewer legal challenges than aggressive use of eminent domain or mandatory installation requirements that disregard property rights.
