## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This memorandum analyzes the legal basis for providing liability protection — commonly referred to as a "liability shield" — to (1) manufacturers of geomagnetic disturbance (GMD) protection equipment, including GIC-blocking capacitors, MXene shielding films, CNT-doped dielectric composites, and related products, and (2) electric utilities that install federally mandated or subsidized GIC protection measures.

The analysis identifies four independent legal models that provide a basis for liability protection, listed in descending order of legal robustness: (1) the Price-Anderson Act model of catastrophic risk indemnification (42 U.S.C. §2210); (2) the SAFETY Act framework for anti-terrorism technologies (6 U.S.C. §§441-444); (3) the government contractor defense established in Boyle v. United Technologies Corp., 487 U.S. 500 (1988); and (4) federal preemption of state tort claims for federally mandated or approved activities under the Supremacy Clause.

We conclude that (a) existing law provides limited but incomplete protection; (b) the most effective mechanism is a stand-alone federal indemnification statute modeled on Price-Anderson, which would cap aggregate liability for GMD protection equipment manufacturers at a commercially insurable level and establish a federal indemnification layer above that cap; (c) the government contractor defense, extended by statute to GMD protection equipment produced under DPA priorities and allocations, provides a second layer of protection that can be implemented through executive action without new legislation; and (d) federal preemption of state tort claims related to federally mandated hardening activities should be addressed through explicit statutory language in the legislative package (COVENANT-BILL-004).

The critical policy judgment is this: without a credible liability shield, the private sector will not manufacture GMD protection equipment at the scale required by COVENANT-EO-003, and investor-owned utilities will resist federal mandates to harden their infrastructure. The liability risk is not speculative. A GIC-blocking capacitor that fails during a storm — or is alleged to have failed, even if the transformer was doomed anyway — exposes the manufacturer to class-action litigation from every customer who lost power. The question is not whether litigation will occur after a GMD event. The question is whether we create a legal framework before the event that channels liability into a manageable, insurable system, or whether we allow the tort system to function as a lottery that deters the very manufacturing and hardening activities the Nation needs.

**WILLIAMS PLAIN-LANGUAGE:** If a manufacturer builds a capacitor to protect a transformer, and the transformer still fails during a solar storm, the manufacturer gets sued — even if the transformer was going to fail anyway. Nobody will build these things at the scale we need if every unit is a potential class-action lawsuit. We need to cap manufacturer liability, provide federal backup insurance, and make clear that following federal hardening standards is a defense against lawsuits. This isn't letting manufacturers off the hook for negligence — it's creating a predictable legal environment so they'll build what we need.

## I. THE LIABILITY PROBLEM — MAGNITUDE AND CONSEQUENCES

### A. The Catastrophic Risk Profile

A single G5 geomagnetic storm could destroy 300 or more large power transformers simultaneously. Each destroyed transformer represents not only the direct loss of a $2-10 million asset but the indirect economic consequences of multi-year power outages affecting millions of customers. Tort claims against equipment manufacturers, utilities, and potentially federal and state regulators could aggregate to hundreds of billions of dollars — far exceeding the commercial insurance capacity of the global reinsurance market.

The liability exposure falls into several categories:

1. **Manufacturing Defect Claims.** If a GIC-blocking capacitor, MXene shielding film, or CNT-doped dielectric composite fails to prevent transformer damage during a GMD event, the manufacturer faces products liability claims from every utility and every affected customer.

2. **Failure to Warn / Design Defect Claims.** If a manufacturer markets its product as providing "G5-level protection" but a transformer equipped with the product still fails, claimants will argue that the product was defectively designed or that warnings were inadequate, even if the failure resulted from a storm exceeding design specifications.

3. **Utility Negligence Claims.** Investor-owned utilities that implement GIC hardening in some locations but not others face claims that their hardening decisions were negligent or that they failed to adequately protect customers.

4. **Regulatory Negligence Claims.** Federal agencies — particularly FERC, NERC, and DOE — that mandate or approve GIC protection standards face claims that the standards were inadequate or negligently designed.

5. **Cross-Border Claims.** A transformer failure in Canada that cascades into the United States raises complex questions of international tort liability and choice of law.

### B. The Deterrent Effect

The anticipated response of the private sector to this liability exposure is well-documented in analogous industries: vaccine manufacturers facing liability for rare adverse reactions ceased production (leading to the National Vaccine Injury Compensation Program, 42 U.S.C. §§300aa-1 et seq.); nuclear plant operators could not obtain commercial insurance (leading to the Price-Anderson Act); and anti-terrorism technology developers faced untenable liability exposure (leading to the SAFETY Act).

The same dynamic applies to GMD protection equipment. Without a liability framework that caps exposure and provides federal indemnification, manufacturers will not invest in DPA-enabled production facilities, and utilities will resist mandatory hardening requirements. The market failure is clear, and it is a failure that only federal legislation can correct.

**WILLIAMS PLAIN-LANGUAGE:** History shows what happens when we don't address catastrophic liability: the product doesn't get made. Vaccine manufacturers stopped making vaccines until Congress created a compensation fund. Nuclear power couldn't get private insurance until Price-Anderson. Anti-terrorism tech wasn't deployed until the SAFETY Act capped liability. GMD protection equipment faces the same dynamic. No liability shield = no factories.

## II. MODEL 1 — THE PRICE-ANDERSON ACT FRAMEWORK

### A. Statutory Overview

The Price-Anderson Act, 42 U.S.C. §2210, as amended, provides the most comprehensive model for catastrophic risk indemnification in the United States. The Act establishes a three-tier liability system for nuclear power plant accidents:

- **Tier 1 — Primary Insurance:** Each nuclear plant operator must maintain the maximum amount of commercial liability insurance available (currently approximately $450 million per reactor site).

- **Tier 2 — Secondary Retrospective Premiums:** If damages exceed the primary insurance layer, all U.S. nuclear plant operators are assessed a retrospective premium — effectively a mutual insurance pool — currently capped at approximately $131 million per reactor, generating a secondary layer of approximately $13 billion.

- **Tier 3 — Federal Indemnification:** If damages exceed the combined primary and secondary layers, the federal government indemnifies the remaining liability. Congress retains the authority to determine the scope and mechanism of such indemnification.

Price-Anderson also establishes a federal cause of action for "public liability actions" arising from nuclear incidents, with exclusive federal jurisdiction and consolidation of all claims in a single federal district court. This "channeling" mechanism prevents a multiplicity of lawsuits in different state and federal courts applying different legal standards.

### B. Applicability to GMD Protection Equipment

A Price-Anderson-style framework for GMD protection equipment would include:

1. **Primary Insurance Layer.** Require manufacturers of GMD protection equipment to maintain commercial liability insurance at the maximum amount available in the market. Given the nascent state of the GMD protection industry, this amount may initially be modest (estimated $25-50 million per manufacturer) but would increase as the industry matures.

2. **Secondary Industry Pool.** All manufacturers of GMD protection equipment would be assessed retrospective premiums — capped at a percentage of annual revenue — to fund a secondary liability layer. The pool would be administered by a federally chartered entity modeled on American Nuclear Insurers.

3. **Federal Indemnification.** Congress would appropriate funds to indemnify liability exceeding the combined primary and secondary layers, up to a specified aggregate cap. Alternatively, Congress could authorize the Secretary of the Treasury to draw on appropriated funds or the Judgment Fund (31 U.S.C. §1304) to satisfy claims exceeding the industry-funded layers.

4. **Channeling Provision.** All civil actions arising from a GMD event that involve claims against GMD protection equipment manufacturers would be consolidated in a single federal district court — likely the U.S. District Court for the District of Columbia or a specially designated multidistrict litigation (MDL) panel — with federal law providing the exclusive cause of action and preempting state tort claims.

### C. Constitutional Considerations

Price-Anderson has survived multiple constitutional challenges, including claims that it violates due process (by limiting common-law tort remedies) and equal protection (by providing special treatment to a single industry). The Supreme Court upheld Price-Anderson's constitutionality in Duke Power Co. v. Carolina Environmental Study Group, Inc., 438 U.S. 59 (1978), holding that:

- The Act's limitation on liability is a "classic example of an economic regulation — a legislative effort to structure and accommodate the burdens and benefits of economic life";
- Congress rationally concluded that the nuclear industry could not function without a liability cap, and that the cap was a reasonable exercise of Congress's power to "provide for the general welfare";
- The Act's substitution of a federal cause of action for state tort claims does not violate due process because it provides a "reasonable, prompt, and effective mechanism" for compensating victims.

The same reasoning applies with equal force to GMD protection equipment. Congress may rationally conclude that the GMD protection industry cannot function without a liability framework, and that the framework is a reasonable exercise of congressional authority under the Commerce Clause and the Necessary and Proper Clause.

**WILLIAMS PLAIN-LANGUAGE:** The Supreme Court already said Congress can cap liability for catastrophic risks when the alternative is that nobody provides the protection. That's exactly the situation with GMD equipment. The Price-Anderson model survived every constitutional challenge. We can copy it.

## III. MODEL 2 — THE SAFETY ACT FRAMEWORK

### A. Statutory Overview

The Support Anti-Terrorism by Fostering Effective Technologies Act of 2002 (SAFETY Act), 6 U.S.C. §§441-444, provides a second liability protection model. The SAFETY Act authorizes the Secretary of Homeland Security to designate certain anti-terrorism technologies as "Qualified Anti-Terrorism Technologies" (QATTs) and to provide liability protections to the sellers of such technologies, including:

- Limits on the amount of liability (typically capped at the amount of insurance coverage required by DHS);
- Prohibition of punitive damages;
- Exclusive federal jurisdiction for claims against QATT sellers;
- A "government contractor defense" for QATT sellers who comply with DHS specifications.

### B. Statutory Expansion to GMD Protection

The SAFETY Act model could be extended to GMD protection through two mechanisms:

1. **Legislative Expansion.** Congress could amend the SAFETY Act to include "Qualified Critical Infrastructure Protection Technologies" (QCIPTs) alongside QATTs, covering technologies designed to protect critical infrastructure against natural electromagnetic threats as well as terrorist threats. The expansion would be relatively straightforward — adding "geomagnetic disturbance protection" to the definition of covered threats and expanding the Secretary of Homeland Security's designation authority accordingly.

2. **Analogous Stand-Alone Statute.** Congress could create a stand-alone "Geomagnetic Protection Liability Act" modeled on the SAFETY Act, with the following key features:

    (a) **Designation Process.** The Secretary of Homeland Security, in coordination with the Secretary of Energy, would designate specific GMD protection technologies as "Qualified Geomagnetic Protection Technologies" (QGPTs) upon a determination that the technology (i) is designed to protect critical infrastructure against GMD events, (ii) performs as intended, and (iii) is safe for deployment.

    (b) **Liability Cap.** Liability for QGPT sellers would be capped at the amount of commercial liability insurance required by the Secretary as a condition of designation — expected to be in the range of $25-100 million, depending on the size and risk profile of the manufacturer.

    (c) **Punitive Damages Prohibition.** Punitive damages would be prohibited in any action against a QGPT seller arising from a GMD event.

    (d) **Exclusive Federal Jurisdiction.** Federal district courts would have exclusive jurisdiction over all claims against QGPT sellers, preempting state court jurisdiction and state tort law.

    (e) **Government Contractor Defense.** QGPT sellers who comply with federal technical specifications, manufacturing standards, and quality control requirements — including those established by COVENANT-EO-003 and the associated CSMFAB technical specifications — would be entitled to the government contractor defense, discussed in Part IV below.

### C. Advantages of the SAFETY Act Model

The SAFETY Act model has several advantages over the Price-Anderson model: (a) it is easier to implement administratively, as DHS already has the institutional infrastructure for designating technologies and administering liability protections; (b) it applies on a per-technology or per-manufacturer basis rather than requiring the creation of an industry-wide mutual insurance pool; (c) it can be structured to provide liability protection immediately upon designation, without waiting for the insurance market to develop; and (d) it avoids the constitutional challenges that might be raised against a Price-Anderson-style industry-wide mandatory pooling arrangement.

The primary disadvantage is that the SAFETY Act model does not create a secondary industry-funded insurance pool, leaving the federal government as the sole source of indemnification above the primary insurance layer. This increases the federal fiscal exposure but also simplifies the administrative structure.

## IV. MODEL 3 — THE GOVERNMENT CONTRACTOR DEFENSE

### A. The Boyle Doctrine

In Boyle v. United Technologies Corp., 487 U.S. 500 (1988), the Supreme Court held that government contractors are immune from state tort liability for design defects in products supplied to the federal government, provided that: (1) the United States approved reasonably precise specifications; (2) the equipment conformed to those specifications; and (3) the supplier warned the United States about dangers known to the supplier but not to the United States.

The Boyle defense is grounded in the Federal Tort Claims Act's discretionary function exception (28 U.S.C. §2680(a)) and the Supremacy Clause: where the federal government has made a discretionary policy decision about the design of a product, state tort law cannot second-guess that decision by imposing liability on the contractor who built the product to the government's specifications.

### B. Extension to GMD Protection Equipment

The Boyle defense would apply to GMD protection equipment manufactured under the DPA priorities and allocations authorities activated by COVENANT-EO-003, provided that:

1. **Reasonably Precise Specifications.** The federal government — through the Department of Energy, NIST, or the Department of Homeland Security — issues "reasonably precise specifications" for GMD protection equipment. The CSMFAB/TRANSFORMER-001 through TRANSFORMER-040 and CSMQuantum/SHIELD-001 through SHIELD-025 technical specifications, once adopted as federal standards, would satisfy this requirement.

2. **Conformance.** The manufacturer demonstrates that its equipment conforms to the federal specifications. This requires a certification and testing regime — which could be administered by NIST or a designated conformity assessment body.

3. **Warning of Known Dangers.** The manufacturer warns the government of any dangers known to the manufacturer but not reflected in the federal specifications. This is a notice requirement, not an obstacle: it ensures that the government has full information when making its specification decisions.

### C. Statutory Codification

While the Boyle defense exists at common law, its application to GMD protection equipment is not automatic and would depend on case-by-case judicial determination of whether the federal specifications are "reasonably precise" and whether the manufacturer conformed to them. To avoid litigation over these threshold questions, Congress should codify the Boyle defense for GMD protection equipment by statute, establishing a clear statutory defense for manufacturers who:

    (i) manufacture GMD protection equipment in accordance with technical specifications adopted by the Secretary of Energy or the Director of NIST;

    (ii) obtain certification of conformance from a federally approved conformity assessment body; and

    (iii) disclose to the Secretary any material safety or performance risks not reflected in the applicable specifications.

Such a statute would transform the Boyle defense from a case-by-case judicial determination to an administratively administered certification system, providing the certainty that manufacturers require before making the capital investments in production facilities contemplated by COVENANT-EO-003.

**WILLIAMS PLAIN-LANGUAGE:** If you build something to the government's exact specifications and it still fails, the government absorbs the liability — not you. This is called the "government contractor defense" and the Supreme Court recognized it in 1988. We can extend it to GMD protection equipment. Build to our specs, pass our tests, tell us about known risks — and you're protected from lawsuits if the equipment fails during a storm it wasn't designed to handle.

## V. MODEL 4 — FEDERAL PREEMPTION OF STATE TORT CLAIMS

### A. The Preemption Doctrine

Under the Supremacy Clause of Article VI, federal law preempts conflicting state law. Congress may preempt state tort claims through: (1) express preemption (explicit statutory language); (2) field preemption (where federal regulation is so comprehensive that it occupies the entire field); or (3) conflict preemption (where compliance with both federal and state law is impossible, or where state law stands as an obstacle to the accomplishment of congressional objectives).

### B. Application to GMD Hardening

Federal preemption of state tort claims related to federally mandated GMD hardening activities is supported by conflict preemption analysis: state tort law that penalizes a utility for complying with federal GMD hardening mandates — or penalizes a manufacturer for producing equipment to federal specifications — stands as an obstacle to the accomplishment of congressional objectives to protect the electrical grid against GMD events.

However, courts are generally reluctant to find implied preemption of state tort law absent clear congressional intent. Express preemption — through statutory language explicitly preempting state tort claims for federally mandated GMD hardening activities — is therefore the preferred mechanism. The legislative package (COVENANT-BILL-004) should include express preemption language.

### C. Proposed Statutory Language

The Geomagnetic Infrastructure Protection Act (COVENANT-BILL-004) should include a preemption provision along the following lines:

> "No State or political subdivision thereof may establish or continue in effect any law, regulation, or cause of action that imposes liability, or requires indemnification, for any act or omission arising from the design, manufacture, installation, operation, or maintenance of geomagnetic disturbance protection equipment or systems that (1) are manufactured or installed in compliance with technical specifications adopted by the Secretary of Energy or the Director of the National Institute of Standards and Technology under this Act; and (2) have been certified by a federally recognized conformity assessment body as conforming to such specifications. This section shall not preclude claims for damages caused by willful misconduct, gross negligence, or knowing violation of federal law."

The exception for willful misconduct, gross negligence, and knowing violations preserves the core function of state tort law — deterring and punishing genuinely culpable conduct — while providing manufacturers and utilities with immunity from routine products liability and negligence claims arising from federally compliant equipment.

**WILLIAMS PLAIN-LANGUAGE:** Federal law can say "state courts can't punish companies for following our rules." If a utility installs a capacitor that meets federal specs, a state court can't find the utility negligent. The exception: if a company knowingly cut corners, lied about testing, or committed fraud — they're still on the hook. But if they followed the federal playbook and it still wasn't enough, the federal government absorbs the liability, not the individual company.

## VI. RECOMMENDED LEGISLATIVE ARCHITECTURE

Based on the foregoing analysis, we recommend a three-layer liability protection framework for GMD protection equipment and utility hardening:

**Layer 1 — Statutory Government Contractor Defense (immediate, via COVENANT-BILL-004).** Codify the Boyle defense for GMD protection equipment through explicit statutory language establishing immunity from state tort claims for equipment manufactured and installed in compliance with federal specifications. This provides immediate protection for the initial GMD protection equipment produced under COVENANT-EO-003.

**Layer 2 — SAFETY Act Expansion (within 2 years, via COVENANT-BILL-004).** Amend the SAFETY Act to include "Qualified Geomagnetic Protection Technologies," authorizing the Secretary of Homeland Security to designate specific technologies and provide administrative liability protections, including liability caps and punitive damages prohibition.

**Layer 3 — Geomagnetic Protection Indemnification Act (within 3 years, via new legislation).** Enact a stand-alone indemnification statute modeled on Price-Anderson that: (a) requires manufacturers to maintain primary commercial liability insurance; (b) creates an industry-funded secondary insurance pool; (c) provides federal indemnification for claims exceeding the industry-funded layers; (d) channels all claims to a single federal court; and (e) preempts state tort claims.

The Layer 1 protections should be included in the COVENANT-BILL-004 legislative package, which addresses mandatory critical infrastructure hardening. The Layer 2 protections should be included either in COVENANT-BILL-004 or in COVENANT-BILL-005 (Insurance Backstop). The Layer 3 protections will require separate legislation once the GMD protection equipment industry has matured sufficiently to support an industry-funded insurance pool.

## VII. CONCLUSION

The liability exposure facing GMD protection equipment manufacturers and hardening utilities is catastrophic in magnitude and is not insurable in the commercial insurance market. The predictable consequence of this exposure is market failure: manufacturers will not produce GMD protection equipment at scale, and utilities will resist mandatory hardening requirements, regardless of the Defense Production Act authorities activated by COVENANT-EO-003.

Congress has addressed analogous market failures — in nuclear power (Price-Anderson), vaccines (National Vaccine Injury Compensation Program), and anti-terrorism technology (SAFETY Act) — by creating federal liability frameworks that cap private-sector exposure and provide federal indemnification. The same approach is necessary for GMD protection equipment. The recommended three-layer framework — statutory government contractor defense, SAFETY Act expansion, and Price-Anderson-style indemnification — provides comprehensive, legally defensible liability protection that will enable the private sector to manufacture, deploy, and maintain GMD protection equipment at the scale required by national policy.

The cost of the federal indemnification layer is unknown but bounded: in a catastrophic GMD event that destroys 300+ transformers despite GIC protection (because the storm exceeds design specifications), the claims against GMD equipment manufacturers would be a small fraction of the total economic damage — likely $10-50 billion — and would be subject to the liability caps and channeling provisions of the statutory framework. The cost of NOT providing liability protection — measured in the grid protection equipment that will not be manufactured, the transformers that will not be hardened, and the economic damage that will result — is $1-2 trillion. The benefit-cost ratio of the liability shield is self-evident: even a $50 billion federal indemnification is a 20:1 bargain compared to $1 trillion in avoided damage.
