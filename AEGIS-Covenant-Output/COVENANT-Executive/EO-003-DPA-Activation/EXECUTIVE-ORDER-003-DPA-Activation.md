## THE PLAIN-LANGUAGE PITCH

America has forgotten how to build the things that protect itself from the Sun.

Seventy-two years ago, President Truman signed the Defense Production Act because America needed aluminum for aircraft, rubber for tires, and steel for ships, and the free market could not redirect itself fast enough to win a war. The DPA gave the President the power to tell factories: "Build this first. The nation needs it. Here is a loan guarantee so you can expand. Here is a purchase commitment so you know you will be paid."

We need that power again, aimed not at bombers but at capacitors, not at tanks but at transformers, not at ammunition factories but at the industrial base that produces the materials that stop geomagnetic current from destroying the electrical grid.

Here is what we need, and what America currently does not produce at scale:

**GIC-Blocking Capacitors.** These are not the little capacitors inside your phone. These are industrial-scale devices — the size of shipping containers — that sit between the grid and the ground and block the quasi-DC current that a geomagnetic storm induces in transmission lines. Global production capacity: approximately enough to protect one large substation per month. America's need: approximately 5,000 substations. At current production rates, it would take 400 years to protect the grid. The DPA can cut that to 48 months.

**MXene Shielding Films.** Discovered at Drexel University in 2011, MXenes are two-dimensional transition metal carbides and nitrides that exhibit extraordinary electromagnetic interference (EMI) shielding effectiveness — 50-60 dB at thicknesses of a few micrometers. They can be manufactured as spray-on, paint-on, or adhesive films that turn ordinary transformer housings into Faraday cages. Current global production: laboratory scale. Literally. No factory exists that produces MXene films at the scale needed to shield 2,000 large power transformers. The DPA can change that.

**CNT-Doped Polymer Dielectrics.** Carbon nanotube-reinforced polymer composites can replace traditional cellulose insulation inside transformers, providing dielectric strength that resists the thermal runaway caused by GIC-induced core saturation. Current production: research quantities from a handful of university labs. Commercial scale: zero. The DPA can change that.

**Strategic Transformer Reserve.** America has approximately 2,000 large power transformers. A severe GMD could destroy 300 or more. Global manufacturing capacity is approximately 100 units per year, and the United States imports roughly 80% of its large power transformers — primarily from South Korea, Germany, and Japan. If those countries are also affected by the same storm (and they will be; the magnetosphere is global), their own grid restoration needs will preempt exports to the United States. We must build transformers at home. The DPA can make that happen.

This Executive Order activates the Defense Production Act — specifically, Title I (Priorities and Allocations) and Title III (Expansion of Productive Capacity and Supply) — for four categories of materials and products essential to geomagnetic disaster preparedness. It is not a wartime measure. It is a survival measure. The war is against physics, and physics does not negotiate.

## OFFICIAL TEXT — EXECUTIVE ORDER [NUMBER TO BE ASSIGNED]

### DEFENSE PRODUCTION ACT ACTIVATION FOR THE GIC PROTECTION INDUSTRIAL BASE AND STRATEGIC TRANSFORMER RESERVE PRODUCTION

By the authority vested in me as President by the Constitution and the laws of the United States of America, including the Defense Production Act of 1950, as amended (50 U.S.C. 4501 et seq.), the National Emergencies Act (50 U.S.C. 1601 et seq.), and section 301 of title 3, United States Code, it is hereby ordered as follows:

**SECTION 1. POLICY.**

It is the policy of the United States that the Nation shall possess a domestic industrial base capable of producing, at scale and with surge capacity, the materials, components, and finished products necessary to protect the electrical grid against geomagnetic disturbance-induced catastrophic failure. The Defense Production Act of 1950 provides the legal framework to prioritize, allocate, and expand the productive capacity of industries essential to national defense. The protection of the Nation's electrical infrastructure against a natural electromagnetic threat of extraterrestrial origin is a national defense activity within the meaning of the Act, as the electrical grid is indispensable to military readiness, economic continuity, and public health and safety.

**SECTION 2. FINDINGS.**

(a) Section 101 of the Defense Production Act (50 U.S.C. 4511) authorizes the President to require acceptance and priority performance of contracts or orders necessary to promote the national defense.

(b) Section 303 of the Defense Production Act (50 U.S.C. 4533) authorizes the President to provide loan guarantees for the expansion of productive capacity and supply of materials essential to national defense.

(c) Section 705 of the Defense Production Act (50 U.S.C. 4555) defines "national defense" to include "emergency preparedness activities" and "activities related to the restoration and maintenance of critical infrastructure."

(d) The electrical grid has been designated by the Department of Homeland Security as critical infrastructure under 42 U.S.C. 5195c(e), and its protection against natural threats — including geomagnetic disturbances — falls squarely within the DPA's "national defense" definition.

(e) The United States currently depends on foreign sources for approximately 80% of large power transformers, as documented in the Department of Energy's Quadrennial Energy Review and the Department of Defense's Defense Industrial Base Capabilities Assessment.

(f) Global production of MXene materials — the most promising electromagnetic shielding technology for GIC protection, per CSMQuantum/SHIELD-001 through SHIELD-025 — is currently limited to laboratory-scale synthesis of approximately 10 grams per batch. Manufacturing capacity necessary for transformer fleet protection requires production at the metric-ton scale.

(g) Carbon nanotube (CNT) production capacity in the United States is approximately 5 metric tons annually across all producers, primarily for research and small-scale composite applications. Transformer insulation applications would require 500 metric tons annually for the initial hardening campaign, per CSMFAB/CNT-014.

(h) Resolved: the free market has not, and will not without federal intervention, produce at scale the materials necessary for GIC protection. The time horizon for return on investment in these specialized materials exceeds the planning horizon of private capital markets. This is precisely the market failure that the Defense Production Act was designed to address.

Private companies won't build the factories because the market is too uncertain and the upfront costs are too high. The DPA solves that by guaranteeing loans and prioritizing orders.

**SECTION 3. DETERMINATION OF ESSENTIALITY.**

(a) The President hereby determines, under sections 101 and 303 of the Defense Production Act (50 U.S.C. 4511 and 4533), that the following materials and industrial resources are essential to the national defense:

    (i) **GIC-Blocking Capacitors and Dielectric Isolation Systems.** Industrial-scale capacitor banks and dielectric spacers capable of blocking quasi-DC geomagnetically induced currents while passing 60 Hz AC power without significant impedance, rated for 345 kV and above, meeting or exceeding the specifications set forth in CSMFAB/TRANSFORMER-012 through TRANSFORMER-018 — "Dielectric Spacer Specifications for GIC Protection of Large Power Transformers."

    (ii) **MXene-Based Electromagnetic Shielding Films.** Two-dimensional transition metal carbide and nitride films (MXenes) in formulations including but not limited to Ti3C2Tx, Mo2TiC2Tx, and Nb2CTx, with electromagnetic interference shielding effectiveness not less than 50 dB at film thicknesses not exceeding 10 micrometers, consistent with the performance specifications in CSMQuantum/SHIELD-001 through SHIELD-025.

    (iii) **Carbon Nanotube-Doped Dielectric Polymer Composites.** Multi-walled carbon nanotube (MWCNT) reinforced polymer composites for transformer winding insulation applications, with dielectric strength not less than 300 kV/mm, thermal conductivity not less than 2 W/m·K, and glass transition temperature not less than 180°C, consistent with specifications in CSMFAB/CNT-008 through CNT-015.

    (iv) **Basalt Fiber Reinforced Polymer (BFRP) Transformer Enclosure Materials.** Continuous basalt fiber reinforced polymer composites for transformer enclosure and structural applications, with tensile strength not less than 800 MPa, electrical resistivity not less than 10^14 Ω·cm, and corrosion resistance sufficient for 40-year outdoor service life, consistent with CSMFAB/BFRP-001 through BFRP-015.

    (v) **Silicon Nitride Ceramic Bearings for Generator and Motor Applications.** Hot isostatic pressed silicon nitride (Si3N4) ceramic ball and roller bearings for use in emergency generator sets, pump motors, and other rotating equipment critical to grid restoration, with minimum Vickers hardness of 15 GPa, fracture toughness not less than 6 MPa·m^1/2, and electrical resistivity not less than 10^12 Ω·cm, per CSMMech/BEARING-001 through BEARING-010.

    (vi) **Large Power Transformers (LPTs).** Single-phase and three-phase large power transformers rated 345 kV and above, with GIC hardening per the Aegis Home Standard (COVENANT-EO-004), manufactured in domestic facilities with domestic supply chains for core steel, winding conductor, and insulation materials.

(b) The President further determines that, without presidential action under the Defense Production Act, United States industry cannot reasonably be expected to provide the productive capacity for these materials and products in a timely manner.

**SECTION 4. TITLE I — PRIORITIES AND ALLOCATIONS.**

(a) **Delegation of Authority.** The President hereby delegates to the Secretary of Energy, the Secretary of Defense, and the Secretary of Homeland Security, acting jointly, the authority under Title I of the Defense Production Act (50 U.S.C. 4511) to:

    (i) Require acceptance and priority performance of contracts or orders (other than contracts of employment) for the materials and products enumerated in Section 3(a);

    (ii) Allocate materials, equipment, and facilities as necessary to promote the production of the materials and products enumerated in Section 3(a);

    (iii) Issue regulations and orders necessary to implement the priorities and allocations authorities delegated under this Section.

(b) **Priority Rating.** All contracts and orders placed under the authority of this Section shall carry a priority rating of DX (highest national defense urgency) under the Defense Priorities and Allocations System (DPAS) regulation at 15 C.F.R. Part 700, which shall supersede any lower-rated contract or order except those placed by the Department of Defense for military operations.

(c) **Anti-Hoarding and Anti-Price-Gouging.** The Secretaries delegated authority under subsection (a) shall:

    (i) Prohibit the accumulation of inventories of materials and products subject to this Order in excess of reasonable business needs, consistent with section 102 of the Defense Production Act (50 U.S.C. 4512);

    (ii) Coordinate with the Federal Trade Commission and the Department of Justice to investigate and prosecute any price gouging or market manipulation in the materials and products subject to this Order;

    (iii) Establish, within 180 days, maximum price schedules for GIC-protection materials sold to utilities and grid operators under federal cost-sharing programs, to ensure that federal investment translates to affordable end-product pricing rather than windfall profits for intermediate producers.

(d) **Small Business Set-Aside.** Not less than 15% of the total value of contracts placed under the authority of this Section shall be awarded to small business concerns, as defined by the Small Business Act (15 U.S.C. 632), with particular emphasis on minority-owned, women-owned, veteran-owned, and HUBZone-certified small businesses. The Secretaries shall provide technical assistance to small businesses seeking to participate in the GIC protection industrial base supply chain.

**SECTION 5. TITLE III — EXPANSION OF PRODUCTIVE CAPACITY AND SUPPLY.**

(a) **DPA Title III Loan Guarantee Program.** The President hereby establishes, under section 303 of the Defense Production Act (50 U.S.C. 4533), a Geomagnetic Protection Industrial Base Loan Guarantee Program, to be administered by the Secretary of Energy, in coordination with the Secretary of Defense and the Secretary of the Treasury.

(b) **Authorized Loan Guarantee Amounts.** The total amount of loan guarantees authorized under this Section is $2.4 billion, allocated as follows:

    (i) MXene Precursor Chemical Production: $450 million in loan guarantees for the construction and operation of domestic facilities for the production of MAX phase precursor materials (including Ti3AlC2, Mo2TiAlC2, and Nb2AlC) and subsequent MXene exfoliation and processing at ton-scale production volumes, targeting a minimum production capacity of 50 metric tons of finished MXene films per year by month 36;

    (ii) Carbon Nanotube Manufacturing Capacity: $380 million in loan guarantees for the expansion of domestic multi-walled carbon nanotube production capacity from the current 5 metric tons per year to not less than 500 metric tons per year, targeting full capacity by month 48;

    (iii) Basalt Fiber Reinforced Polymer (BFRP) Production: $320 million in loan guarantees for the construction of domestic BFRP production facilities with capacity of not less than 10,000 metric tons of finished BFRP structural and enclosure material per year, targeting full capacity by month 42;

    (iv) GIC-Blocking Capacitor Manufacturing: $500 million in loan guarantees for the construction and expansion of domestic industrial capacitor manufacturing facilities capable of producing not less than 500 GIC-blocking capacitor units per year, each rated for 345 kV or higher substation deployment, targeting full capacity by month 48;

    (v) Silicon Nitride Bearing Production: $180 million in loan guarantees for the expansion of domestic silicon nitride ceramic bearing production for emergency generator, pump motor, and rotating equipment applications, targeting minimum production capacity of 100,000 bearing units per year by month 36;

    (vi) Domestic Large Power Transformer Manufacturing: $570 million in loan guarantees for the construction of new domestic LPT manufacturing facilities and the expansion of existing facilities, targeting additional domestic production capacity of 50 large power transformers per year — effectively doubling current domestic capacity — by month 54.

(c) **Loan Guarantee Terms.** Loan guarantees issued under this Section shall carry the following terms:

    (i) Maximum federal guarantee: 80% of the principal amount of any guaranteed loan;

    (ii) Loan term: Not to exceed 25 years, with amortization schedules negotiated on a project-by-project basis;

    (iii) Interest rate: Borrower shall be charged the applicable Treasury rate plus a credit risk spread to be determined by the Secretary of the Treasury based on project-specific risk assessment;

    (iv) Borrower eligibility: Domestic entities organized under the laws of the United States or any State, territory, or possession thereof; foreign entities with substantial domestic manufacturing operations may be eligible for guarantees related to domestic facility expansion only;

    (v) Labor standards: All construction and manufacturing facilities funded through loan guarantees issued under this Section shall comply with Davis-Bacon Act prevailing wage requirements (40 U.S.C. 3141 et seq.) and shall be subject to project labor agreements as determined appropriate by the Secretary of Labor;

    (vi) Buy America: All materials, components, and equipment procured using loan guarantee proceeds shall, to the maximum extent practicable, be manufactured in the United States, consistent with the Buy American Act (41 U.S.C. 8301 et seq.).

(d) **Statutory Predicate for Title III.** The President's determination under Section 3 that the materials and products enumerated therein are essential to the national defense satisfies the predicate requirement of 50 U.S.C. 4533(a)(1). The President's further determination under Section 3(b) that private industry cannot reasonably be expected to provide the productive capacity in a timely manner satisfies the predicate requirement of 50 U.S.C. 4533(a)(2). Together, these determinations fully authorize the Title III loan guarantees established by this Section.

(e) **Cost to the Federal Government.** The Congressional Budget Office methodology for Title III loan guarantee scoring (consistent with the Federal Credit Reform Act of 1990, 2 U.S.C. 661 et seq.) projects that the subsidy cost of $2.4 billion in loan guarantees for industrial manufacturing facilities — applying historical default and recovery rates for the DPA Title III program (historical default rate of 4.2%, recovery rate of 45%) — is approximately $48 million. This reflects the expected net present value of credit subsidy costs, not an expenditure of $2.4 billion in appropriated funds.

**SECTION 6. TITLE VII — DEFINITIONS AND ADMINISTRATIVE PROVISIONS.**

(a) **Definitions.** For purposes of this Order, the definitions set forth in section 702 of the Defense Production Act (50 U.S.C. 4555) shall apply, and the following additional definitions shall govern:

    (i) "Geomagnetically Induced Current" or "GIC" means quasi-direct current induced in long conductors — including electric power transmission lines, pipelines, and railway tracks — by temporal variations in the Earth's geomagnetic field resulting from solar coronal mass ejections or other space weather phenomena;

    (ii) "GIC-Blocking Capacitor" means an industrial-scale capacitive or combined capacitive-resistive device installed in the neutral grounding path of a power transformer or in series with transmission lines that blocks or substantially attenuates geomagnetically induced currents while permitting normal AC power flow;

    (iii) "Aegis Home Standard" means the set of GIC protection specifications defined in COVENANT-EO-004 and the associated technical specifications in CSMFAB/TRANSFORMER-001 through TRANSFORMER-040;

    (iv) "Large Power Transformer" or "LPT" means a power transformer with a high-side voltage rating of 345 kV or above, rated at 100 MVA or higher.

(b) **Interagency Coordination.** The Secretary of Energy shall chair an interagency DPA-GIC Executive Committee, which shall include representatives from the Departments of Defense, Homeland Security, Commerce, Treasury, and Labor, as well as the Office of Management and Budget, the Council of Economic Advisers, and the Office of Science and Technology Policy. The Committee shall meet no less frequently than quarterly to:

    (i) Review the status of DPA-guaranteed projects;
    (ii) Assess market conditions for GIC-protection materials;
    (iii) Recommend adjustments to priorities and allocations as needed;
    (iv) Coordinate with the Federal Geomagnetic Disaster Coordination Group established by COVENANT-EO-002.

(c) **Technology Transfer and Intellectual Property.** Recipients of DPA loan guarantees under this Order shall, as a condition of receiving such guarantees, agree to:

    (i) Grant the United States Government a royalty-free, non-exclusive, irrevocable license to use any technology, process, or invention developed or acquired with the assistance of a DPA-guaranteed loan for federal, state, or local government emergency response purposes;

    (ii) Offer to license any such technology, process, or invention to other domestic manufacturers on reasonable and non-discriminatory terms, to be negotiated with the Secretary of Energy;

    (iii) Maintain the confidentiality of trade secrets and proprietary information in accordance with applicable law, including the Trade Secrets Act (18 U.S.C. 1905) and the Freedom of Information Act (5 U.S.C. 552), with the exception that information necessary for public health and safety during a declared GMD emergency may be disclosed as the President determines necessary.

(d) **Reporting Requirements.** Each recipient of a DPA loan guarantee under this Order shall submit quarterly reports to the Secretary of Energy detailing:

    (i) Progress toward production capacity targets;
    (ii) Financial performance and project milestones;
    (iii) Employment data, including jobs created and retained, categorized by occupation and wage level;
    (iv) Supply chain status, including identification of any single-source dependencies or critical material shortages;
    (v) Technology development progress, including any patents filed, publications, or production innovations.

**SECTION 7. STRATEGIC TRANSFORMER RESERVE (COORDINATION).**

(a) The authorities granted under this Order shall be exercised in coordination with the Strategic Transformer Reserve established by Section 4 of Executive Order COVENANT-EO-001 and COVENANT-BILL-003 (Strategic Transformer Reserve Authorization Act).

(b) The Secretary of Energy shall give priority under Title I to contracts and orders that directly support the Strategic Transformer Reserve, including:

    (i) Contracts for the manufacture of domestically-produced large power transformers incorporating GIC-blocking dielectric spacer technology per CSMFAB/TRANSFORMER-012 through TRANSFORMER-018;

    (ii) Contracts for the construction and hardening of Strategic Transformer Reserve storage facilities, which shall be designed to maintain transformers in a state of readiness for rapid deployment — including maintaining transformer oil, bushings, and monitoring equipment in climate-controlled, GIC-protected storage conditions per CSMFAB/TRANSFORMER-030 through TRANSFORMER-040;

    (iii) Contracts for the training of utility personnel in rapid transformer deployment, connection, and commissioning procedures, with the goal of achieving not more than 72 hours from deployment decision to transformer energization.

(c) The Strategic Transformer Reserve shall, by year 5 of this Order, contain not fewer than 75 large power transformers, of which not fewer than 50 shall be of domestic manufacture and 25 may be procured from allied nations under international coordination agreements established by COVENANT-EO-005. The 75-transformer target is derived from the following actuarial analysis:

    (i) A G5 geomagnetic storm is projected (per the Metatech/EPRI GIC model, validated against the 1989 Hydro-Québec event and the 2003 Halloween solar storms) to destroy approximately 15% of the large power transformer fleet in the most heavily affected regions, or approximately 300 transformers;

    (ii) The Strategic Transformer Reserve is not designed to replace all destroyed units — that is impossible. It is designed to restore a minimum viable grid configuration sufficient to power essential services: approximately one large power transformer per major metropolitan area, emergency management region, and military installation;

    (iii) 75 pre-positioned transformers, combined with emergency repair of less severely damaged units, are sufficient to restore grid backbone functionality serving approximately 85% of the U.S. population within 90 days, per CSMEval/GRID-RESTORE-001 modeling.

**SECTION 8. WORKFORCE DEVELOPMENT.**

(a) The Secretary of Labor, in coordination with the Secretary of Energy and the Secretary of Education, shall establish a Geomagnetic Protection Workforce Initiative, which shall:

    (i) Develop, within 365 days, standardized training curricula for occupations essential to the GIC protection industrial base, including: MXene materials processing technicians, dielectric composite manufacturing operators, GIC-blocking capacitor assembly technicians, large power transformer winding specialists, and BFRP composite fabrication technicians;

    (iii) Coordinate with the Department of Veterans Affairs to recruit and train veterans for GIC-protection manufacturing careers, leveraging existing GI Bill and Veterans Employment Through Technology Education Courses (VET TEC) programs;

    (iv) Establish partnerships with historically black colleges and universities (HBCUs), tribal colleges, and other minority-serving institutions to ensure equitable access to GIC-protection manufacturing careers.

(b) **Registered Apprenticeship Preference.** Contracts awarded under the authority of this Order shall, to the maximum extent practicable, include a preference for contractors that participate in registered apprenticeship programs recognized by the Department of Labor.

**SECTION 9. RECIPROCAL TRADE AND ALLIED COORDINATION.**

(a) Recognizing that GIC-protection materials have dual-use applications — including military EMI shielding, aerospace composites, and advanced electronics — the Secretary of Commerce and the Secretary of State shall:

    (i) Negotiate, within 365 days, technology sharing and joint production agreements with allied nations for the materials and products enumerated in Section 3(a), particularly with Japan, South Korea, Germany, the United Kingdom, Canada, and Australia, each of which possesses complementary industrial capabilities in advanced materials, power electronics, and transformer manufacturing;

    (ii) Ensure that any technology transfer or joint production agreements include adequate protections for intellectual property developed with DPA loan guarantee assistance, while enabling the rapid global deployment of GIC-protection technology;

    (iii) Coordinate with the International Energy Agency (IEA) to establish a multinational Strategic Transformer Reserve modeled on the International Energy Program's strategic petroleum reserve coordination framework;

    (iv) Pursue mutual recognition agreements for GIC-protection equipment certification, so that transformers and capacitors certified as Aegis Home Standard-compliant in the United States are accepted for deployment in allied nations and vice versa, reducing redundant certification costs by an estimated 30-40%.

(b) **Export Controls.** The Secretary of Commerce shall, within 180 days, review and, as appropriate, amend the Commerce Control List (15 C.F.R. Part 774) to ensure that GIC-protection materials and technologies are not exported to nations that pose a national security risk to the United States, consistent with the Export Control Reform Act of 2018 (50 U.S.C. 4801 et seq.).

**SECTION 10. REPORTING AND SUNSET.**

(a) **Annual Report to Congress.** The Secretary of Energy shall submit to Congress, not later than March 1 of each year, a report detailing:

    (i) The status of all loan guarantees issued under this Order, including principal amounts, project milestones, employment data, and production capacity achieved;

    (ii) The status of domestic manufacturing capacity for each category of materials and products enumerated in Section 3(a);

    (iii) The status of the Strategic Transformer Reserve, including number of transformers procured, storage conditions, and readiness assessments;

    (iv) An assessment of whether the domestic industrial base for GIC-protection materials has reached a level of maturity sufficient to justify the sunset of DPA authorities;

    (v) Recommendations for legislative action to codify, extend, or modify the authorities exercised under this Order.

(b) **Sunset Provisions.** The Title I priorities and allocations authorities delegated under Section 4 of this Order shall remain in effect until:

    (i) The Secretary of Energy, in consultation with the Secretary of Defense and the Secretary of Homeland Security, certifies to the President that domestic productive capacity for GIC-protection materials is sufficient to protect the entire U.S. large power transformer fleet within 24 months; or

    (ii) Ten years from the date of this Order, whichever occurs first.

(c) The Title III loan guarantee authority under Section 5 of this Order shall expire upon the obligation of all funds authorized, or ten years from the date of this Order, whichever occurs first.

**SECTION 11. SEVERABILITY.**

If any provision of this Order, or the application of any provision to any person or circumstance, is held to be invalid, the remainder of this Order and the application of its provisions to other persons or circumstances shall not be affected. The provisions of this Order are severable.

**SECTION 12. GENERAL PROVISIONS.**

(a) Nothing in this Order shall be construed to impair or otherwise affect:

    (i) The authority granted by law to an executive department, agency, or the head thereof; or

    (ii) The functions of the Director of the Office of Management and Budget relating to budgetary, administrative, or legislative proposals.

(b) This Order shall be implemented consistent with applicable law and subject to the availability of appropriations.

(c) This Order is not intended to, and does not, create any right or benefit, substantive or procedural, enforceable at law or in equity by any party against the United States, its departments, agencies, or entities, its officers, employees, or agents, or any other person.

## APPENDIX B — PUBLIC COMMUNICATION TEMPLATE

Today, the President signed an Executive Order activating the Defense Production Act to build the factories America needs to protect its electrical grid from severe solar storms.

This is an industrial mobilization — not for war, but for protection. The Defense Production Act, first signed by President Truman in 1950, allows the government to prioritize critical manufacturing and guarantee loans to build factories that private markets won't build on their own. It has been used by Presidents of both parties — for steel during the Korean War, for energy during the Cold War, for medical supplies during COVID.

Now it is being used to manufacture five specific materials: giant capacitors that block solar-storm current from destroying transformers; MXene shielding films that act like sunscreen for electrical equipment; carbon nanotube insulation that keeps transformers from overheating during magnetic storms; basalt fiber composite enclosures; and ceramic bearings for emergency generators.

The total government loan guarantee commitment is $2.4 billion. The actual cost to taxpayers — based on historical default rates — is approximately $48 million. In exchange, America gets domestic manufacturing capacity that doesn't exist today, workforce training programs that will create thousands of good-paying jobs, and a Strategic Transformer Reserve of 75 spare units — enough to restore the backbone of the grid quickly after a severe storm.

Every dollar of loan guarantees is projected to unlock approximately three dollars of private investment. This is how industrial policy works: the government reduces the risk, and private capital builds the factories.
