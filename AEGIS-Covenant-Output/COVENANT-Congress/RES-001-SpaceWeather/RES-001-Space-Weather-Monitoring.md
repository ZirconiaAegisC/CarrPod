## CONCURRENT RESOLUTION

Expressing the sense of Congress that the United States should maintain and enhance its space weather monitoring, research, and prediction capabilities.

Whereas solar coronal mass ejections pose a credible threat to the electrical grid, satellite operations, aviation communications, and national security;

Whereas the Deep Space Climate Observatory (DSCOVR) spacecraft, currently the Nation's primary solar wind monitor at the Sun-Earth L1 Lagrange point, is operating beyond its design life;

Whereas the loss of L1 monitoring capability would eliminate the Nation's ability to provide timely warning of geomagnetic disturbances;

Whereas NOAA's Space Weather Prediction Center, the USGS Geomagnetism Program, and NASA's heliophysics research programs require sustained funding to maintain and improve monitoring and prediction capabilities;

Whereas international coordination of space weather monitoring through the World Meteorological Organization and the International Space Environment Service enhances global preparedness:

Now, therefore, be it Resolved by the House of Representatives (the Senate concurring), That it is the sense of Congress that—

(1) The United States should ensure continuous, redundant solar wind monitoring at the Sun-Earth L1 Lagrange point, including through the expeditious development and launch of a replacement for the DSCOVR spacecraft or equivalent capability;

(2) NOAA's Space Weather Prediction Center should be funded at levels sufficient to maintain 24/7 forecasting operations and to enhance its GIC modeling and prediction capabilities;

(3) NASA's heliophysics research programs should be sustained to advance the scientific understanding of coronal mass ejections and their interactions with Earth's magnetosphere;

(4) The United States should seek international agreements for the sharing of space weather monitoring data and the coordination of warning dissemination.
