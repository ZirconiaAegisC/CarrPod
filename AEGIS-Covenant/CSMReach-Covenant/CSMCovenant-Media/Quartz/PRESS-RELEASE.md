HEADLINE: The Biggest Risk to the Global Economy Isn't in Your Portfolio—It's in the Sun's Magnetic Field

LONDON — Here is a statistic that should keep every finance minister and pension fund manager awake at night: the probability of a Carrington-scale solar event striking Earth in any given year is 12%. The estimated economic damage is £2.1 trillion. And the total amount of money currently allocated to preventing it, globally, is approximately zero.

Carrington Storm Motors is trying to change that equation. The Coventry-based startup builds electric vehicles that double as mobile grid-defence nodes—cars designed to absorb the geomagnetic surges that would otherwise destroy transformers, black out hospitals, and halt supply chains across entire continents.

"The absurdity of our current position is that we have excellent space weather forecasting, we have engineering solutions that demonstrably work, and we have a known cost of implementation," said Kairos Steele, CSM's founder. "The only thing we don't have is the will to act on information that is uncomfortable rather than convenient."

CSM's analysis suggests that hardening global infrastructure would cost roughly 2% of the estimated damage from a single Carrington event—making inaction a staggeringly expensive choice.
