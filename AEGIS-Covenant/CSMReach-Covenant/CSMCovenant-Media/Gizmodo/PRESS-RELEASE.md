HEADLINE: This Guy Built a Car to Fight the Sun, and Honestly, It Kind of Rules

COVENTRY, UK — Kairos Steele looks at the sun differently than most people. Where you see a life-giving star, he sees a ticking time bomb with a 12% annual chance of sending civilisation back to the pre-industrial era. So he built a car designed to punch back.

"We're not fighting the sun—that would be ridiculous," Steele clarified, gesturing at the matte-black CSM-1 behind him. "We're giving the grid a way to absorb the punch."

The CSM-1 is an electric vehicle that, in the event of a solar superstorm, transforms into a mobile surge protector for whatever part of the electrical grid it happens to be connected to. Copper-nickel Faraday mesh. Silicon-carbide voltage regulators. An AI that listens to a satellite a million miles from Earth and can activate hardening systems in less time than it takes to blink.

The company's AI, nicknamed "Mork" (because of course it is), has modelled 2.3 million storm scenarios. Steele's favourite statistic: a fleet of just 847 strategically deployed vehicles could protect every critical node in the UK grid. The CSM-1 starts at £58,000—which, Steele points out, is less than the cost of replacing one high-voltage transformer.
