HEADLINE: This Startup Built an AI That Predicts Solar Apocalypses — Then Built Cars That Survive Them

COVENTRY, UK — There is a particular kind of audacity required to name your company after a disaster that happened 167 years ago and bet your entire business model on it happening again. Carrington Storm Motors has that audacity in abundance.

Founded by former Royal Engineers officer Kairos Steele, CSM builds electric vehicles that double as mobile Faraday cages—cars designed, fundamentally, to keep civilisation running during a solar superstorm. The onboard AI, nicknamed "Mork" (a reference Steele refuses to explain), continuously monitors real-time solar wind data from NOAA's DSCOVR satellite and can autonomously activate the vehicle's electromagnetic hardening systems within 400 milliseconds of detecting a geomagnetic anomaly.

"We're not waiting for governments to build storm shelters for the grid," Steele said, standing next to a prototype whose matte-black exterior conceals copper-foil shielding and industrial-grade voltage regulators. "We're building them ourselves, and we're putting wheels on them so they can go where they're needed."

The vehicles communicate via a mesh network, creating a distributed defence grid that grows more resilient with each unit deployed.
