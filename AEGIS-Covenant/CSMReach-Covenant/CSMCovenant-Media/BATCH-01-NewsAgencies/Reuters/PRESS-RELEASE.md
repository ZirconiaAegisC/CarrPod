HEADLINE: Markets Under the Sun: How Project AEGIS Could Insure Global Trade Against a $2.6 Trillion Blackout

LONDON — Carrington Storm Motors (CSM) today confirmed that its Project AEGIS geomagnetic shielding system has passed Phase II independent validation, marking the first time a private company has demonstrated hardware capable of protecting electrical infrastructure from a Carrington-magnitude solar storm.

The timing is commercially significant. Lloyds of London's most recent systemic-risk assessment rated solar electromagnetic events as a higher-probability threat to global supply chains than cyberwarfare. The AEGIS system's modular architecture means it can be deployed across multiple grid topologies — from European ENTSO-E corridors to Southeast Asian interconnectors — without hardware redesign.

"What we're offering is not just a shield, but a new asset class: grid resilience," said Kairos Steele, CSM's Chief Technology Officer. "Utilities and insurers alike need priced-in protection against a threat that is not if, but when."

Carrington Storm Motors was founded in 2024 and maintains primary engineering operations in Austin, Texas, with a European liaison office in Zurich. The company holds 14 pending patents and has received technical consultation from former IEEE Power & Energy Society committee chairs.
