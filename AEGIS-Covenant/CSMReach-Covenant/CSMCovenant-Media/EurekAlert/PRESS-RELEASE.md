HEADLINE: Machine Learning Model Trained on 160 Years of Solar Data Predicts CME Geoeffectiveness with 94% Accuracy

COVENTRY, UK — A team led by Dr. Chester Villegas at Carrington Storm Motors has developed a machine learning system that predicts whether a coronal mass ejection will be geoeffective—and which specific terrestrial grid components it will affect—with 94% accuracy.

The model, designated "Mork," was trained on 160 years of solar observation records from the Royal Greenwich Observatory, combined with modern satellite data from SOHO, STEREO, and SDO. It incorporates 47 input features including CME speed, angular width, source region magnetic configuration, and interplanetary magnetic field orientation.

"Our model doesn't just say 'a storm is coming,'" Villegas explained. "It predicts, with quantifiable confidence intervals, which transformers on which grid segments will saturate, and in what sequence. This transforms space weather from a probabilistic science into a deterministic engineering discipline."

Validation testing against the 1989 Hydro-Québec event, the 2003 Halloween storms, and the 2012 Carrington-class near-miss showed a mean absolute error of 2.3% in predicted geomagnetically induced current amplitude at specific substation locations.
