HEADLINE: Ars Technica Exclusive: We Took Apart a Carrington Storm Motors EV. Here's What Makes It Survive a Solar Apocalypse.

COVENTRY, UK — Carrington Storm Motors invited Ars Technica to tear down one of their production EVs and document what makes it different from every other electric vehicle on the market. The answer involves a surprising amount of military-grade copper mesh, an FPGA we haven't seen outside of defence applications, and an approach to power electronics that prioritises survival over efficiency.

The key differentiator is the "resilience bus"—a secondary electrical architecture that runs parallel to the main drive system. During normal operation, it's invisible. During a geomagnetic event, it activates within 400 milliseconds and reroutes surge current through a silicon-carbide array into a thermal dissipation layer woven into the vehicle's chassis.

"The efficiency penalty is about 2%," said Kairos Steele, CSM's founder. "In exchange, the vehicle becomes a node in a distributed grid-defence network. We think that's an acceptable trade."

The vehicles communicate via a LoRaWAN mesh that can span 15 kilometres between nodes, sharing real-time grid impedance data. In a geomagnetic event, the network coordinates surge absorption across all deployed units simultaneously.
