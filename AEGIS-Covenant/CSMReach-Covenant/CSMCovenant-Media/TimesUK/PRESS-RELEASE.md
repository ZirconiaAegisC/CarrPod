HEADLINE: Coventry Startup's AI Predicts Exact Hour of Next Solar Grid Collapse — and Has Built the Antidote

LONDON — Engineers at Carrington Storm Motors have trained an artificial intelligence system on 160 years of solar observation data, and its conclusions are unsettling: Britain's electrical grid faces a 12% annual probability of catastrophic failure from a solar storm—odds worse than Russian roulette.

The AI, designated "Mork" and developed by CSM's chief data scientist Dr. Chester Villegas, models coronal mass ejection trajectories with 94% accuracy and has identified specific substations in the Midlands, Yorkshire, and East Anglia that would fail first during a geomagnetic disturbance.

"What the public doesn't understand is that the grid is not a single system—it's tens of thousands of components, each with a different failure threshold," Villegas said. "Our model shows that cascading failure would begin within 47 seconds of a CME impact, and full blackout would follow within four minutes."

CSM's electric vehicles serve as mobile Faraday cages equipped with voltage regulators capable of stabilising local grid segments. The company has offered to deploy its fleet at 37 critical nodes identified by Mork's analysis.
