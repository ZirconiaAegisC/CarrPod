HEADLINE: Multi-Institutional Study Confirms: Earth's Magnetic Shield Has Weakened 9% Since 1859, Amplifying Modern Solar Storm Risk

LONDON — A collaborative study between Carrington Storm Motors, the British Geological Survey, and the University of Colorado Boulder has confirmed that the Earth's magnetosphere has weakened by approximately 9% since the 1859 Carrington Event, meaning an identical solar storm today would produce geomagnetically induced currents roughly 15-20% stronger than those that set Victorian telegraph systems ablaze.

The research, conducted over 18 months using data from ESA's Swarm satellite constellation and ground-based magnetometer arrays, quantifies for the first time the compound risk of declining geomagnetic shielding and increasing technological vulnerability. "We are simultaneously weakening our natural defences and expanding our technological attack surface," said Dr. Chester Villegas, the study's lead author. "It is a convergence of risk without historical precedent."

CSM's Kairos Steele noted the practical implications: "The 1989 Hydro-Québec event—which blacked out six million people—would be roughly 18% more severe if it happened today. Our infrastructure hardening standards haven't changed in three decades."
