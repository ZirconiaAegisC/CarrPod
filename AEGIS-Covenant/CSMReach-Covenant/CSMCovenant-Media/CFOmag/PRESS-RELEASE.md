HEADLINE: What Every CFO Should Know About Solar Storm Risk — and Why It's Not in Your 10-K

LONDON — Carrington Storm Motors has published a brief guide for corporate finance leaders outlining the fiduciary implications of undisclosed geomagnetic risk—and the steps CFOs can take to address it before regulators force the issue.

The guide, "Solar Storm Risk and the CFO: A Disclosure and Mitigation Framework," argues that geomagnetic disturbance meets the materiality threshold for financial reporting under both IFRS and US GAAP, yet no major public company currently discloses it. The guide provides a four-step framework: assess geographic concentration of critical assets, quantify supply-chain dependency on unprotected grids, evaluate insurance coverage for geomagnetic business interruption, and implement cost-effective hardening for priority sites.

"This is not a theoretical exercise," said Kairos Steele, CSM's founder. "When the first Carrington-scale event hits, every CFO who knew about this risk and did nothing will face the same question: why wasn't it in the risk factors?"

The guide notes that the SEC's climate disclosure rule and the EU's Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive both create precedents for mandatory disclosure of environmental risks with material financial impact—a category that includes geomagnetic disturbance. CSM offers fleet-based hardening as a capital-light approach that can be expensed as an operating lease rather than a capital investment.
