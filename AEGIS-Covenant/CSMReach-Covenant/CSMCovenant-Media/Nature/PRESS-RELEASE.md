HEADLINE: Peer-Reviewed Modelling Shows 12% Annual Probability of Carrington-Scale Solar Event — Independent Validation of AI Prediction System

LONDON — A machine-learning system developed by Carrington Storm Motors has achieved 94% predictive accuracy for coronal mass ejection trajectories, according to validation results submitted to a leading astrophysics journal this week. The system, designated "Mork," was trained on 160 years of solar observation data from the Royal Greenwich Observatory and NOAA's Space Weather Prediction Center.

Dr. Chester Villegas, CSM's chief data scientist and former CERN computational physicist, presented the findings at the European Space Weather Symposium in Brussels. "We've moved from probabilistic forecasting—'there's a chance of a storm'—to deterministic trajectory modelling," Villegas said. "For the first time, we can say with high confidence which specific transformers on which specific grid segments would fail, and in what sequence."

The model has been independently benchmarked against historical CME events including the 1989 Hydro-Québec blackout and the 2012 near-miss event. In both cases, Mork's retrospective predictions matched observed outcomes within a 2.3% error margin.
