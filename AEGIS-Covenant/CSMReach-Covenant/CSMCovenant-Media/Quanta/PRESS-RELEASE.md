HEADLINE: The Mathematical Challenge of Predicting Chaos: How Carrington Storm Motors Solved the CME Trajectory Problem

COVENTRY, UK — Predicting the exact terrestrial impact of a coronal mass ejection is a nonlinear dynamics problem of formidable complexity: a billion-ton cloud of magnetised plasma, travelling at millions of kilometres per hour through the turbulent interplanetary medium, interacting with a planetary magnetosphere whose shape changes in real time. For decades, space weather forecasters have relied on probabilistic models with wide error margins.

Carrington Storm Motors' research division claims to have narrowed those margins dramatically. Their machine learning system, "Mork," approaches the problem through an ensemble of physics-informed neural networks that incorporate 47 input features—from CME source-region magnetic topology to solar wind density gradients at L1—and output not a probability cone but a deterministic impact map with 94% accuracy.

"The key insight was treating the interplanetary magnetic field not as a static boundary condition but as a dynamic field that co-evolves with the CME," said Dr. Chester Villegas, the system's architect. "We embedded the governing MHD equations directly into the neural network's loss function, constraining the model to physically plausible solutions."

The result is a system that predicts, for any given CME, which transformers on which grid segments will saturate—and in what sequence.
