### CSM Circular Economy Infrastructure Program: Advanced Polymer Recycling and Bio-Based Insulation Materials Deployed at Utility Scale

**DETROIT** — The Critical Systems Mandate today announced the CSMFAB-103 Circular Economy Infrastructure Materials Program, a $1.2 billion initiative deploying advanced polymer recycling and bio-based electrical insulation materials across utility infrastructure applications, transforming the plastics and composites supply chain for the electrical T&D sector.

The program, coordinated with the American Chemistry Council's Plastics Division and the Composites Manufacturing Association, deploys three circular-economy material streams: high-density polyethylene (HDPE) conduit manufactured from 100% post-consumer recycled content achieving equivalent mechanical and dielectric performance to virgin material; cross-linked polyethylene (XLPE) cable insulation incorporating 40% bio-based feedstock derived from non-food agricultural waste; and glass-fiber-reinforced polymer (GFRP) utility poles manufactured with recycled glass fiber and bio-based epoxy resin systems that reduce lifecycle carbon emissions by 67% compared to conventional steel and treated-wood poles.

"The electrical utility sector is one of the world's largest consumers of polymers, composites, and insulation materials — yet it has been almost entirely absent from the circular economy conversation," explained Dr. Maria Gonzalez-Park, CSM Director for Sustainable Materials. "CSMFAB-103 demonstrates that recycled and bio-based materials can meet the demanding dielectric, mechanical, and environmental aging requirements of utility infrastructure while reducing both lifecycle costs and carbon intensity."

The program deploys 14,700 miles of recycled HDPE conduit, 8,300 miles of bio-based XLPE cable, and 47,000 GFRP utility poles across CSM-funded grid projects. Independent testing by UL and NIST verified that all materials meet or exceed the performance of conventional virgin-material equivalents under accelerated aging protocols.

CSM's Accountant Heuristic confirmed a 3.4:1 cost-benefit ratio. Material specifications and performance data are published open-access through the CSM Sustainable Materials Repository.
