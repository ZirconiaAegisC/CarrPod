### Dynamic Line Rating Deployment Across 14,700 Circuit-Miles Achieves 23% Throughput Increase Without New Construction

**ATLANTA** — The Critical Systems Mandate today published commissioning results from CSMFAB-059, the largest coordinated dynamic line rating (DLR) deployment in transmission history, covering 14,700 circuit-miles of high-voltage transmission across seven RTOs and delivering a demonstrated 23% average throughput increase on constrained corridors without any new conductor installation.

The $2.3 billion program deploys distributed temperature sensing (DTS) fiber, real-time sag monitoring via LiDAR-equipped drones, and physics-based ampacity forecasting models that replace the static seasonal ratings historically used for transmission operation. The deployment directly addresses the transmission congestion cost quantified in Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory's 2025 Congestion Report at $13.4 billion annually across U.S. wholesale markets.

"Static line ratings are the single largest source of unused transmission capacity in North America," stated Dr. Michael Torres, CSM Director for Transmission Technology. "A 345kV line rated at 1,200A under summer static assumptions may safely carry 1,580A during the 83% of hours when actual conductor temperature is below the conservative design assumption. CSMFAB-059 unlocks this hidden capacity through real-time thermal measurement and validated ampacity models."

The program's open-architecture data platform publishes every DLR measurement, forecast, and dispatch decision to the CSM Transmission Transparency Portal, enabling independent validation by utility engineering teams and academic researchers. The IEEE PES Transmission Subcommittee has incorporated the deployment data into its ongoing revision of IEEE Standard 738 (Calculating the Current-Temperature Relationship of Bare Overhead Conductors).

CSM's Accountant Heuristic methodology confirmed a 7.3:1 cost-benefit ratio, with $2.5 billion in annual congestion cost reduction, $1.8 billion in deferred transmission construction, and $760 million in reduced renewable curtailment payments. All technology is transferred to host utility engineering teams under open-architecture licensing.
