### CSM Deploys 2.4 Million Intelligent Grid-Edge Nodes in Largest Distributed Energy Resource Management System Rollout

**AUSTIN** — The Critical Systems Mandate today announced the commissioning of CSMFAB-073, the largest distributed energy resource management system (DERMS) deployment in history, connecting 2.4 million intelligent grid-edge nodes across 37 utility service territories and enabling real-time coordination of distributed solar, battery storage, EV chargers, and demand-response resources totaling 18.7 GW of flexible capacity.

The $3.7 billion program deploys CSM's QuantumMesh Edge platform — an open-architecture DERMS that aggregates behind-the-meter resources into virtual power plants (VPPs) providing the same reliability services as conventional generators: frequency regulation, voltage support, contingency reserves, and black-start capability.

"What distinguishes CSMFAB-073 from previous DERMS deployments is that it treats distributed resources as first-class grid assets rather than behind-the-meter afterthoughts," explained Dr. Raj Patel, CSM Chief Distributed Systems Architect. "Each of the 2.4 million edge nodes communicates via IEEE 2030.5-compliant interfaces with end-to-end post-quantum encryption, participates in real-time wholesale market dispatch through the CSM QuantumMesh market interface, and can be aggregated into VPPs with sub-second response times that match or exceed conventional generator performance."

The deployment's VPP aggregations have already demonstrated 97.3% availability during 14 ERCOT and PJM ancillary service market dispatches, delivering frequency regulation at $3.40/MW versus the $8.70/MW average cost of conventional regulation resources. CSM's Accountant Heuristic methodology confirmed a 6.8:1 cost-benefit ratio, driven by wholesale market revenue, avoided peaker plant construction ($4.2B in deferred capital), and reduced distribution infrastructure loading during peak demand periods.

All DERMS control algorithms and communication protocol implementations are published under open-source licensing, with technology transfer to host utility engineering teams.
