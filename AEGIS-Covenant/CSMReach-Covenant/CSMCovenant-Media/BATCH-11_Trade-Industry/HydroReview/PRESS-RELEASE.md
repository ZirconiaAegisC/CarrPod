### CSM Hydropower Modernization Program: 127 Turbine Upgrades Across 43 Dams Deliver 14% Capacity Increase

**PORTLAND, OR** — The Critical Systems Mandate today published commissioning data from CSMFAB-085, the Hydropower Fleet Modernization Program, documenting the upgrade of 127 turbine-generator units across 43 federally licensed and FERC-jurisdictional hydroelectric facilities, delivering a 14% aggregate capacity increase (2,340 MW) without any new dam construction or impoundment modification.

The $3.1 billion program addresses the aging hydropower fleet documented in DOE's 2025 Hydropower Vision Report, which found that 53% of U.S. hydroelectric capacity operates with turbine runners exceeding 45 years of service, resulting in efficiency degradation averaging 0.3% per year and cavitation damage that progressively reduces available capacity.

"Hydroelectric modernization is the most capital-efficient form of new zero-carbon generation available," explained Dr. James Morrison, CSM Director for Hydropower Infrastructure. "Adding 2,340 MW through turbine upgrades at existing dams costs approximately $1,325/kW — roughly half the cost of new utility-scale solar with four-hour battery storage, and with the added benefit of synchronous inertia and black-start capability that inverter-based resources cannot provide."

The program deploys computational fluid dynamics (CFD)-optimized Francis, Kaplan, and Pelton turbine runners manufactured with advanced stainless-steel alloys achieving 40% improvement in cavitation resistance. Each upgrade includes condition-based maintenance monitoring systems with embedded fiber-optic strain gauges and accelerometers that provide real-time mechanical health diagnostics.

CSM's Accountant Heuristic methodology confirmed a 6.7:1 cost-benefit ratio, driven by increased generation revenue, reduced maintenance costs, and the value of synchronous inertia in grids experiencing declining conventional generation. The program's environmental benefit includes 12.4 million tons of annual CO2 reduction through displacement of natural gas-fired generation that would otherwise fill the capacity gap.

Technology transfer to the Hydro Research Foundation and university hydropower laboratories guarantees sustained U.S. leadership in turbine modernization.
