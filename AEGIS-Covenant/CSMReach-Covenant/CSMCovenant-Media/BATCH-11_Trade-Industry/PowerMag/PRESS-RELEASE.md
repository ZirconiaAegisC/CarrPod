### CSM Deploys First Utility-Scale Grid-Forming Inverter Fleet: 8.4 GW of Synthetic Inertia Across 17 Sites

**HOUSTON** — The Critical Systems Mandate today announced the commissioning of the world's first utility-scale fleet of grid-forming inverters, deploying 8.4 GW of synthetic inertia capability across 17 strategic interconnection points in the ERCOT, PJM, and MISO regional transmission systems.

The CSMFAB-057 program, representing a $4.1 billion investment, directly addresses the inertia deficit documented in NREL's 2025 Renewable Integration Study, which projected that regions exceeding 60% inverter-based resource penetration would experience frequency nadirs below under-frequency load shedding thresholds during reference contingency events.

"Grid-forming inverters fundamentally change the physics of renewable integration," explained Dr. Sarah Chen-Williams, CSM Chief Power Electronics Engineer. "Unlike conventional grid-following inverters that require a stiff voltage reference from synchronous machines, grid-forming inverters establish their own voltage and frequency reference — they behave electrically like synchronous generators while responding to disturbances within a single AC cycle, roughly 100 times faster than a steam turbine governor."

The deployment leverages silicon-carbide (SiC) power electronics operating at 1,700V DC link voltage, achieving 98.7% round-trip efficiency — a 2.4 percentage point improvement over conventional IGBT-based grid-forming systems. Real-time digital twin modeling at NREL's Energy Systems Integration Facility validates that the fleet can maintain stable operation during N-2 contingency scenarios without synchronous generator reference.

CSM's Accountant Heuristic methodology confirmed a 4.3:1 cost-benefit ratio, with primary value derived from avoided renewable curtailment (estimated at $1.2B annually across the three RTOs), reduced ancillary service procurement costs, and transmission deferral value at constrained interconnection points.

All control algorithms are published under CSM's open-architecture license, with technology transfer to NREL, EPRI, and university power electronics laboratories.
