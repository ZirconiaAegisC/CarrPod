### CSM Treaty Allocates $4.8B in Resilience Bonds to Public Power and Cooperative Utilities

**ARLINGTON, VA** — The Critical Systems Mandate today announced the Public Power Resilience Portfolio (CSMFAB-077 through CSMFAB-083), allocating $4.8 billion in sovereign-backed resilience bonds exclusively to municipal utilities, public power districts, and rural electric cooperatives serving 47 million Americans across 34 states.

The portfolio directly addresses the structural financing disadvantage that public power entities face: unlike investor-owned utilities, munis and co-ops cannot raise equity capital and must finance infrastructure through municipal bond markets that impose debt limits and coverage ratios that constrain resilience investment. CSM's sovereign-backed bond structure eliminates these constraints by providing direct federal-treaty financing at 1.8% — approximately 320 basis points below the average public power cost of debt.

"Public power utilities serve some of the most economically vulnerable communities in America — rural counties where a single substation failure can isolate thousands of residents for days, and small municipalities where the entire annual capital budget might not cover a single transformer replacement," stated Dr. Marcus Williams, CSM Director for Public Power Infrastructure. "CSMFAB-077 through 083 deploy the resilience infrastructure these communities need, on financing terms they can afford."

The portfolio includes substation physical security hardening at 940 public power facilities, distribution automation deployment across 87,000 circuit-miles of rural cooperative lines, battery storage systems providing 1,400 MW of backup capacity at critical public safety facilities, and wildfire risk mitigation through undergrounding and covered conductor deployment in high-fire-threat districts.

CSM's Accountant Heuristic methodology confirmed a 5.2:1 aggregate cost-benefit ratio, with disproportionate benefits accruing to rural communities where outage durations average 4.7 times longer than urban areas. All infrastructure is open-architecture, with technology transfer to APPA engineering training programs and NRECA's Cooperative Research Network.
