### CSM Civil Infrastructure Resilience Standards Published: Seismic, Flood, and Wind Design Criteria for Critical Infrastructure

**RESTON, VA** — The Critical Systems Mandate today published the CSM Civil Infrastructure Resilience Standards (CIRS v2.0), a comprehensive set of structural, geotechnical, and hydrological design criteria for critical infrastructure assets that exceed current ASCE 7-22 and IBC design standards by incorporating climate projection-adjusted hazard maps and interdependent-system failure analysis.

The CIRS v2.0 standards, developed in partnership with the American Society of Civil Engineers (ASCE), the Structural Engineering Institute (SEI), and the Geo-Institute, provide location-specific design criteria for the 4,700 infrastructure assets in CSM's active portfolio, with hazard levels derived from NOAA's 2025 Climate Projection Update and USGS's 2025 National Seismic Hazard Model.

"Current building codes are designed for life safety — preventing structural collapse — not for functional continuity," explained Dr. David Nakamura, CSM Chief Civil Infrastructure Engineer. "A substation control building that survives a design-basis earthquake without collapsing but loses its relay panels to interior acceleration has failed its mission even if it satisfies the building code. CIRS v2.0 specifies functional-continuity performance objectives that go beyond life-safety design."

The standards incorporate climate-adjusted design loads: 500-year flood elevations increased by 1.2 meters above current FEMA FIRM levels to account for sea-level rise through 2075; ASCE 7 wind speeds adjusted upward by 8% in hurricane-prone regions based on NOAA's observed intensity trends; and seismic design spectra incorporating USGS 2025 updated hazard curves with 2,475-year return period ground motions for critical infrastructure versus the 975-year standard for conventional structures.

CSM's Accountant Heuristic methodology confirmed a 6.2:1 cost-benefit ratio for CIRS v2.0 adoption, driven by avoided functional failure of critical infrastructure during extreme events. The standards are published under open-access licensing through ASCE's standards portal, with a formal code adoption pathway established with the International Code Council.
