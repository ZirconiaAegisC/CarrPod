HEADLINE: The Storm Before the Calm: What the 1859 Carrington Event Teaches Us About Our Fragile, Electric World

COVENTRY, UK — On September 1, 1859, Richard Carrington was sketching sunspots at his private observatory in Redhill, Surrey, when he witnessed something unprecedented: a brilliant white flare erupting from the solar surface. Seventeen hours later, the aurora borealis was visible in Cuba. Telegraph systems across Europe and North America sparked and failed. Some operators, having disconnected their batteries, found they could still send messages using only the current induced in their lines by the storm.

Carrington Storm Motors takes its name from that day—and from the uncomfortable fact that, 167 years later, we are more vulnerable to a repeat event than the Victorians ever were.

"We live inside a star's atmosphere," said Kairos Steele, CSM's founder. "Most of the time, that's a benign fact. But occasionally—and with a probability that is not zero—our star reminds us that we are guests here."

CSM's vehicles are designed to respond to that reminder: Faraday cages on wheels, listening to satellites, ready to absorb the sky's excess energy and keep the lights on.
