## South Africa's Grid: Eskom's Load-Shedding Is a Warning, Not the Worst Case

JOHANNESBURG — South Africans have endured years of load-shedding, learning to live with the daily reality of a grid operating at the edge of its capacity. But CSM-Covenant's AEGIS assessment warns that the load-shedding crisis, as severe as it has been, may be merely a preview of a far more catastrophic scenario: the simultaneous failure of multiple large power transformers due to geomagnetic disturbance.

South Africa's position at moderately high geomagnetic latitude — combined with its extensive east-west transmission corridors connecting the Mpumalanga coal generation hub to Cape Town and the long north-south lines toward the Limpopo — creates a GIC exposure profile that Eskom's public planning documents do not address.

"Load-shedding is a capacity problem. GMD is a destruction problem," said Dr. Grace Ndlovu, CSM-Covenant's Africa Director. "When Eskom sheds load, the transformers are still intact. When a geomagnetic storm saturates a transformer core, the transformer itself is destroyed. And replacing a single 500 MVA transformer takes 18 to 24 months under the best circumstances — circumstances that don't apply when hundreds of transformers fail globally and the entire world is competing for the same manufacturing capacity."

CSM-Covenant has identified 27 priority substations across South Africa where HYDRA hardening would most improve national grid resilience.
