## Nigeria's Grid: Africa's Largest Economy Needs Invisible Protection

LAGOS — Nigeria's electrical grid serves Africa's largest economy and its most populous nation — over 220 million people. The grid's well-documented challenges with generation capacity, transmission losses, and distribution reliability are the subject of ongoing reform efforts. But CSM-Covenant's AEGIS assessment has identified an additional dimension that Nigeria's grid planners have not yet addressed: geomagnetic vulnerability.

Nigeria's equatorial position provides significant natural protection against GMD. However, the country's long transmission corridors — stretching from the Niger Delta gas generation hub to load centers in the north — and the growing number of high-voltage interconnections with neighboring countries create pathways for geomagnetic current that have not been systematically assessed.

"Nigeria is at a pivotal moment in its energy development," said Dr. Ndlovu. "The Siemens presidential power initiative, the expansion of the Transmission Company of Nigeria's network, the new interconnections — these are generational investments. Building GMD resilience into these projects from the start would cost a fraction of retrofitting them later. It's the definition of 'cheaper to do it now.'"

CSM-Covenant's analysis identifies 31 priority substations across Nigeria's six geopolitical zones where HYDRA hardening would provide the greatest national benefit. The total cost — approximately NGN 42 billion — represents less than 3% of the Siemens power initiative's Phase 1 budget.
