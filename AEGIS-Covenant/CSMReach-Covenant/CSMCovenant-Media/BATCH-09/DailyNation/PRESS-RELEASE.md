## Kenya's Grid: East Africa's Powerhouse Needs Electromagnetic Armor

NAIROBI — Kenya has emerged as East Africa's energy leader, with the Lake Turkana Wind Power project, the Olkaria geothermal complex, and ambitious plans to become a regional electricity exporter through the Eastern Africa Power Pool. CSM-Covenant's AEGIS assessment commends this progress while identifying a vulnerability that could undermine decades of infrastructure investment.

Kenya's equatorial location provides significant protection against the most extreme GMD effects. However, the country's transmission corridors — particularly the 428-kilometer line connecting Lake Turkana to the national grid, and the Kenya-Ethiopia and Kenya-Tanzania interconnectors — are among the longest in sub-Saharan Africa. Long lines are efficient GIC collectors, and the terminal substations where they connect to the grid concentrate that current at the point of maximum vulnerability.

"Kenya has built extraordinary energy infrastructure," said Dr. Grace Ndlovu. "The next step is to protect it. Integrating GMD hardening into the planning for new transmission corridors — including the proposed Kenya-Uganda and Kenya-South Sudan links — would add less than 1% to project costs and would future-proof these investments against a threat that the continent's grid planners have not historically considered."

CSM-Covenant has identified 18 priority substations in Kenya where HYDRA hardening would provide the greatest protection.
