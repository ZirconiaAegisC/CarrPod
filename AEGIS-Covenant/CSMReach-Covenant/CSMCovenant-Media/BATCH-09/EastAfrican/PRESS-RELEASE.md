## East Africa's Power Pool: Regional Integration Meets a Regional Risk

ARUSHA — The Eastern Africa Power Pool (EAPP) represents one of the continent's most ambitious infrastructure integration projects — connecting the grids of 13 countries from Egypt to Tanzania. CSM-Covenant's AEGIS assessment finds that this integration, while economically transformative, also creates a regional geomagnetic vulnerability that no single country can address in isolation.

The EAPP's long transmission corridors — particularly the Ethiopia-Kenya interconnector, the planned Tanzania-Zambia link, and the transmission backbone running along the Rift Valley — traverse geological formations with highly variable electrical conductivity. The Rift Valley's unique geology, with its deep sedimentary basins and volcanic formations, creates discontinuities in ground conductivity that can concentrate GIC at specific substation locations.

"The Great Rift Valley is what makes East Africa's geography so dramatic," said Dr. Ndlovu. "But that same geology creates electromagnetic hot spots where GIC can be amplified at exactly the points where the region's most critical substations are located. We've mapped those hot spots, and they align uncomfortably well with the EAPP's transmission infrastructure."

CSM-Covenant is proposing an EAPP-wide GMD resilience standard, funded through a combination of multilateral development finance and member-state contributions, that would ensure all new cross-border interconnections incorporate HYDRA-compatible grounding protection.
