## Turkey's Grid: A Continental Bridge Faces a Solar Risk

ANKARA — Turkey's unique position as an energy corridor between Europe, Asia, and the Middle East makes its electrical grid both strategically vital and, CSM-Covenant's AEGIS model reveals, geomagnetically exposed in ways that national planning has not fully addressed.

Turkey's transmission network spans a wide range of geomagnetic latitudes — from the Black Sea coast at relatively high GMD exposure to the Mediterranean south at more moderate risk. The country's long east-west transmission corridors, carrying power from hydroelectric and coal generation in the east to load centers in the Marmara region, create effective GIC collection pathways. The interconnections with the European ENTSO-E grid and the Georgian and Iranian networks mean that Turkey's transformers face GIC originating from multiple directions.

"Turkey is an energy bridge between continents," said Dr. Fatima Al-Rashid. "But a bridge carries traffic in both directions — including electromagnetic traffic. A GMD event affecting the European grid would propagate into Turkey through the interconnectors, and vice versa. Resilience must be multilateral."

CSM-Covenant's analysis identifies 35 priority substations across Turkey where HYDRA hardening would provide the greatest regional benefit. The strategic case for action is strengthened by Turkey's growing transformer manufacturing capability, which could supply GMD-resistant units to the broader region.
