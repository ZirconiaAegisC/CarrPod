## The Electricity That Binds: Why GMD Resilience Is a Regional Security Imperative

LONDON — The Middle East's electrical grids are interconnected in ways that most policymakers don't fully appreciate. The GCCIA backbone, the Egypt-Jordan interconnection, the Turkey-ENTSO-E link, the Iran-Iraq-Turkey transmission corridors — these connections create a regional electrical nervous system that is both a triumph of cooperation and, CSM-Covenant warns, a unified vulnerability surface.

A geomagnetic disturbance affecting one country's grid propagates across interconnections into neighboring systems. Transformers in Amman can fail from GIC originating in Cairo. Substations in Baghdad can saturate from currents induced in Tehran. The physics doesn't respect borders, alliances, or ceasefire lines.

"Electrical interconnection is physical interconnection," said Dr. Al-Rashid. "Every cross-border transmission line is a two-way street for geomagnetic current. The region's growing grid integration, which is economically and politically beneficial, also means that no country can fully protect itself in isolation. This is a collective action problem."

CSM-Covenant is proposing a MENA Grid Resilience Compact — a multilateral framework for coordinating GMD hardening standards, sharing space weather data, and establishing mutual aid protocols. The compact would operate under the auspices of the League of Arab States' energy cooperation mechanisms and would be open to all countries in the region regardless of political alignment.
