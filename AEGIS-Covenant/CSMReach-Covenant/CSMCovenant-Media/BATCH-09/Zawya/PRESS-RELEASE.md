## Islamic Finance and Infrastructure Resilience: A Natural Alignment

DUBAI — The global Islamic finance industry, with assets exceeding $4 trillion, operates on principles that align naturally with infrastructure resilience investment: asset-backing, risk-sharing, and the prohibition of excessive uncertainty (gharar). CSM-Covenant's newly published white paper, "Shariah-Compliant Financing for Grid Resilience," proposes a sukuk-based funding mechanism for GMD hardening that would unlock Islamic capital markets for critical infrastructure protection.

The paper identifies a structural gap in infrastructure finance: GMD hardening projects are too small for traditional project finance, too large for corporate balance sheets, and too urgent to wait for government budget cycles. Sukuk — asset-backed Islamic bonds — offer an elegant solution. A GMD Resilience Sukuk would be structured against the protected transformers themselves, with returns linked to the avoided cost of grid failure.

"The principles of Islamic finance are remarkably well-suited to resilience investment," said Dr. Omar Hassan, CSM-Covenant's Director of Islamic Finance Partnerships. "Shariah requires that financing be tied to real, productive assets — and transformers are among the most productive assets in any economy. Risk-sharing aligns with the collective nature of grid protection. And the prohibition on gharar — excessive uncertainty — is satisfied by the well-characterized physics of GMD risk."

CSM-Covenant is in discussions with several GCC-based Islamic financial institutions to structure the first GMD Resilience Sukuk, with an initial target issuance of $500 million to fund HYDRA deployment at the 31 priority GCC substations.
