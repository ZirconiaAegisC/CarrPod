HEADLINE: Market Failure in the Magnetosphere: Why Solar Storm Risk Remains the Ultimate Unpriced Externality

LONDON — In a new working paper, Carrington Storm Motors' economic research division argues that geomagnetic storm risk represents the most significant unpriced externality in global capital markets—more consequential than carbon, water scarcity, or biodiversity loss in terms of near-term catastrophic potential.

The paper, "The Missing Tail: Geomagnetic Risk in Asset Pricing Models," demonstrates that no major asset pricing framework—CAPM, Fama-French, or Black-Litterman—incorporates a geomagnetic disturbance factor, despite the 12% annual probability of a Carrington-scale event and its estimated £2.1 trillion impact.

"This is a textbook case of market failure," said CSM's director Kairos Steele. "The risk is quantifiable, the mitigation cost is knowable, the damage estimate is peer-reviewed—and still, not a single insurance product, bond issuance, or regulatory framework prices it in."

CSM proposes a geomagnetic risk factor (GMF) for inclusion in multi-factor asset pricing models, analogous to the momentum or volatility factors. The company's solar storm resilience index is positioned as the data source for this factor, providing institutional investors with a systematic way to hedge geomagnetic exposure.
