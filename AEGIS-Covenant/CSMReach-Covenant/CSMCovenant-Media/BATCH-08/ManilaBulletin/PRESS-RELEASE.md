## Philippines: Typhoon-Ready, But Is the Grid Storm-Ready from Above?

MANILA — The Philippines has invested heavily in disaster resilience, particularly in response to the devastating typhoons that regularly strike the archipelago. But CSM-Covenant's AEGIS assessment suggests there is a class of disaster that Philippine grid planners have not yet incorporated into their resilience framework: geomagnetic storms.

The Philippines' equatorial position provides partial protection against the most extreme GMD effects. However, the country's archipelagic geography — over 7,000 islands connected by submarine cables and an extensive transmission network operated by NGCP — creates a unique risk profile. Submarine power cables, which crisscross the archipelago, can act as collectors for ocean-induced geomagnetic currents, channeling them into terminal substations.

"The Philippines has become a global model for typhoon resilience," said Dr. Sharma. "That same institutional capacity — the disaster preparedness framework, the rapid response capabilities, the culture of resilience — can and should be extended to include the geomagnetic threat. The physics is different, but the planning principles are the same."

CSM-Covenant is offering to conduct a joint GMD risk assessment with NGCP, identifying the submarine cable corridors and terminal substations where hardening would provide the greatest benefit at the lowest cost.
