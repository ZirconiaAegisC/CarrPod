## Editorial: Japan's Disaster Preparedness Must Include the Solar Threat

TOKYO — Japan is rightly celebrated for its disaster preparedness. The nation's earthquake early warning system, tsunami defenses, and nuclear safety protocols — repeatedly tested and refined since 3.11 — represent a global gold standard in resilience engineering.

But there is a gap in Japan's preparedness architecture that CSM-Covenant's research has brought into sharp focus: the nation's electrical grid remains largely unprotected against geomagnetic disturbance, despite Japan's geographic position making it one of the most exposed developed nations on Earth.

The physics is well understood. During a geomagnetic storm, induced currents flow through the grounding systems of high-voltage transformers, saturating their magnetic cores and causing rapid internal heating. The result is irreversible winding damage requiring complete replacement — a process that takes 12 to 24 months per unit for the custom-built transformers Japan relies on.

Japan has approximately 1,200 large power transformers in service. CSM-Covenant's AEGIS model identifies 42 as critically exposed, concentrated along the Pacific Coast corridor from Tohoku to Kansai. Hardening these 42 substations would cost less than ¥60 billion and would prevent an estimated ¥30 trillion in first-year economic losses in the event of a major GMD.

"The lessons of 3.11 are clear: low-probability, high-consequence events are not hypothetical," said Dr. Nakamura. "They happen. And when they do, the cost of preparation is always less than the cost of recovery."
