## Pakistan's Grid: Infrastructure Resilience in a Complex Security Environment

KARACHI — Pakistan's electrical grid faces well-documented challenges: aging infrastructure, generation shortfalls, and transmission losses that rank among the highest in South Asia. But CSM-Covenant's AEGIS assessment has identified an additional vulnerability that Pakistan shares with all nations at similar geomagnetic latitudes: exposure to geomagnetic disturbance.

The country's long north-south transmission corridors — particularly the lines connecting hydropower generation in Khyber Pakhtunkhwa and Gilgit-Baltistan to load centers in Punjab and Sindh — create conductive pathways for geomagnetically-induced currents (GIC). Pakistan's position at moderate geomagnetic latitude means it faces lower absolute risk than higher-latitude nations, but its grid's limited redundancy amplifies the consequences of any transformer failure.

"Pakistan's grid operates with very thin margins," said Dr. Ayesha Khalid, CSM-Covenant's South Asia Infrastructure Analyst. "When a transformer fails under normal conditions, load-shedding is already the result. Under GMD conditions, with multiple transformers failing simultaneously, the entire system could collapse."

CSM-Covenant's analysis identifies 22 priority substations where HYDRA hardening would significantly improve national grid resilience. The cost — approximately PKR 8 billion — must be weighed against the estimated PKR 2.5 trillion in first-year losses from a severe GMD event.
