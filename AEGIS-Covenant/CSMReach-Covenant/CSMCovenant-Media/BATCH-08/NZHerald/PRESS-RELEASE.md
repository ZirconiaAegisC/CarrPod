## New Zealand's Grid: Islands at the Edge of the Auroral Zone

AUCKLAND — New Zealand occupies a unique position on the geomagnetic risk map. Straddling latitudes where the aurora australis is a regular occurrence — and where the auroral electrojet, the primary driver of geomagnetically-induced current, is most intense — the country's long, narrow geography and island-to-island HVDC interconnector create a GMD risk profile that CSM-Covenant's AEGIS model has now characterized in detail.

The Cook Strait cable — the HVDC link connecting the North and South Island grids — is a critical single-point-of-failure in New Zealand's electrical infrastructure. Under GMD conditions, the cable's submarine segment, its terminal converter stations, and the long AC transmission lines feeding into it from both islands all become part of an integrated GIC collection system.

"New Zealanders are used to thinking about earthquakes, volcanoes, and extreme weather," said Dr. Foster. "But the country's position at the edge of the auroral zone, combined with the Cook Strait cable and the long, linear geography of both islands, creates a geomagnetic risk that has not been part of the national resilience conversation."

CSM-Covenant's analysis identifies 18 priority substations across both islands where HYDRA hardening would provide the greatest benefit. The cost — approximately NZD 185 million — would protect against an estimated NZD 55 billion in first-year economic losses from a severe GMD event.
