## India's Grid: The World's Largest Synchronous Network Faces a Solar Test

NEW DELHI — India operates the world's largest synchronous power grid, connecting over 400 gigawatts of generation capacity across a subcontinent spanning 3.2 million square kilometers. The sheer scale of the Indian grid — and its rapid expansion, with annual growth rates exceeding 8% — creates a geomagnetic vulnerability profile that CSM-Covenant's AEGIS model has now quantified.

India's relatively low geomagnetic latitude provides partial protection against the most severe GMD effects. However, the grid's extraordinary geographical extent means that even moderate geomagnetic disturbance can generate significant GIC across long north-south transmission corridors — particularly the corridors connecting hydroelectric generation in the Himalayas to load centers in the southern states.

"The Indian grid is an engineering triumph," said Dr. Vikram Patel, CSM-Covenant's South Asia Director. "But it is also untested against a Carrington-scale event. The 2012 blackout that affected 620 million people was triggered by relatively minor grid instability. A GMD event would be orders of magnitude more severe."

CSM-Covenant's analysis identifies 65 critical substations across 12 Indian states where HYDRA hardening would significantly reduce national GMD risk. The total cost — approximately ₹3,500 crore — represents less than 2% of the Power Grid Corporation of India's annual capital expenditure.
