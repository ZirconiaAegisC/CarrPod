## China's Ultra-High-Voltage Grid: A Marvel at Risk

BEIJING — China operates the world's most extensive ultra-high-voltage (UHV) transmission network, with over 40,000 kilometers of UHV lines carrying power from western generation centers to eastern population hubs. These lines — operating at 800 kV DC and 1,000 kV AC — represent the pinnacle of power transmission engineering.

They also represent an unprecedented geomagnetic vulnerability.

UHV lines, by virtue of their extreme length and high current-carrying capacity, act as extraordinarily efficient collectors of geomagnetically-induced current (GIC). A single UHV corridor stretching 2,000 kilometers from Xinjiang to Shanghai can accumulate GIC across its entire length, concentrating the induced current at terminal substations where the largest and most expensive transformers are located.

"China has built the most advanced transmission system in history," said Dr. Wei Zhang, CSM-Covenant's China Research Lead. "But advanced doesn't mean protected. UHV transformers, because of their high power density and custom design, are even more susceptible to GIC saturation than conventional transformers. And their replacement timelines are measured in years, not months."

CSM-Covenant's AEGIS model identifies 78 critical UHV substations in China where HYDRA hardening would prevent catastrophic cascading failure. The technology is compatible with both AC and DC transmission systems and can be installed without disrupting grid operations.
