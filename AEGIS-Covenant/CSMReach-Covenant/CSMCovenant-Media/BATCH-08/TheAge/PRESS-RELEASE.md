## Victoria's Grid: The Renewable Transition Meets an Unexpected Risk

MELBOURNE — Victoria's ambitious renewable energy transition — wind farms in the west, solar in the north, the massive interconnectors linking to New South Wales and Tasmania — represents one of Australia's most significant infrastructure transformations. CSM-Covenant's AEGIS model reveals that this very transformation may be creating new geomagnetic vulnerabilities that the state's planners have not systematically evaluated.

Long transmission lines are the most effective collectors of geomagnetically-induced current. Victoria's expanding network of high-voltage lines connecting remote renewable generation zones to Melbourne load centers creates precisely the kind of infrastructure that maximizes GIC exposure. The new interconnectors — longer, higher-capacity, and more numerous than the infrastructure they replace — amplify the antenna effect.

"Renewable energy is essential and urgent," said Dr. Foster. "But we need to build it smart. Every new transmission line connecting a remote wind or solar farm to the grid is also a new pathway for geomagnetic current. HYDRA-compatible grounding at the substation level can neutralize that risk for a marginal addition to construction cost — less than 1% of the transmission project budget."

CSM-Covenant is engaging with the Victorian government and the Australian Energy Market Operator to incorporate GMD resilience standards into the state's renewable energy zone planning framework.
