## Thailand's Grid: The ASEAN Interconnection and the Geomagnetic Risk Multiplier

BANGKOK — Thailand's position as the ASEAN power grid interconnection hub brings enormous economic benefits — and, CSM-Covenant's research reveals, a previously unexamined geomagnetic risk. As the central node in the ASEAN Power Grid, with interconnections to Laos, Cambodia, Myanmar, and Malaysia, Thailand's grid acts as both an electricity trader and a risk concentrator.

The physics of geomagnetic disturbance operates regardless of national borders. Induced currents originating in one country's transmission network flow freely across interconnections into neighboring grids. Thailand's transformers — exposed to GIC from multiple directions via multiple cross-border links — face a compound risk that no single bilateral power purchase agreement addresses.

"Thailand has done an excellent job positioning itself as the regional electricity hub," said Dr. Sharma. "But that hub position means that Thailand's grid absorbs risk from every direction. A GMD event affecting the Lao hydro corridors and the Malaysian interconnectors simultaneously would stress Thai substations from multiple vectors. The ASEAN interconnection amplifies risk, not just opportunity."

CSM-Covenant is proposing an ASEAN-wide GMD resilience standard that would require all cross-border interconnections to incorporate HYDRA-compatible neutral grounding protection, ensuring that the regional grid's benefits are not undermined by its shared vulnerabilities.
