## Australia's Grid: The Southern Hemisphere's Overlooked Vulnerability

SYDNEY — Australia is often assumed to be insulated from severe geomagnetic disturbance by its mid-latitude position. That assumption, CSM-Covenant's AEGIS model demonstrates, is dangerously incomplete.

Australia's long transmission corridors — particularly the interconnectors linking the eastern states and the lines carrying power from remote renewable generation zones — create effective antennas for geomagnetically-induced current. The continent's unique geology compounds the risk: the highly conductive sedimentary basins of the Great Artesian Basin and the Murray-Darling system provide low-resistance pathways that can concentrate GIC at specific substation locations.

"Australia's grid is well managed and highly reliable under normal conditions," said Dr. James Foster, CSM-Covenant's Oceania Director. "But the grid was not designed with geomagnetic storms in mind. The interconnectors, the long transmission lines, the geological conductivity patterns — all of these factors combine to create a risk profile that the Australian Energy Market Operator has not publicly assessed."

CSM-Covenant's analysis identifies 28 critical substations across Queensland, New South Wales, Victoria, and South Australia where HYDRA hardening would provide the greatest resilience improvement. The total cost — approximately AUD 580 million — would prevent an estimated AUD 180 billion in first-year economic losses from a Carrington-scale event.
