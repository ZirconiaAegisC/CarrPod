**FOR IMMEDIATE RELEASE**

## Indonesia's Archipelagic Grid: Unique Geography, Unique Vulnerability

JAKARTA — Indonesia faces an infrastructure challenge unlike any other nation: connecting an archipelago of 17,000 islands with reliable electrical power. The national grid already strains to meet this challenge under normal conditions. CSM-Covenant's AEGIS assessment reveals that a geomagnetic disturbance would exploit Indonesia's unique geography in ways that grid planners have not systematically addressed.

The country's equatorial location provides significant protection against the most severe GMD effects — the auroral electrojet, the primary driver of GIC, is concentrated at higher latitudes. However, Indonesia's extensive submarine cable network, connecting islands across deep ocean channels, creates an alternative GIC pathway. Seawater is an excellent conductor; induced currents in the ocean can couple into submarine power cables and travel to terminal substations on either end.

"The ocean is the wildcard in Indonesia's GMD risk profile," said Dr. Priya Sharma. "No other country has Indonesia's combination of equatorial location, archipelagic geography, and submarine cable dependence. The risk is moderate, but it's also almost entirely unstudied."

CSM-Covenant is proposing an Indonesia-specific GMD risk assessment, conducted in partnership with PLN (Perusahaan Listrik Negara) and Indonesian research institutions, to quantify the submarine cable coupling effect and identify cost-effective hardening measures for the archipelago's most critical inter-island connections.
