## Singapore's Position: A Financial Hub's Hidden Infrastructure Risk

SINGAPORE — The Lion City boasts one of the world's most reliable electrical grids, with a SAIDI (System Average Interruption Duration Index) of less than one minute per year. But beneath this remarkable reliability lies a vulnerability that CSM-Covenant's AEGIS model has brought into focus: Singapore's near-total dependence on imported energy and its interconnected grid make it uniquely exposed to cascading regional failure triggered by geomagnetic disturbance.

Singapore's grid is connected to the broader ASEAN network through undersea cables and cross-border interconnections. A GMD event that disables critical transformers in Malaysia, Indonesia, or Thailand would have immediate knock-on effects on Singapore's power stability — even if the island's own substations are not directly affected.

Moreover, as a global financial center, Singapore's exposure to grid disruption is amplified by its economic structure. The Monetary Authority of Singapore estimates that a single day of financial market disruption costs the Singapore economy approximately SGD 1.2 billion.

"Financial centers are the canaries in the coal mine for infrastructure risk," said Dr. Priya Sharma, CSM-Covenant's Southeast Asia Director. "Singapore cannot protect itself by protecting only its own grid. Regional cooperation is essential."

CSM-Covenant is proposing an ASEAN-wide GMD resilience framework, with Singapore as a natural coordination hub given its financial resources, technical expertise, and neutral diplomatic position.
