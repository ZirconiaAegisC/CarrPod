## Solar Superstorms: A Threat to Global Infrastructure That Demands International Cooperation

BEIJING — In an era of great power competition, some threats transcend geopolitical boundaries. A Carrington-magnitude solar storm, CSM-Covenant's research demonstrates, would affect every nation with an electrical grid, regardless of alliance, ideology, or economic system.

The physics is universal. When a coronal mass ejection strikes Earth's magnetosphere, ground-induced currents flow wherever geology and infrastructure provide a conductive path. China's UHV grid, the Russian Unified Energy System, the European ENTSO-E network, and the North American interconnections would all face transformer saturation simultaneously. There is no national solution to a planetary problem.

"This is one of the few threat scenarios where international cooperation is not just desirable but mathematically necessary," said Dr. Natalia Volkov, CSM-Covenant's Director of International Cooperation. "No single nation can protect itself in isolation. The global transformer supply chain serves everyone; if 500 transformers fail simultaneously across three continents, the manufacturing backlog would affect all nations equally."

CSM-Covenant is proposing a G20-level initiative to coordinate GMD hardening standards, share space weather data, and establish mutual aid protocols for transformer replacement in the event of a major geomagnetic storm. The framework would build on existing cooperation through the World Meteorological Organization's space weather coordination group.
