## The Neuroscience of Infrastructure Stress: What Grid Failure Does to the Human Brain

STANFORD, CA — When CSM-Covenant models a prolonged grid-down scenario, the numbers that get the most attention are economic: $2.6 trillion in first-year losses, a decade-long recovery timeline, cascading supply chain failures. But the human cost — measured in neurological and psychological terms — is potentially more devastating.

Research on extended blackout scenarios reveals a predictable cascade of cognitive decline. Within 72 hours without power, sleep disruption from loss of climate control triggers measurable decreases in executive function. Within one week, food and water insecurity elevates cortisol to levels associated with PTSD. Within one month, social cohesion begins to fracture in ways that neuroimaging studies are only beginning to understand.

"The infrastructure we take for granted is a scaffolding for mental health," said Dr. Sarah Chen, CSM-Covenant's Human Factors Director. "When the lights go out, the brain goes into survival mode. And survival mode is not designed for the long term."

CSM-Covenant's resilience framework explicitly models the neurobiological consequences of infrastructure failure, integrating findings from disaster psychology, chronobiology (circadian disruption from loss of artificial light), and social neuroscience into its risk assessments.

The conclusion is clear: hardening the grid is, in a very real sense, an investment in collective mental health — preventing not just economic collapse but a mass psychological trauma event of unprecedented scale.
