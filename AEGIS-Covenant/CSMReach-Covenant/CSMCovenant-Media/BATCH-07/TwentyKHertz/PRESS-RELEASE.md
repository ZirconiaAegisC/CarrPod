## Listening to the Grid: The Sonic Landscape of Electrical Infrastructure

BERLIN — Every power transformer has a voice. It hums at the fundamental frequency of the alternating current passing through it — 50 Hz in Europe, 60 Hz in North America — along with a rich spectrum of harmonics that tell a story about the health of the machine, the quality of the power, and the state of the grid.

CSM-Covenant's acoustic monitoring program has recorded thousands of hours of transformer audio across three continents. The recordings reveal something remarkable: the sound of a transformer under geomagnetic stress is audibly different from a healthy transformer. The harmonic distortion produced by GIC saturation creates a characteristic frequency signature — a kind of distress call — that can be detected before catastrophic failure occurs.

"We're essentially building a stethoscope for the grid," said Dr. Lena Bergström, CSM-Covenant's Acoustic Analysis Lead. "Every substation has a unique acoustic fingerprint. When that fingerprint changes, we know something is wrong. In some cases, we can hear the problem before any electrical sensor detects it."

CSM-Covenant is now integrating real-time acoustic monitoring into its HYDRA deployment package, creating a multi-modal detection system that combines electromagnetic sensing, thermal imaging, and spectral audio analysis.
