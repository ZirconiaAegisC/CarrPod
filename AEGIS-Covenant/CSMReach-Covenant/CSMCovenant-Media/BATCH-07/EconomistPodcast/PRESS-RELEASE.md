## Infrastructure Neglect as a Market Failure: The Economist's Lens on Grid Risk

LONDON — In classical economics, rational actors insure against catastrophic risks when the premium is less than the expected loss. The expected annual loss from geomagnetic disturbance is approximately $340 billion. The cost of hardening the 200 most critical North American substations is approximately $4 billion — a one-time capital expenditure.

By any rational calculus, the investment should already have been made. It has not.

CSM-Covenant's newly published white paper, "The GMD Market Failure: Why Rational Actors Underinvest in Low-Probability Catastrophe Protection," applies behavioral economics, public choice theory, and institutional analysis to explain the persistent underinvestment in grid resilience. The conclusions are uncomfortable for policymakers and regulators alike.

"The problem is a perfect storm of perverse incentives," said Dr. Amir Hassan, CSM-Covenant's Policy Director. "Utility rate structures don't reward resilience investment. Insurance markets can't price the tail risk. Political systems discount the future at rates that make catastrophic prevention look 'uneconomical' on a 10-year budget horizon. And the public doesn't know the risk exists, so there's no political constituency for action."

CSM-Covenant's framework proposes a new model: resilience-as-infrastructure, funded through a combination of utility rate reform, federal loan guarantees, and parametric insurance instruments.
