## Science Friday: The Solar Storm That Changed Everything — And What We're Doing About the Next One

NEW YORK — On September 1, 1859, British astronomer Richard Carrington observed an intense white-light solar flare. Seventeen hours later, the largest geomagnetic storm in recorded history struck Earth. Telegraph networks worldwide failed — some operators were shocked by induced currents, while others found they could disconnect their batteries and continue transmitting using only the auroral current flowing through the lines.

It was the first recorded instance of what CSM-Covenant now calls a GMD event. And it was a warning we failed to heed.

"If the Carrington Event happened today, the telegraph wires would be high-voltage transmission lines, and the consequences would be millions of times more severe," said Dr. David Okonkwo, CSM-Covenant's Historian of Technology. "The physics hasn't changed. What's changed is our dependence on the infrastructure that the physics threatens."

CSM-Covenant's HYDRA protocol is the first comprehensive response to the GMD threat. It combines passive hardware protection at critical substations with an early warning system that draws on data from solar observatories worldwide. The technology is proven, the economics are compelling, and the alternative — waiting for the next Carrington Event to demonstrate the vulnerability — is unacceptable.
