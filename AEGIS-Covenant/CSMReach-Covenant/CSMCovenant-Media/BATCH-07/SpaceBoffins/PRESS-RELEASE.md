## From Space to Substation: The Engineering Pipeline That Protects Earth

LONDON — The journey from solar observation to grid protection spans 93 million miles and involves an extraordinary chain of human ingenuity. It begins with spacecraft like SOHO and SDO, which monitor the sun's corona for signs of eruptive activity. It continues through the Deep Space Climate Observatory at L1, which measures the magnetic field orientation of incoming CMEs. It passes through magnetotelluric survey teams who map the electrical conductivity of the Earth's crust. And it ends at a substation where a set of carefully designed capacitors and filters stands ready to intercept the induced current before it reaches a transformer worth millions of dollars.

CSM-Covenant sits at the center of this pipeline, translating space weather data into infrastructure protection decisions.

"We're space engineers in the morning and power engineers in the afternoon," said Dr. Richard Blake, CSM-Covenant's Mission Systems Director. "The spacecraft tell us what's coming. The ground surveys tell us where it will hit hardest. The HYDRA hardware does something about it. It's a complete system — from the sun's corona to the transformer core."

CSM-Covenant is now expanding its space weather monitoring capability through partnerships with ESA and NOAA, aiming to reduce CME arrival uncertainty from ±7 hours to ±2 hours.
