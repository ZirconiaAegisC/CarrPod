## The Daily: Inside the Race to Harden America's Grid

NEW YORK — There are approximately 2,000 large power transformers in the United States. Each one takes 12-24 months to manufacture, costs $2-7 million, and is custom-built to the specifications of a specific substation. There is no inventory. There is no spare capacity. If 350 of them fail simultaneously — as CSM-Covenant's AEGIS model predicts during a Carrington-scale geomagnetic storm — the replacement pipeline would take 4 to 10 years.

In the meantime, everything that requires electricity stops working. Hospitals. Water treatment plants. Refrigeration. Telecommunications. Financial markets. The cascading failures would make Hurricane Katrina look like a traffic jam.

CSM-Covenant was founded to address this vulnerability before the event, not after. The organization has secured funding for an initial deployment of HYDRA blocking hardware at 200 critical substations, with a target of completing the first 47 installations by Q4 2026.

"The transformer supply chain is the single biggest bottleneck in grid recovery," said Dr. Elena Voss. "We can't speed up manufacturing by an order of magnitude. What we can do is prevent the transformers from failing in the first place. That's the mission."
