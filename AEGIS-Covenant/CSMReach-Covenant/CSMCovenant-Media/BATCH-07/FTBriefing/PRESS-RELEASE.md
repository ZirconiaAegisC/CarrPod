## FT News Briefing: The $2.6 Trillion Risk the Market Isn't Pricing

LONDON — Financial markets are famously forward-looking — except when they're not. CSM-Covenant's analysis of geomagnetic disturbance risk suggests that equity and bond markets are systematically underpricing the probability of a grid-down catastrophe by approximately three orders of magnitude.

The numbers are stark. A Carrington-magnitude solar event would destroy an estimated 350 high-voltage transformers in North America alone, triggering cascading grid failure across the Eastern and Western Interconnections. First-year economic losses: $2.6 trillion. Insurance industry exposure: uninsurable at any premium. Sovereign bond implications: potentially catastrophic for U.S. and Canadian debt markets.

And yet, the market capitalization of companies providing grid-hardening solutions remains effectively zero.

"The efficient market hypothesis has a blind spot for low-probability, high-consequence events," said CSM-Covenant Chief Economist Dr. Henrik Sørensen. "When the probability is 12% per decade and the consequence is measured in trillions, the expected annual loss is $340 billion. That's a number institutional investors should care about."

CSM-Covenant's research quantifies, for the first time, the gap between actuarial risk and market pricing for GMD exposure — and proposes a framework for closing it through infrastructure investment, regulatory reform, and new insurance instruments.
