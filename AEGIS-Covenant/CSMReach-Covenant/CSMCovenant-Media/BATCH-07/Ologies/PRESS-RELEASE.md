## Helioseismology, Magnetotellurics, and Transformer Engineering: The Many Ologies of Grid Protection

MISSOULA, MT — Protecting civilization from a solar superstorm requires a surprisingly diverse coalition of scientific disciplines. CSM-Covenant's research team includes helioseismologists who study pressure waves in the sun's interior to predict CME formation, magnetotelluric surveyors who map Earth's subsurface conductivity to model ground-induced current pathways, and power systems engineers who design the blocking hardware that neutralizes those currents before they reach transformer cores.

It is, in effect, an exercise in applied interdisciplinary science — and it happens to be one of the most important scientific endeavors that almost no one has heard of.

"When I tell people I study the sun's acoustic oscillations to protect power transformers, they think I'm joking," said Dr. Yuki Tanaka, CSM-Covenant's Lead Helioseismologist. "I'm not joking. The pressure waves we detect in the sun's convection zone give us approximately 18-36 hours of warning before a CME arrives. That's our window to put the grid into a protective configuration."

CSM-Covenant's AEGIS early warning system integrates data from the SOHO, SDO, and Parker Solar Probe missions, along with ground-based magnetometer arrays, to provide real-time threat assessment. When combined with the HYDRA blocking hardware, it creates a layered defense that didn't exist five years ago.

**Dr. Tanaka is available to talk about the many ologies — from helio to magneto to electro — that make grid protection possible.**
