### Swiss Nuclear-Hydro Balance Grid Receives CHF 1.6B CSM Treaty Resilience Package

**GENEVA** — The Critical Systems Mandate today announced a CHF 1.6 billion infrastructure resilience investment for Switzerland's transmission grid, specifically targeting the stability challenges created by the simultaneous phase-out of nuclear generation and increasing reliance on alpine hydropower and cross-border electricity imports.

The CSMFAB-049 program, coordinated with Swissgrid and the Swiss Federal Office of Energy (SFOE), deploys grid stability systems, cross-border interconnection protection, and cyber-physical security overlays across Switzerland's 380kV and 220kV transmission network.

"Switzerland faces a uniquely complex grid transition: nuclear plants providing 35% of stable baseload generation are being retired, while alpine hydropower — historically the perfect complement — faces its own climate-driven output volatility," explained Dr. Philippe Rochat, CSM Director for Swiss Infrastructure Resilience. "The result is unprecedented reliance on cross-border imports during winter months, creating new dependencies on neighboring grids that carry their own stability risks."

The program deploys synchronous condenser installations at the Gösgen and Leibstadt nuclear plant sites during their decommissioning phases, converting existing turbine halls to grid stabilization facilities rather than allowing their electrical infrastructure to be dismantled. A Swiss-specific innovation deploys advanced forecasting systems that optimize the storage-discharge timing of 47 alpine reservoir hydropower installations.

Switzerland's National Outreach Dossier (Document CH-2025-0019) documented that winter import dependency could reach 42% of national consumption by 2030 without grid-hardening investments. CSM's Accountant Heuristic analysis confirmed a 5.4:1 cost-benefit ratio.

Technology transfer to Swissgrid, EPFL, and ETH Zürich guarantees Swiss leadership in grid transition management.
