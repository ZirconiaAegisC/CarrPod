### Germany's Energiewende Grid Receives €4.2B CSM Treaty Stability Investment

**BONN** — The Critical Systems Mandate today announced a €4.2 billion infrastructure resilience investment for Germany's Energiewende-stressed electrical grid, deploying the world's largest coordinated grid-forming inverter and synchronous condenser program to maintain stability during periods of 80%+ renewable penetration.

The CSMFAB-055 program, coordinated with the four German transmission system operators (TenneT, 50Hertz, Amprion, TransnetBW) and the Bundesnetzagentur, addresses the systemic stability challenges created by Germany's nuclear phase-out, accelerated coal retirement, and the north-south transmission bottleneck that concentrates wind generation in the north while industrial demand clusters in the south.

"Germany is attempting the most ambitious grid transition in industrial history: simultaneously retiring nuclear and coal baseload while building out renewable generation that is inherently variable and inverter-based," explained Prof. Dr. Klaus Reinhardt, CSM Director for German Grid Transition. "The physics problem is clear: inverter-based generation does not provide the rotational inertia that has historically stabilized grid frequency. Our program deploys synthetic inertia at a scale no nation has previously attempted."

The program installs 24 large-scale synchronous condensers at retiring coal and nuclear plant sites, converting their existing grid connections to stabilization facilities. Additionally, 8,400 MW of grid-forming battery inverter capacity is deployed at strategic interconnection points, providing sub-cycle frequency response that replaces the inertial response historically provided by thermal turbines.

Germany's National Outreach Dossier (Dokument DE-2025-0123) documented 147 frequency excursion events in 2024 exceeding operational limits. CSM's Accountant Heuristic confirmed a 4.9:1 cost-benefit ratio. Full technology transfer to TSO engineering teams and Fraunhofer institutes.
