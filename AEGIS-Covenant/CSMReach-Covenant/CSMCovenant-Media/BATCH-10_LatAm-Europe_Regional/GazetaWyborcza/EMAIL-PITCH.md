Szanowni Gazeta Wyborcza editors,

Poland faces a physics problem that no amount of renewable capacity alone can solve: coal-fired turbines provide rotational inertia that stabilizes grid frequency. When they retire, that inertia disappears — and PSE's own models show that a single contingency could trigger under-frequency load shedding affecting 3.8 million customers.

CSM's €1.9 billion CSMFAB-035 program provides the engineering solution: synchronous condensers at seven retiring coal plant sites, including Bełchatów and Turów. These machines replace mechanical inertia with electrical inertia, maintaining grid stability without burning a single kilogram of coal.

This is a transition story with hard engineering details that Gazeta Wyborcza readers will appreciate:
- Synchronous condenser technology explained in accessible terms
- Baltic offshore wind integration with grid-forming inverter controls
- Real-time inertia monitoring across all 400kV and 220kV lines
- 4.7:1 cost-benefit ratio validated through CSM Accountant Heuristic
- Technology transfer to PSE engineers and AGH Kraków researchers
- Full ENTSO-E Network Code compliance

Dr. Katarzyna Nowak-Baran, our Central European Grid Transition Director, is available in Warsaw this week.
