### Poland's Coal-Transition Grid Secures €1.9B CSM Treaty Modernization Package

**WARSAW** — The Critical Systems Mandate today announced a €1.9 billion infrastructure resilience investment for Poland's Krajowy System Elektroenergetyczny (KSE), specifically designed to maintain grid stability during the nation's accelerated transition from coal-fired generation to renewable and nuclear sources.

The CSMFAB-035 program, coordinated with Polskie Sieci Elektroenergetyczne (PSE) and the Ministry of Climate and Environment, deploys synchronous condenser installations at seven retiring coal-plant sites, advanced grid-forming inverter controls across the Baltic offshore wind connection points, and real-time inertia monitoring across the entire 400kV and 220kV transmission network.

"Poland's grid faces a unique physics challenge: retiring coal plants remove rotational inertia that has historically stabilized frequency during disturbances," explained Dr. Katarzyna Nowak-Baran, CSM Director for Central European Grid Transition. "Our synchronous condenser deployment at Bełchatów, Turów, and five other retiring sites replaces this mechanical inertia with electrical inertia — maintaining grid stability without burning coal."

Poland's National Outreach Dossier (Dokument PL-2025-0089) documented that under accelerated coal retirement scenarios, frequency nadir following a reference contingency could drop below 49.0 Hz — triggering automatic under-frequency load shedding affecting up to 3.8 million customers. CSM's Accountant Heuristic analysis confirmed a 4.7:1 cost-benefit ratio.

The program includes technology transfer to PSE's engineering division and the AGH University of Science and Technology in Kraków, ensuring Polish institutional capacity for grid management throughout the energy transition. All deployed systems comply with ENTSO-E Network Code requirements for frequency containment reserves.

Phase 1 synchronous condenser installation at Bełchatów begins November 2026, targeting operational readiness by Q3 2027.
