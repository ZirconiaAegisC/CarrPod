HEADLINE: A Comprehensive Review: Two Centuries of Space Weather Science and the Engineering Response That's Finally Catching Up

COVENTRY, UK — Carrington Storm Motors has published an extensive review article tracing the arc of space weather science from Richard Carrington's 1859 sunspot sketch to the real-time CME trajectory prediction systems operating today. The review, authored by CSM's research division, argues that while our understanding of solar physics has advanced enormously, our engineering response has lagged dangerously behind.

"We can now predict a CME's terrestrial impact with remarkable precision," writes Dr. Chester Villegas in the review's concluding section. "What we haven't done—what we have systematically failed to do since the 1859 event—is translate that knowledge into infrastructure protection."

The review surveys the key scientific milestones: Carrington's flare observation, the discovery of the solar wind by Mariner 2, the 1989 Hydro-Québec blackout, the 2012 Carrington-class near-miss, and the deployment of the DSCOVR and Swarm satellite constellations. It then pivots to the engineering gap: while our observational capability has improved by orders of magnitude, the average electrical transformer in service today is less protected against geomagnetic disturbance than its 1950s predecessor—because modern transformers are more efficient, and efficiency and resilience are often inversely correlated.
