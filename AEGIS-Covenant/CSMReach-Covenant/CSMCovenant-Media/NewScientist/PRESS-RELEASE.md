HEADLINE: Earth's Magnetic Field Is Weakening Faster Than Predicted — and Our Grids Are More Vulnerable Than We Knew

LONDON — New research from Carrington Storm Motors' science division reveals that the South Atlantic Anomaly—a region where Earth's magnetic field is anomalously weak—has expanded 7% faster than satellite models predicted, potentially accelerating the time frame for solar storm vulnerability across the Southern Hemisphere and parts of Europe.

The finding, based on analysis of Swarm constellation data from 2013-2026, has implications beyond space weather: it suggests current models of core-mantle boundary dynamics may need revision. "We're observing magnetic field changes at rates that weren't supposed to happen for another century," said Dr. Chester Villegas, CSM's chief scientist.

For CSM, the practical implication is clear. "Every year of delay in hardening infrastructure is a year in which the natural shielding that protected us in the 20th century continues to degrade," said Kairos Steele. "We are in a race against geophysics."

The company is deploying its hardened EV fleet at an accelerated pace, targeting 847 critical grid nodes across Europe by 2028.
