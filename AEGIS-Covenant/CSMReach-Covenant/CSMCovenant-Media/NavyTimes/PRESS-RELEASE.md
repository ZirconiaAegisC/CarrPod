**FLEET RESILIENCE: AEGIS COVENANT PROPOSES MXENE HARDENING FOR CARRIER STRIKE GROUP ELECTRICAL SYSTEMS**

NORFOLK, Va. — The AEGIS Covenant has submitted a fleet-wide electromagnetic resilience proposal to Naval Sea Systems Command, calling for MXene shielding retrofit of critical electrical systems across all eleven carrier strike groups.

The proposal is based on a classified vulnerability assessment conducted at Naval Station Norfolk, which modeled the effects of a G5-class geomagnetic disturbance on the integrated electrical architecture of a Nimitz-class carrier. The assessment identified cascading-failure pathways through the ship's electrical distribution system, combat systems power modules, and propulsion control electronics.

"A carrier has enough electrical generation capacity to power a small city," said Capt. David Chen (Ret.), AEGIS Maritime Division Lead and former CO of USS Shoup. "But generation means nothing if distribution is compromised. Geomagnetically induced currents don't care about your reactors — they target the transformers, the switchgear, the control systems. The things that make the power usable."

MXene shielding applied to shipboard transformers, switchgear cabinets, and critical combat-systems power modules would require 14 days of shipyard availability per vessel, at a material cost of approximately $2.1 million per carrier. For the surface fleet — cruisers and destroyers — the retrofit timeline drops to 7 days and $340,000 per hull.

NAVSEA has acknowledged receipt and has assigned a technical evaluation team from the Naval Surface Warfare Center, Philadelphia Division. Preliminary discussions are also underway with the Royal Navy, which has expressed interest in MXene hardening for the Queen Elizabeth-class carriers.

"Naval power projection depends on electrons as much as ordnance," said Chen. "We secure the ordnance against every conceivable threat. It's time we secure the electrons too."
