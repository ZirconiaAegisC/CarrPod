HEADLINE: How a Combat Engineer Turned an EV Skateboard Into a Grid-Stabilisation Platform

COVENTRY, UK — The most interesting part of a Carrington Storm Motors vehicle isn't the battery pack or the motor—it's a custom silicon-carbide voltage regulator, machined in-house, that can absorb a 50,000-volt geomagnetic surge and dissipate it as heat through a copper-nickel mesh woven into the chassis.

"We started with a standard EV skateboard platform," said Kairos Steele, CSM's founder and a veteran of the Royal Engineers. "Then we asked: what would it take for this platform to protect a hospital? The answer was about 18 months of engineering and three complete redesigns of the power electronics."

The technical heart of the system is a silicon-carbide MOSFET array capable of switching at 200kHz while handling transient currents that would vaporise a standard IGBT. It's controlled by an FPGA running a real-time predictive model of local grid impedance—an approach Steele's team adapted from military generator-synchronisation algorithms.

"Most surge protection is passive. Ours is predictive," Steele said. "By the time the geomagnetic current arrives, the vehicle has already calculated where it needs to route the excess energy."
