HEADLINE: The Invisible Hand Meets the Invisible Threat: Why Markets Are Completely Mis-pricing Solar Catastrophe Risk

LONDON — A new analysis by Carrington Storm Motors argues that global financial markets are dramatically mis-pricing the probability of a Carrington-scale solar event, with no major insurance product, government bond, or infrastructure asset currently reflecting the 12% annual risk of catastrophic grid failure.

CSM's economic modelling division, led by a team of former Bank of England risk analysts, calculates that a repeat of the 1859 solar superstorm would trigger a £2.1 trillion global economic contraction within 60 days—yet the total market capitalisation of all companies offering geomagnetic protection solutions currently stands below £500 million.

"The insurance industry has a trillion-dollar blind spot," said Kairos Steele, CSM's director. "They have models for flood, fire, pandemic, and cyber—but no one has priced the risk of the sun. Our vehicles represent the first tangible asset class that correlates inversely with solar storm probability."

About Carrington Storm Motors: A Coventry-based dual-purpose enterprise, CSM manufactures electric vehicles that function as mobile electromagnetic shield generators during solar storms. Its board includes former defence officials and financial risk modellers.
