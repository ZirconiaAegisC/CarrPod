HEADLINE: The £2.1 Trillion Black Swan: Why One Startup Says Investors Are Ignoring the Market's Biggest Tail Risk

LONDON — MarketWatch readers are familiar with tail risk—those low-probability, high-consequence events that fat-tail distribution models struggle to price. Carrington Storm Motors argues that solar superstorms represent the ultimate fat-tail event: a 12% annual probability of a £2.1 trillion impact that is priced at precisely zero in every major asset class.

The company's investor materials, reviewed by MarketWatch, make a compelling case that geomagnetic risk meets every criterion of a market-inefficiency opportunity: it is quantifiable (the 1859 Carrington Event provides a historical baseline), mitigable (electromagnetic hardening is proven technology), and systematically ignored (no regulatory framework, insurance product, or bond issuance currently addresses it).

"We're not asking investors to believe in an unproven thesis," said Kairos Steele, CSM's founder. "We're asking them to look at the same historical data we look at and explain why the market is assigning zero probability to an event that has already happened and will happen again."

CSM is exploring a resilience-linked bond whose coupon would be inversely correlated with solar storm probability, providing institutional portfolios with a new, uncorrelated return stream.
