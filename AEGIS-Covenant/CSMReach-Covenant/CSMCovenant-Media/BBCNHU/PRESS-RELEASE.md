**THE GREATEST NATURAL HISTORY STORY NEVER FILMED: AEGIS COVENANT OPENS ACCESS TO DOCUMENTARY PARTNERS**

LONDON — The AEGIS Covenant has issued an open call to documentary filmmakers worldwide, offering unprecedented access to what may be the most visually spectacular and consequential story of the decade: the race to protect Earth's electrical civilization from the Sun itself.

"This is the BBC Natural History Unit's next great commission waiting to happen," said Dr. Vasquez. "You have a natural phenomenon — coronal mass ejections — that creates the most beautiful light show in the solar system: the aurora borealis. And on the same day that light show becomes visible in Cuba and Honduras, it also destroys the electrical grid of every nation in its path. Beauty and terror, in the same image. That's the film."

The Covenant is offering approved documentary partners access to: the MXene production cleanrooms where 45-micron transparent films — thinner than a human hair — are manufactured to shield entire continents; the Charlemagne fleet: 47 retrofitted decommissioned military landing craft modified for amphibious humanitarian delivery, captured in formation exercises off the coast of Portugal; the 18 AEGIS Agents — a diverse coalition of scientists, veterans, diplomats, and engineers — each with their own narrative arc; and the Aurora Monitoring Network: 200 ground-based observatories capturing real-time CME arrival data, from the fjords of Norway to the Chilean Andes.

"Every frame of this story contains visual wonder," said Vasquez. "You have nanotech at 45 microns. You have amphibious fleets. You have the aurora. You have 18 protagonists from every continent. It is, literally, impossible to make a boring shot of this story."
