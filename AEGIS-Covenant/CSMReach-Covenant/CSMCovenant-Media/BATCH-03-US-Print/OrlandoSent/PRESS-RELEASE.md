HEADLINE: Duke Energy, Hurricane Season, and the Solar Threat Florida's Grid Hasn't Accounted For

ORLANDO — Carrington Storm Motors (CSM) today announced the successful Phase II independent validation of Project AEGIS, a development with dual relevance for Central Florida — a region that has perfected hurricane preparedness but has done nothing to harden against the one natural threat that could outstrip any Category 5 in economic damage.

Duke Energy Florida serves 1.9 million customers, many of them in the Orlando metropolitan area. The utility has invested heavily in storm-hardening: concrete poles, underground lines, smart-grid technology. But a Carrington-level geomagnetic storm is not a weather event you can track on radar. It is an electromagnetic event that arrives at the speed of light and induces currents across thousands of miles of transmission infrastructure simultaneously. NOAA's 28% probability of a major X-flare impact makes this a preparedness gap that Central Florida ratepayers deserve to understand.

"Florida knows storms. But this is a different category of storm entirely — and until now, no one had built the category of protection it requires," said Kairos Steele, CSM's CTO. "We've built it. We've tested it. The preparedness gap is now a deployment question."

CSM was founded in Austin in 2024, holds 14 patents pending, and offers CTO availability, lab documentation, and Florida-specific analysis.
