HEADLINE: Your Phone, Your Fridge, Your Life: The Grid Has No Surge Protector — Until Now

McLEAN, Va. — Carrington Storm Motors (CSM) today announced the successful Phase II independent validation of Project AEGIS, delivering a story with the kind of kitchen-table relevance, clear stakes, and forward-looking consumer impact that defines USA TODAY's editorial voice.

Here is what every American should understand in the time it takes to finish a cup of coffee. The sun goes through cycles. We are entering the peak of one. Sometimes the sun throws a flare big enough to turn the Earth's magnetic field into an electrical pulse that flows through the ground and into power lines like a million lightning strikes at once. That happened in 1859 — it melted telegraph wires. It almost happened in 2012, missing Earth by nine days. NOAA says the chance of a direct hit in the next two years is 28%. And the U.S. grid — the thing that powers your phone, your fridge, your hospital — has zero deployed protection.

"The grid is the most complex machine humanity has ever built, and it doesn't have a surge protector for the sun," said Kairos Steele, CSM's CTO. "We just built one. Independent labs confirm it works. Now America needs to know it exists."
