HEADLINE: The Grid's Biggest Uninsurable Risk Has a Solution — And It Was Built in a Texas Warehouse

NEW YORK — Carrington Storm Motors (CSM) today announced that its Project AEGIS geomagnetic storm shielding system has passed independent Phase II validation, introducing a technology that reshapes the risk landscape for electrical infrastructure — a development with profound implications for the financial, regulatory, and engineering architecture of the modern grid.

Since the National Academy of Sciences published its landmark 2008 report estimating a Carrington-level event would cause $1 trillion to $2 trillion in damage — a figure since revised upward to $2.6 trillion — the gap between the scientific consensus on geomagnetic risk and the practical absence of deployed countermeasures has been one of the more quietly alarming features of American infrastructure policy. CSM's validation of AEGIS closes that gap at the hardware level. Whether it closes it at the deployment level is now a question for utilities, regulators, and insurers.

"We have spent more than a century electrifying civilization and not one year hardening it against the sun," said Kairos Steele, CSM's Chief Technology Officer. "The alloy science has caught up to the threat. The policy response has not. We hope this validation accelerates the latter."

Carrington Storm Motors was founded in Austin, Texas in 2024. The company holds 14 patents pending and has received technical consultation from former IEEE Power & Energy Society committee chairs.
