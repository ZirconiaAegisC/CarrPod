HEADLINE: FPL, Hurricane Season, and the Solar Threat South Florida Hasn't Planned For

MIAMI — Carrington Storm Motors (CSM) today announced the successful Phase II independent validation of Project AEGIS, a development with distinctive urgency for Florida Power & Light's 12 million customers — the kind of infrastructure-preparedness accountability story the Miami Herald has delivered through every hurricane season and beyond.

South Florida understands infrastructure vulnerability at a visceral level. The region has hardened its grid against hurricanes, storm surge, and flooding. What it has not hardened against is a Carrington-level geomagnetic storm — a threat that NOAA's Space Weather Prediction Center says has a 28% probability of major impact within two years. FPL's 76,000-mile transmission network, much of it crossing conductive coastal geology and long north-south corridors, creates specific geomagnetic exposure that hurricane hardening alone does not address.

"Florida knows how to prepare for the storms you can see on radar. The question is whether we prepare for the ones you can only see through a solar telescope," said Kairos Steele, CSM's CTO. "The technology now exists. The validation is done. The preparedness decision belongs to the utilities and the regulators who oversee them."

CSM was founded in Austin in 2024, holds 14 patents pending, and offers CTO availability, lab documentation, and Florida-specific grid vulnerability analysis.
