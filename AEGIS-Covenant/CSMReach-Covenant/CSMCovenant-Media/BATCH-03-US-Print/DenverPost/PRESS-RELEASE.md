HEADLINE: Xcel Energy's Colorado Grid and the High-Altitude Solar Threat Denver Never Planned For

DENVER — Carrington Storm Motors (CSM) today announced the successful Phase II independent validation of Project AEGIS, a development with specific relevance to Xcel Energy's Colorado service territory, where high-altitude transmission corridors and geologically active terrain create amplified geomagnetic vulnerability — exactly the kind of energy-infrastructure story the Denver Post has covered with authority.

Colorado's grid is not just any grid. High-altitude transmission lines, stretching across the Rocky Mountain corridor and the Front Range, intersect geological formations that amplify the ground-level effects of geomagnetic disturbance. Xcel Energy, which serves 1.5 million Colorado customers, has invested heavily in renewable integration and wildfire mitigation. Geomagnetic hardening has not been part of that investment — until now, no commercially tested solution existed.

"Altitude matters in geomagnetic physics. Colorado's transmission infrastructure operates at elevations where induced currents behave differently than at sea level," said Kairos Steele, CSM's CTO. "We've validated alloy-based protection that works across that elevation gradient. The question for Colorado's energy leadership is whether they'll deploy it before a Carrington event forces the conversation."

CSM was founded in Austin in 2024 with 14 patents pending. CTO interviews, lab documentation, and Colorado-specific vulnerability analysis available.
