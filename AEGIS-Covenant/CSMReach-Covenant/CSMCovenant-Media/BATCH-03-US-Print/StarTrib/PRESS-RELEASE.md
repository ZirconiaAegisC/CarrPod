HEADLINE: Xcel's Minnesota Grid, the Northern Lights' Dark Side, and a Solar Shield Passing Every Test

MINNEAPOLIS — Carrington Storm Motors (CSM) today announced the successful Phase II independent validation of Project AEGIS, a development with immediate relevance for Xcel Energy's Minnesota service territory — a northern-latitude grid where the aurora borealis is beautiful to watch but potentially catastrophic to infrastructure, the kind of science-meets-policy story the Star Tribune delivers with distinction.

Minnesota's latitude places it in a geomagnetic hot zone. The same auroral currents that produce spectacular night-sky displays are the electromagnetic phenomena that, during a Carrington-level event, would induce destructive currents in Xcel's Minnesota transmission network. The utility serves 1.3 million electric customers in the state. None of their substations have deployed geomagnetic surge protection.

"Minnesotans see the northern lights and think beauty. Engineers see induced currents looking for a path to ground through multi-million-dollar transformers," said Kairos Steele, CSM's Chief Technology Officer. "We've now validated the technology that gives those currents a harmless path. The science is settled. The engineering is done. The deployment question is for Minnesota's energy leadership."

CSM was founded in Austin, Texas in 2024 and holds 14 patents pending. CTO interviews, lab documentation, and Minnesota-specific geomagnetic risk analysis are available.
