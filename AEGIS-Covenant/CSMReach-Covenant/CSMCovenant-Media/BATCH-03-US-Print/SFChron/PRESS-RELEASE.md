HEADLINE: PG&E's Next Chapter: The Grid Shield California's Most Scrutinized Utility Hasn't Installed

SAN FRANCISCO — Carrington Storm Motors (CSM) today announced the successful Phase II independent validation of Project AEGIS, a development that raises a direct and pointed question for PG&E, the California Public Utilities Commission, and the Bay Area's technology-literate public — the kind of accountability inquiry the San Francisco Chronicle has built its reputation on.

PG&E's infrastructure challenges are well-documented: wildfire liability, aging equipment, and the largest utility bankruptcy in American history. Yet one threat vector remains entirely unaddressed across their 70,000-square-mile service territory — geomagnetic surge protection at the substation level. NOAA's 28% probability of a major X-flare impact within two years makes this omission an urgent regulatory question, not a hypothetical future concern.

"PG&E has been through a reckoning on infrastructure resilience. The question now is whether that reckoning extends to threats that haven't yet materialized — but that science says inevitably will," said Kairos Steele, CSM's CTO. "The hardware exists. The validation is done. The ball is in the utility's court."

CSM was founded in Austin in 2024, holds 14 patents pending, and offers CTO availability, lab validation reports, and California-specific grid vulnerability analysis.
