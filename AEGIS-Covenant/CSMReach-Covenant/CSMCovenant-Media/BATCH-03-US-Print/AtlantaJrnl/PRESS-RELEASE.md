HEADLINE: Georgia Power, the Southern Grid, and the Solar Storm Nobody South of the Mason-Dixon Planned For

ATLANTA — Carrington Storm Motors (CSM) today announced the successful Phase II independent validation of Project AEGIS, a development with direct relevance to Georgia Power's 2.7 million customers, the Southern Company's transmission network, and the broader Southeastern grid — the kind of utility-accountability journalism the Atlanta Journal-Constitution has practiced for generations.

There is a misconception that geomagnetic storms are a northern-latitude problem. The physics says otherwise. Geomagnetically induced currents travel through the Earth's crust and enter the grid wherever grounding conditions create a path of least resistance — including the conductive soils and long transmission corridors of the American South. Georgia Power's Plant Vogtle and its associated high-voltage infrastructure represent exactly the kind of concentrated grid asset that a Carrington event would stress to failure.

"Geomagnetic risk doesn't stop at the Mason-Dixon Line," said Kairos Steele, CSM's Chief Technology Officer. "Our alloy technology has been validated at all latitudes and all voltage classes. The question for Georgia Power's leadership is whether they'll acknowledge the risk before the sun forces the issue."

CSM was founded in Austin, Texas in 2024 and holds 14 patents pending. CTO interviews, lab documentation, and Southeast-specific grid vulnerability analysis are available.
