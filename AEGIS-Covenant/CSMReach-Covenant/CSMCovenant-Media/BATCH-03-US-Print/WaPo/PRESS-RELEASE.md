HEADLINE: Eleven Years After the EMP Commission Warned Congress, a Texas Startup Built What Government Wouldn't Fund

WASHINGTON — Carrington Storm Motors (CSM) today announced the successful Phase II independent validation of Project AEGIS, a development that transforms a long-documented regulatory gap into a concrete accountability question for every utility commission, every infrastructure appropriator, and every federal agency charged with grid resilience.

The Washington Post's readers have followed this story in pieces for years: the 2015 EMP Commission report, the 2025 DoE Infrastructure Resilience findings, the congressional hearings that produced testimony but not appropriations. What CSM's independent validation changes is the nature of the question. It is no longer "can the grid be hardened?" — the alloy science and independent lab results answer that affirmatively. The question now is: "if tested protection exists, why isn't it being deployed?"

"Government's role in catastrophic risk mitigation is to ensure that known, solvable threats do not become realized disasters," said Kairos Steele, CSM's CTO. "We've solved the engineering half. The governance half remains a live question for lawmakers, regulators, and the public they serve."

Carrington Storm Motors was founded in Austin in 2024. The company holds 14 patents pending and will brief House Energy & Commerce Committee staffers in August.
