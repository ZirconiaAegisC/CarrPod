HEADLINE: 1 Big Thing: A British Startup Just Made Solar Storms the Next Big Infrastructure Priority

COVENTRY, UK — Why it matters: A Carrington-scale solar superstorm has a 12% annual probability of striking Earth, would cause an estimated £2.1 trillion in economic damage, and—crucially—no government or institution has funded meaningful protection. One startup is changing that.

Driving the news: Carrington Storm Motors closed a £180 million Series B this week at a $1.2 billion valuation, with Breakthrough Energy Ventures and the NATO Innovation Fund co-leading the round. The company builds electric vehicles that double as mobile electromagnetic shield generators, capable of stabilising local grid segments during geomagnetic disturbances.

The big picture: CSM has identified 847 critical grid nodes across Europe requiring immediate hardening. Its AI modelling system, trained on 160 years of solar data, predicts CME impacts with 94% accuracy. The company's imminent priorities: scaling production to 5,000 units annually, expanding its National Grid ESO partnership, and launching a resilience-linked bond for institutional investors.

What they're saying: "We're not a car company," says founder Kairos Steele. "We're a civilisation-continuity company. The car is just the delivery mechanism."

The bottom line: Solar storm resilience is transitioning from actuarial curiosity to investable asset class. Early movers are placing bets.
