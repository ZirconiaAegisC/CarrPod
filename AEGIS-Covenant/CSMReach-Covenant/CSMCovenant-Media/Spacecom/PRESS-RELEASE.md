HEADLINE: Space Weather Startup Uses DSCOVR Satellite Data to Predict CME Impact Within 400 Milliseconds

COVENTRY, UK — Carrington Storm Motors has integrated real-time telemetry from NOAA's Deep Space Climate Observatory (DSCOVR) satellite into its vehicle fleet, creating what the company claims is the fastest operational space weather response system on Earth.

The architecture works as follows: DSCOVR, stationed at the L1 Lagrange point roughly 1.5 million kilometres from Earth, detects changes in solar wind density and magnetic field orientation. That data is streamed to CSM's ground network and broadcast to every vehicle in the fleet via a LoRaWAN mesh supplemented by satellite downlink. Onboard FPGAs at each vehicle process the data and, if a CME is detected on an Earth-intercept trajectory, activate electromagnetic hardening within 400 milliseconds.

"DSCOVR gives us 15 to 60 minutes of warning," said Dr. Chester Villegas, CSM's chief scientist. "We use every millisecond of that. By the time the CME reaches Earth's magnetosphere, our fleet is already in defence posture."

CSM has proposed integrating its telemetry stream with ESA's planned Vigil mission at L5, which would provide stereoscopic CME tracking and extend warning times to 3-5 days for certain event profiles.
