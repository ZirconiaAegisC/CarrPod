HEADLINE: Earth's Magnetic Shield Weakening Faster Than Expected, Amplifying Solar Storm Risk, Study Finds

COVENTRY, UK — New research published today indicates that Earth's protective magnetosphere has weakened approximately 9% since the 1859 Carrington Event, compounding the risk posed to modern electrical infrastructure by solar superstorms.

The multi-institutional study, led by Carrington Storm Motors in collaboration with the British Geological Survey and the University of Colorado Boulder, analysed 13 years of data from ESA's Swarm satellite constellation alongside historical magnetometer records. The findings suggest that an identical solar event today would produce geomagnetically induced currents 15-20% stronger than those that disrupted Victorian telegraph systems.

"We are observing a compound risk without historical precedent," said Dr. Chester Villegas, the study's lead author. "Our natural electromagnetic shield is degrading at the same time our technological dependence on unprotected electronics is accelerating."

The research team has developed a risk map identifying 847 critical infrastructure nodes across Europe requiring immediate electromagnetic hardening, and is deploying a fleet of protected electric vehicles as mobile grid-stabilisation units at 37 of the most vulnerable sites.
