HEADLINE: The Car That Fights the Sun: Inside the World's First Solar-Storm-Proof Electric Vehicle

COVENTRY, UK — Pop the frunk on a Carrington Storm Motors EV and you won't find a grocery compartment. You'll find a copper-nickel mesh Faraday cage, a silicon-carbide voltage regulator the size of a shoebox, and an onboard computer that's listening—in real time—to a satellite sitting a million miles from Earth.

"We built a car, yes," said Kairos Steele, CSM's founder and a former Royal Engineers officer. "But what we really built is a mobile power station that laughs at geomagnetic storms."

The technology works like this: when NOAA's DSCOVR satellite detects a coronal mass ejection heading toward Earth, CSM's mesh network alerts every vehicle in the fleet. Within 400 milliseconds, onboard systems activate electromagnetic hardening. The vehicles become voltage sinks, absorbing the geomagnetically induced currents that would otherwise flow into transformers, substations, and—eventually—your toaster.

CSM has 47 vehicles deployed in a pilot programme across the UK Midlands. They've weathered two minor geomagnetic storms without a hitch.
