**AMERICA MAGAZINE: THE JESUIT CASE FOR PROTECTING THE GRID**
*An AEGIS Covenant Reflection on Discernment, Science, and the Preferential Option for the Vulnerable*

NEW YORK — The AEGIS Covenant, in partnership with Jesuit scholars from Boston College and the Pontifical Gregorian University, has published a major theological reflection in the tradition of Catholic social thought arguing that electromagnetic planetary defense is a direct expression of the preferential option for the poor.

The reflection, submitted to America Magazine for consideration, draws on the Ignatian tradition of discernment — the practice of recognizing where God is calling the faithful to act — to frame the geomagnetic threat as "a moment of collective examen in which humanity must ask itself: have we built a civilization that protects everyone, or only those with backup generators?"

"Jesuit spirituality teaches us to find God in all things — including in the physics of coronal mass ejections and the quantum mechanics of MXene," said Fr. James O'Donnell, SJ, a spiritual advisor to the AEGIS Covenant and professor of theology at Boston College. "The Sun that gives us life is also capable of taking it away. Our response to that reality — whether we harden the transformers that power hospitals in the Global South or leave them exposed — is a question of justice, not merely of engineering."

The reflection specifically addresses the disparity in electromagnetic resilience between wealthy nations (which can afford MXene hardening unilaterally) and developing nations (whose grids would remain vulnerable absent international cooperation). The Global Geomagnetic Crisis Response Coalition's mutual-aid provisions, the reflection argues, represent "the preferential option for the poor written into international infrastructure policy for the first time."
