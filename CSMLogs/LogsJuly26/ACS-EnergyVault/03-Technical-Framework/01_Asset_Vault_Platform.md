# Asset Vault Own & Operate Platform

## Platform Architecture

The Asset Vault is Energy Vault's institutional co-investment platform designed to own, operate, and optimize utility-scale energy storage assets. Structured as a series of closed-end funds, each Asset Vault vehicle aggregates third-party limited partner (LP) capital with Energy Vault's sponsor co-investment to acquire and operate a diversified portfolio of G-VAULT and B-VAULT assets under long-term offtake contracts.

## Revenue Architecture

The Asset Vault platform generates revenue through a multi-layered structure that decouples Energy Vault's earnings from the cyclicality of project development:

### Layer 1 — Asset Management Fees
- **Annual Management Fee**: 1.0–1.5% of committed capital during investment period; 0.75–1.0% on deployed capital thereafter.
- **Deployment Fees**: One-time acquisition and integration fees (1–2% of transaction value) earned upon financial close of each asset.
- **Estimated Run Rate**: $6–8 million annually during Fund I deployment phase.

### Layer 2 — Operations & Maintenance (O&M)
- Long-term O&M agreements with each Asset Vault portfolio company.
- Revenue linked to availability guarantees and performance-based incentives.
- Recurring, inflation-indexed with annual escalators.
- **Estimated Run Rate**: $15–20 million annually at 1.5 GW deployed.

### Layer 3 — Technology Supply Margin
- Energy Vault sells G-VAULT and B-VAULT equipment to Asset Vault portfolio companies at arm's-length commercial terms.
- Gross margins of 20–30% on equipment sales recognized as revenue at delivery milestones.
- Creates a virtuous cycle: Asset Vault deployment creates technology revenue which funds further product development.

### Layer 4 — Asset-Level Equity Returns
- Energy Vault's sponsor co-investment captures pro-rata share of portfolio cash flows.
- Upon asset stabilization (typically Year 3–4 post-COD), assets may be recapitalized or sold, crystallizing carried interest and sponsor equity returns.
- **Target**: $60 million annual recurring EBITDA from Fund I upon full deployment, scaling to $100–150 million by 2029 across multiple fund vintages.

## Financial Engineering and Capital Recycling

The Asset Vault platform employs a structured capital recycling model:

1. **Acquisition Phase** — Fund deploys capital into greenfield development and construction of storage assets.
2. **Stabilization Phase** — Assets achieve COD and demonstrate 12–24 months of operational history, derisking revenue forecasts.
3. **Harvest Phase** — Stabilized assets are recapitalized (project finance debt introduced) or sold to long-term infrastructure owners (pension funds, infrastructure funds, utilities).
4. **Recycling Phase** — Realized proceeds are returned to LPs and the sponsor, or reinvested into new development projects at higher expected returns.

This model enables Energy Vault to deploy institutional capital at development-stage returns (12–18% IRR) while ultimately holding mature assets financed with low-cost infrastructure debt (5–7% WACC), capturing the spread.

## Fund II — Expansion Plans

With Fund I approaching full deployment, Energy Vault has indicated plans for a successor fund (Fund II) targeting $500–750 million in commitments, with an expanded LP base including sovereign wealth funds and insurance general accounts seeking long-duration, inflation-linked infrastructure cash flows. Fund II is expected to incorporate a higher proportion of G-VAULT gravity assets given the maturation of the EVx 2.0™ technology platform and the Eskom reference project.

## Strategic Significance

The Asset Vault platform represents the single most important strategic initiative in Energy Vault's corporate evolution. It transforms the company's revenue profile from episodic project-based technology sales to a durable, compounding infrastructure platform. At scale, the Asset Vault's recurring fee streams, O&M contracts, and asset-level equity returns are projected to constitute over 60% of total company EBITDA — a structural shift that should command a higher valuation multiple as the market transitions from pricing Energy Vault as a development-stage technology company to valuing it as an infrastructure asset manager with proprietary technology differentiation.
