# Astroscale — Growth Strategy & Market Positioning

## Strategic Framework

Astroscale's growth strategy is built on a **three-horizon model**:

```
Horizon 1 (FY2024–FY2027):    Prove the platform
Horizon 2 (FY2027–FY2030):    Scale the platform
Horizon 3 (FY2030+):          Dominate the market
```

---

## Horizon 1 — Prove the Platform (FY2024–FY2027)

### Objectives
1. Demonstrate RPO to a non-cooperative target (ADRAS-J) ✓
2. Secure the debris capture contract (ADRAS-J2) ✓
3. Launch the commercial EOL service (ELSA-M contract signed) ✓
4. Enter the US defense market (APS-R) ✓
5. Achieve public listing for capital access ✓

### Key Decisions
- **Prioritize government contracts over commercial:** Government agencies
  (JAXA, USSF, ESA) are willing to fund technology development and accept
  higher mission risk. This builds flight heritage that de-risks commercial
  offerings.
- **Single segment reporting:** Avoids segment-level margin scrutiny while
  R&D overshadows revenue. All programs share the same technology platform,
  so cost allocation between segments would be arbitrary.
- **Japan HQ, global execution:** Japanese capital markets and government
  relationships for funding and anchor contracts; UK and US offices for
  commercial and defense market access.

### Milestones Achieved
- ADRAS-J: Successfully approached to within 15m, completed 360° fly-around.
- ADRAS-J2: ¥13.2B contract from JAXA.
- ELSA-M: Eutelsat OneWeb as anchor customer, ESA/UKSA PPP funding.
- TSE Prime Market listing (186A.T) with ¥30.6B May 2026 capital raise.

## Horizon 2 — Scale the Platform (FY2027–FY2030)

### Objectives
1. Execute ADRAS-J2 capture and deorbit successfully.
2. Complete ELSA-M Mission 1 (demonstration) and secure Missions 2–5.
3. Deliver APS-R prototype; secure USSF production contract.
4. Win at least one additional commercial megaconstellation EOL contract
   (Starlink, Kuiper, or Lightspeed).
5. Achieve positive gross margin (FY2028 target).
6. Reach EBITDA breakeven (FY2029 target).

### Growth Levers

#### Lever 1: From Single-Mission to Multi-Mission Servicers
- ADRAS-J2 is a single-target mission. Future servicers (ELSA-M design)
  handle 3–5 targets per launch, dramatically lowering per-target cost.
- **Target cost per removal:** ¥1.5B for Mission 1, declining to ¥0.8B
  by Mission 5 as non-recurring engineering costs amortize and
  manufacturing learning curves apply.

#### Lever 2: Platform Commonality
- All servicer spacecraft share a common bus (power, propulsion, GNC,
  communications). Mission-specific elements (capture mechanism, sensor
  package) account for <30% of spacecraft cost.
- Common bus production volumes enable supplier negotiations, bulk
  procurement discounts, and manufacturing efficiency.

#### Lever 3: Geographic Expansion
- **United States:** APS-R provides a foothold in the US defense market,
  which is expected to be the largest single source of on-orbit servicing
  revenue. A US-based manufacturing capability may be required for
  national security missions.
- **Europe:** ELSA-M and COSMIC establish Astroscale as the default
  provider for European institutional ADR/EOL missions. The Isar Aerospace
  Spectrum launch for ELSA-M builds European industrial participation.
- **Asia-Pacific:** ISSA-J1 (PSLV launch) opens the Indian market — ISRO
  operates one of the world's largest satellite fleets and will eventually
  need debris removal services.

#### Lever 4: Recurring Revenue Model
- The EOL service model (pre-paid over the satellite's operational life)
  creates a **subscription-like revenue stream**.
- Example: If Astroscale signs 500 satellites at ¥50M/year each over a
  5-year satellite life, annual recurring revenue = ¥25B.
- This transforms Astroscale from a project-based contractor into a
  space-infrastructure utility.

## Horizon 3 — Dominate the Market (FY2030+)

### "Commonplace by 2030" Vision

By 2030, Astroscale aims to make on-orbit servicing a routine, commercially
self-sustaining industry where:

- Satellite operators purchase removal services at launch, like insurance.
- Refueling is as routine as an oil change, extending satellite life from
  15 to 25+ years.
- Debris removal missions are regular commercial launches, not one-off
  government demonstrations.
- Astroscale operates a fleet of 10–20 servicer spacecraft, repositioning
  in orbit to service multiple clients.

### Market Position Goals

| Market | Target Share | Revenue Potential |
|--------|:-----------:|------------------:|
| Government ADR (Japan) | 90% | ¥20B/year |
| Government ADR (Global) | 40% | ¥50B/year |
| Commercial EOL | 30% | ¥45B/year |
| Commercial Life Extension | 15% | ¥15B/year |
| Commercial Refueling | 20% | ¥20B/year |
| **Total Target Revenue FY2035** | | **~¥150B/year** |

### Competitive Moats at Maturity

1. **Flight Heritage:** ADRAS-J/J2 flight data cannot be replicated without
   flying missions — 5+ year head start on any competitor.
2. **IP Portfolio:** 80+ patent families creating licensing barriers.
3. **Regulatory Expertise:** Having navigated the OST Article VIII consent
   process for ADRAS-J creates institutional knowledge that new entrants
   must replicate.
4. **Government Relationships:** JAXA, USSF, ESA, and UKSA as reference
   customers create a barrier to switching for follow-on contracts.
5. **Capital Markets Access:** Public listing provides transparent access
   to Japanese and global capital that privately-held competitors lack.

### Risks to the Strategy

- **Execution Failure:** A failed ADRAS-J2 capture would damage credibility
  and potentially trigger contract termination.
- **Competition:** Northrop Grumman (MEV), ClearSpace, and well-funded
  startups (Starfish Space) are pursuing similar markets.
- **Regulatory Delay:** If the OST consent bottleneck is not resolved,
  commercial ADR may remain confined to government-arranged missions.
- **Capital Dependency:** The company requires continued access to capital
  markets. A prolonged downturn in space-sector valuations could constrain
  growth.
