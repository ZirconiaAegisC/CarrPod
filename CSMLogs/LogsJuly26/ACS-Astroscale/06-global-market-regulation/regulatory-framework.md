# Space Debris — Regulatory & Legal Framework

## International Treaties

### Outer Space Treaty (1967) — Article VI & VIII

The foundational space law instrument. Article VI holds states
"internationally responsible for national activities in outer space,"
including activities carried out by non-governmental entities. This means
any commercial debris removal operation requires **authorization and
continuing supervision by the appropriate State Party**.

Article VIII establishes that a State retains "jurisdiction and control"
over objects launched into outer space — including debris. This creates the
paradox at the heart of debris removal: **a servicer cannot legally touch a
debris object without permission from the State that launched it**, even if
that object is defunct and hazardous.

### Liability Convention (1972)

Establishes absolute liability on the launching State for damage caused by
its space objects on the surface of the Earth or to aircraft in flight, and
fault-based liability for damage caused elsewhere (i.e., in orbit).

Implication for debris removal: **if a servicer accidentally fragments a
debris object during a failed capture attempt, the servicer's State may be
liable for the resulting debris cloud.** This creates a significant
regulatory barrier that has slowed commercial ADR activity.

### Registration Convention (1975)

Requires States to maintain a registry of objects launched into outer space.
This is the basis for identifying debris owners, but the register is often
incomplete or outdated for decades-old debris objects.

## National Regulatory Frameworks

### Japan

Japan's **Space Activities Act (2016)** provides the licensing framework
for commercial space activities, including on-orbit servicing. A licensed
Japanese operator must:

- Submit a debris mitigation plan to the Cabinet Office.
- Carry third-party liability insurance (minimum ¥20B).
- Obtain debris owner consent (per Article VIII of the OST).
- Report any anomalies, close approaches, or fragmentations.

JAXA's CRD2 program, executed by Astroscale, operates under a specific
government-to-government framework where JAXA obtains the consent of the
launching State for the target debris object.

### United States

The **FCC Orbital Debris Mitigation Rules (2022)** mandate a 5-year
post-mission disposal requirement for all US-licensed satellites. This is
the primary regulatory driver for the commercial EOL market.

The **Orbital Sustainability (ORBITS) Act (2023)** directs NASA to establish
a demonstration program for active debris removal and authorizes procurement
of commercial ADR services. It also directs the Department of Commerce to
develop standards for space traffic coordination.

The **Mission Authorization framework** under development at the Department
of Commerce will provide a streamlined licensing process for novel space
activities, including on-orbit servicing and debris removal.

### United Kingdom

The **Space Industry Act 2018** enables licensing of commercial debris
removal operations. The UK Civil Aviation Authority (CAA) is the licensing
body. Key requirements include:

- A debris removal license specifying the target object.
- Consent from the launching State (if not the UK).
- An orbital debris mitigation plan.
- Insurance or other financial surety.

### European Union

The EU's **Space Law proposal (2025)** would create a unified regulatory
framework for EU member states, including provisions for on-orbit servicing
and debris removal. A key feature is the proposed **"Zero Debris Charter"**
committing signatories to a net-zero debris generation policy by 2030.

## Open Regulatory Questions

1. **Abandonment & Salvage:** Maritime law has well-developed concepts of
   salvage rights and abandoned vessels. Space law has no equivalent. Can a
   debris object be considered "abandoned" if the launching State has no
   practical ability to remove it?

2. **Consent Bottleneck:** Must a servicer obtain consent from the launching
   State for every piece of debris? If so, does a State have the right to
   unreasonably withhold consent, effectively blocking removal of hazardous
   debris that threatens all space users?

3. **Liability Cap & Indemnification:** Commercial operators will not engage
   in debris removal without a clear liability framework. Governments will
   likely need to provide indemnification or liability caps for debris
   removal operations, similar to nuclear power (Price-Anderson Act) or
   aviation (government-backed terrorism insurance).

4. **Dual-Use Technology:** The RPO technologies used for debris removal are
   indistinguishable from those used for anti-satellite operations. Export
   controls (ITAR in the US, FEFTA in Japan) may restrict international
   commercial debris removal missions.

## Astroscale's Regulatory Exposure

Astroscale benefits from Japan's clear licensing framework and JAXA's
diplomatic engagement to secure debris owner consent. However, expansion
into the US, European, and commercial markets will require navigating
multiple regulatory regimes simultaneously, creating compliance costs and
potential mission delays.
