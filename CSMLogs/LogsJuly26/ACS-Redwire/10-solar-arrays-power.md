# ACS-Redwire-Space-Infrastructure Dossier
## Document 10 — ROSA Solar Arrays and ELSA Power Systems

### 10.1 ROSA Technology Overview

The Roll-Out Solar Array (ROSA) is Redwire's flagship power generation product,
a flexible-blanket photovoltaic system that deploys from a stowed cylindrical
configuration to a tensioned planar array in orbit. Unlike rigid-panel solar
arrays that fold accordion-style and require complex hinge mechanisms, ROSA
uses composite booms that unfurl and tension a flexible photovoltaic blanket,
achieving higher power-to-mass ratios and stowed volumetric efficiency than
conventional designs.

### 10.2 Heritage and Flight Experience

ROSA has accumulated an extensive flight heritage record that establishes it
as one of the most flight-proven flexible solar array systems available:

**International Space Station (2017):** A ROSA technology demonstration unit
was deployed and tested on the ISS, validating deployment dynamics, structural
stability, and power generation in the LEO environment.

**DART Mission (2021):** NASA's Double Asteroid Redirection Test, the world's
first planetary defense mission, flew ROSA arrays as its primary power source.
The arrays operated throughout the spacecraft's 10-month cruise to the Didymos
asteroid system and during the terminal approach and impact sequence, providing
flawless power generation under deep-space thermal and radiation conditions.

**Artemis Program:** ROSA arrays have been selected for power generation on
elements of NASA's Artemis lunar exploration architecture, leveraging the
demonstrated deep-space performance to support cislunar and lunar surface
operations.

**Commercial Applications:** Multiple commercial geostationary communications
satellites have adopted ROSA as their primary solar array, attracted by the
combination of flight heritage, mass efficiency, and competitive pricing
relative to incumbent rigid-panel suppliers.

### 10.3 Technical Specifications

ROSA arrays are offered in multiple standard sizes with power outputs ranging
from several kilowatts to more than 20 kilowatts per wing. The flexible
blanket uses triple-junction solar cells with beginning-of-life efficiency
exceeding 30 percent. The composite boom deployment mechanism achieves full
extension in under one minute with deployment loads an order of magnitude
lower than hinged rigid panels, reducing the risk of deployment anomalies.

Stowed volumetric density of approximately 40 to 60 kilowatts per cubic meter
represents a two- to three-fold improvement over rigid-panel alternatives of
equivalent power. This density advantage is particularly significant for
rideshare missions where payload volume is the binding constraint, and for
high-power spacecraft that would exceed fairing volume limits with rigid
arrays.

### 10.4 ELSA: European Large Solar Array

The European Large Solar Array (ELSA) is a variant of the ROSA technology
optimized for European institutional and commercial missions. ELSA
incorporates European-manufactured solar cells and composite materials,
qualifying the system for ESA and European Union space programs with domestic
content requirements.

Redwire achieved a significant commercial milestone with ELSA in the form of
its first sale to Moog Inc., a leading supplier of spacecraft propulsion and
attitude control subsystems. This sale validates ELSA's competitiveness in
the merchant supplier market, where Redwire sells solar arrays to other
spacecraft prime contractors rather than only for internal use on Redwire-built
satellites. The Moog relationship positions ELSA to capture additional
merchant market share as satellite operators seek dual-source strategies for
critical subsystems.

### 10.5 Competitive Positioning

ROSA and ELSA compete with the incumbent rigid-panel solar array suppliers
including Spectrolab and SolAero, which dominate the geostationary
communications satellite market, and with emerging flexible-array competitors
including the Air Force Research Laboratory's ROSA-derived designs. Redwire's
competitive advantages include demonstrated deep-space heritage, an established
manufacturing supply chain, and integration with Redwire's own spacecraft
platforms, which provides a captive demand base that independent array
suppliers cannot match.

### 10.6 Manufacturing and Supply Chain

Redwire manufactures ROSA arrays at its U.S. facilities, with ELSA production
split between U.S. and European facilities depending on program-specific
content requirements. The company sources solar cells from external suppliers
while retaining in-house capability for composite boom fabrication, blanket
assembly, and system-level integration and testing. This vertical integration
strategy captures a greater share of array value than a pure integrator model.
