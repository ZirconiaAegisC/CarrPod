## 2. Unit Economics

### Equipment Sale Model
Per 40-foot container module (15-25 MWh-thermal, 0.5-2 MW electrical):

| Line Item | Current (2024) | Target (2028) | Target (2032) |
|-----------|---------------|---------------|---------------|
| Revenue (equipment sale) | $600K-1,000K | $400K-700K | $300K-500K |
| Cost of goods sold | $400K-700K | $250K-450K | $180K-320K |
| Gross margin | 25-35% | 35-45% | 40-50% |
| Gross profit per module | $150-350K | $150-320K | $120-250K |

### Energy-as-a-Service (EaaS) Model
For a 100 MWh-thermal system serving an industrial customer under a 20-year Heat Purchase Agreement:

| Parameter | Value |
|-----------|-------|
| System capital cost (installed) | $4-7M |
| Annual electricity cost for charging (at $20/MWh, 365 cycles/year) | ~$800K |
| Annual O&M cost | $80-150K |
| Annual revenue (heat sold at $35-50/MWh-thermal) | $1.0-1.5M |
| Annual gross profit | $50-500K |
| Project IRR (unlevered, 20-year life) | 8-15% |
| Project IRR (levered, 60% debt at 6%) | 12-22% |

The EaaS model generates lower near-term revenue but higher lifetime value and more predictable recurring cash flows.

### Key Profitability Drivers
1. **Electricity price spread**: The difference between charging cost (daytime solar, $15-25/MWh) and the value of delivered heat (displaced natural gas at $5-12/MMBTU plus carbon savings). Wider spreads dramatically improve margins.
2. **TPV cell cost**: The single largest cost reduction lever. Each $1/W reduction in TPV cell cost saves $500K-2M per module (depending on TPV capacity).
3. **Manufacturing scale**: Assembly labor and overhead amortization over higher volumes.
4. **Standardization**: Reduced engineering and installation cost as designs standardize.
5. **Utilization rate**: The number of full charge/discharge cycles per year; higher utilization amortizes capital cost over more delivered energy.

## 4. Path to Profitability

### Breakeven Analysis
Antora's path to profitability depends on revenue model:
- **Equipment sales model**: Breakeven at ~$150-200M annual revenue (assuming ~30% gross margin, $50-60M annual operating expense)
- **EaaS model**: Cash flow breakeven earlier (project-level financing reduces corporate-level capital intensity) but corporate profitability is a function of the aggregate EaaS portfolio scale

### Cash Flow Profile
The thermal battery business exhibits a "J-curve" cash flow profile typical of industrial equipment companies:
1. **Investment phase** (2024-2027): Negative cash flow; R&D and first deployments consume cash
2. **Transition phase** (2027-2029): Cash flow approaches breakeven as initial commercial revenue scales
3. **Growth phase** (2029+): Positive cash flow; each incremental installation is cash-generative; manufacturing scale drives margin improvement

### Financing Requirements
To reach sustained profitability, Antora may require:
- **Series C** (2026-2027): $150-250M for manufacturing scale-up and EaaS project financing
- **Project finance** (2027+): Non-recourse debt for EaaS projects; infrastructure funds and green banks are natural providers
- **DOE LPO** (2026+): Low-cost debt from DOE Loan Programs Office for US manufacturing and deployment
- **Total additional capital to profitability**: $300-600M depending on growth rate and revenue model mix

### Exit Scenarios
**IPO** (2028-2031): Once Antora demonstrates commercial revenue traction (>$100M/year), consistent gross margins, and a clear path to profitability. Public markets provide permanent capital for scaling. Thermal storage and industrial decarbonization are investable themes for ESG and climate-focused public market investors.

**Strategic Acquisition** (anytime): Potential acquirers include:
- Industrial conglomerates expanding into energy transition (Siemens, ABB, GE Vernova, Schneider Electric)
- Energy companies diversifying into decarbonization (utilities, oil majors)
- TPV/optoelectronics companies seeking new markets for III-V semiconductor capabilities

**Remain Private / Profitable**: If the EaaS model generates sufficient cash flow, Antora could remain private indefinitely, financing growth through project finance and reinvested earnings — similar to how independent power producers operate.
