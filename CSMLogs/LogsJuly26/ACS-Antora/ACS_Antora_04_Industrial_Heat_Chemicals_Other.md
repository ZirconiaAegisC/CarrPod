# ACS Antora Energy | Industrial Heat Applications: Chemicals & Other Sectors

## 1. Chemical Industry Application

### Chemical Process Heat Requirements
The chemical industry is the largest industrial energy consumer, with process heat requirements spanning the full temperature range:

| Process | Temperature Required | Current Heat Source | Key Products |
|---------|---------------------|--------------------|-------------|
| Steam cracking (olefins) | 750-900°C (cracking furnace) | Natural gas burners in radiant tubes | Ethylene, propylene (plastics precursors) |
| Steam methane reforming (H₂) | 700-1,000°C | Natural gas combustion + chemical reaction | Hydrogen, ammonia |
| Ammonia synthesis | 400-500°C (reactor) + 150-200 bar | Natural gas (as feedstock and fuel) | Ammonia (fertilizer) |
| Methanol synthesis | 200-300°C (reactor) | Natural gas (as feedstock and fuel) | Methanol |
| Refinery distillation | 300-400°C (crude heating) | Natural gas, refinery fuel gas | Gasoline, diesel, jet fuel |
| Glass melting | 1,400-1,600°C | Natural gas or electric boost | Container glass, flat glass |

### Antora Integration Points in Chemicals

**Steam Cracking**: Ethylene production via steam cracking is the single largest energy consumer in the chemical industry:
- Cracking furnaces operate at 750-900°C with hydrocarbon feedstock passing through radiant tubes heated by natural gas burners
- Antora thermal battery can replace natural gas burners by providing 900°C+ radiant heat to the cracking tubes
- Each cracking furnace requires 50-100 MW-thermal; a large olefins plant may have 10-20 cracking furnaces
- TPV-generated electricity powers feed compressors, product compressors, and separation systems
- CO₂ reduction potential: ~1.5 tons CO₂/ton ethylene produced

**Steam Methane Reforming (SMR) for Hydrogen**:
- Current SMR process: CH₄ + H₂O → CO + 3H₂ (endothermic, requires 700-1,000°C heat from natural gas combustion)
- Antora can provide the high-temperature heat for the reforming reaction without additional natural gas combustion
- However, SMR still uses natural gas as a chemical feedstock (the methane provides the hydrogen atoms); Antora addresses only the combustion fuel portion, not the feedstock
- For true green hydrogen, SMR with carbon capture or electrolysis are the primary pathways; Antora thermal batteries play an enabling role in carbon capture by providing heat for solvent regeneration

**Ammonia Production**:
- Haber-Bosch process requires heat at 400-500°C for the synthesis reactor
- Antora thermal batteries can provide this heat, replacing natural gas burners
- If paired with green hydrogen (from electrolysis), ammonia production can be fully decarbonized

**Refinery Operations**:
- Crude distillation, hydrocracking, catalytic reforming all require process heat in the 300-550°C range
- Antora thermal batteries are well-matched to refinery heat requirements
- TPV electricity can power refinery pumping and compression loads
- Refineries represent a large, concentrated heat demand that favors thermal battery deployment at scale

## 2. Glass Industry Application

### Glass Manufacturing Heat Requirements
Glass production is a continuous, high-temperature process well-suited to thermal battery integration:

| Process Step | Temperature | Current Heat Source |
|-------------|-------------|--------------------|
| Batch melting | 1,400-1,600°C | Natural gas burners + electric boost |
| Refining (fining) | 1,400-1,500°C | Maintained by burners |
| Conditioning (forehearth) | 1,000-1,200°C | Natural gas burners or electric |
| Annealing lehr | 500-600°C → ambient | Natural gas or electric |

Glass furnaces operate continuously for 10-15 years between rebuilds. The continuous nature of glass production means thermal batteries must provide uninterrupted heat, requiring careful sizing of storage capacity vs. charging cycles.

**Antora Integration**:
- High-temperature heat for melting and refining stages
- Electric boost (typically 10-30% of furnace energy) can be supplied by TPV electricity
- Annealing lehr heat from lower-temperature exhaust
- Glass industry CO₂ emissions: ~0.5-0.8 tons CO₂/ton of glass; fuel switching with thermal batteries can eliminate fuel combustion CO₂

## 3. Food and Beverage Industry

### Lower-Temperature Applications
The food and beverage industry requires process heat primarily in the low-to-medium temperature range, which Antora's system can serve efficiently:

| Process | Temperature | Function |
|---------|------------|---------|
| Pasteurization | 72-95°C | Microbial inactivation in milk, juices, beer |
| Sterilization (UHT) | 135-150°C | Extended shelf life products |
| Drying (spray drying) | 150-250°C (inlet air) | Powdered milk, coffee, ingredients |
| Cooking/Baking | 100-250°C | Various food products |
| Cleaning/CIP (Clean-in-Place) | 60-85°C | Equipment sanitation |
| Evaporation/Concentration | 60-120°C | Juice concentrate, dairy products |

**Antora Integration**:
- Thermal battery provides hot water, steam, or hot air at required process temperatures
- TPV electricity powers motors, refrigeration, compressed air, and facility loads
- Food industry is an attractive early adopter market due to:
  - Lower temperature requirements (proven technology)
  - Strong corporate sustainability commitments from major food companies
  - Predictable, continuous process heat demand
  - Lower capital intensity per installation vs. steel/cement/chemicals

## 4. Pulp and Paper Industry

### Process Heat Requirements
The pulp and paper industry is a major industrial heat consumer:

| Process | Temperature | Application |
|---------|------------|------------|
| Chemical pulping (Kraft process) | 160-180°C (digester) | Wood chip delignification |
| Black liquor evaporation | 120-150°C | Recovery of pulping chemicals |
| Paper drying | 120-180°C (dryer cylinders) | Paper web drying (largest energy consumer) |
| Lime kiln (chemical recovery) | 900-1,000°C | CaCO₃ calcination to regenerate CaO |

**Antora Integration**:
- Steam generation for paper drying (the dominant energy use)
- Lime kiln heat from high-temperature thermal battery output
- TPV electricity for mill drives and auxiliaries
- Pulp and paper mills often have on-site biomass boilers; Antora can complement or replace these with carbon-free heat

## 5. District Heating and Commercial Buildings

### Lower-Temperature Opportunity
While Antora's technology is optimized for high-temperature industrial applications, the thermal battery can also serve district heating and large commercial building applications:

- **District heating networks**: Hot water at 80-120°C for residential and commercial space heating
- **Hospital and university campuses**: Central steam plants can integrate thermal batteries for combined heat and power
- **Data centers**: Server waste heat can be upgraded (via heat pump) and stored in thermal batteries for campus heating or re-used for absorption chilling

These lower-temperature applications face more competition from heat pumps and conventional thermal storage (water tanks, phase-change materials), but Antora's combined heat and power capability provides differentiation.

## 7. Techno-Economic Competitiveness by Sector

The economic viability of Antora's thermal battery varies significantly by sector, driven primarily by the cost of the incumbent fuel and the applicable carbon pricing:

### Levelized Cost of Heat (LCOH) Comparison
| Sector | Incumbent Fuel Cost ($/MWh-thermal) | Antora LCOH* ($/MWh-thermal) | Premium (Discount) |
|--------|------------------------------------|------------------------------|---------------------|
| Steel (US, natural gas) | $15-20 | $25-40 | +$10-20 |
| Steel (EU, natural gas + carbon) | $40-60 | $25-40 | -$15-20 (Antora cheaper) |
| Cement (US, coal/petcoke) | $8-12 | $25-40 | +$13-28 |
| Chemicals (US, natural gas) | $15-20 | $25-40 | +$10-20 |
| Chemicals (EU) | $40-60 | $25-40 | -$15-20 |
| Food (US, natural gas) | $15-25 | $30-50 | +$15-25 |

*Antora LCOH assumes $20/MWh electricity for charging, 90% thermal efficiency, capital amortization over 20 years at 8% WACC.

**Key insight**: Antora is immediately competitive in jurisdictions with high fossil fuel costs (Europe) or significant carbon pricing. In the US, adoption depends on policy incentives (IRA tax credits, DOE grants) and corporate decarbonization commitments.

This geographic cost disparity explains Antora's initial deployment focus on California, where electricity prices have a high spread between midday (cheap solar) and evening (expensive), and where strong climate policies create compliance-driven demand for industrial decarbonization solutions.
