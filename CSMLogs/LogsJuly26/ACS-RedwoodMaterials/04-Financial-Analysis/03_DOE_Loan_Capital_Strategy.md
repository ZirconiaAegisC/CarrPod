## 10.1 DOE ATVM Loan Program Overview

The Department of Energy's Advanced Technology Vehicles Manufacturing (ATVM) Loan Program, established under Section 136 of the Energy Independence and Security Act of 2007, provides direct loans to eligible manufacturers of advanced technology vehicles and qualifying components. The program was originally capitalized at $25 billion and was subsequently expanded under the Inflation Reduction Act of 2022, which appropriated an additional $3 billion in credit subsidy and removed the previous $25 billion aggregate loan cap.

The ATVM program offers terms significantly more favorable than private credit markets: interest rates at or near US Treasury borrowing costs, maturities of up to 30 years, and construction-period interest capitalization. Loans are direct from the US Treasury, not guaranteed — placing the US government in a senior secured lending position.

## 10.2 Redwood's ATVM Commitment

Redwood Materials received a conditional commitment for a $2 billion ATVM loan in January 2025 following a multi-year application and due diligence process. The commitment was issued during the final weeks of the Biden administration and represents the second-largest ATVM facility awarded to a battery supply chain company (after the Ford-SK On BlueOval SK joint venture's $9.2 billion commitment).

The loan is specifically designated for capital expenditure on manufacturing facilities — predominantly the Sparks, Nevada CAM production expansion and the Charleston, South Carolina greenfield development.

## 10.3 Conditions Precedent & Disbursement Risk

As a conditional commitment, the $2 billion facility remains subject to satisfaction of conditions precedent typical of DOE loan programs, including: final environmental review under the National Environmental Policy Act (NEPA), final engineering and construction contract execution, demonstration of sufficient equity co-investment, and satisfaction of "reasonable prospect of repayment" requirements. DOE ATVM loans have historically experienced extended periods between conditional commitment and first disbursement (12–18 months is typical). As of July 2026 — 18 months post-commitment — finalization status is not publicly confirmed, though no cancellation has been announced.

The change in administration following the 2024 election introduced uncertainty regarding the ATVM program's future. While the Trump administration did not formally terminate the program, it imposed a freeze on new commitments and subjected existing conditional commitments to enhanced review. Redwood's $2 billion commitment, having been issued prior to the transition, falls into a category of pre-existing obligations, though disbursement may face administrative delay.

## 10.4 Capital Strategy & Financing Alternatives

Redwood's capital strategy reflects a deliberate sequencing of financing sources:

1. **Private Equity (Series A–E):** $2.3 billion raised. Equity capital funds early-stage R&D, pilot operations, and initial commercial-scale deployment. Dilution is managed through valuation growth across rounds.

2. **DOE ATVM Debt:** $2.0 billion conditional commitment. Non-dilutive, low-cost, long-tenor debt for capital-intensive manufacturing expansion. Strategic value extends beyond the cost of capital: DOE imprimatur signals federal government validation of the company's technology and business model, reducing perceived risk for commercial offtake partners.

3. **Commercial Project Finance (Anticipated):** As Redwood Energy storage assets reach deployment scale, project-level non-recourse debt financing becomes viable. Infrastructure and energy-focused lenders (including pension funds, insurance companies, and infrastructure debt funds) provide long-tenor, fixed-rate debt against contracted cash flows from power purchase agreements, analogous to renewable energy project finance.

4. **Public Equity (Anticipated):** The recruitment of former Tesla CFO Deepak Ahuja, the Series E round's composition of public-market-oriented investors (Goldman Sachs, T. Rowe Price), and the company's reported $6 billion+ valuation collectively signal preparation for an initial public offering. An IPO would provide liquidity for early investors, an acquisition currency in the form of publicly traded equity, and a permanent capital base.

## 10.5 Comparative Capital Efficiency

Relative to mining-sector comparables, Redwood's capital efficiency is structurally advantaged: the company's "ore body" — end-of-life batteries and manufacturing scrap — is delivered to its facilities at negative or near-zero cost, eliminating the exploration, mine development, and beneficiation capital expenditure that constitutes 60–80% of a mining project's total capital budget. This positions Redwood, at full scale, to produce battery-grade metal chemicals at an estimated capital intensity of $3–$5 per kilogram of annual lithium carbonate equivalent capacity, compared to $8–$15 per kilogram for conventional lithium brine or hard-rock mining projects.
