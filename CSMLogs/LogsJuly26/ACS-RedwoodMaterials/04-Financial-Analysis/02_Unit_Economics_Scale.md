## 9.1 Recycling Unit Economics

The core unit-economic proposition of battery recycling derives from the differential between the cost of acquiring, transporting, and processing end-of-life batteries and the market value of recovered materials. Redwood's vertically integrated model captures margin across the full value chain, from inbound tipping fees through to finished cathode material sales.

**Representative Unit Economics — NCM 811 Battery Pack Processing (per metric ton of black mass):**

| Cost/Revenue Line | Range (USD) | Notes |
|-------------------|-------------|-------|
| Inbound tipping fee (revenue) | $500–$1,500 | Paid by OEM or collector; varies by pack condition |
| Freight & logistics (cost) | ($200–$600) | Distance-dependent; declining with Charleston facility |
| Mechanical processing (cost) | ($300–$500) | Shredding, separation, black mass production |
| Hydrometallurgical refining (cost) | ($1,000–$2,000) | Leaching, SX, precipitation per ton black mass |
| Recovered metal value (revenue) | $3,000–$8,000 | Variable with spot metal prices |
| **Net per metric ton black mass** | **$1,000–$6,000+** | Highly price-dependent |

The wide range in net value reflects commodity price volatility for nickel, cobalt, and lithium — the three metals that collectively constitute 60–80% of recovered material value. Redwood's CAM production capability functions as a structural hedge: converting recovered metals into finished cathode materials captures an additional $3–$8 per kilogram of value-add beyond raw metal salt sales, compressing the effective commodity exposure.

Cost advantages derive from eliminated mining, beneficiation, and trans-oceanic logistics costs, offset partially by collection, discharge, and mechanical processing costs not present in virgin-mined supply chains.

## 9.3 Economies of Scale

Battery recycling and materials production exhibit strong economies of scale deriving from:

**Feedstock Density:** Per-unit logistics cost declines as collection density increases within a geographic region. The Charleston facility is explicitly sited to reduce average freight distances to southeastern gigafactories and Atlantic ports.

**Process Intensification:** Continuous-flow hydrometallurgical reactors exhibit capital cost scaling exponents below 0.7 (i.e., doubling throughput increases capital cost by approximately 60–65%), consistent with chemical process industry benchmarks. Equivalent batch-processing facilities scale closer to linearly.

**Chemical Reagent Recovery:** Solvent extraction and leaching reagents are recovered and recirculated. Recovery rates improve with larger continuous-flow volumes, reducing reagent consumption per unit of metal produced.

**Fixed-Cost Absorption:** The 775-acre aggregate physical footprint, DCS process control system, R&D laboratories, and administrative infrastructure represent largely fixed costs amortizable over expanding throughput volumes. The transition from 20 GWh to 100+ GWh processing capacity at the Sparks facility is expected to reduce per-unit fixed cost allocation by 60–70%.

## 9.4 IRA Section 45X Impact

The Inflation Reduction Act's Section 45X Advanced Manufacturing Production Credit provides:

- **10% of production cost** for electrode active materials (cathode and anode materials) produced in the United States
- **$35 per kWh** for battery cells produced in the United States (flowing through to Redwood's CAM customers, strengthening demand)

For Redwood, at 100 GWh of CAM production capacity, a 10% production cost credit on cathode active material with an estimated production cost of $15–$25/kg translates to $1.50–$2.50/kg in direct tax credit value, or approximately $150–$250 million annually at full capacity utilization (assuming 100,000 metric tons of CAM production). This credit is direct-payable (refundable) and is not subject to the limitations applicable to investment tax credits.

## 9.5 Long-Run Margin Structure

At mature scale (500 GWh CAM production, plus recycling and storage operations), Redwood's consolidated gross margin is projected to stabilize in the 25–35% range, consistent with specialty chemical and advanced materials manufacturing benchmarks. Operating margin will be a function of selling, general, and administrative expense leverage, which at current scale is elevated relative to mature industrial comparables but should compress as revenue scales beyond $1 billion annually. Redwood Energy storage assets, once financed at the project level with non-recourse debt, offer the potential for higher-margin, infrastructure-like returns with contracted, predictable cash flows.
