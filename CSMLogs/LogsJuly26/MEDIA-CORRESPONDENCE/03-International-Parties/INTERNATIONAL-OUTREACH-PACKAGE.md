# INTERNATIONAL OUTREACH PACKAGE
## ACS Congressional Liaison Office — Cross-Border Correspondence Guide
### Prepared: July 19, 2026 | Classification: Business Development — Internal Use

## 1.0 — PACKAGE OVERVIEW

This document provides country-specific guidance for ACS Congressional Liaison
outreach to non-US entities. Each section covers: diplomatic context, cultural
communication norms, key companies in country, relevant bilateral agreements,
US Embassy coordination points, and recommended template adaptations.

## 2.0 — COUNTRY SECTIONS

### 2.1 — NORWAY
**Companies:** Hystar (electrolyzers), Nel ASA (electrolyzers), Equinor (energy)
**Capital:** Oslo
**US Embassy:** Oslo (Amb. Marc Nathanson)
**Bilateral Framework:** US-Norway Science & Technology Agreement; NATO ally;
    Nordic-US Green Shipping Corridor Initiative
**Key Sectors:** Hydrogen, offshore wind, CCS, maritime, oil & gas transition
**Communication Style:** Direct, concise, egalitarian. Norwegians value substance
    over formality. Avoid excessive hierarchy references. Business English widely
    spoken — do not assume need for Norwegian translation.
**Meeting Protocol:** Punctuality expected. Decision-making is consensus-driven.
    Summer holiday (July) is a dead zone — avoid scheduling.
**Template Recommendation:** Template D. Emphasize technology cooperation and
    shared energy transition goals. Reference Norway's hydrogen strategy and
    Equinor's Northern Lights CCS project as context.

### 2.5 — JAPAN
**Companies:** Toyota Motor (automotive), Honda Motor (automotive), Panasonic
    (batteries/electronics), JAXA (Japan Aerospace Exploration Agency)
**Capital:** Tokyo
**US Embassy:** Tokyo (Amb. Rahm Emanuel)
**Bilateral Framework:** US-Japan Security Treaty; US-Japan Clean Energy and
    Energy Security Initiative; US-Japan Competitiveness and Resilience (CoRe)
    Partnership; Quad (US-Japan-Australia-India)
**Key Sectors:** Automotive, batteries, electronics, robotics, aerospace, hydrogen
**Communication Style:** Highly formal, hierarchical, indirect. Use last name +
    "-san" in verbal communication; written correspondence in English is acceptable.
    Meishi (business cards) exchanged with both hands. Avoid direct "no" — listen
    for "it may be difficult" or "we will consider" as refusals.
**Meeting Protocol:** Punctuality absolute. Senior-most person speaks first.
    Decision-making via nemawashi (consensus-building behind the scenes). Expect
    multiple meetings before decisions. Gift-giving customary (quality, not cost).
**Template Recommendation:** Template D. Address senior-most executive. Emphasize
    long-term partnership and mutual respect. Reference US-Japan alliance history.
    Mention specific Japanese government initiatives (Green Growth Strategy, Moonshot).

### 2.7 — TAIWAN
**Companies:** TSMC (semiconductors), ProLogium (solid-state batteries)
**Capital:** Taipei
**US De Facto Embassy:** American Institute in Taiwan (AIT), Taipei (Director
    Sandra Oudkirk)
**Bilateral Framework:** Taiwan Relations Act; US-Taiwan Initiative on 21st-Century
    Trade; US-Taiwan Science and Technology Cooperation Agreement; CHIPS and
    Science Act (TSMC Arizona)
**Key Sectors:** Semiconductors (world leader), electronics, batteries, ICT
**IMPORTANT — Diplomatic Sensitivity:** Taiwan is not a UN member state. The
    United States maintains unofficial relations under the One China Policy.
    Do NOT use "country" — use "economy" or "partner." Do NOT reference
    bilateral "treaty" — use "framework." Correspondence must avoid any
    language implying formal diplomatic recognition.
**Communication Style:** Formal but warm. Mandarin Chinese titles respected.
    Guānxì (relationships/connections) matter. Business entertaining expected
    (banquets, tea). Gift-giving appropriate.
**Meeting Protocol:** Punctuality expected. Senior person deferred to. Consensus
    slower than South Korea but faster than Japan.
**Template Recommendation:** Template D — HEAVILY MODIFIED. Remove all "country"
    references. Use "US-Taiwan technology partnership" framing. Reference
    American Institute in Taiwan, not US Embassy. Use "partner" not "ally."

## 3.0 — DIPLOMATIC CLEARANCE PROTOCOLS

### 3.1 — Standard Outreach
For companies in NATO/close ally countries (Norway, UK, Germany, France, Denmark,
Italy, Netherlands, Sweden, Canada, Australia, South Korea, Japan), Template D
may be used with routine notification to the ACS International Liaison Desk.
No State Department pre-clearance required for initial contact.

### 3.2 — Enhanced Sensitivity
For companies in countries/subjects listed below, advance clearance required:

| Country/Entity | Issue | Clearance Required |
|----------------|-------|--------------------|
| Taiwan (TSMC) | One China Policy sensitivity | State Dept East Asia Bureau |
| ASML (Netherlands) | Export controls / Wassenaar | State Dept + Commerce/BIS |
| Saudi Arabia | CFIUS / Aramco | State Dept Near East Bureau |
| Any defense-adjacent company | ITAR/EAR | DDTC / BIS |

### 3.3 — Prohibited Destinations
No outreach permitted to entities in countries subject to comprehensive US
sanctions (OFAC-administered): Iran, North Korea, Syria, Cuba, and designated
regions of Ukraine (Crimea, Donetsk, Luhansk).

## 4.0 — EMBASSY COORDINATION MATRIX

For all international outreach, notify the relevant US Embassy Commercial
Service or Economic Section 72 hours before first contact. Provide:
- Company name and executive contact
- Template letter to be sent (draft)
- Purpose of engagement (brief paragraph)
- ACS tracking ID

Embassy contacts are maintained in the ACS Secure Directory.
Do not include embassy contact details in this document (unclassified version).
