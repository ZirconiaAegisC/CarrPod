# Competitive Landscape — Electrolyzer Industry Analysis

## Market Structure
The electrolyzer market is fragmented and rapidly evolving. No single company
dominates, and technology choices (PEM, alkaline, SOEC, AEM) create distinct
competitive segments. The market is transitioning from engineer-to-order custom
projects to standardized products — a transition that favors companies with
manufacturing scale and product maturity.

## Direct Competitors (Large-Scale PEM)

### ITM Power (UK, LSE: ITM)
| Parameter              | Detail                              |
|------------------------|-------------------------------------|
| Technology             | PEM electrolysis                    |
| Module Size            | 20 MW (Poseidon platform)           |
| Manufacturing          | Sheffield, UK (1 GW/yr target by 2024) |
| Key Projects           | REFHYNE (10MW, Germany), multiple UK/Europe |
| Key Investors/Partners | Linde, Snam, Sumitomo               |
| Financials             | Publicly traded, revenue positive, cash burn historically high |

ITM Power is EH2's closest competitor in terms of scale and manufacturing
philosophy. Key differences:
- **Smaller module size** (20MW vs 100MW) but with commercial track record
- **European market focus** vs. EH2's US-centric strategy
- **Stronger existing project portfolio** but weaker US presence (IRA benefits)

### Plug Power (US, NASDAQ: PLUG)
| Parameter              | Detail                              |
|------------------------|-------------------------------------|
| Technology             | PEM electrolysis + fuel cells       |
| Module Size            | 5 MW (GenDrive stack)               |
| Manufacturing          | Rochester, NY (Gigafactory); Georgia |
| Key Projects           | Multiple US projects; integrated H₂ ecosystem |
| Key Investors/Partners | Amazon, Walmart, SK Group, Renault   |
| Financials             | Publicly traded, negative EBITDA, cash concerns |

Plug Power is vertically integrated (electrolyzers + fuel cells + hydrogen
production + distribution + fueling), creating a broad but cash-intensive
business model. Smaller module sizes than EH2.

### Cummins / Accelera (US, NYSE: CMI)
| Parameter              | Detail                              |
|------------------------|-------------------------------------|
| Technology             | PEM (via Hydrogenics acquisition)   |
| Module Size            | 20 MW+ (HyLYZER platform)           |
| Manufacturing          | Multiple global facilities          |
| Key Projects           | Douglas County WA (20MW), multiple |

Cummins has the balance sheet and industrial manufacturing expertise but
electrolyzers represent a small portion of its business (diesel engines
dominant). May not prioritize electrolyzers with the same urgency as pure-play
competitors.

## Alkaline Competitors

### Nel Hydrogen (Norway, OSE: NEL)
| Parameter              | Detail                              |
|------------------------|-------------------------------------|
| Technology             | Alkaline (primary) + PEM (emerging) |
| Module Size            | Atmospheric alkaline units          |
| Manufacturing          | Herøya, Norway (500MW/yr expanding to 2GW) |
| Key Projects           | Multiple global, strong EU presence |
| Financials             | Publicly traded, negative EBITDA    |

Nel is the historical market leader in alkaline electrolysis. Key competitive
dynamics vs. EH2:
- **Lower capex but larger footprint** and slower dynamic response
- **Established supply chain** and customer relationships
- **PEM effort appears secondary** to alkaline core business
- **No US manufacturing presence** for IRA benefits

### Thyssenkrupp Nucera (Germany, FWB: NCH2)
| Parameter              | Detail                              |
|------------------------|-------------------------------------|
| Technology             | Alkaline electrolysis               |
| Module Size            | 20 MW modules                       |
| Manufacturing          | Germany                             |
| Key Projects           | NEOM (2.2 GW, with Air Products), multiple |
| Financials             | Publicly traded (IPO 2023)          |

Thyssenkrupp Nucera's 20MW alkaline modules compete on capex but lack the
dynamic response and compact footprint of PEM. The NEOM project is the
largest single electrolyzer order in history.

## Emerging Competitors

### Hysata (Australia, Private)
Hysata has claimed breakthrough efficiency (41.5 kWh/kg, ~95% efficiency) using
capillary-fed alkaline electrolysis. If commercialized at scale, this would
represent a significant competitive threat to PEM on efficiency grounds.
However, Hysata is at a much earlier stage (Series B, ~$100M raised) compared
to EH2.

### Ohmium (US, Private)
Ohmium manufactures smaller (2MW) PEM electrolyzers with a modular approach.
Raised ~$250M. Targets smaller-scale projects and distributed hydrogen production
rather than utility-scale deployments like EH2.

### Verdagy (US, Private)
Verdagy has developed an advanced alkaline electrolyzer with claims of dynamic
operation capability comparable to PEM. Raised ~$100M+. If alkaline can match
PEM's dynamic response while maintaining lower capex, it would challenge PEM's
core advantage.

## EH2's Strategic Advantages
1. **Largest module size (100MW)** — reduces project complexity and balance-of-plant costs
2. **US manufacturing with IRA benefits** — creates cost moat vs. imported electrolyzers
3. **Proprietary MEA with low iridium loading** — addresses critical material constraint
4. **Clean-sheet manufacturing approach** — designed for scale from inception
5. **Strong investor consortium** — BP, Amazon, Microsoft, Temasek provide strategic depth

## Key Risks
- **Technology risk**: First-of-kind 100MW systems may encounter scaling challenges
- **Execution risk**: Simultaneously scaling manufacturing and deploying first projects
- **Competitive catch-up**: ITM Power, Cummins, and others may close the scale gap
- **Technology disruption**: Hysata's efficiency claims or alkaline dynamic improvement could shift competitive dynamics
- **Policy dependency**: IRA changes could erode US manufacturing advantage
