# Boston Metal — Market Impact Analysis

## Global Steel Market Context

The global steel market produced approximately 1.89 billion tonnes of crude steel in 2024, representing a market value of approximately $1.1 trillion at average realized prices. The industry is the largest industrial emitter of CO₂, responsible for approximately 7–9% of global anthropogenic greenhouse gas emissions — roughly 3.6 billion tonnes of CO₂ annually.

Steel demand is projected to grow by approximately 20–30% through 2050, driven by urbanization in developing economies, renewable energy infrastructure buildout (wind turbines, solar mounting structures, transmission towers), and electric vehicle manufacturing. Decarbonizing steel production is therefore not merely a matter of reducing the emissions of existing capacity but also of ensuring that new capacity additions do not lock in decades of high-emissions production.

## Addressable Market for MOE Technology

Boston Metal's MOE technology targets the ironmaking step of steel production, which accounts for approximately 70–80% of the steel industry's total CO₂ emissions. The addressable market can be segmented as follows:

### Primary Steelmaking (Blast Furnace Replacement)
- Approximately 1.3 billion tonnes of steel are currently produced via the blast furnace–basic oxygen furnace route annually
- This represents the largest and most emissions-intensive segment
- MOE directly replaces blast furnaces while integrating with existing downstream BOF or EAF infrastructure

### Greenfield Capacity Additions
- Approximately 200–300 million tonnes of new steelmaking capacity will be built by 2040, predominantly in India, Southeast Asia, and Africa
- These regions typically have abundant solar resources for low-cost renewable electricity, making MOE economically attractive for new builds
- Avoiding carbon lock-in in these growth markets is critical for global emissions trajectories

### Scrap-Based EAF Supplement
- Electric arc furnace (EAF) steelmaking using scrap requires high-quality scrap to avoid tramp element accumulation
- MOE-produced iron can serve as a clean dilution source for scrap-based EAFs, improving product quality while maintaining low emissions
- This is analogous to the current use of DRI/HBI as a scrap supplement

## Competitive Landscape

### Hydrogen-Based Direct Reduction (HYBRIT, H2 Green Steel, etc.)
- **Strengths**: Mature DRI technology base, established EAF integration, strong policy support in Europe
- **Weaknesses**: Requires high-grade DRI pellets (Fe >67%), dependent on green hydrogen availability and cost, limited to regions with both renewable energy and premium ore
- **MOE advantage**: No ore grade requirement, simpler process chemistry, no hydrogen infrastructure needed

### Electra (Low-Temperature Ironmaking)
- **Strengths**: Very low operating temperature (~60°C), modular, potentially very low capital cost
- **Weaknesses**: Early stage (TRL 4–5), requires high-purity iron ore concentrates, acid regeneration loop adds complexity
- **MOE advantage**: Higher TRL, industrial temperature regime is well-understood, higher throughput per cell

### Helios (Sodium-Based Reduction)
- **Strengths**: Uses abundant sodium rather than electricity directly, potential for integrated carbon capture
- **Weaknesses**: Sodium cycle energy penalty, early stage, limited published data
- **MOE advantage**: Established industrial electrolysis precedents (aluminum), higher technology readiness

### Carbon Capture on Blast Furnaces
- **Strengths**: Retrofit existing assets, familiar technology
- **Weaknesses**: Only captures 50–60% of emissions, high energy penalty, does not eliminate coal use
- **MOE advantage**: Eliminates rather than captures emissions, no ongoing capture and sequestration costs

## Steel Decarbonization Regulatory Drivers

### European Union Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM)
The EU CBAM, which entered its transitional phase in October 2023 and will impose full carbon pricing on imported steel from 2026, creates a powerful economic incentive for low-emissions steel production. By 2034, free allowances under the EU Emissions Trading System will be fully phased out for domestic producers, and imported steel will face equivalent carbon costs. MOE-produced steel imported into the EU would face minimal CBAM charges, providing a significant cost advantage versus conventional steel.

### U.S. Federal Buy Clean Initiatives
The Biden administration's Federal Buy Clean Task Force and the Inflation Reduction Act's advanced industrial facilities deployment program (Section 50161, approximately $5.8 billion) provide procurement preferences and direct funding for low-emissions industrial materials, including steel.

### Voluntary Green Steel Premiums
Automotive manufacturers (including Volvo, Mercedes-Benz, and BMW) and consumer goods companies have committed to purchasing green steel at premiums of 20–30% over conventional steel, driven by scope 3 emissions reduction targets. MOE-produced steel would qualify for these premium markets.

## Market Penetration Scenarios

### Conservative Scenario
- First commercial plant 2032, producing 2 million tonnes/year
- Five licensed plants by 2040
- Total MOE steel production: ~10 million tonnes/year by 2040 (approximately 0.5% of global production)
- Emissions reduction: ~18 million tonnes CO₂/year

### Base Case Scenario
- First commercial plant 2030, producing 2 million tonnes/year
- Twenty licensed plants by 2040
- Total MOE steel production: ~40 million tonnes/year by 2040 (approximately 2% of global production)
- Emissions reduction: ~72 million tonnes CO₂/year

### Accelerated Scenario
- First commercial plant 2029
- Rapid adoption driven by CBAM, carbon pricing, and green steel premiums
- Fifty licensed plants by 2040
- Total MOE steel production: ~100 million tonnes/year by 2040 (approximately 5% of global production)
- Emissions reduction: ~180 million tonnes CO₂/year
