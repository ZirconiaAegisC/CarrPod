# Boston Metal — Core Technology Overview

## Molten Oxide Electrolysis (MOE)

Boston Metal's foundational innovation is Molten Oxide Electrolysis, a direct electrification pathway for ironmaking that eliminates the need for blast furnaces, coking coal, and carbon capture infrastructure. The process applies an electric current across an electrolytic cell containing a molten oxide electrolyte — primarily iron oxide dissolved in a mix of silica, lime, and magnesia — maintained at approximately 1600°C.

### Electrochemistry

At the cathode, iron cations accept electrons and reduce to liquid metallic iron, which sinks to the bottom of the cell due to density. At the anode, oxide anions lose electrons and evolve as molecular oxygen gas. The net reaction is the decomposition of iron oxide into elemental iron and oxygen:

    2 Fe₂O₃ → 4 Fe + 3 O₂

This is the same net chemistry as the conventional blast furnace route but achieved without carbon as a reducing agent. The only gaseous byproduct is pure oxygen — no CO, no CO₂, no NOₓ, no SOₓ.

### Inert Anode Technology

A critical differentiator is Boston Metal's proprietary inert anode. Unlike the carbon anodes used in aluminum smelting (Hall-Héroult process), which are consumed and generate CO₂, Boston Metal's anode material is designed to be dimensionally stable and non-consumable under the extreme conditions of molten oxide electrolysis. This anode technology derives from decades of research funded by NASA into lunar oxygen production, where consumable anodes would be impractical. The anode material composition is a closely guarded trade secret, but published literature indicates it is based on chromium-iron alloy compositions with oxide ceramic protective layers.

### Feedstock Flexibility

MOE accepts all grades of iron ore — hematite, magnetite, limonite, and even low-grade ores and steelmaking residues that are uneconomical in conventional routes. This contrasts sharply with hydrogen-based direct reduction (HYBRIT, H2 Green Steel), which requires high-grade DRI pellets with low gangue content. The ability to utilize abundant lower-grade ores dramatically expands the economically viable resource base and reduces upstream beneficiation costs.

### Modular Architecture

Each MOE cell is a self-contained ironmaking unit producing on the order of thousands of tonnes per year. Scaling production is achieved by adding cells in parallel — a modular approach analogous to electrolytic hydrogen stacks or battery gigafactories — rather than building ever-larger blast furnaces. This modularity enables phased capital deployment, standardized manufacturing, and geographic flexibility in plant siting.

### Process Heating

The 1600°C operating temperature is maintained primarily by resistive heating from the current passing through the electrolyte itself. When powered by renewable electricity, the entire process — heating, melting, and electrochemical reduction — produces zero scope 1 or scope 2 greenhouse gas emissions. Total electrical energy consumption is estimated at 3.5–4.0 MWh per tonne of liquid iron produced.

### Byproduct Valorization

The oxygen byproduct stream is high-purity and can be captured for industrial use or sold into merchant oxygen markets. The slag phase, primarily calcium aluminosilicate, has potential as a supplementary cementitious material or aggregate, creating an additional revenue stream and aligning with circular economy principles.

### Emissions Impact

The global steel industry accounts for approximately 7–9% of anthropogenic CO₂ emissions, or roughly 3.6 billion tonnes of CO₂ annually. Each tonne of steel produced via the conventional blast furnace–basic oxygen furnace (BF-BOF) route emits approximately 1.8–2.0 tonnes of CO₂. MOE eliminates roughly 90% of these emissions — approximately 1.6–1.8 tonnes of CO₂ avoided per tonne of steel — when powered by low-carbon electricity.

### Technology Readiness

MOE has progressed from laboratory-scale cells (grams of iron per hour) through bench-scale (kilograms per hour) to semi-industrial pilot operations at Boston Metal's Woburn, Massachusetts facility. The technology is currently at TRL 6–7, with the first commercial-scale demonstration cell targeted for operation in 2026.

## Key Personnel

Tadeu Carneiro, CEO, brings four decades of metals industry experience, including 28 years at the Brazilian metals giant CBMM, where he served as CEO. He also chairs the board of directors at the São Paulo Stock Exchange-listed mining company. Professor Donald Sadoway of MIT, co-founder, originated the molten oxide electrolysis concept through research spanning over twenty years at the Department of Materials Science and Engineering.

## Intellectual Property

Boston Metal holds a substantial patent portfolio covering cell design, anode composition, electrolyte chemistry, and process control systems. The company has filed patents in all major steel-producing jurisdictions, including the United States, European Union, China, Japan, South Korea, Brazil, and India.
