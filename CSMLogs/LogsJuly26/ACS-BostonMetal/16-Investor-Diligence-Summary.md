## Investment Thesis

### The Problem
Steel production accounts for 7–9% of global CO₂ emissions, making it the largest industrial emitter. The chemistry of the dominant production route — blast furnace ironmaking using carbon (coal/coke) as the reducing agent — inherently produces CO₂. Even with complete renewable energy supply to a steel plant, the chemical reduction step would continue to emit ~1.4 tonnes of CO₂ per tonne of iron unless the reducing agent is changed.

### The Solution
Boston Metal replaces carbon-based reduction with electrical reduction via molten oxide electrolysis. This eliminates process CO₂ emissions entirely, producing only iron and oxygen. When powered by low-carbon electricity, the technology can reduce ironmaking emissions by approximately 90%.

### Why Now
Multiple converging trends create favorable conditions for MOE commercialization:
1. Expanding carbon pricing (EU CBAM, EU ETS price increases, expanding compliance markets)
2. Green steel demand from automotive and consumer goods sectors willing to pay premiums
3. Declining renewable electricity costs making electricity-based processes increasingly competitive
4. Policy support for industrial decarbonization (IRA, EU Innovation Fund, federal procurement standards)
5. Blast furnace reline cycle approaching for many assets globally, creating decision points for replacement technology

### Competitive Moats
1. **Ore feedstock flexibility**: No requirement for premium-grade DRI pellets — uses all iron ore grades
2. **Process simplicity**: Single-step ore-to-iron in one reactor versus multi-step hydrogen-based routes
3. **No reagent dependency**: No dependency on green hydrogen availability or cost
4. **Modular scalability**: Incremental capacity addition reduces capital risk
5. **IP portfolio**: Broad patent coverage with early priority dates from MIT research
6. **First-mover advantage**: Highest TRL among direct iron electrolysis companies
7. **Strategic investor base**: ArcelorMittal (world's #2 steelmaker), Microsoft (steel consumer), IFC (developing country deployment)

## Key Risks

### Technology Risk (HIGH)
The transition from pilot-scale validation to commercial-scale deployment introduces risks related to anode lifetime, cell reliability, electrolyte stability, and iron quality at commercial scale that cannot be fully retired until the Brazil demonstration plant is operational.

### Electricity Price Risk (MEDIUM)
MOE economics require electricity costs below approximately $40–50/MWh to be competitive with conventional ironmaking without carbon pricing. This dependency constrains deployment to regions with low-cost renewable electricity, limiting the total addressable market.

### Adoption Timeline Risk (MEDIUM-HIGH)
Steel industry capital cycles are measured in decades. Even with demonstrated technical and economic viability, adoption by risk-averse steel producers may be slower than projected. The licensing model depends on convincing licensees to invest in first-of-a-kind technology.

### Competition Risk (MEDIUM)
Hydrogen-based DRI is further along the commercialization path and benefits from extensive government support in Europe. If green hydrogen costs decline faster than anticipated, hydrogen-DRI could capture market share in regions where MOE would otherwise be competitive.

### Policy Risk (MEDIUM)
Carbon pricing and green procurement policies are subject to political change. A reversal of climate policy ambition in major steel-producing or -consuming countries would reduce the economic incentive for adoption of MOE technology.

- Size of addressable market ($300B+ global ironmaking market)
- Capital efficiency of licensing model versus asset-heavy production model
- Technology readiness relative to competitors
- Strategic value to incumbent steel producers seeking decarbonization pathways

1. **IPO**: Public market listing once commercial-scale validation is achieved and initial licensing revenue is established
2. **Strategic acquisition**: Acquisition by a major steel producer, mining company, or industrial technology conglomerate
3. **Continued private operation**: Long-term private company generating licensing and component revenue

## Diligence Questions for Management

1. What is the current demonstrated anode lifetime at pilot scale, and what degradation mechanisms have been observed? What is the target anode lifetime for commercial cells?

2. What is the measured Faraday efficiency at pilot scale, and how does it vary with current density, electrolyte composition, and ore feedstock?

3. What is the projected levelized cost of iron for the Brazil demonstration plant, and what are the key sensitivities (electricity price, anode life, capacity factor, capital cost)?

4. What is the timeline and budget for the Brazil demonstration plant phases, and what are the key go/no-go decision criteria at each phase?

5. What is the status of discussions with potential licensees, and what terms (licensing fee, royalty rate, exclusivity) are being contemplated?

6. How does Boston Metal plan to manufacture anodes at commercial scale (in-house vs. contract manufacturing), and what is the projected anode cost per tonne of iron produced?

7. What intellectual property protection exists in China and India, the two largest steel-producing countries, and what is the freedom-to-operate assessment?

8. What is the plan for managing the Brazil demonstration plant workforce, including recruitment, training, and knowledge transfer from the Woburn team?

9. How does Boston Metal plan to ensure quality control and technology protection across licensed plants operated by different licensees in different jurisdictions?

10. What are the key lessons learned from pilot-scale operations at Woburn that have informed commercial cell design?
