# ACS OneD Battery Sciences | Patent Portfolio & IP Strategy

## 1. Patent Portfolio Overview

### Scale and Scope
OneD Battery Sciences has built a patent portfolio exceeding 200 granted and pending patents, concentrated in three primary technology families: silicon nanowire growth processes, anode integration methods, and battery manufacturing process control. The portfolio has been assembled over the company's decade-plus history (founded 2013) and includes patents acquired from earlier silicon anode technology companies.

### Geographic Coverage
OneD maintains patent protection in key lithium-ion battery manufacturing jurisdictions:
- **United States**: Core portfolio; all key inventions filed at USPTO
- **China**: Critical coverage given China's dominance in battery manufacturing (~70% of global cell production)
- **South Korea**: Coverage for Korean battery manufacturers (LG Energy Solution, SK On, Samsung SDI)
- **Japan**: Coverage for Japanese battery manufacturers (Panasonic, AESC, Toyota)
- **Europe**: EPO filings covering European automotive manufacturing

## 2. Key Patent Families

### Family A: Nanowire Growth Fundamentals
**Representative Patent**: US 10,xxx,xxx "Method for Growing Silicon Nanowires within Porous Carbon Structures"

This foundational patent family covers the core SINANODE concept: introducing a silicon-containing precursor gas to graphite powder under conditions that cause silicon nanowire nucleation and growth selectively within the graphite pore network. Key claims include:
- Temperature range of 350-500°C for selective pore-filling vs. external deposition
- Silane partial pressure ranges that favor axial nanowire growth over conformal coating
- Catalyst-free growth methods using graphite surface chemistry for nucleation
- Sequential growth protocols (multiple precursor pulses) for controlled loading

**Significance**: This patent family establishes OneD's priority position on the core SINANODE concept. Competitors seeking to grow silicon nanostructures within graphite pores must design around these claims or license the technology.

### Family B: Slurry-Phase Silicon Integration
**Representative Patent**: US 10,xxx,xxx "Integration of Silicon Nanostructure Growth into Electrode Slurry Manufacturing"

This family covers the process integration that distinguishes SINANODE from conventional silicon anode material approaches:
- Introduction of silane gas directly into the anode slurry mixing vessel
- Simultaneous mixing and silicon deposition (one-pot process)
- Control of slurry rheology during and after nanowire growth
- Temperature management during exothermic silane decomposition

**Significance**: These patents protect the specific manufacturing integration that enables the drop-in advantage. Competitors can manufacture silicon anode materials but cannot practice the integrated slurry-phase growth process without a license.

### Family C: Graphite-Silicon Interface Engineering
**Representative Patent**: US 10,xxx,xxx "Engineered Graphite-Silicon Nanowire Interfaces for Lithium-Ion Anodes"

This family addresses the critical interface between silicon nanowires and the graphite host:
- Surface functionalization of graphite pores to promote nanowire adhesion
- Interfacial layers that accommodate differential expansion during cycling
- Nanowire root anchoring structures that prevent detachment
- Graded silicon density within pores to optimize expansion distribution

**Significance**: Interface engineering is the key to cycle life. These patents protect the methods that enable SINANODE anodes to achieve automotive-grade cycle life (>800 cycles) with silicon loadings of 15-30%.

### Family D: Manufacturing Process Control
**Representative Patent**: US 10,xxx,xxx "In-Situ Monitoring and Control System for Silicon Nanostructure Growth in Electrode Manufacturing"

This family covers the process control infrastructure required for production-quality SINANODE manufacturing:
- In-situ silicon loading measurement via gas consumption monitoring
- Real-time temperature mapping and feedback control
- Batch-to-batch consistency algorithms
- Fault detection and process abort protocols

**Significance**: Automotive qualification requires demonstrated process capability (Cpk > 1.67). These patents protect the measurement and control methods that differentiate production-quality SINANODE from laboratory demonstrations.

## 3. IP Strategy Framework

### Defensive Strategy
OneD's patent portfolio serves primarily as a defensive moat: by securing broad claims on pore-level nanowire growth in graphite during slurry mixing, the company blocks competitors from pursuing similar process-integration strategies. This channels the silicon anode industry toward the capital-intensive "build a separate silicon material factory" approach that Sila and Group14 are pursuing.

### Licensing Architecture
OneD's IP strategy is inseparable from its business model. Unlike material companies that use patents to protect manufactured products, OneD's patents protect a process that must be licensed to be commercialized:

1. **Patent License**: Cell manufacturers receive rights to practice SINANODE process
2. **Know-How Transfer**: OneD provides the process specifications, control parameters, and technical support required to implement the process
3. **Ongoing Royalty**: Per-kWh royalty provides recurring revenue aligned with customer production volume
4. **Improvement Rights**: License includes access to OneD's ongoing process improvements and new patent filings

### Freedom-to-Operate Assessment
OneD's patent portfolio is focused on process integration — the intersection of silicon nanowire growth and anode slurry manufacturing. The company does not hold foundational patents on:
- Silicon as an anode material (expired Stanford patents)
- Carbon scaffold manufacturing (Group14 domain)
- Silicon-dominant composite architectures (Sila domain)

This focused IP strategy allows OneD to claim a distinct technical niche without needing to navigate the dense patent thickets around silicon anode material manufacturing.

## 4. Competitive Patent Landscape

### Overlapping Claims and Risk Areas
| Area | OneD Position | Competitor Position | Risk |
|------|-------------|---------------------|------|
| Pore-level silicon growth | Strong patent position | Group14 has carbon scaffold patents | Potential interference |
| Slurry-phase integration | First-to-file advantage | No known competitor filings | Low risk |
| Nanowire VLS growth | Strong position | Amprius has nanowire anode patents | Amprius patents cover electrode-level nanowires, not pore-level |
| Silane CVD process | Good prior art (semiconductor industry) | Broad industry knowledge | Low patentability for basic CVD |
| Electrolyte additives for Si | Growing position | Multiple competitors active | Competitive landscape |

### IP Litigation Risk
The silicon anode patent landscape is increasingly crowded but currently non-litigious. Factors influencing litigation risk:
- Market size is still small (silicon anodes <5% of anode market); litigation typically follows revenue
- Multiple distinct technical approaches reduce direct patent conflicts
- Cross-licensing interest as silicon anode adoption scales

OneD's process-focused IP is less likely to conflict with material-focused patents from Group14 and Sila, reducing litigation exposure relative to competitors with overlapping material claims.

## 5. IP Portfolio Strengths and Gaps

### Strengths
1. Clear technical differentiation from material-based competitors
2. Strong alignment between patent strategy and business model (licensing)
3. Process integration focus creates a distinct patent landscape niche
4. 200+ patent count signals commitment to building a defensible IP position

### Gaps
1. Limited manufacturing equipment patents (silane delivery systems are largely off-the-shelf)
2. No foundational silicon anode material patents (unable to prevent competitors from selling silicon materials)
3. Geographic coverage is thinner in emerging battery manufacturing regions (India, Southeast Asia)
4. Limited electrolyte patent portfolio (cell-level performance optimization largely left to cell manufacturers)
