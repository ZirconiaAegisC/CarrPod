# ACS OneD Battery Sciences | Manufacturing Scale-Up Roadmap

## 1. Scale-Up Philosophy

### The Licensing Scale Model
OneD's manufacturing scale-up is fundamentally different from every other silicon anode company. Rather than building and operating its own factories, OneD scales by deploying SINANODE process capability into existing battery cell manufacturing facilities operated by licensees. This creates a unique set of scale-up challenges and advantages:

**Advantages**:
- Capital expenditure is borne by licensees, not OneD
- Each licensee's factory provides a replication of the scale-up process, enabling parallel deployment across multiple sites
- Process validation occurs in production-representative environments from the start
- No "lab to factory" translation risk: the process is developed on and for production equipment

**Challenges**:
- OneD must support multiple licensees with different equipment, processes, and organizational capabilities
- Process consistency must be maintained across diverse manufacturing environments
- Knowledge transfer and training at scale becomes a critical organizational capability
- OneD's revenue depends on licensee execution, which OneD influences but does not control

### Scale-Up Phases

**Phase 1: Anchor Learning (2022-2026)**
- Focus on GM as the sole deployment partner
- Process parameters optimized for a single set of equipment and graphite specifications
- All process learning concentrated in a controlled development environment
- Key output: Validated, production-ready process specification

**Phase 2: Controlled Replication (2026-2028)**
- First GM commercial production line deployment
- Process specification finalized and documented
- Training program and technical support infrastructure established
- Second licensee (non-GM) begins pilot deployment

**Phase 3: Multi-Site Rollout (2028-2030)**
- SINANODE deployed across multiple GM and non-GM factories
- Standardized equipment packages (silane systems) sourced from qualified suppliers
- Remote monitoring and support capabilities enable efficient multi-site operations
- Silane supply chain scaled to support aggregate demand

**Phase 4: Industry Adoption (2030+)**
- SINANODE recognized as industry-standard process for silicon anode integration
- Equipment suppliers offer pre-engineered SINANODE-compatible mixer systems
- Silane supply chain mature with battery-grade specifications
- OneD's role shifts from active deployment support to technology maintenance and next-generation development

## 2. Equipment and Supply Chain Scale-Up

### Silane System Standardization
As SINANODE moves from single-site pilot to multi-site deployment, equipment standardization becomes critical:

1. **System Design Package**: OneD develops a standardized silane delivery and abatement system design that can be adapted to different mixer configurations
2. **Qualified Equipment Suppliers**: Gas cabinet, piping, and abatement system suppliers qualified to provide SINANODE-compatible systems
3. **Modular Architecture**: Systems designed as pre-fabricated modules that minimize on-site construction time and ensure consistency
4. **Factory Integration Standards**: Interface specifications between SINANODE systems and factory MES, safety systems, and utilities

### Silane Supply Chain Development
The semiconductor and polysilicon industries already consume ~50,000 metric tons of silane annually. Each GWh of SINANODE-enhanced cell production requires approximately 50-80 metric tons of silane:

| Annual Battery Production | Silane Required | Current Global Silane Market |
|--------------------------|-----------------|------------------------------|
| 1 GWh | 50-80 tons | 0.1-0.15% of market |
| 10 GWh | 500-800 tons | 1-1.5% of market |
| 100 GWh | 5,000-8,000 tons | 10-16% of market |
| 500 GWh | 25,000-40,000 tons | 50-80% of market |

At scale (>100 GWh), SINANODE deployment would require significant silane capacity expansion. This is manageable: silane production scales with polysilicon/semiconductor demand, and the solar industry is the primary driver of silane capacity expansion. Battery-grade silane specifications are less stringent than semiconductor-grade, potentially enabling use of lower-cost production routes.

## 3. Knowledge Transfer Infrastructure

### Training Program Architecture
As OneD transitions from direct process operation to licensee support, knowledge transfer becomes a core organizational capability:

1. **Process Documentation**: Detailed process specifications, standard operating procedures, troubleshooting guides, and quality control protocols
2. **Training Curriculum**: Structured training program for licensee engineers and operators covering silane safety, process theory, equipment operation, and quality control
3. **On-Site Support**: OneD process engineers embedded at licensee facilities during initial deployment and ramp-up phases
4. **Remote Support Center**: Centralized engineering support for troubleshooting, process optimization, and continuous improvement

### Quality Management Across Sites
Maintaining process consistency across multiple factories requires:
- Standardized incoming material specifications (graphite, silane, binder)
- Common process capability metrics and statistical process control methods
- Cross-site data sharing for continuous improvement (anonymized if required by licensees)
- Periodic process audits to ensure licensees maintain specified process parameters

## 4. Cost Reduction Trajectory

### Learning Curve Effects
As SINANODE deployment scales across multiple factories:

1. **Equipment Cost**: Standardized silane system designs and competitive supplier base reduce equipment cost per mixer from $2-3M (first deployments) to $1-1.5M (mature deployments)
2. **Silane Cost**: Increased demand drives silane production scale, reducing cost from $20-25/kg to $10-15/kg at >100 GWh aggregate demand
3. **Process Yield**: Learning curve improvement from 90% initial yield to >98% mature yield, reducing silane waste and rework
4. **Royalty Rate**: Per-kWh royalty rates may decline over time as competition intensifies, offset by higher volumes

### Target Cost Roadmap
| Timeline | Silane Cost | Equipment Cost/GWh | Royalty/GWh | Total Incremental Cost/kWh |
|----------|------------|--------------------|------------|---------------------------|
| 2026 (First commercial) | $20-25/kg | $0.5-1.0 | $3-5 | $5-8/kWh |
| 2028 (Multi-site) | $15-20/kg | $0.3-0.7 | $2-4 | $4-6/kWh |
| 2030 (Scaled) | $10-15/kg | $0.2-0.5 | $1-3 | $3-5/kWh |

## 5. Capacity Expansion Scenarios

### Conservative Scenario
- GM deployment only through 2028
- One additional licensee by 2029
- Total deployed capacity: 50-100 GWh by 2030
- OneD revenue: $50-150M/year

### Base Case Scenario
- GM commercial deployment 2026-2027
- 2-3 additional licensees by 2028
- Total deployed capacity: 100-200 GWh by 2030
- OneD revenue: $150-400M/year

### Accelerated Scenario
- GM commercial deployment 2026
- 5+ licensees by 2028 (including Korean cell manufacturers)
- Total deployed capacity: 200-400 GWh by 2030
- OneD revenue: $400-800M/year

The key variable separating scenarios is the speed at which non-GM automakers commit to SINANODE deployment. GM's public success with SINANODE in production vehicles is the critical catalyst for accelerated adoption.
