# Manufacturing Operations and Financial Outlook

## Manufacturing Operations

### Production Facility — South Burlington, Vermont
Beta Technologies operates a 180,000 square foot production center at its headquarters campus
in South Burlington, Vermont. The facility is designed for scalable electric aircraft
manufacturing, incorporating composite fabrication, systems integration, final assembly,
and flight test delivery functions under one roof.

### Production Capacity and Scaling

| Year | Planned Aircraft Deliveries | Production Lines |
|------|----------------------------|------------------|
| 2026 | 20 aircraft | 2 lines |
| 2027 | 80 aircraft | 2+ lines (expanded) |
| 2028+ | Not publicly guided | Scaling with demand and certification progress |

### Manufacturing Processes

#### Composite Airframe Fabrication
- Automated fiber placement (AFP) and automated tape laying (ATL) for large composite structures
- Out-of-autoclave (OOA) curing processes for production throughput
- Non-destructive inspection (NDI) — ultrasonic, thermographic, and X-ray CT
- Shared tooling between CX300 and A250 airframes (common blended-wing-body design)
- In-house composite repair station for production and customer support

#### Propulsion System Production
- H500A electric motor assembly (winding, magnet installation, rotor balancing)
- Power electronics assembly and functional testing
- Propulsion system acceptance testing (ATP) for each production unit
- Dedicated motor production line for Eve Air Mobility supply agreement
- Battery pack assembly from cells (cell sourcing from strategic battery partners)

#### Final Assembly and Delivery
- Moving or pulsed assembly line for aircraft integration
- Systems integration testing (electrical, avionics, flight controls, propulsion)
- Ground runs and functional checks prior to first flight
- Production flight testing (conformity and customer acceptance flights)
- Customer delivery center with pilot and maintenance training facilities

### Supply Chain

Beta's supply chain strategy balances vertical integration (propulsion, batteries, composites)
with strategic outsourcing (avionics, landing gear, interior components, raw materials):

| System | In-House or Sourced |
|--------|-------------------|
| Airframe (Composite Structures) | In-house fabrication |
| H500A Electric Motors | In-house manufacturing |
| Power Electronics | In-house manufacturing |
| Battery Packs | In-house assembly; cells from strategic partners |
| Avionics | Garmin (modified with Beta-specific software) |
| Landing Gear | Tier-1 aerospace supplier |
| Fly-by-Wire Actuators | Tier-1 aerospace supplier |
| Interior / Seats | Tier-1 aerospace supplier |
| Fasteners and Standard Parts | Aerospace distribution |

### Manufacturing Workforce
The Vermont facility employs manufacturing personnel across:
- Composite fabrication and assembly technicians
- Electric motor and power electronics production technicians
- Quality assurance and NDI inspectors
- Supply chain and logistics specialists
- Production engineering and manufacturing technology
- Flight test and delivery pilots and mechanics

## Key Financial Risks

### Certification Timing Risk
- CX300 Part 23 certification targeted H2 2027; any delay defers aircraft delivery revenue
  by a corresponding period, extending cash burn
- A250 VTOL certification (~2028) has greater schedule uncertainty
- H500A engine certification (H1 2026) is the nearest-term binary catalyst

### Production Ramp Risk
- Scaling from 20 aircraft (2026) to 80 (2027) is a 4x increase requiring flawless execution
  across supply chain, manufacturing workforce, and quality systems
- New aerospace manufacturers historically experience production ramp challenges
  (learning curve, supplier quality, rework)

### Market Adoption Risk
- Electric aviation is an unproven market; customer adoption may be slower than backlog implies
- Aircraft financing and insurance markets for electric aircraft are undeveloped
- Infrastructure dependency: customer operations depend on Beta's charging network availability

### Competition Risk
- Multiple well-funded competitors (Joby, Archer, Lilium, Volocopter) targeting overlapping markets
- Established aerospace OEMs (Airbus, Boeing, Embraer, Textron) may enter electric aviation
- Technology risk of battery chemistry breakthroughs by competitors enabling superior range/payload

### Macroeconomic Risk
- Interest rate environment affects customer aircraft financing costs
- Inflation impacts labor, materials, and supplier costs
- Potential recession could defer customer fleet decisions

## Investment Thesis Summary

### Bull Case
- First-mover advantage with H500A engine certification (H1 2026)
- $3.9B order backlog provides revenue visibility through production ramp
- Vertical integration (aircraft + propulsion + charging) creates ecosystem lock-in
- Defense and propulsion supply diversifies revenue beyond commercial aircraft sales
- $1.59B cash provides runway to positive operating cash flow

### Bear Case
- Pre-revenue with significant and continuing cash consumption
- Certification delays are the historical norm in aviation, not the exception
- Production ramp from 20 to 80+ aircraft is unproven for any electric aviation company
- End-market demand for electric regional aviation remains unvalidated at scale
- Competition from well-capitalized incumbents and startups may compress margins
