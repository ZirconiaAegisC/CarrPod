# Capital History: Two Decades of Fusion Investment at TAE Technologies

## Early-Stage Funding (1998-2010)

TAE Technologies was founded in 1998 with initial seed capital from a small
group of venture investors who recognized the potential of the beam-driven
FRC approach to fusion energy. The company's first decade was characterized
by modest capital requirements as the team developed theoretical foundations,
computational models, and the initial experimental devices — C-1 and early
C-2 configurations — at relatively small physical scale.

The early investor base reflected the long-term, high-risk nature of fusion
energy development. Rather than traditional venture capital firms with
five-to-seven-year exit horizons, TAE attracted family offices,
high-net-worth individuals, and institutional investors with extended
investment timeframes and a willingness to accept technology risk in
exchange for the potentially transformative returns of successful energy
commercialization.

## Institutional Scaling (2010-2020)

The 2010 to 2020 period saw TAE transition from early-stage experimentation
to a systematic development program with corresponding increases in capital
requirements. The construction and operation of C-2U and C-2W required
investments of $40 million to $80 million per device, representing a step
change from the earlier experimental scale. New institutional investors
entered the capitalization during this period, including:

- Goldman Sachs, which led multiple funding rounds and provided strategic
  advisory services
- Venrock, a venture capital firm with a history of deep-technology
  investments
- New Enterprise Associates, one of the world's largest venture capital firms
- The Wellcome Trust, a UK-based charitable foundation with a substantial
  investment portfolio
- Google, which entered a collaborative research partnership with TAE in 2014
  and subsequently participated in funding rounds

By 2020, TAE had raised approximately $700 million in cumulative capital
across eight funding rounds, establishing the company as one of the best-funded
private fusion energy enterprises globally. The investor base had expanded to
include sovereign wealth funds, pension funds, and strategic corporate
investors alongside the original venture and family-office base.

## Pre-Merger Capitalization (2020-2024)

The four years preceding the TMTG merger announcement saw TAE raise an
additional approximately $600 million across three funding rounds, bringing
the cumulative total to over $1.3 billion. These rounds funded the Norm
construction and commissioning program, the initial site selection activities
for Da Vinci, and the continued development of the subsidiary businesses.

The Series I round in 2022, totaling approximately $250 million, represented
the largest single private funding round in fusion energy at that time. The
round attracted new investors from the energy and industrial sectors, reflecting
growing confidence that fusion energy was transitioning from scientific
experiment to commercial development.

## Subsidiary Monetization

In addition to primary equity raises, TAE generated capital through the partial
monetization of its subsidiary businesses. TAE Power Solutions, focused on
energy storage and electric vehicle power management systems, attracted
external investment from strategic partners in the automotive and grid-storage
sectors. TAE Life Sciences, developing accelerator-based boron neutron capture
therapy for cancer treatment, secured dedicated funding from healthcare investors.

These subsidiary monetization events provided non-dilutive capital to the
parent company while validating the commercial potential of technologies
spin out from the fusion program. The subsidiary strategy — leveraging fusion
expertise in particle beams, plasma physics, and advanced power systems to
address nearer-term commercial markets — became a distinctive element of
TAE's overall capitalization approach.

## Comparative Capital Efficiency

Relative to other major fusion energy enterprises, TAE has achieved a
comparatively high capital efficiency as measured by progress toward net
energy per dollar of investment. While direct comparisons are complicated
by differing technology approaches and reporting standards, TAE's cumulative
$1.3 billion investment has produced five device generations culminating in
Norm's demonstrated 70 million degree Celsius steady-state operation and
NBI-only formation capability.

This capital efficiency reflects the inherent scaling advantages of the
linear FRC geometry: each successive device generation could be built at
modest incremental cost by extending the length and increasing beam power,
rather than requiring a step change to a fundamentally larger toroidal
device as tokamak scaling would demand. The modular approach has enabled
TAE to maintain a development cadence of roughly one new device generation
every three to four years, each building directly on its predecessor's
infrastructure.

## Post-Merger Capital Strategy

The TMTG merger transforms TAE's capital access model from periodic private
funding rounds to continuous public-market access. The combined entity's
strategy for funding the Da Vinci construction program — estimated at
$1.5 billion to $2.5 billion total cost — contemplates a mix of:

- Existing cash reserves from the merger proceeds
- Project-level debt financing secured by the plant assets and power purchase
  agreements
- Government loan guarantees through Department of Energy programs for
  advanced nuclear and fusion energy
- Follow-on equity offerings as the development program achieves milestones
  and de-risks the path to commercial operation
