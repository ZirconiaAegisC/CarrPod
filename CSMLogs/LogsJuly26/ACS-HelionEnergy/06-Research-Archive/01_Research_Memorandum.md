# Research Memorandum: Helion Energy Fusion Power Program

**Date:** 15 July 2026
**Classification:** Corporate Dossier — Section 06
**Author:** ACS Research Division

---

## Purpose & Scope

This research memorandum provides a consolidated analytical assessment of Helion Energy's fusion power program, synthesizing technical, commercial, financial, and regulatory data as of July 2026. It is intended to support strategic decision-making regarding investment, partnership, policy engagement, and competitive positioning in the commercial fusion energy sector.

---

## 1. Company Overview

Helion Energy, Inc. (Everett, Washington; founded 2013; CEO David Kirtley) is the highest-valued private fusion company globally at $15.5 billion post-money (Series G, June 2026, $465 million led by Thrive Capital). Total capital raised: $1.5 billion. The company is constructing the world's first commercial fusion power plant (Orion, 50 MW, Malaga, WA) and holds the first fusion power purchase agreement (Microsoft, 2023, 50 MW by 2028 with financial penalties). Helion's technical approach — pulsed field-reversed configuration (FRC) with direct electromagnetic electricity recovery — is unique among major fusion developers in eliminating the steam turbine cycle.

---

## 2. Technical Assessment

### 2.1 Architecture Advantages
- **Direct electricity recovery** bypasses thermal conversion losses and capital costs. This is the single most consequential architectural distinction from all tokamak, stellarator, and laser ICF competitors.
- **Aneutronic fuel cycle (D-He3)** eliminates the tritium breeding blanket requirement and dramatically reduces neutron activation, waste, and materials degradation.
- **Compact plant footprint** enabled by high-beta FRC confinement and the absence of steam plant infrastructure.
- **Seven generations of prototypes** provide experimental depth; Polaris is the first privately funded machine to demonstrate D-T fusion.

### 2.2 Architecture Risks
- **Helium-3 supply** at commercial scale does not exist. This is the most significant unresolved technical-commercial risk.
- **FRC stability** at commercial pulse rates (~1 Hz) and temperatures (~200M°C) has not been demonstrated.
- **Capacitor lifetime** for millions of charge-discharge cycles at 50 MJ scale is unproven.
- **First-of-kind integration risk** for Orion — combining all subsystems at scale for the first time on a compressed schedule.

### 2.3 Assessment
Helion's technical approach is credible, differentiated, and — if successful at commercial scale — structurally advantaged over thermal-conversion fusion architectures. The Polaris prototype has validated core physics (FRC formation, merging, compression, D-T fusion). The remaining technical risk resides primarily in engineering integration, pulse repetition rate scaling, and He3 fuel cycle commercialization.

## 3. Commercial Assessment

### 3.1 Microsoft PPA
The 2023 Microsoft PPA is both Helion's greatest asset and its greatest liability:
- **Asset:** It provides contractual revenue certainty, market validation, and a hard deadline that disciplines execution.
- **Liability:** The 2028 deadline is aggressive for a first-of-kind fusion plant; financial penalties for non-performance could materially impact capital reserves.

### 3.2 Nucor Development Agreement
The 500 MW Nucor agreement validates industrial-scale demand beyond the technology-sector early-adopter market. However, the agreement is a development agreement, not a binding PPA; specific delivery timelines and penalty provisions (if any) have not been publicly disclosed.

### 3.3 Market Timing
Helion benefits from a confluence of favorable market conditions:
- Technology sector demand for 24/7 carbon-free power (Microsoft, Google, Amazon, Meta, Apple)
- Industrial decarbonization mandates creating demand for firm clean power
- Coal and natural gas plant retirements creating baseload capacity gaps
- Limited competition in the "firm clean power" category (advanced fission SMRs, enhanced geothermal, long-duration storage)

### 3.4 Assessment
Helion's commercial positioning is stronger than any fusion competitor. The combination of a binding PPA with a blue-chip counterparty, a plant under construction, and a second large-scale development agreement creates a commercial moat that competitors will require years to replicate.

## 4. Financial Assessment

### 4.1 Valuation
The $15.5 billion Series G valuation reflects:
- Successful Polaris D-T fusion demonstration (2026)
- Washington DOH licensure (June 2026)
- Orion construction progress
- Thrive Capital's lead investment as a signal of institutional conviction

At this valuation, Helion is priced for successful Orion commercial operation and a pathway to serial plant deployment. Downside scenarios — schedule delay, technical setback, or PPA penalty — could result in significant valuation compression.

### 4.2 Capital Requirements
Estimated remaining capital required to reach commercial operation: $500M–$1B. The Series G ($465M) provides approximately 12–24 months of runway at current burn rates. Additional capital raises (equity, debt, project finance, or strategic partnership) are likely required before Orion reaches positive cash flow.

### 4.3 Investor Concentration
Sam Altman's approximately one-third ownership creates both alignment (significant personal stake in success) and concentration risk (influence over governance, potential liquidity events). Altman's March 2026 board resignation while retaining equity introduces an unusual governance dynamic.

### 4.4 Assessment
Helion is adequately capitalized for near-term operations but will require additional funding to complete Orion and initiate Nucor-scale deployment. The valuation is aggressive relative to the pre-revenue stage and execution risks, but defensible given the technical milestones achieved and the unique commercial position.

## 5. Regulatory Assessment

### 5.1 First-Mover Advantage
Helion's June 2026 Washington DOH RML and RAEL licenses are precedential — no other fusion company globally holds commercial fusion facility licenses. This creates:
- A regulatory learning curve that competitors cannot replicate without going through the same process
- Established compliance infrastructure (procedures, personnel, monitoring systems) that can be replicated for subsequent plants
- A reference case for other Agreement States and for NRC federal rulemaking under the ADVANCE Act

### 5.2 Framework Stability
The combination of the ADVANCE Act (2024, federal) and Washington laws HB 1924/HB 1018 (2023/2025, state) provides a stable, fusion-specific regulatory framework that significantly de-risks the investment thesis. The framework is unlikely to be reversed given bipartisan support for fusion energy and the economic development implications.

### 5.3 Assessment
Regulatory risk has been substantially mitigated. The remaining regulatory uncertainty centers on:
- NRC rulemaking timeline under the ADVANCE Act (federal harmonization)
- Potential legal challenges to licensure from environmental groups
- Long-term waste disposition framework for activated fusion components

## 8. Conclusions

1. **Helion Energy is the most advanced private fusion company globally**, measured by technical milestones, commercial traction, regulatory authorization, and construction progress.

2. **The Microsoft PPA creates a binary 2028 inflection point.** On-time delivery establishes Helion as the undisputed first-mover in commercial fusion; delay triggers penalties and reputational consequences.

3. **The D-He3 fuel cycle is the linchpin of the long-term commercial thesis.** Without a viable He3 supply chain, Helion's aneutronic advantage over tokamak competitors diminishes, though the direct electricity recovery advantage persists.

4. **Regulatory first-mover status provides a durable competitive advantage.** The Washington DOH licensure creates a precedential reference case that reduces uncertainty for Helion's subsequent plants while competitors must navigate the licensing process for the first time.

5. **The $15.5 billion valuation prices in substantial execution success.** Investors have underwritten not only Orion commercial operation but a pathway to serial plant deployment and a multi-GW fleet. Execution risk — particularly schedule risk on Orion — is the principal threat to valuation sustainability.

6. **Helion represents a unique concentration of technical capability, commercial validation, and regulatory enablement** in the fusion energy sector. The company's progress over the next 24 months (July 2026–July 2028) will determine whether fusion energy transitions from scientific achievement to commercial reality.
