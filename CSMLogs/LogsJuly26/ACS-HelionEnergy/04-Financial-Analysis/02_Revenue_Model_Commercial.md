# Revenue Model & Commercial Pathway

**Date:** 15 July 2026
**Classification:** Corporate Dossier — Section 04.2

---

## Overview

Helion Energy's revenue model is structured around the sale of electricity generated by fusion power plants, with a distinctive cost structure enabled by the elimination of the steam turbine cycle. The company's first commercial revenue will derive from the Microsoft Power Purchase Agreement (50 MW by 2028). Follow-on revenue pathways include the Nucor 500 MW development agreement and potential plant sales, licensing, or joint ventures for additional commercial deployments.

### Tertiary Revenue Pathways (Speculative)

1. **Plant Sales / Turnkey Delivery:** Helion manufactures and sells complete fusion power plants to utility or industrial customers, with ongoing service and fuel supply contracts.
2. **Technology Licensing:** Helion licenses FRC fusion technology to third-party manufacturers or operators, generating royalty revenue.
3. **Joint Venture Ownership:** Helion co-owns and operates plants with strategic partners (utilities, sovereign wealth funds, industrial conglomerates), sharing capital costs and revenue.
4. **Carbon Credit / Clean Energy Certificate Revenue:** Depending on jurisdictional treatment, fusion electricity may qualify for renewable energy certificates (RECs), carbon offsets, or production tax credits.
5. **Helium-3 Supply:** If Helion develops or controls a He3 production pathway, He3 sales to other fusion operators could become a secondary revenue stream.

## Cost Structure

### Capital Expenditure (Capex)

| Plant Scale | Estimated Capex | Basis |
|-------------|-----------------|-------|
| Orion (50 MW) | $500M–$1.5B | First-of-kind; R&D cost allocation; in-house manufacturing |
| Second Plant (50 MW) | $300M–$800M | Learning curve; standardized design |
| Nth Plant (50 MW) | $200M–$500M | Mature manufacturing; supply chain optimization |
| Nucor Scale (500 MW) | $2B–$5B | Modular scaling; multiple 50 MW units or larger single unit |

**Capex Drivers:**
- Capacitor bank manufacturing (in-house, high-value)
- Fusion core vessel and magnetic compression coils
- High-vacuum pumping systems
- Power electronics for grid interconnection
- Site preparation, construction, and balance of plant
- Tritium handling systems (D-T operations)
- Control systems and diagnostics

**Capex Advantages vs. Fission:**
- No pressure vessel (no ASME Section III nuclear code compliance)
- No emergency core cooling systems
- No spent fuel pool or dry cask storage
- Reduced security requirements (no special nuclear material)
- Smaller exclusion zone / emergency planning zone

### Operating Expenditure (Opex)

| Category | Estimated Annual Cost (50 MW Plant) |
|----------|--------------------------------------|
| Fuel (D-T or D-He3) | $5M–$50M (wide range depending on He3 availability) |
| Operations workforce | $10M–$25M |
| Maintenance & component replacement | $10M–$30M |
| Insurance & regulatory compliance | $5M–$15M |
| Capacitor replacement reserve | $5M–$10M |
| Grid interconnection charges | $1M–$5M |
| Total Estimated Opex | $36M–$135M |

### Levelized Cost of Electricity (LCOE) Estimate

LCOE = (Annualized Capex + Annual Opex + Annual Fuel) / Annual MWh Generated

| Scenario | LCOE Estimate ($/MWh) |
|----------|----------------------|
| First-of-kind (Orion, high capex, D-T fuel) | $150–$300 |
| Nth plant (mature manufacturing, D-He3) | $50–$120 |
| Comparative: New nuclear fission (US) | $120–$180 |
| Comparative: Combined-cycle natural gas (US, without carbon capture) | $40–$70 |
| Comparative: Utility-scale solar + storage (US, 2026) | $45–$90 |
| Comparative: Offshore wind (US, 2026) | $80–$150 |

**LCOE Competitive Position:** At mature manufacturing scale with D-He3 fuel, Helion's fusion electricity could be broadly competitive with other firm clean power sources. The elimination of steam turbine capex and opex is the primary structural cost advantage. The key uncertainty is fuel cost — D-He3 pricing at commercial scale is entirely speculative.

## PPA Pricing Analysis

The Microsoft PPA strike price has not been publicly disclosed. The following factors inform pricing estimates:

**Bear Case Factors (Lower PPA Price):**
- Microsoft's leverage as sole fusion offtaker
- Unproven technology risk premium demanded by offtaker
- Helion's need to demonstrate commercial viability regardless of price

**Bull Case Factors (Higher PPA Price):**
- Scarcity premium for firm, carbon-free, 24/7 power
- Microsoft's carbon-negative commitment creating willingness to pay premium
- Competitive tension if multiple corporate offtakers expressed interest
- Regulatory and reputational value to Microsoft of supporting first fusion plant

**Estimated Range:** $50–$120 per MWh, based on typical corporate renewable PPA pricing plus a technology risk premium.

## Capital Return Profile

For the $1.5 billion invested to date to generate venture-scale returns (3–5x MOIC), Helion must achieve an enterprise value of $4.5B–$7.5B at minimum — a threshold already exceeded at the $15.5B Series G valuation. For later-stage investors (Series E–G at higher valuations), required exit valuations are proportionally higher.

The $15.5B valuation implies investor expectations of:
- Successful Orion operation by 2028–2030
- Multiple follow-on plants (Nucor and beyond)
- Pathway to $1B+ annual revenue within 10 years
- Defensible competitive position enabling premium pricing or market share capture
