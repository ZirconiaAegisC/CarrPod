# Fusion Supply Chain: ARPA-E Program & Industry Development

## ARPA-E Fusion Supply Chain Initiative (April 2026)
In April 2026, the Advanced Research Projects Agency-Energy (ARPA-E) announced $135 million in funding for fusion energy supply chain development. This program recognizes that the transition from fusion science demonstration to commercial deployment requires building entirely new domestic supply chains for fusion-specific components, materials, and fuels.

### Program Objectives
The ARPA-E fusion supply chain program targets five key areas:

1. **Advanced Magnet Technologies**
   - High-temperature superconducting (HTS) tape manufacturing capacity
   - Structural materials for high-field magnets (cryogenic steels, reinforcement)
   - Quench detection and protection systems
   - Cryogenic cooling systems for fusion magnets

2. **Fusion Materials & Components**
   - Plasma-facing materials (tungsten, beryllium, liquid metals)
   - Neutron-resistant structural materials (reduced-activation ferritic/martensitic steels, vanadium alloys, SiC composites)
   - Tritium-compatible seals, gaskets, and valves
   - Breeder blanket materials (lithium ceramics, lead-lithium eutectic)

3. **Power Systems & Pulse Power**
   - High-energy-density capacitor manufacturing
   - High-power solid-state switches (SiC, GaN)
   - Pulsed power supplies and energy storage
   - Grid integration power electronics

4. **Fuel Supply Chain**
   - Deuterium production and purification
   - Helium-3 production and recovery technologies
   - Tritium handling, storage, and accounting systems
   - Isotope separation technologies

5. **Diagnostics & Instrumentation**
   - Fusion-specific neutron detectors and spectrometers
   - High-temperature plasma diagnostics (Thomson scattering, interferometry)
   - Tritium monitoring and accounting systems
   - Real-time control systems for pulsed fusion

### Helion-Specific Relevance
Of these five areas, the most critical for Helion's near-term supply chain are:
- **Area 3 (Pulse Power):** Capacitor manufacturing scale-up and power electronics directly support Orion's pulsed architecture
- **Area 4 (Fuel Supply Chain):** He-3 production technologies and tritium management systems are essential for the D-He3 fuel cycle
- **Area 5 (Diagnostics):** Tritium monitoring and real-time control systems are required for Orion's operational license compliance

## Domestic Supply Chain Gaps

### Critical Materials
| Material | Application | Domestic Supply | Gap |
|----------|------------|----------------|-----|
| Helium-3 | D-He3 fusion fuel | Minimal (stockpile decay only) | Critical — no commercial-scale domestic production |
| Deuterium | Fusion fuel (all concepts) | Adequate (heavy water production) | Low — existing supply chain |
| Tritium | D-T fuel, regulatory compliance | Limited (DOE stockpile only) | High — no commercial production |
| High-Purity Copper | Coil conductors, bus bars | Adequate | Low-Medium |
| Fused Silica (Quartz) | Formation section tubes | Adequate | Low-Medium |
| Dielectric Films | Capacitor manufacturing | Limited (import-dependent) | High |
| Tungsten | Plasma-facing components | Limited (import-dependent) | Medium |
| Beryllium | Plasma-facing components (D-T concepts) | No domestic production | Critical (but less relevant for Helion) |
| SiC Power Semiconductors | Power electronics | Limited (rapidly growing) | Medium |
| Specialty Stainless Steels | Vacuum vessels, structural | Adequate | Low |

### Manufacturing Capacity Gaps
- **Pulsed Capacitors:** No domestic manufacturer produces capacitors at the scale and volume Helion requires. Helion's in-house production is currently the only domestic source.
- **Fusion-Grade Vacuum Systems:** While general vacuum equipment is commercially available, fusion-specific large-scale vacuum systems with tritium compatibility have no established supply chain.
- **Tritium Handling Equipment:** Commercial tritium-compatible pumps, valves, and instrumentation are extremely limited; most existing supply is for CANDU heavy-water reactors and defense applications.

## DOE's Role in Supply Chain Development
Beyond ARPA-E, the Department of Energy supports fusion supply chain development through:

### Fusion Energy Sciences (FES) Program
- **INFUSE Program:** Innovation Network for Fusion Energy — pairs private fusion companies with national laboratory capabilities and expertise
- **MILESTONE Program:** Milestone-based funding for fusion technology development milestones
- **Fusion Prototypic Neutron Source (FPNS):** Planned facility for testing fusion materials under neutron irradiation

### Loan Programs Office (LPO)
The DOE LPO has $40+ billion in loan authority for innovative clean energy projects. The LPO could provide debt financing for:
- First-of-a-kind commercial fusion plants (like Orion's successor)
- Fusion component manufacturing facilities
- Helium-3 production facilities
- Tritium management infrastructure

## Supply Chain Risk Assessment for Orion

### Near-Term (2026-2028)
| Risk | Severity | Mitigation |
|------|----------|-----------|
| Capacitor production capacity | High | In-house manufacturing; modular design allows parallel production |
| He-3 fuel availability | Medium-High | D-D self-breeding pathway; small initial inventory required |
| Tritium-compatible components | Medium | In-house design; limited tritium inventory at Orion scale |
| Power electronics availability | Medium | SiC supply chain is scaling rapidly; commercial alternatives exist |
| Skilled manufacturing workforce | Medium | Workforce development partnerships; competitive compensation |

### Long-Term (2028-2035)
| Risk | Severity | Mitigation |
|------|----------|-----------|
| He-3 supply for multi-plant fleet | High | D-D breeding ratio improvement; tritium decay farming; potential lunar sourcing |
| Capacitor production scaling (10x+) | High | Automated manufacturing; potential external supplier development |
| Copper raw material supply | Medium | Established commodity market; recycling programs |
| Advanced structural materials | Medium | DOE/ARPA-E funded materials development programs |
| Fusion-specific skilled trades | Medium | Community college and trade school partnerships |

## International Supply Chain Considerations
- **Export Controls:** Certain fusion technologies (high-field magnets, tritium handling, pulsed power) may be subject to export controls under ITAR or EAR
- **Foreign Supplier Risk:** Reliance on foreign suppliers for critical materials (rare earths for magnets, specialty alloys, semiconductor materials) creates geopolitical supply chain risk
- **Allied Supply Chains:** The UK, Japan, South Korea, and the EU are developing domestic fusion supply chains; international coordination through the IAEA and bilateral agreements could reduce duplication and accelerate development

## Helion's Supply Chain Strategy
Helion's approach to the fusion supply chain challenge combines:
1. **In-Source Critical Items:** Capacitors, compression coils, formation sections — the items with no existing supply chain — are manufactured in-house
2. **Adapt Commodity Items:** Standard industrial components (vacuum pumps, cryocoolers, power supplies) are sourced commercially and adapted for fusion use
3. **Partner for Specialized Items:** Tritium handling equipment, specialty diagnostics, and grid interconnection hardware are developed with partner companies
4. **Advocate for Public Investment:** Helion actively supports DOE and ARPA-E supply chain programs that benefit the broader fusion ecosystem
