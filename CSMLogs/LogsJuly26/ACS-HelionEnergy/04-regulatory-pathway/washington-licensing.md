# Washington State Fusion Licensing: RML & RAEL

## Historic First: Fusion Licensing in Washington
In June 2026, Helion Energy became the first fusion company in U.S. history to receive both a Radioactive Materials License (RML) and a Radioactive Air Emissions License (RAEL) from the Washington State Department of Health (WA DOH). This milestone represents the culmination of a multi-year effort to establish a state-level regulatory framework for fusion energy distinct from the Nuclear Regulatory Commission's (NRC) fission licensing regime.

## Regulatory Framework Evolution

### HB 1018 (2023)
Washington House Bill 1018, passed in 2023, directed the WA DOH to study and recommend a regulatory framework for fusion energy facilities that acknowledges the fundamental safety differences between fusion and fission. Key provisions:
- Recognized that fusion does not involve a sustained chain reaction and cannot experience a meltdown
- Directed WA DOH to develop fusion-specific licensing criteria
- Instructed the Department of Ecology to develop fusion-specific environmental review guidance
- Established a legislative intent to streamline fusion permitting while maintaining public health and safety

### HB 1924 (2024)
House Bill 1924, signed by Governor Jay Inslee in 2024, enacted the fusion-specific regulatory framework:
- Created separate licensing categories for fusion facilities (distinct from fission reactor licenses)
- Established an agreement state framework for fusion regulation under WA DOH (rather than the NRC)
- Defined fusion facility requirements: radioactive material handling, air emissions, waste management, decommissioning
- Required financial assurance for decommissioning
- Established public participation requirements for fusion facility siting and licensing
- Authorized WA DOH to enter into agreements with the NRC for fusion oversight

## Radioactive Materials License (RML)

### Scope
The RML authorizes Helion to possess, handle, and store radioactive materials at the Orion facility. Fusion-relevant radioactive materials include:
- **Tritium:** Produced as a D-D side-reaction product; beta emitter with 12.3-year half-life
- **Deuterium:** Non-radioactive but regulated as a fusion fuel component
- **Activated Structural Materials:** Neutron activation of fusion core components (copper coils, stainless steel vacuum vessels, etc.)
- **He-3:** Non-radioactive but regulated as a strategic material

### License Conditions (June 2026)
The specific conditions of Helion's RML are not fully public, but typical WA DOH RML requirements include:
- **Inventory Limits:** Maximum tritium inventory on site (grams to tens of grams range, estimated)
- **Containment Systems:** Double containment for tritium storage; continuous air monitoring
- **Personnel Training:** Radiation safety officer (RSO) and worker training requirements
- **Record Keeping:** Detailed inventory tracking, waste shipment manifests, exposure records
- **Emergency Planning:** On-site and off-site emergency response plans
- **Financial Assurance:** Bond or letter of credit for decommissioning and waste disposal
- **Periodic Reporting:** Quarterly or annual reports to WA DOH on inventory, releases, and compliance

### Tritium Management Plan
A critical component of the RML is the tritium management plan:
- **Tritium Accounting:** Real-time tracking of tritium production, inventory, decay, and releases
- **Tritium Recovery:** Systems to capture tritium from vacuum pump exhaust before release
- **Tritium-Compatible Materials:** Material selection to minimize tritium permeation and embrittlement
- **Environmental Monitoring:** Perimeter air sampling, groundwater monitoring wells, soil sampling
- **Release Limits:** Annual tritium release limits (curies per year) consistent with public dose limits (typically <10 mrem/year to the maximally exposed individual)

## Radioactive Air Emissions License (RAEL)

### Scope
The RAEL authorizes Helion to emit trace quantities of radioactive materials to the air from the Orion facility. In a fusion facility, airborne radioactive emissions could include:
- **Tritium:** As tritiated water vapor (HTO) or elemental tritium (HT), released through vacuum system exhaust
- **Activated Particulates:** Neutron-activated dust or erosion products from fusion core components
- **Noble Gases:** Argon-41 (from neutron activation of atmospheric argon) and other activation products

### License Conditions (June 2026)
- **Emission Limits:** Annual and hourly emission rate limits for each radionuclide
- **Stack Monitoring:** Continuous stack monitoring with alarm thresholds
- **Atmospheric Dispersion Modeling:** Computer modeling to demonstrate compliance with dose limits at the site boundary and nearest residence
- **Ambient Air Monitoring:** Perimeter and community air sampling stations
- **Reporting:** Annual emissions reports; immediate notification for exceedances

### Public Dose Standards
WA DOH applies the following dose standards to fusion facilities:
- **Public Dose Limit:** 10 mrem/year to the maximally exposed individual (consistent with NRC standards for fission facilities)
- **ALARA Principle:** Radiation doses must be As Low As Reasonably Achievable, considering economic and social factors
- **Collective Dose:** Total population dose from facility emissions is considered in the licensing decision

## Implications for the Fusion Industry

### Precedent Value
Helion's Washington licenses establish a regulatory precedent for fusion facilities nationwide:
- Demonstrates that fusion facilities can be licensed under a state-level framework (agreement state model)
- Provides a template for other states considering fusion-specific legislation
- Validates the fusion industry's position that fusion should NOT be regulated under the same framework as fission (10 CFR Part 50 / Part 52)

### NRC Coordination
The ADVANCE Act of 2024 directs the NRC to develop a fusion-specific regulatory framework within 3-4 years. Helion's Washington licenses provide a working example for the NRC to reference:
- RML structure and conditions
- RAEL structure and conditions
- Tritium inventory limits and monitoring requirements
- Decommissioning financial assurance approach

### Challenges for Other States
- Not all states have agreement state status with the NRC for radioactive materials regulation
- State-level regulatory capacity for fusion licensing varies significantly
- A patchwork of state regulations could complicate multi-state deployment
- Federal preemption questions remain unresolved (NRC vs. state authority for fusion)
