# Federal Fusion Regulatory Framework: ADVANCE Act & NRC

## Historical Context: Fusion vs. Fission Regulation
Prior to 2024, the U.S. had no dedicated regulatory framework for fusion energy. The Nuclear Regulatory Commission (NRC), established under the Atomic Energy Act of 1954, has jurisdiction over fission reactors, nuclear materials, and byproduct materials. Fusion occupied a regulatory gray zone:
- Not explicitly excluded from NRC jurisdiction
- No licensing pathway existed for commercial fusion facilities
- Uncertainty about whether fusion would be regulated under 10 CFR Part 50 (fission reactor licensing), Part 30 (byproduct materials), or a new framework

The fusion industry, including Helion, Commonwealth Fusion Systems, TAE Technologies, and Zap Energy, advocated for a fusion-specific regulatory framework that recognizes the inherent safety differences between fusion and fission.

## ADVANCE Act of 2024

### Legislative Background
The ADVANCE Act (Accelerating Deployment of Versatile, Advanced Nuclear for Clean Energy), signed into law on July 9, 2024, as part of the Fire Grants and Safety Act (S. 870), is primarily an advanced nuclear (fission) bill. However, it includes specific provisions for fusion energy regulation:

### Key Fusion Provisions
1. **NRC Rulemaking on Fusion:** Directs the NRC to develop and promulgate a regulatory framework for commercial fusion energy systems within 3 to 4 years of enactment (by 2027-2028).
2. **Distinct Framework:** Requires that the fusion framework be separate from the fission licensing framework, reflecting the different risk profiles.
3. **Risk-Informed Approach:** Directs NRC to use a risk-informed, performance-based approach rather than prescriptive requirements.
4. **Byproduct Materials:** Establishes a clear regulatory pathway for fusion byproduct materials (tritium, activated components) under the NRC's existing byproduct materials authority or under agreement state programs.
5. **International Harmonization:** Encourages coordination with international fusion regulatory efforts (UK, Japan, IAEA).
6. **Pre-Application Engagement:** Authorizes the NRC to engage with fusion developers before formal license applications are submitted, enabling iterative regulatory development.

### NRC Rulemaking Content (Expected)
The NRC's fusion rulemaking is expected to address:
- **Licensing Categories:** Different categories for experimental, demonstration, and commercial fusion facilities
- **Safety Analysis:** Required safety analysis content (accident scenarios, source terms, dose calculations)
- **Containment Requirements:** Tritium and activated material containment and monitoring
- **Emergency Planning:** Scaled emergency planning zones (likely much smaller than fission EPZs)
- **Decommissioning Funding:** Financial assurance requirements for decommissioning
- **Environmental Review:** NEPA integration with the licensing process
- **Operational Requirements:** Staffing, training, security, and operational programs
- **Byproduct Material Management:** Tritium accounting, waste classification, disposal pathways

## NRC Jurisdictional Question
A key unresolved issue is whether the NRC has jurisdiction over fusion facilities by default or whether fusion falls outside the NRC's statutory authority. The ADVANCE Act side-stepped this question by directing the NRC to develop a framework rather than explicitly expanding or confirming NRC jurisdiction. The fusion industry generally prefers either:
- **Option A (Preferred by most):** A dedicated fusion regulatory agency or office (similar to the FAA's Office of Commercial Space Transportation for launch vehicles)
- **Option B (Pragmatic):** NRC regulation under a fusion-specific, streamlined Part 53-style framework
- **Option C (Least Preferred):** Regulation under modified fission licensing frameworks

## DOE Role in Fusion Regulation
The Department of Energy has a supporting (non-regulatory) role in fusion deployment:
- **DOE Office of Fusion Energy Sciences (FES):** Funds fusion R&D, operates national laboratories
- **Milestone-Based Fusion Development Program:** Provides funding and technical support to private fusion companies
- **DOE Loan Programs Office (LPO):** Could provide loan guarantees for first-of-a-kind commercial fusion plants
- **DOE Office of Clean Energy Demonstrations (OCED):** Potential funding for fusion demonstration projects

## NEPA Environmental Review

### Environmental Assessment (EA) for Orion
Orion is undergoing a National Environmental Policy Act (NEPA) review:
- **Lead Agency:** U.S. Department of Energy (most likely) or NRC
- **Review Level:** Environmental Assessment (EA) — less intensive than an Environmental Impact Statement (EIS)
- **Timeline:** 12-18 months for an EA (est. H2 2025 to early 2027)
- **Key Issues:** Tritium releases, neutron activation, waste disposal, land use, grid interconnection

### Why EA Instead of EIS?
Fusion's minimal environmental footprint may justify an EA rather than a full EIS:
- No long-lived high-level waste (unlike fission spent fuel)
- No meltdown risk (no critical mass, no chain reaction)
- Minimal thermal discharge (no steam cycle)
- No large-scale fuel supply chain impacts
- Small physical footprint (~30 acres)

## Federal Preemption vs. State Regulation
The Atomic Energy Act generally preempts state regulation of radiological safety for commercial nuclear power plants. However:
- States can regulate non-radiological aspects (siting, zoning, water use)
- Agreement states (including Washington) can regulate certain radioactive materials under agreements with the NRC
- The ADVANCE Act does not explicitly resolve whether fusion falls under federal preemption
- Helion's Washington RML and RAEL operate under the agreement state framework, which could be challenged if the NRC asserts exclusive jurisdiction over commercial fusion

## International Regulatory Context
The U.S. is not alone in developing fusion regulations:
- **United Kingdom:** The UK's Energy Act 2023 established a fusion regulatory framework separate from fission; the Environment Agency and Health and Safety Executive are developing fusion-specific guidance
- **Japan:** Japan's 2023 fusion strategy includes a "regulatory sandbox" approach for early fusion facilities
- **IAEA:** The International Atomic Energy Agency is developing fusion safety standards and guidance documents
- **Canada:** The Canadian Nuclear Safety Commission (CNSC) has initiated a fusion regulatory framework study

## Implications for Helion's Timeline
The federal regulatory situation creates both opportunities and risks for Helion:
- **Opportunity:** The WA state licensing pathway is operational NOW, allowing Orion to proceed while the NRC develops its framework
- **Risk:** Future NRC regulation could impose additional requirements that differ from or exceed Washington's requirements
- **Risk:** Federal preemption litigation could disrupt Helion's state-based licensing approach
- **Opportunity:** Helion's Washington licenses provide practical experience that can inform the NRC's rulemaking
