# Helion Energy: Funding History & Capitalization

## Funding Summary
As of June 2026, Helion Energy has raised approximately $1.5 billion in total funding across seven major rounds, making it one of the most heavily capitalized private fusion companies in the world. The company's $15.5 billion post-money valuation (Series G) places it among the most valuable private energy technology companies globally.

## Funding Rounds

### Seed & Early Rounds (2013-2015)
- **Seed (2013-2014):** Initial founding capital from the founding team and angel investors
- **Series A (2014):** Approximately $3-5 million (estimated); led by Mithril Capital
- **Key Investors:** Mithril Capital, Sam Altman (initial), Capricorn Investment Group

### Series B (2015-2016)
- **Amount:** ~$10-15 million (estimated)
- **Lead:** Not publicly disclosed; Sam Altman increased position
- **Purpose:** Construction of IPA-C and Venti prototypes; FRC compression demonstration

### Series C (2018)
- **Amount:** ~$25-35 million (estimated)
- **Lead:** Not publicly disclosed
- **Purpose:** Grand prototype construction; scaling validation

### Series D (2020)
- **Amount:** ~$40-50 million
- **Lead:** Sam Altman ($50+ million personal investment in this round)
- **Purpose:** Trenta prototype construction; 100 million °C temperature demonstration

### Series E (2021)
- **Amount:** $500 million
- **Lead:** Sam Altman ($375 million of this round)
- **Participants:** Capricorn Investment Group, Mithril Capital, Dustin Moskovitz (Good Ventures)
- **Valuation:** ~$3-4 billion (estimated post-money)
- **Purpose:** Polaris construction; expanded Everett manufacturing facility; Orion site acquisition
- **Significance:** At the time, one of the largest private fusion investments in history

### Series F (2024)
- **Amount:** $425 million
- **Lead:** SoftBank Vision Fund 2
- **Participants:** Lightspeed Venture Partners, Sam Altman (follow-on), existing investors
- **Valuation:** ~$8-10 billion (estimated post-money)
- **Purpose:** Orion construction; capacitor manufacturing scale-up; He-3 supply chain development

### Series G (June 2026)
- **Amount:** $465 million
- **Lead:** Thrive Capital (founded by Josh Kushner)
- **Participants:** SoftBank, Lightspeed, Bill Ford, existing investors
- **Post-Money Valuation:** $15.5 billion
- **Purpose:** Orion construction completion; manufacturing capacity expansion; working capital
- **Significance:** Thrive Capital's first major energy investment; signals crossover investor interest before potential IPO

## Capital Efficiency Comparison
Helion's capital efficiency (valuation per dollar raised) compares favorably with other fusion companies:

| Company | Total Raised | Latest Valuation | Valuation/Raised Ratio |
|---------|-------------|-----------------|----------------------|
| Helion | $1.5B | $15.5B | 10.3x |
| Commonwealth Fusion Systems | $2B+ | $10B+ (est.) | ~5x |
| TAE Technologies | $1.3B+ | $6-8B (est.) | ~5-6x |
| General Fusion | $400M+ | $2-3B (est.) | ~5-7x |
| Zap Energy | $300M+ | $1-2B (est.) | ~3-5x |

Helion's higher ratio may reflect: (a) the Microsoft PPA and Nucor agreement providing revenue visibility; (b) Polaris's 150M°C achievement reducing technology risk; (c) Orion's physical construction reducing execution risk; (d) the Washington regulatory licenses reducing permitting risk.

## Use of Proceeds (Series G, June 2026)
Per Helion's public statements, the Series G capital is allocated approximately as:
- **Orion Construction:** ~40-50% — completion of the Malaga facility, fusion core installation, capacitor bank assembly
- **Manufacturing Scale-Up:** ~20-25% — expansion of in-house capacitor and coil manufacturing capacity
- **Polaris Operations:** ~10-15% — continued testing, diagnostics, and technology validation
- **Fuel Supply Chain:** ~5-10% — He-3 sourcing and tritium management infrastructure
- **Regulatory & Permitting:** ~5% — NEPA compliance, NRC engagement, licensing costs
- **Working Capital:** ~5-10% — general corporate purposes

## Investor Profile: Sam Altman
Sam Altman's investment in Helion is extraordinary by venture capital standards:
- **Total Invested:** $375+ million (personal funds, not through a fund)
- **Ownership:** ~33% (approximately one-third of the company)
- **Investment Span:** 2014-2024 (across multiple rounds)
- **Board Role:** Served on Helion's board from ~2014 to March 2026
- **Public Statements:** Has called Helion "the most important company in the world" and fusion "the only path to abundant, cheap, clean energy"
- **Investment Rationale:** Altman has described his fusion investment as a hedge against AI's future energy demands; he believes exponential AI compute growth requires a similarly exponential energy solution

## Capital Needs Going Forward

### Near-Term (2026-2029)
- **Orion Completion:** $200-400 million (remaining construction costs)
- **Operations Ramp:** $50-100 million/year for Orion operations and maintenance
- **Polaris Operations:** $30-50 million/year for continued R&D
- **Estimated Total:** $500-800 million through commercial operation

### Medium-Term (2029-2035)
- **Second Plant (Nucor or similar):** $1-2 billion for a 500 MW facility
- **Manufacturing Factory:** $200-500 million for dedicated fusion component factory
- **Fuel Infrastructure:** $100-300 million for He-3 production and tritium management
- **Estimated Total:** $2-4 billion for gigawatt-scale deployment

### Exit Considerations
Helion has several potential exit pathways:
1. **IPO:** Given the $15.5B valuation, Helion is IPO-eligible in terms of scale. However, pre-revenue status and technology risk may deter public market investors. An IPO after Orion achieves commercial operation (2029-2030) is the most likely scenario.
2. **Strategic Acquisition:** A major energy or technology company (e.g., Microsoft, an oil major transitioning to clean energy, a utility holding company) could acquire Helion. However, the $15.5B valuation makes acquisition expensive; a strategic partnership or JV is more likely.
3. **Stay Private / Project Finance:** If Orion demonstrates commercial viability, Helion could finance subsequent plants through project finance (debt secured by PPA revenues) rather than equity dilution.

## Financial Risks
- **PPA Penalties:** If Orion does not deliver 50 MW by 2028, Helion will owe financial penalties to Microsoft (amount not disclosed). These penalties would need to be covered by investor capital.
- **Capital Intensity:** Fusion plants are capital-intensive; each 50 MW core is estimated to cost $150-300 million (target). Even with high margins, the upfront capital requirement is substantial.
- **Technology Risk:** If Polaris or Orion encounter a fundamental physics or engineering barrier, the valuation could be impaired.
- **Dilution Risk:** If additional capital raises are required before commercial revenue, existing shareholders (including Sam Altman) face dilution.
- **Regulatory Risk:** Changes in federal or state fusion regulation could impose additional costs or delays.
