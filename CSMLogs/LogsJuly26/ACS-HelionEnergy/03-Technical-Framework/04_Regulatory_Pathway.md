# Regulatory Pathway & Licensure

**Date:** 15 July 2026
**Classification:** Corporate Dossier — Section 03.4

---

## Executive Summary

Helion Energy is the first fusion company to receive state-level radioactive materials and air emissions licenses for a commercial fusion facility. The regulatory framework governing fusion energy in the United States has undergone transformational change between 2023 and 2026, with Helion as both a beneficiary and a driving stakeholder. The ADVANCE Act of 2024 codified a distinct regulatory pathway for fusion at the federal level, while Washington State laws HB 1924 and HB 1018 established the state-level licensing structure. Helion received its Washington Department of Health Radioactive Materials License (RML) and Radiation Air Emissions License (RAEL) in June 2026 — precedential firsts for the global fusion industry.

---

## Federal Framework

### ADVANCE Act of 2024
The Accelerating Deployment of Versatile, Advanced Nuclear for Clean Energy (ADVANCE) Act was signed into law in 2024. Key fusion-relevant provisions:

- **Fusion distinct from fission:** The ADVANCE Act explicitly separates fusion energy regulatory treatment from nuclear fission, recognizing the fundamentally different risk profiles (no chain reaction, no high-level waste, minimal source-term inventory).
- **NRC framework development:** Directs the Nuclear Regulatory Commission to establish a technology-inclusive, risk-informed regulatory framework for fusion systems within a defined timeline.
- **Byproduct material classification:** Establishes that fusion facilities are regulated under a byproduct materials framework (governed by the NRC or Agreement States) rather than under the commercial reactor licensing framework (10 CFR Part 50/52).
- **Agreement State delegation:** Preserves the ability of Agreement States (states that have assumed NRC authority for certain categories of radioactive materials) to regulate fusion facilities under state law — a provision directly applicable to Washington State and Helion's Orion plant.

### ARPA-E Fusion Investment
In April 2026, the Advanced Research Projects Agency–Energy (ARPA-E) allocated $135 million to fusion energy research and commercialization. This federal investment signals:
- Bipartisan recognition of fusion's strategic importance
- A policy environment supportive of public-private partnership models
- Potential for follow-on federal funding mechanisms (loan guarantees, production tax credits, R&D consortia)

## Washington State Framework

### HB 1924 (2023)
Washington House Bill 1924, enacted in 2023, established the statutory foundation for fusion regulation at the state level. Key provisions:
- Authorized the Washington Department of Health to regulate fusion facilities under existing radioactive materials authority
- Established fusion as a distinct category from fission for regulatory purposes
- Directed DOH to develop fusion-specific licensing criteria

### HB 1018 (2025)
Washington House Bill 1018, enacted in 2025, refined and strengthened the fusion regulatory framework:
- Codified specific fusion licensing standards
- Established timelines for DOH review of fusion license applications
- Addressed public participation and environmental review requirements
- Harmonized state requirements with the federal ADVANCE Act framework

### Washington DOH Licensing
The Washington State Department of Health is the primary regulatory authority for Helion's Orion plant. Fusion facilities in Washington are regulated under the state's Agreement State authority, delegated by the NRC.

| License | Abbreviation | Issued | Purpose |
|---------|-------------|--------|---------|
| Radioactive Materials License | RML | June 2026 | Authorization to possess and handle radioactive materials including tritium |
| Radiation Air Emissions License | RAEL | June 2026 | Authorization for controlled airborne emissions of activated materials |

The June 2026 issuance of both licenses represents the first time any fusion company globally has received regulatory authorization for a commercial fusion facility.

## Regulatory Comparison: Fusion vs. Fission

| Aspect | Fission | Fusion (Helion/Orion) |
|--------|---------|----------------------|
| Regulatory Body (Federal) | NRC (10 CFR Part 50/52) | NRC byproduct materials framework (Agreement State-delegable) |
| Regulatory Body (WA) | N/A (no commercial fission in WA) | Washington DOH |
| Fuel Inventory | Years of fissile material (criticality risk) | Minutes of tritium inventory (no criticality pathway) |
| Accident Scenarios | Core damage, melt, large release | Plasma termination; no chain reaction |
| High-Level Waste | Spent nuclear fuel | None (aneutronic D-He3 target) |
| Emergency Planning Zone | 10-mile radius (US) | Site boundary (proposed) |
| Decommissioning Fund | Required (NRC) | Not yet defined for fusion |

The fundamental divergence in regulatory treatment stems from the absence of a self-sustaining chain reaction in fusion systems. In a fusion device, plasma disruption terminates the reaction instantaneously; there is no decay heat removal requirement, no criticality safety concern, and no spent fuel management burden.

## International Context

| Jurisdiction | Regulatory Approach | Status |
|-------------|---------------------|--------|
| United Kingdom | Fusion not regulated as nuclear; regulated by HSE/EA | Established (2021 Green Paper) |
| United States | ADVANCE Act; fusion-specific framework via NRC byproduct materials | Established (2024–2026) |
| Japan | Fusion regulated under existing nuclear framework with adaptations | Under development |
| European Union | Member state discretion; EU-level framework under discussion | Fragmented |
| Canada | CNSC regulating fusion under existing Nuclear Safety Act | Active (General Fusion, BC) |

The UK and US frameworks — both of which separate fusion from fission regulation — are emerging as global standards. Helion's Washington DOH licensure provides the first operational precedent under the US framework.

## Strategic Implications

Helion's regulatory first-mover position confers a durable competitive advantage:

1. **Precedential value of RML/RAEL issuance:** Future applicants in Washington and other Agreement States will encounter an established review standard.
2. **Operational experience under license:** Helion will accumulate data on tritium handling, emissions monitoring, and compliance reporting that will inform the broader regulatory framework — and provide the company with compliance expertise that competitors lack.
3. **Project finance de-risking:** Regulatory licensure is a gating condition for project finance. The June 2026 licenses unlock the next phase of capital deployment for Orion.
4. **First-mover in federal harmonization:** As the NRC develops its fusion framework under the ADVANCE Act, Helion's Washington DOH experience will serve as a de facto reference case, potentially shaping federal rulemaking.
