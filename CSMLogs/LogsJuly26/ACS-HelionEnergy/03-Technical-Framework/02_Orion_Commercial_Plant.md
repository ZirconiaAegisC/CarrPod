# Orion Commercial Plant & Site Development

**Date:** 15 July 2026
**Classification:** Corporate Dossier — Section 03.2

---

## Plant Overview

Orion is Helion Energy's first commercial fusion power plant, under construction in Malaga, Washington, within Chelan County. The plant broke ground in July 2025 and represents the first purpose-built commercial fusion electricity generation facility in the world. Orion is designed to deliver 50 megawatts of net electrical output using Helion's pulsed FRC magnetic compression architecture with direct electricity recovery.

## Plant Architecture

### Generator Building
The Orion generator building houses the fusion core — a scaled derivative of the Polaris FRC prototype. Unlike conventional power plants, Orion does not require:
- Steam generators
- Turbine hall
- Cooling towers (substantially reduced thermal rejection)
- Heat exchangers for primary power conversion
- Fuel handling infrastructure for solid or liquid fuels

The plant's direct electricity recovery architecture dramatically reduces the physical footprint and capital cost compared to thermal-conversion fusion designs.

### Capacitor Bank Infrastructure
Pulsed magnetic compression requires enormous instantaneous power delivery. Orion's capacitor banks — derived from the 50 MJ Polaris system — store energy between pulses and discharge it synchronously with plasma compression. Capacitor manufacturing is performed in-house by Helion, representing a critical proprietary capability.

### Balance of Plant
- **Grid interconnection:** Chelan County PUD transmission lines provide access to the Pacific Northwest grid.
- **Tritium handling:** Required for D-T validation campaigns; tritium systems are licensed under Washington DOH RML.
- **Helium-3 storage:** Long-term requirement for commercial D-He3 operations; initial operations may rely on D-T or D-D.
- **Control room:** Integrated plasma diagnostics, pulse control, and grid synchronization systems.

## Regulatory Status

Helion became the first fusion company to receive Washington Department of Health Radioactive Materials License (RML) and Radiation Air Emissions License (RAEL) in June 2026. These licenses authorize:
- Possession and handling of tritium and other radioactive materials
- Controlled airborne emissions of activated materials
- Operation of fusion devices producing neutron radiation

The Orion plant is being constructed and will be operated under this regulatory framework, established through the ADVANCE Act of 2024 and Washington state laws HB 1924 (2023) and HB 1018 (2025).

## Grid Integration

The transmission interconnection agreement with Chelan County Public Utility District is a critical-path item for Orion. Key considerations include:

- **Power quality:** Pulsed fusion generation produces inherently variable output; power electronics must condition output to grid-standard AC.
- **Startup power:** The compression cycle requires substantial input power; hydroelectric supply from Chelan County PUD provides a clean, stable source.
- **Net energy ratio:** The ratio of gross fusion power to recirculated input power (engineering Q) must substantially exceed unity for net grid delivery.
- **Islanding capability:** The plant must be capable of safe shutdown and isolation during grid disturbances.

## Environmental Considerations

- **Land use:** Compact footprint compared to equivalent-capacity solar, wind, or fossil fuel plants.
- **Water use:** Minimal — no steam cycle eliminates the largest water consumption pathway.
- **Waste:** D-He3 aneutronic reactions produce negligible long-lived radioactive waste. D-T operations generate short-lived activation products in structural materials.
- **Emissions:** No CO2 emissions during operation. RAEL license governs controlled airborne releases.
