# ES-30 Airframe & Aerodynamics

## Configuration

The ES-30 employs a high-wing monoplane configuration with a conventional
empennage and retractable tricycle landing gear. The high-wing layout was
selected for several reasons unique to the electric regional mission:

- **Propeller ground clearance:** Large-diameter propellers driven by the
  electric motors require substantial clearance. A high wing places the
  propeller arcs well above the ground plane even with the landing gear
  extended, reducing foreign object debris ingestion risk.

- **Battery integration:** The wing-fuselage junction and wing spar box
  provide structural volume for battery pack installation close to the
  aircraft center of gravity, minimizing trim changes as battery charge
  depletes during flight.

- **Passenger boarding:** The high wing eliminates overhead-wing emergency
  exits on the fuselage sides, simplifying cabin layout. Boarding doors
  can be placed for optimal jet-bridge or airstair access without
  propeller arc interference.

- **Runway requirement:** The high-lift characteristics of a clean-sheet
  high-wing design contribute to the 1,100-meter runway performance target,
  opening operations at thousands of regional airports.

## Wingspan and Planform

The 32-meter wingspan of the X1 demonstrator represents a substantial
wingspan for the aircraft category. For comparison, the Saab 340B has a
21.4-meter wingspan and the ATR 42-600 has a 24.6-meter wingspan. The larger
span reduces induced drag and improves climb performance on battery power,
where energy conservation is paramount.

The wing planform is optimized for operations at lower altitudes typical of
regional flying (below 20,000 feet), where induced drag constitutes a larger
fraction of total drag. Aspect ratio, taper ratio, and airfoil sections were
selected through computational fluid dynamics optimization specific to the
cruise speeds and altitudes of regional electric operations.

## Structural Materials

Heart Aerospace has pursued a mix of aluminum alloy primary structure and
composite secondary structures. The fuselage uses conventional aluminum
construction with composite fairings, wing-body fairings, and control
surfaces. This material strategy balances:

- **Certification path:** Aluminum primary structure aligns with well-
  established EASA and FAA certification standards and allows the use of
  known fatigue and damage-tolerance analysis methods.

- **Manufacturability:** Aluminum fabrication processes are mature and
  available in the Gothenburg aerospace supply chain, which benefits from
  decades of Saab regional aircraft production heritage.

- **Repairability:** Regional airlines operate in diverse environments with
  varying maintenance capability. Aluminum repair techniques are universally
  understood by maintenance organizations worldwide.

## Distributed Electric Propulsion Aerodynamics

The ES-30's four electric motors are distributed along the wing leading edge,
not clustered on the fuselage centerline as in a conventional twin-turboprop.
This distributed propulsion architecture produces aerodynamic benefits:

- **Propeller-induced lift augmentation:** The accelerated airflow over the
  inboard wing sections from the four propellers increases lift at low
  airspeeds, reducing stall speed and improving takeoff and landing
  performance.

- **Reduced single-engine-out yaw:** With four independent powerplants, the
  loss of any single motor produces a smaller asymmetric thrust moment than
  a conventional twin. This may allow a smaller vertical stabilizer and
  rudder, saving weight and drag.

- **Noise distribution:** Four smaller propellers operating at lower tip
  speeds generate less community noise than two large propellers running at
  higher tip speeds. This is important for airports with strict noise curfews.

## Cabin and Fuselage

The fuselage cross-section was sized for a 2–1 or 2–2 abreast configuration.
The pressurization differential is designed for the 20,000-foot service
ceiling, which is lower and less structurally demanding than the 25,000-foot
ceiling of conventional regional turboprops. This reduces fuselage structural
weight, partially offsetting the weight of the battery system.

The cabin length accommodates 30 seats at a pitch comparable to or exceeding
current regional turboprop standards. A rear lavatory and galley area provide
passenger amenities. The forward cabin section includes a cockpit accessible
from the cabin, with provisions for a single-pilot or two-pilot cockpit
layout depending on the certification path and operator preference.

## Empennage Design

The ES-30 uses a conventional T-tail configuration. The horizontal stabilizer
is mounted at the top of the vertical fin, keeping it clear of propeller
wake from the wing-mounted motors and the rear fuselage turbogenerator
exhaust. The T-tail also provides a longer moment arm for pitch control,
important given the relatively forward battery mass and the potential for
center-of-gravity variation between different payload and hybrid-mode
configurations.
