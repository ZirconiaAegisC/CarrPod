# ES-30 Electric & Hybrid Range Performance

## Battery-Electric Flight Envelope

The ES-30 achieves its foundational performance capability through a
large-capacity battery system integrated into the aircraft structure. In
all-electric mode, the four wing-mounted electric motors draw power exclusively
from the onboard batteries, delivering a certified range of 200 kilometers with
a full payload of 30 passengers. This covers short-haul regional city pairs
without any in-flight emissions.

All-electric missions are the primary use case for routes where ground charging
infrastructure is available at both origin and destination airports. The
30-minute charge time enables rapid turnaround between flights, allowing the
aircraft to operate multiple short legs per day. On a typical eight-hour
operational window, an ES-30 could complete up to six all-electric cycles
assuming 30-minute flights and 30-minute charges.

The 200-kilometer all-electric range places the ES-30 within reach of routes
such as Gothenburg to Oslo (approximately 220 kilometers road distance but
shorter by air), island connections in the Aegean or Caribbean, and short-hop
routes in the United States currently operated by Cessna Caravans and similar
utility aircraft.

## Hybrid Propulsion Mode

When missions exceed the all-electric range, the ES-30 engages its hybrid
propulsion system. Two turbogenerators located in the rear fuselage provide
supplementary electrical power. These generators do not mechanically drive
propellers; they recharge the batteries and supply the electric motors, an
architecture Heart Aerospace terms "independent hybrid propulsion."

The hybrid configuration delivers:

- **400 kilometers with 25 passengers:** The turbogenerators sustain battery
  charge levels during cruise, effectively doubling the all-electric range.
  This covers approximately 75% of all regional routes worldwide.

- **Approximately 300 kilometers with 30 passengers:** Higher payload weight
  increases energy consumption, reducing the effective hybrid range. Still
  covers major regional corridors at full load.

## Energy Management Strategy

Heart Aerospace designed the ES-30's energy management system to optimize
between battery and turbogenerator usage across each flight phase:

- **Taxi and ground operations:** Battery power only. Zero ground emissions.

- **Takeoff and initial climb:** Maximum power from batteries to the four
  electric motors. Turbogenerators remain offline during this phase to
  minimize noise near airports.

- **Climb to cruise altitude:** Batteries continue to provide primary power.
  Turbogenerators start and spool up if the flight plan requires hybrid
  operation.

- **Cruise:** In hybrid mode, turbogenerators supply cruise power requirements
  directly to the motors and trickle-charge the batteries. In all-electric
  mode, batteries sustain cruise at reduced power settings.

- **Descent and landing:** Batteries handle the power demands. Regenerative
  energy capture from propeller windmilling during descent is under research
  but not confirmed for the initial certification configuration.

- **Reserve energy:** The system maintains a 30-minute reserve at all times,
  compliant with IFR reserve requirements for the intended operational category.

## Environmental Performance

The ES-30 delivers a 90% reduction in CO2 emissions when operating in
all-electric mode compared to a conventional turboprop of similar capacity.
In hybrid mode, the reduction is approximately 50% to 70% depending on
mission length and the proportion of the flight spent under battery power.

Beyond CO2, the elimination of leaded aviation fuel (common in piston
aircraft used on similar short routes), elimination of NOx emissions during
all-electric operation, and significant noise reduction from the distributed
electric propulsion architecture all contribute to a substantially smaller
environmental footprint.

## Operational Flexibility

The hybrid architecture provides a critical advantage over purely electric
designs: operational flexibility for airlines. If ground charging infrastructure
is unavailable at a destination, the turbogenerators allow the aircraft to
self-charge during ferry flights or to operate the return leg in hybrid mode.
This eliminates range anxiety and gives operators the confidence to deploy the
aircraft on routes where charging infrastructure may develop over time.

Furthermore, the hybrid capability preserves dispatch reliability in cold
weather conditions where battery performance degrades. The turbogenerators
can compensate for reduced battery output during winter operations, an
important consideration for airlines in northern climates such as Scandinavia,
Canada, and the northern United States.
