# United Airlines Partnership

## Strategic Rationale

United Airlines is the most significant airline partner for Heart
Aerospace, holding dual roles as both the largest commercial customer
(100 firm orders plus 50 options) and a strategic equity investor
through United Airlines Ventures. United's commitment to the ES-30
program is part of a broader corporate strategy to lead the airline
industry's transition to sustainable propulsion technologies.

United operates one of the largest hub-and-spoke networks in the world,
with major hubs at:

- Denver International Airport (DEN)
- Chicago O'Hare International Airport (ORD)
- Houston George Bush Intercontinental Airport (IAH)
- Newark Liberty International Airport (EWR)
- San Francisco International Airport (SFO)
- Washington Dulles International Airport (IAD)
- Los Angeles International Airport (LAX)

Each of these hubs generates a catchment area of small and medium-sized
communities within 200–400 kilometers that are either unserved or
underserved by current regional aircraft types.

## Route Opportunities

### Denver Hub

Denver's Mountain West position makes it the most promising initial ES-30
operating base. Within 400 kilometers lie dozens of communities with
limited or no scheduled air service: Pueblo CO (~170 km), Cheyenne WY
(~160 km), Scottsbluff NE (~250 km), Dodge City KS (~380 km hybrid mode),
plus high-yield tourist markets like Aspen, Vail/Eagle, and Steamboat
Springs. The 1,100-meter runway requirement enables operations at smaller
airports that cannot support regional jets.

### Chicago and Houston Hubs

The Chicago hub generates demand to communities in Illinois, Indiana,
Michigan, Wisconsin, and Iowa where the ES-30 could replace larger
regional jets on thin routes. The Houston hub serves Texas and Louisiana
communities where hybrid capability is relevant—summer heat reduces
battery performance and turbogenerators compensate.

## United's Sustainability Strategy

United committed to net-zero greenhouse gas emissions by 2050 without
voluntary carbon offsets. The ES-30 contributes by replacing 50-seat
regional jets on short routes (least fuel-efficient per seat-kilometer),
restoring service to communities that lost connectivity, and building
operational knowledge applicable to larger electric aircraft as battery
technology improves.

## Operational Integration

Integrating the ES-30 requires planning across: pilot training for
regional partners (Mesa Air Group), maintenance organization adaptation
for electric propulsion, dispatch and network planning system updates,
and charging infrastructure collaboration with hub airport authorities.

## Mesa Air Group's Role

Mesa Air Group's matching order of 100 firm ES-30s (plus 50 options) is
directly linked to United's commitment. Mesa operates regional flights
under the United Express brand through a capacity-purchase agreement.
Under such agreements, the major airline (United) controls scheduling,
pricing, and revenue management, while the regional operator (Mesa)
provides aircraft and crew at a contracted rate.

The ES-30 would likely enter United Express service operated by Mesa,
rather than being flown directly by United mainline pilots. This
contract structure allows United to benefit from the ES-30's economics
while Mesa manages the operational complexities of introducing a new
aircraft type.
