# ES-30 Operational Economics & Emissions

## Direct Operating Cost Structure

Heart Aerospace projects that the ES-30 will deliver approximately 30%
lower direct operating costs compared to conventional 30-to-50-seat
regional turboprops currently in service. The cost advantage derives
from three primary sources:

### Energy Cost Reduction

Electricity costs for battery charging are substantially lower per
kilowatt-hour delivered to the propeller than Jet-A or sustainable
aviation fuel on an equivalent energy basis. On all-electric missions,
fuel cost drops to zero, replaced by electricity costs estimated at
roughly 20-40% of equivalent Jet-A expense, depending on local
electricity rates and time-of-use pricing. The remaining energy cost
gap narrows on hybrid missions but remains favorable because the
turbogenerators operate at their optimal thermal-efficiency point as
generators, rather than cycling through the wide power variations of
conventional turboprop engines across climb, cruise, and descent.

### Maintenance Cost Reduction

Electric motors have dramatically fewer moving parts than turboprop
engines—no hot-section components, no accessory gearbox, no fuel
control unit, no oil system for the motor itself. Heart Aerospace
estimates motor time-between-overhaul intervals far exceeding
conventional turbine engines. The turbogenerators, while turbine-based,
operate at steady-state conditions as generators rather than as
propulsion engines, reducing thermal-cycle fatigue and extending
maintenance intervals.

The battery system requires periodic health monitoring and eventual
cell replacement, but battery maintenance is primarily a condition-based
monitoring activity rather than a heavy-maintenance event. The absence
of liquid fuel systems eliminates fuel pump, fuel nozzle, and fuel
control maintenance tasks.

### Crew and Operational Efficiency

The ES-30's modern flight deck can be configured for single-pilot
operation under CS-23, reducing crew costs compared to legacy regional
turboprops that require two pilots under CS-25. Even when operators
choose two-pilot operation for standardization, the flight deck's
modern avionics and automation reduce workload.

## Emissions Profile

### All-Electric Operations

On all-electric missions of up to 200 kilometers, the ES-30 produces
zero in-flight CO2 emissions. When accounting for the full lifecycle,
including electricity generation, the emissions reduction compared to
a conventional turboprop is approximately 90% with a modern grid mix
and can approach 100% when charged from renewable sources.

Other regulated emissions eliminated during all-electric flight include:

- **Nitrogen oxides (NOx):** Zero in-flight production, reducing
  contribution to ground-level ozone formation near airports.
- **Carbon monoxide:** Not produced by electric propulsion.
- **Unburned hydrocarbons:** Eliminated entirely.
- **Particulate matter:** No combustion byproducts from the propulsion
  system during electric operation.
- **Sulfur oxides:** No Jet-A fuel means no sulfur content.

### Hybrid Operations

On hybrid missions, the turbogenerators operate at their most efficient
thermal point and can burn sustainable aviation fuel. Heart Aerospace
estimates a 50-70% emissions reduction on hybrid flights compared to
conventional turboprops, depending on the split between battery and
turbogenerator power during the mission.

The turbogenerators are sized for cruise power only, not for takeoff
and climb, so they are smaller, lighter, and more thermally efficient
than the propulsion engines they replace. At steady-state cruise
operation, they achieve brake-specific fuel consumption figures
comparable to ground-based diesel generators running at their design
point.

## Noise Footprint

Noise reduction is a significant operational benefit for regional
airlines serving communities with curfews and noise-sensitive
surroundings:

- **All-electric operations:** The four wing-mounted electric motors
  produce dramatically less noise than conventional turboprop engines,
  especially during takeoff and climb when turboprop noise is highest.
  Estimated reduction of 15–25 dB in community noise contour area.

- **Hybrid operations:** The turbogenerators are mounted in the rear
  fuselage and can be acoustically treated with isolation mounts and
  internal soundproofing. Since they are not mechanically connected
  to propellers, there is no propeller-induced gearbox whine.

- **Propeller design:** The four-blade propellers are designed for
  low tip speeds, reducing the characteristic turboprop cabin drone
  and external noise signature.

## Route Network Economics

Heart Aerospace targets routes of 100 to 400 kilometers, which represent
the core of the regional aviation market. At the 200-kilometer all-electric
range, the ES-30 can serve routes that are currently economical only for
50-seat regional jets or turboprops by reducing trip costs sufficiently to
operate profitably at 30 seats. At the 400-kilometer hybrid range, the
aircraft competes directly with ATR 42 and Dash 8 operations.

The 1,100-meter runway requirement expands the network of usable airports
beyond those served by regional jets, which typically require 1,500 meters
or more. Thousands of general aviation airports with runways longer than
1,100 meters could become viable scheduled-service destinations, opening
point-to-point routes that bypass congested hubs.
