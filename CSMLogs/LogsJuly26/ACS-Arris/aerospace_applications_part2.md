## Certification Pathway for Composite Aerospace Structures

The certification of composite structural components for aerospace applications follows a well-defined framework established by the FAA and EASA, articulated most comprehensively in the FAA Advisory Circular AC 20-107B, "Composite Aircraft Structure." Understanding this framework is essential for evaluating Arris's timeline to production adoption.

### The Building Block Approach

Aerospace composite certification employs a "building block" methodology that progresses through increasing levels of structural complexity: coupons (small material samples), elements (simple geometric features), details (individual design features), sub-components (assemblies of details), components (major structural sections), and full-scale (complete airframe). Test data from lower levels of the pyramid validates the analytical methods used to predict performance at higher levels.

For Arris, this means that qualifying an Additive Molding bracket for a Boeing or Airbus platform involves not only demonstrating that the specific bracket meets its design requirements but that the entire chain of material characterization, process specification, and analytical prediction is validated at each level of the building block pyramid.

### Material Qualification

Material qualification establishes the statistically based design allowables — the mechanical property values that engineers use to design structures with specified margins of safety. For metallic materials, this is relatively straightforward: aluminum alloy 7075-T6, for example, has well-characterized properties that are documented in MIL-HDBK-5 and accepted across the industry.

For composite materials, qualification is more involved because the material properties depend on the manufacturing process. A carbon fiber/PEEK laminate produced by Additive Molding may have different properties than the same fiber and matrix combination processed by autoclave consolidation. The material qualification program must characterize properties under multiple environmental conditions (cold-dry, room temperature-dry, hot-wet), multiple loading modes (tension, compression, shear), and with statistical rigor sufficient to establish B-basis allowables.

The cost of a full material qualification program for aerospace composites ranges from $1 million to $5 million and requires 12-24 months of testing. This cost is typically shared between the material supplier, the part manufacturer, and the aircraft OEM, but the timeline constraint is real and must be factored into any projection of Arris's revenue trajectory.

### Process Specification and Control

Aerospace certification requires that the manufacturing process be documented in a controlled specification that defines all process parameters, acceptable ranges, and quality acceptance criteria. Any change to the process specification — a new mold release agent, a different fiber supplier, a modified molding temperature — requires requalification testing to demonstrate that the change does not affect structural performance.

For a process as novel as Additive Molding, establishing the initial process specification requires extensive designed experiments to characterize the sensitivity of mechanical properties to each process parameter. This is labor-intensive but provides long-term process knowledge and defensibility once completed.

### Equivalency and Substitution

One pathway to accelerated adoption is equivalency: demonstrating that an Additive Molding component is a "form, fit, and function" replacement for an existing qualified component, with no degradation in structural performance. Under this approach, the Additive Molding part is tested against the same requirements as the incumbent part, without requiring re-certification of the broader structure into which it integrates.

This approach is most applicable to brackets and fittings that are attached to primary structure but are not themselves primary structure. The aircraft OEM can authorize substitution of an equivalent composite bracket for an aluminum bracket through an engineering change order rather than a full supplemental type certificate, reducing the timeline and cost of adoption.

## Defense Applications and the US Army Partnership

Arris's partnership with the US Army represents a distinct market access channel with different requirements and timelines than commercial aerospace. Defense applications, while subject to rigorous qualification, operate under military standards (MIL-STD, MIL-HDBK) that, while equally demanding, follow different procedures than FAA certification.

The Army's interest in lightweighting spans multiple domains: individual soldier equipment (reducing the weight carried by infantry), ground vehicles (improving fuel efficiency and transportability of tactical vehicles), and aviation (enhancing the performance of rotorcraft and unmanned systems). Each domain presents specific requirements for environmental resistance, ballistic tolerance, and field maintainability.

The defense channel offers Arris several advantages relative to commercial aerospace. Defense procurement can fund development and qualification costs directly through research contracts and Small Business Innovation Research (SBIR) awards, reducing the capital burden on the company. Military qualification timelines, while not short, are typically more predictable than commercial aircraft development programs that are subject to market-driven schedule changes.

## Airbus Relationship

Airbus has been identified as an Arris customer, though the scope and status of the relationship are less publicly documented than the Boeing HorizonX investment. Airbus maintains an active advanced materials and manufacturing technology scouting function, with particular interest in technologies that can reduce the weight and cost of single-aisle aircraft structures.

The A320 family, Airbus's single-aisle product line, represents the highest-volume commercial aircraft program in history, with production rates exceeding 60 aircraft per month. The volume of brackets and fittings on the A320 is commensurately large, making it a highly attractive program for a supplier seeking to transition from development to production.

The challenge for a supplier to Airbus is the company's demanding cost reduction culture. Airbus procurement historically applies aggressive annual cost reduction targets (3-5% per year) to its supply chain. A composite bracket supplier must not only demonstrate technical equivalence or superiority but must commit to a cost reduction trajectory that aligns with Airbus's commercial expectations.
