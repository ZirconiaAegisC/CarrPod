# Port of Leith Demonstrator

## Project Overview

The Gravitricity 250 kW gravity storage demonstrator was constructed and operated at the
Port of Leith, Edinburgh, Scotland. This facility served as the primary technology
validation platform for the GraviStore concept, proving that controlled raising and
lowering of heavy weights could deliver grid-responsive power with sub-second response
times.

## Physical Configuration

The demonstrator employed a lattice steel tower approximately 15 metres in height, rather
than a mine shaft, to provide the controlled vertical travel path. Two 25-tonne steel
weights were suspended from wire ropes connected to an electric winch system. The
configuration allowed for testing of single-weight and dual-weight operational modes.

The winch drum housed the wire rope with a multi-layer spooling arrangement, and the
motor-generator was a standard industrial AC machine rated at 250 kW continuous duty.
Power electronics comprised a four-quadrant active front-end drive with grid
synchronisation capability.

## Test Programme

The test campaign was conducted in phases:

Phase 1 (Commissioning): Static load testing of structural components, verifying wire rope
tension systems, and confirming guide rail alignment to within ±2 mm tolerance.

Phase 2 (Low-Speed Operation): Raising and lowering at reduced speeds (0.1–0.5 m/s) to
validate the control system response, brake engagement, and emergency stop sequencing.

Phase 3 (Rated Operation): Full-speed cycles (up to 1.0 m/s) with power measurements at
the grid connection point to quantify round-trip efficiency and power quality parameters.

Phase 4 (Grid Services Simulation): Frequency response testing, including primary frequency
response deadband characterisation and synthetic inertia emulation. The system demonstrated
the ability to transition from standby to full power output in under one second.

## Key Results

Round-trip efficiency: 78–82% (AC-to-AC), measured at the point of common coupling with
the local distribution network. The primary losses occurred in the power electronics
(~8%), the motor-generator (~7%), and mechanical friction in the hoist system (~4%).

Response time: The system achieved full power output within 0.8 seconds of receiving a
dispatch signal, significantly faster than open-cycle gas turbines (typically 10–30
seconds) and comparable to flywheel storage and BESS.

Cycle endurance: Over 5,000 charge-discharge cycles were completed during the test
programme with no measurable degradation in wire rope tension characteristics, weight
alignment, or power conversion efficiency.

Power quality: Total harmonic distortion at the grid interface remained below 3% across
all operating points, compliant with UK G59/3 and G99 grid connection requirements.

## Limitations

The demonstrator's 15-metre tower limited the energy capacity to approximately 250 kWh
per cycle—sufficient for control validation but insufficient to demonstrate the economic
case. The tower configuration also eliminated the geotechnical and shaft-lining
considerations that would apply to full-scale mine shaft deployments.

Weight guidance in the demonstrator relied on external guide rails attached to the tower
structure. Full-scale shaft deployments would require different guide systems adapted to
the variable diameters and surface conditions of disused mine shafts, which can exhibit
deviation from the vertical of up to 2 degrees over 1,000 metres.

## Significance

Despite its limited energy capacity, the Port of Leith demonstrator achieved its primary
objective: proving that gravity energy storage with suspended weights is technically
feasible at meaningful power ratings. The sub-second response time and stable efficiency
profile validated the core value proposition for frequency regulation and inertia
services—markets where BESS dominates but where mechanical storage offers compelling
longevity advantages.
