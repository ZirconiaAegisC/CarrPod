## 1. The Fusion Energy Value Proposition

Fusion energy offers a fundamentally differentiated value proposition in the global energy market: baseload-scale, dispatchable, carbon-free electricity generation with no long-lived radioactive waste, no meltdown risk, and fuel resources (deuterium from seawater, lithium for tritium breeding) that are effectively inexhaustible on civilisational timescales. Unlike intermittent renewables, fusion does not require paired storage or firming capacity to deliver reliable grid power. Unlike fission, fusion carries no proliferation risk and minimal post-operation decommissioning burden.

These characteristics position fusion as a "firming baseload" resource capable of displacing coal, natural gas, and ultimately fission nuclear in a decarbonised grid.

## 2. CFS Commercialization Strategy

CFS pursues a three-phase commercialization pathway:

### Phase I: Technology Validation (2026–2027)
SPARC demonstrates Q > 1 net fusion energy, validating the compact high-field architecture and providing the empirical basis for ARC design finalisation. HTS magnet manufacturing capability is established and validated through the 18-magnet production run.

### Phase II: Commercial Demonstration (2028–2032)
The first ARC plant is constructed at the James River Industrial Center. The Google PPA provides revenue certainty and credit enhancement. Construction and commissioning establish the cost, schedule, and performance baseline for subsequent units. NRC licensing establishes the regulatory precedent for fusion power plants.

### Phase III: Fleet Deployment (2032+)
Standardised ARC units are deployed at scale. Manufacturing learning curves reduce unit cost. Multiple magnet production facilities supply the global fleet. PPAs with additional offtakers diversify revenue. International licensing agreements enable deployment in partner jurisdictions.

## 3. Market Sizing

### 3.1 Addressable Market

Global electricity generation in 2025 was approximately 30,000 TWh annually, with approximately 60% derived from fossil fuels (coal and natural gas). The displacement of fossil generation represents an addressable market measured in tens of thousands of terawatt-hours and trillions of dollars in capital expenditure over the coming decades.

At 400 MWe per ARC unit operating at 80% capacity factor, each reactor generates approximately 2.8 TWh annually. Displacing 1% of global fossil generation (~180 TWh) would require approximately 65 ARC units. Displacing all fossil generation would require thousands of units, consistent with CFS's stated ambition.

### 3.2 Target Segments

| Segment | Rationale |
|---------|-----------|
| Corporate PPAs | Early adopter demand for verifiable 24/7 carbon-free energy; premium pricing tolerance |
| Utility baseload replacement | Coal and nuclear plant retirement creates dispatchable capacity need |
| Industrial heat and power | High-temperature process heat for steel, cement, chemicals; hydrogen production |
| Data centre power | Hyper-scale data centre operators require firm, carbon-free power at scale |

## 6. Revenue Model

### 6.1 Primary: Electricity Sales

ARC generates revenue through long-term PPAs with corporate and utility offtakers. The Google PPA establishes the initial pricing benchmark. Subsequent PPAs are expected to benefit from cost reductions as manufacturing experience accumulates.

### 6.2 Secondary: Magnet Sales

HTS magnets for wind turbine generators, MRI systems, scientific instrumentation, and electric propulsion applications provide revenue independent of fusion breakeven. This hedge mitigates investor risk during the fusion development period.

### 6.3 Tertiary: Licensing

International deployment of ARC technology will be enabled through licensing agreements with in-country partners, generating royalty revenue without requiring CFS to directly finance or construct every unit.

### 6.4 Quaternary: Carbon Credits and Policy Incentives

Fusion plants qualify for zero-carbon energy credits, production tax credits, and other policy mechanisms designed to incentivise clean energy deployment. The Inflation Reduction Act and successor legislation provide meaningful revenue enhancement for carbon-free generation.

## 7. Regulatory and Policy Environment

### 7.1 United States

The NRC is developing a fusion-specific regulatory framework under the ADVANCE Act (2024) and related legislation. The framework is expected to treat fusion facilities under a byproduct-materials regulatory approach (10 CFR Part 30) rather than the full reactor licensing regime (10 CFR Part 50/52) applied to fission plants. This distinction reflects fusion's reduced radiological hazard profile.

Key policy developments:
- Fusion Energy Act (2024): Codified NRC jurisdiction
- ADVANCE Act (2024): Directed NRC to develop fusion-specific regulations
- DOE Fusion Energy Strategy: Increased funding for public-private partnerships

### 7.2 International

The United Kingdom has established a fusion regulatory framework separate from fission, administered by the Environment Agency and Health and Safety Executive. The European Union, Japan, and South Korea are developing analogous frameworks. Regulatory divergence across jurisdictions will influence ARC's international deployment sequence.

## 8. Path to Cost Competitiveness

First-of-a-kind ARC unit cost is estimated at $2.5B+. Nth-of-a-kind cost reduction trajectories target:

- **Learning rate on magnets:** ~15–20% cost reduction per doubling of cumulative production
- **Standardised design:** Elimination of engineering non-recurring costs
- **Supply chain maturation:** Competitive procurement of balance-of-plant components
- **Construction experience:** Reduced construction duration through repetition

Target Nth-of-a-kind levelised cost of energy (LCOE): $50–80/MWh, competitive with new-build natural gas combined cycle with carbon capture and new-build onshore wind with storage.
