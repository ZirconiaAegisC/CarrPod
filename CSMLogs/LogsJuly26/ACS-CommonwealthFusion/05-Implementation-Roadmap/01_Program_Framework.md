## 1. Program Architecture

The CFS fusion energy program is structured as three concurrent, interdependent workstreams managed under an integrated program governance framework. The workstreams are sequenced to retire technical risk progressively while enabling parallel advancement of downstream activities.

### 1.1 Workstream Structure

| Workstream | Designation | Scope | Current Phase |
|-----------|-------------|-------|---------------|
| WS-1 | SPARC Demonstration | Net-energy tokamak; Q > 1 validation | Dry Dress Rehearsal |
| WS-2 | Magnet Manufacturing | HTS magnet production; adjacent market sales | Production established |
| WS-3 | ARC Commercial Plant | 400 MWe grid-connected fusion plant | Definitive design |

### 1.2 Governance

The program is governed through an integrated master schedule (IMS) with defined gate reviews at each major milestone transition. Gate criteria include technical performance metrics, resource availability, regulatory status, and market conditions. Executive governance is provided by the CFS leadership team with investor board oversight.

### 2.3 First Plasma Readiness Criteria

Prior to first plasma authorisation, the following conditions must be satisfied:

- All 18 TF magnets installed and individually commissioned
- Cryogenic system at operational temperature; thermal stability demonstrated for > 24 hours
- RF heating systems commissioned at operational power
- Vacuum vessel at base pressure; leak rate certified
- Plasma control system validated through simulation and DDR testing
- Diagnostic systems calibrated and acquiring data
- Safety interlocks tested and verified
- Regulatory approvals obtained for plasma operation
- Tritium handling systems commissioned (not required for initial hydrogen/helium plasma)

### 3.3 Manufacturing Capacity Expansion

To support fleet deployment at the accelerated scenario (50–100 ARC units/year), magnet manufacturing capacity must expand by 20–50x from current Devens throughput. Expansion options:

- **Devens campus expansion:** Additional production lines within existing 60-acre site
- **Virginia facility:** Co-located with ARC construction at James River Industrial Center
- **International facilities:** Manufacturing plants in key licensing jurisdictions to serve local deployment and reduce logistics cost
- **Contract manufacturing:** Qualification of third-party manufacturers for magnet sub-components

## 7. Risk Management Framework

### 7.1 Top Program Risks

| Risk ID | Description | Probability | Impact | Mitigation |
|---------|------------|------------|--------|-----------|
| R-01 | SPARC fails to achieve Q > 1 within design operating space | Low-Medium | Critical | DDR phase de-risks integration; incremental approach to D-T operation |
| R-02 | ARC capital cost exceeds $3B | Medium | High | Modular design; manufacturing learning curve; design-to-cost discipline |
| R-03 | NRC licensing timeline extends beyond 2032 | Medium | High | Early and continuous regulatory engagement; fusion-specific framework advocacy |
| R-04 | HTS magnet supply chain disruption | Low | High | Multi-vendor REBCO qualification; strategic tape inventory |
| R-05 | Key personnel retention | Medium | Medium | Equity compensation; mission-driven culture; competitive compensation |
| R-06 | Competitive technology leapfrog | Low | Medium | Sustained R&D investment; manufacturing scale advantage; PPA lock-in |

### 7.2 Contingency Planning

For each top risk, contingency plans are maintained with trigger conditions, decision authority, and resource allocation pre-authorised. The program governance framework includes quarterly risk reviews with the executive leadership team and annual deep-dive reviews with the investor board.
