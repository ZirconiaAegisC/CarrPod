## 1. THE CHALLENGE

### 1.1 Global Data Center Energy Context

The International Energy Agency (IEA) projects global data center electricity consumption will reach approximately 1,050 terawatt-hours (TWh) by year-end 2026, representing roughly 3.5% of total global electricity demand and approximately 1.2% of global greenhouse gas emissions. This trajectory reflects sustained compound annual growth driven by hyperscale cloud expansion, enterprise digital transformation, and the proliferation of computationally intensive workloads.

Within this landscape, data center cooling infrastructure accounts for approximately 40% of total facility energy consumption in conventionally architected facilities. For a typical 50-megawatt (MW) hyperscale data center operating at a power usage effectiveness (PUE) ratio of 1.4, cooling infrastructure alone consumes approximately 14.3 MW of continuous electrical load — equivalent to the annual electricity consumption of approximately 12,500 average United States households.

### 1.2 Apple Data Center Footprint

Apple operates and is expanding data center facilities across five states within the continental United States: North Carolina (Maiden), Nevada (Reno), Oregon (Prineville), California (Newark), and Iowa (Waukee). Additional international facilities serve European and Asia-Pacific user bases. The aggregate designed capacity across the United States fleet exceeds 400 MW of critical IT load, with expansion plans under active development addressing projected demand from Apple Intelligence inference workloads, iCloud services, and internal computational requirements.

### 1.3 Thermal Density Escalation

The deployment of Apple Intelligence and related artificial intelligence and machine learning (AI/ML) inference and training workloads has materially altered thermal density profiles within Apple data halls. Traditional enterprise and cloud service workloads typically generate 5 to 12 kilowatts (kW) of thermal load per standard 42U rack. AI/ML training clusters utilizing GPU-accelerated compute nodes routinely produce 30 to 80 kW per rack, with next-generation accelerator configurations projected to exceed 120 kW per rack by 2028.

Conventional air-based cooling architectures — including hot-aisle/cold-aisle containment, raised-floor plenum distribution, and computer room air handler (CRAH) configurations — demonstrate sharply diminishing effectiveness above approximately 20 to 30 kW per rack. Beyond this threshold, the volumetric airflow requirements exceed practical limits for raised-floor plenum depth, fan power penalties become economically prohibitive, and localized hot-spot formation compromises equipment reliability.

### 1.4 Regulatory and Sustainability Drivers

The United States Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) climate disclosure rules, the European Union Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD), and California's climate accountability legislation establish mandatory reporting frameworks for Scope 1, Scope 2, and material Scope 3 greenhouse gas emissions. Data center energy consumption — and the associated cooling energy — falls prominently within Scope 2 (purchased electricity) and increasingly within Scope 1 where on-site generation is deployed. Apple's public commitment to carbon neutrality across its entire value chain by 2030 (Apple 2030) necessitates transformative efficiency improvements in data center thermal management.

## 2. IMMERSION COOLING TECHNOLOGY

### 2.1 Fundamental Principles

Immersion cooling is a liquid-cooling methodology wherein information technology (IT) equipment — servers, storage arrays, and networking hardware — is fully submerged in a thermally conductive but electrically non-conductive dielectric fluid. Heat generated by electronic components transfers directly from solid surfaces into the surrounding liquid medium through natural or forced convection, eliminating the air-to-liquid thermal interface resistance inherent in traditional cooling architectures.

The elimination of the air thermal boundary layer — which typically represents 60 to 70% of the total thermal resistance in an air-cooled server thermal path — enables immersion cooling to achieve order-of-magnitude improvements in heat transfer coefficient and corresponding reductions in component junction-to-fluid thermal resistance.

### 2.2 Single-Phase Immersion Cooling (SPIC)

Single-phase immersion cooling circulates dielectric fluid that remains entirely in the liquid phase throughout the thermal cycle. Key operational characteristics include:

**Fluid Circulation Architecture.** Cooled dielectric fluid (typically 25°C to 35°C supply temperature) enters the immersion tank at the bottom or side inlet, flows upward or laterally across submerged server components, absorbs heat through direct contact, and exits at an elevated temperature (typically 40°C to 50°C) to an external heat rejection loop. Circulation is provided by low-head, variable-speed pumps optimized for the dielectric fluid's specific gravity and viscosity characteristics.

**Server Fan Elimination.** Immersion cooling eliminates all server-integral fans — a parasitic electrical load that typically consumes 5 to 8% of total IT power in air-cooled configurations. This reduction flows directly to improved PUE and reduced upstream electrical infrastructure sizing (uninterruptible power supplies, power distribution units, switchgear, and backup generation).

**Heat Capture Efficiency.** Single-phase immersion captures substantially 100% of IT equipment heat into a single liquid stream at a temperature suitable for waste heat recovery applications, including district heating networks, greenhouse agriculture, industrial process pre-heating, and absorption chilling. This contrasts with air-cooled data centers, where exhaust air temperatures of 35°C to 45°C are marginally useful for low-grade heat recovery.

**PUE Performance.** Single-phase immersion cooling systems have demonstrated sustained PUE values below 1.05 in production deployments, compared to 1.2 to 1.6 for best-in-class air-cooled enterprise data centers and 1.1 to 1.3 for advanced chilled-water architectures with economization. The 0.15 to 0.55 PUE differential translates to material reductions in cooling energy consumption, water usage, and associated carbon emissions.

### 2.3 Two-Phase Immersion Cooling

Two-phase immersion cooling employs an engineered dielectric fluid with a boiling point engineered to the range of 40°C to 60°C — slightly above ambient data hall temperature under normal conditions but below the maximum junction temperature of modern silicon devices. Operational characteristics include:

**Passive Phase-Change Cycle.** As server components reach temperatures exceeding the fluid's boiling point, localized nucleate boiling occurs at the silicon-to-fluid interface. The latent heat of vaporization removes thermal energy with substantially higher efficiency than single-phase convective heat transfer. Vapor bubbles rise through the liquid column to the tank's ullage space, where contact with water-cooled condenser coils or an external heat exchanger precipitates condensation back to the liquid phase. The condensed fluid returns to the tank by gravity, completing a fully passive, pumpless thermal cycle.

**Thermal Performance.** Two-phase immersion systems achieve heat transfer coefficients exceeding 20,000 watts per square meter per Kelvin (W/m²K) at the chip surface — approximately 20 to 100 times greater than forced-air convection and 3 to 10 times greater than single-phase liquid cooling. This enables reliable cooling of individual chip thermal design power (TDP) exceeding 1,000 watts without exotic cold-plate configurations.

**Operational Considerations.** Two-phase systems require hermetic sealing to prevent dielectric fluid vapor loss, fluid conditioning systems to manage non-condensable gas accumulation, and material compatibility validation for all wetted components. Fluid loss rates in properly maintained systems are below 0.5% of total fill volume per annum.

## 3. DIELECTRIC FLUID COMPARISON

### 3.1 Fluorocarbon-Based Fluids

**Representative Products:** 3M Novec series (engineered fluids), Solvay SF10.

**Thermal Properties.** Fluorocarbon dielectric fluids offer industry-leading figure-of-merit (FOM) values for thermal performance, characterized by low kinematic viscosity (enabling high Reynolds number convective flow at modest pumping power), high volumetric heat capacity, and chemical inertness across a wide temperature range. These fluids are non-flammable, exhibit zero ozone depletion potential, and have global warming potentials (GWP) substantially lower than legacy perfluorocarbon compounds.

**Environmental Considerations.** Fluorocarbon fluids fall within the per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances (PFAS) chemical classification, which is subject to increasing regulatory scrutiny in the European Union (proposed universal PFAS restriction under REACH), certain United States states, and Japan. While Novec fluids are designed for low environmental persistence relative to long-chain PFAS compounds, the evolving regulatory landscape introduces long-term supply chain uncertainty. Disposal and end-of-life management require specialized handling protocols.

**Cost Profile.** Fluorocarbon dielectric fluids command a significant price premium, typically $80 to $150 per liter in bulk quantities, translating to $400,000 to $2.5 million per immersion tank fill depending on tank capacity and server displacement volume.

### 3.2 Hydrocarbon and Synthetic Ester Fluids

**Representative Products:** BINGYI 797 series, L759 synthetic ester formulations.

**Energy Efficiency.** Independent third-party comparative testing demonstrates that hydrocarbon and synthetic ester dielectric fluids achieve 14 to 54% lower total cooling system energy consumption compared to fluorocarbon-based alternatives when evaluated across the full annualized operating envelope, inclusive of pumping power, heat rejection fan energy, and fluid maintenance energy. The superior energy performance derives from higher specific heat capacity, enabling lower fluid flow rates for equivalent heat removal, and from compatibility with higher supply temperatures that extend economizer operating hours.

**Environmental Profile.** Hydrocarbon dielectric fluids exhibit 5.7 to 94.6% lower carbon dioxide equivalent (CO₂e) emissions over a 10-year lifecycle compared to fluorocarbon alternatives, encompassing manufacturing, transportation, operational leakage, and end-of-life disposal. Coolant acquisition cost reductions of 13.9 to 97.4% have been documented in production-scale deployments relative to fluorocarbon solutions.

**Material Compatibility.** Synthetic esters and hydrocarbons have been validated for compatibility with standard data center cabling insulation (PVC, XLPE, LSZH), connector housings, printed circuit board substrates (FR-4, polyimide), and component conformal coatings. Accelerated aging studies confirm that properly selected fluid formulations do not induce statistically significant degradation of these materials over operational lifetimes exceeding 10 years.

### 3.3 Ester-Based Dielectric Fluids (Bio-Based)

**Representative Products:** L759 Oleon (rapeseed-derived ester formulation).

**Properties.** Bio-based ester dielectric fluids offer excellent electrical resistivity exceeding 10¹² ohm-centimeters at operating temperature, dielectric strength above 30 kilovolts per 2.5 millimeter gap (ASTM D1816), and biodegradability exceeding 90% per OECD 301B testing. High flash point values (typically exceeding 250°C) provide inherent fire safety advantages. Compatibility with PVC-insulated cabling has been validated through extended-duration immersion testing at accelerated temperature and electrical stress conditions.

**Service Life.** Accelerated thermal aging studies conducted at 1.5x to 3x nominal operating temperature demonstrate a projected fluid service life of 5 to 10 years under continuous immersion conditions, with oxidative stability maintained through proprietary additive packages. Fluid condition monitoring through periodic dielectric strength, acid number, and viscosity measurement enables condition-based replacement rather than fixed-interval changeout.

**Sustainability Alignment.** Bio-based ester fluids align with Apple's circular economy objectives through renewable feedstock sourcing, ultimate biodegradability at end-of-life, and substantially lower cradle-to-gate carbon intensity compared to petrochemical-derived dielectric fluids.

### 3.4 Mineral Oil and Polyalphaolefin (PAO) Fluids

Mineral oil and PAO-based dielectric fluids represent the lowest acquisition cost option (typically $3 to $8 per liter) but exhibit thermal conductivity and specific heat capacity values 25 to 40% below synthetic ester alternatives. These fluids are suitable for lower-density immersion deployments where peak heat flux does not exceed approximately 15 watts per square centimeter, but are generally inadequate for AI/ML accelerator cooling without supplementary pumped cold-plate augmentation.

## 4. APPLE-SPECIFIC OPPORTUNITY

### 4.1 Silicon Architecture Synergy

Apple's vertically integrated silicon design — encompassing the M-series system-on-chip (SoC) architecture deployed across Mac, iPad, and increasingly data center server platforms — achieves performance-per-watt metrics that materially exceed industry-average x86 architectures. The M-series unified memory architecture, high-bandwidth on-package DRAM integration, and heterogeneous compute topology (performance cores, efficiency cores, Neural Engine, and media engines within a single package) yield thermal design power (TDP) profiles that are substantially lower per unit of computational throughput than comparable discrete GPU solutions.

Immersion cooling enables M-series-based server designs to operate at elevated sustained clock frequencies without thermal throttling, extracting maximum computational throughput from silicon optimized for efficiency. The absence of server fans eliminates acoustic noise entirely — a secondary benefit for co-location facilities near residential or mixed-use zones.

### 4.2 Rack Density Optimization for AI/ML Workloads

Apple Intelligence inference workloads — including on-device model distillation, server-side large language model (LLM) inference, and differential privacy training infrastructure — demand high-density compute clusters with sustained 100% utilization duty cycles. Immersion cooling enables rack densities of 80 to 150 kW per rack within a standard data hall footprint, compared to 10 to 25 kW for traditional air-cooled configurations. This 4x to 8x density multiplier reduces data hall square footage requirements, structural loading complexity, and inter-rack cabling distances proportionally.

### 4.3 Houston Manufacturing Integration

Apple's 250,000-square-foot server and Mac mini manufacturing facility in Houston, Texas, represents a generational opportunity to integrate immersion cooling compatibility at the point of manufacture rather than as a retrofit to existing designs. Immersion-ready server configurations include: elimination of fan assemblies and associated fan controller circuitry, direct-die thermal interface material (TIM) optimized for liquid immersion rather than air convection, conformal coating of non-submerged board-level components, orientation-optimized chassis for vertical immersion tank insertion, and dielectric-fluid-compatible connector and cable harness specifications.

Designing for immersion from first principles — as opposed to retrofitting air-cooled servers — yields a bill-of-materials (BOM) cost reduction estimated at 8 to 15% through elimination of fans, fan controllers, heatsinks (replaced by direct fluid contact), and air filtration subsystems.

### 4.4 Path to Carbon-Negative Compute

Apple's existing achievement of 100% renewable electricity matching for global corporate operations — including all data center facilities — combined with best-in-class PUE below 1.05 through immersion cooling adoption, creates the foundation for carbon-negative compute operations. The integration of on-site renewable generation (solar photovoltaic, wind), battery energy storage systems (BESS) for temporal load matching, and waste heat recovery for beneficial use enables net-negative operational carbon intensity. This positions Apple data centers as carbon sinks within the broader Apple 2030 framework.

## 5. PVT-ASSISTED COOLING

### 5.1 Photovoltaic Thermal (PVT) Technology

Photovoltaic Thermal (PVT) panels integrate photovoltaic electricity generation with thermal energy collection in a single roof-mounted assembly. A fluid-circulating heat exchanger bonded to the rear surface of the photovoltaic laminate extracts thermal energy from the solar cells, simultaneously improving photovoltaic conversion efficiency (through reduced cell operating temperature) and providing a source of low-grade thermal energy for building services.

During nocturnal operation, PVT panels function as radiative sky cooling surfaces. The panel surface — with high emissivity in the 8 to 13 micrometer atmospheric window wavelength band — radiates thermal energy to the cold background of space (effective sky temperature of approximately -20°C to -40°C under clear-sky conditions with low relative humidity). Circulating fluid through the panel array during nighttime hours extracts cooled thermal energy suitable for data center heat rejection.

### 5.2 Adaptive Reset Control Strategies

Proprietary thermal analytics employing advanced modeling methodologies enable adaptive reset control of chilled water supply temperature setpoints based on real-time ambient conditions, PVT panel surface temperature, data center IT load, and electricity price signals. These control strategies have demonstrated 42.1 to 64.2% cooling energy reduction in instrumented field trials at operational data center facilities, with a median capital investment payback period of 2.11 to 3.75 years inclusive of installation, commissioning, and control system integration costs.

### 5.3 PUE Impact

Production PVT-assisted cooling deployments have demonstrated sustained annualized PUE values below 1.05, representing a 0.15 to 0.35 improvement over conventional air-cooled or chilled-water data centers of comparable IT load. The PUE improvement derives from: reduced chiller plant operating hours (displaced by PVT nighttime radiative cooling), reduced cooling tower fan energy (lower condenser water temperatures), and reduced pumping energy (optimized variable-primary flow configurations).

### 5.4 Apple Real Estate Compatibility

Apple's data center facilities feature extensive roof surface area — the Maiden, North Carolina, facility alone encompasses approximately 500,000 square feet of building footprint — providing substantial deployment area for PVT arrays. Apple's existing on-site solar photovoltaic installations (including a 100-acre, 20 MW solar farm at the Maiden facility and a 200 MW procurement agreement for the Reno facility) provide the electrical infrastructure and site experience for expanded rooftop energy system deployment.

## 6. IMPLEMENTATION STRATEGY

### 6.1 Hybrid Cooling Architecture

The recommended implementation strategy employs a hybrid cooling architecture:

- **Immersion cooling** deployed for high-density AI/ML compute clusters, GPU-accelerated training infrastructure, and next-generation Apple Intelligence inference servers, representing approximately 40 to 60% of future IT load.
- **Advanced air cooling** retained for legacy infrastructure, low-density storage arrays, networking equipment, and support systems where the capital cost of immersion conversion is not justified by energy savings — representing approximately 40 to 60% of remaining IT load.

This hybrid approach yields the majority of immersion cooling energy benefits while avoiding the stranded asset risk associated with wholesale air-cooling infrastructure retirement.

### 6.2 Industry Energy Savings Projection

The IEA 4E (Energy Efficient End-Use Equipment) Technology Collaboration Programme has modeled global data center cooling energy consumption under alternative technology adoption scenarios:

- **Business-as-usual trajectory:** Continued dominance of air cooling with incremental efficiency improvements yields global data center cooling energy consumption of approximately 370 TWh in 2030.
- **Realistic hybrid adoption (IEA 4E DTC model):** Partial immersion cooling penetration in high-density segments saves approximately 22.6 TWh per year by 2030 relative to business-as-usual.
- **Optimized immersion scenario:** Aggressive adoption of immersion cooling with PVT-assisted heat rejection and waste heat recovery saves approximately 70.8 TWh per year by 2030 — equivalent to the annual electricity consumption of approximately 6.7 million United States households.

### 6.3 Apple Fleet Contribution

Apple's scaled data center fleet, combined with the Houston manufacturing facility's immersion-first server design philosophy, positions the company to capture a meaningful share of the addressable immersion cooling energy savings. Proprietary thermal analytics projections indicate that full immersion cooling adoption across Apple's United States data center fleet — integrated with PVT-assisted heat rejection and M-series server silicon — would reduce annual cooling energy consumption by an estimated 65 to 80% relative to current air-cooled baselines while simultaneously increasing computational throughput per square foot of data hall area by 300 to 500%.

### 6.4 Phased Deployment

Phase 1 (2026–2028): Immersion cooling pilot deployment in one Apple data hall (target: 2 MW IT load), validation of dielectric fluid compatibility with M-series server hardware, PVT array installation and instrumentation.

Phase 2 (2028–2030): Expansion to all new Apple data hall construction, immersion-first server specification for Houston-manufactured hardware, operational PUE target below 1.05 fleet-wide.

Phase 3 (2030–2035): Retrofit of existing high-density data halls to immersion cooling, full PVT integration across Apple data center real estate, waste heat recovery partnership agreements with adjacent industrial or municipal entities.

## 7. WASTE HEAT RECOVERY AND BENEFICIAL REUSE

### 7.1 District Heating Integration

Immersion cooling's single-liquid-stream heat capture architecture — delivering dielectric fluid at 45°C to 55°C return temperature — is ideally suited for district heating network integration. At this temperature, the waste heat stream can supply space heating and domestic hot water preheating for adjacent commercial, residential, or industrial facilities without supplementary heat pump lift. A 50 MW immersion-cooled data center rejecting heat at 50°C can provide approximately 175,000 megawatt-hours thermal (MWth) per year of useful heat — sufficient for approximately 12,000 to 15,000 residential units in a temperate climate zone. Apple's data center site selection criteria include proximity to potential district heating offtake partners, with preliminary feasibility assessments completed for multiple United States and European data center locations.

### 7.2 Greenhouse Agriculture

Data center waste heat at 35°C to 50°C is well-matched to the root-zone and ambient heating requirements of greenhouse agriculture. Controlled-environment agriculture (CEA) facilities — producing leafy greens, tomatoes, cucumbers, peppers, and berries — require continuous low-grade heating during cold-weather months and nighttime hours. A 20-acre greenhouse facility co-located with a data center can utilize approximately 5 to 8 MWth of waste heat during winter peak conditions, reducing or eliminating the greenhouse's natural gas or propane heating demand. Apple has engaged with controlled-environment agriculture operators in Oregon and North Carolina to evaluate co-location feasibility.

### 7.3 Absorption Chilling

Higher-temperature waste heat streams — those above approximately 85°C, achievable through heat pump boosting of the 50°C dielectric fluid return — can drive single-effect or double-effect lithium bromide (LiBr) absorption chillers to produce chilled water for facility cooling. This creates a thermally cascaded system: IT equipment heat is captured by immersion cooling fluid, boosted to absorption chiller generator temperature through a water-source heat pump, and converted to chilled water that serves the remaining air-cooled data hall infrastructure or facility comfort cooling. Absorption chilling powered by data center waste heat can reduce facility chiller plant electrical consumption by 30 to 50% during cooling season operation.

## 8. ECONOMIC ANALYSIS

### 8.1 Capital Cost Comparison

Immersion cooling tank infrastructure carries a capital cost premium of approximately 15 to 30% over conventional air cooling for equivalent IT capacity when evaluated at the data hall level. However, this premium is partially or fully offset by: elimination of raised-floor plenum construction (saving $40 to $80 per square foot of data hall area), reduction in computer room air handler (CRAH) and chiller plant capacity (each 1 MW of IT load migrated to immersion cooling eliminates approximately 300 kW of chiller plant electrical load), elimination of hot-aisle/cold-aisle containment infrastructure, and reduction in data hall square footage requirements (immersion-cooled racks can be placed at higher density with reduced aisle width requirements).

### 8.2 Total Cost of Ownership (TCO)

A 10-year total cost of ownership analysis comparing immersion cooling with air cooling for a 10 MW IT load data hall demonstrates: capital expenditure parity within ±10% (immersion infrastructure premium offset by air-cooling infrastructure elimination and data hall footprint reduction), operational expenditure savings of 35 to 50% (dominated by cooling energy reduction of 60 to 80%, water consumption reduction of 80 to 100% where dry coolers replace cooling towers, and IT fan energy elimination of 5 to 8% of IT load), and net present value (NPV) advantage of $18 million to $32 million for immersion cooling at a 7% discount rate over 10 years, varying with local electricity price, water cost, and climate zone.

### 8.3 Server Hardware Longevity

Immersion cooling's superior thermal management — characterized by reduced component junction temperature, reduced thermal cycling amplitude, and elimination of airborne contaminants (dust, corrosive gases, humidity) — has been demonstrated to extend server hardware service life by 20 to 40% relative to air-cooled equivalents, based on accelerated life testing and field reliability data. Reduced hardware failure rates decrease service parts consumption, technician dispatch frequency, and associated logistics carbon emissions, contributing to both economic and environmental TCO benefits.

## 9. REGULATORY AND STANDARDS LANDSCAPE

### 9.1 Building Codes and Fire Safety

Immersion cooling deployments must comply with local building codes, fire codes (NFPA 75: Standard for the Fire Protection of Information Technology Equipment; NFPA 70: National Electrical Code), and environmental regulations governing dielectric fluid storage, handling, and spill containment. Dielectric fluids with flash points above 300°C provide inherent fire safety and may qualify for reduced fire suppression infrastructure requirements relative to air-cooled data halls. Secondary containment systems (tank bunding, floor sealing, leak detection) are required for all dielectric fluid volumes exceeding applicable threshold quantities.

### 9.2 Industry Standards Development

The Open Compute Project (OCP) Advanced Cooling Solutions workstream has published immersion cooling requirements (Revision 2.2, 2024) covering: fluid-material compatibility testing protocols, tank design and thermal performance characterization standards, server chassis design guidelines for immersion compatibility, and monitoring and instrumentation requirements. ASHRAE Technical Committee 9.9 is developing standardized thermal guidelines for liquid-cooled IT equipment. Apple's participation in these standards development processes ensures that Apple's requirements are reflected in industry standards and that Apple's immersion cooling deployments benefit from industry consensus best practices.
