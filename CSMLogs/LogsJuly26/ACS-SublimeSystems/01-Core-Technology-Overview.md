# Sublime Systems — Core Technology: Electrochemical Cement Production

## The Cement Emissions Problem

Portland cement production is responsible for approximately 8% of global anthropogenic CO₂ emissions — roughly 3 billion tonnes annually. This staggering figure arises from two distinct sources:

1. **Process (Chemical) Emissions (~60%)**: The thermal decomposition of limestone (CaCO₃) into lime (CaO) releases CO₂ as an intrinsic product of the chemical reaction: CaCO₃ + heat → CaO + CO₂. This chemistry is unavoidable regardless of the heat source used.

2. **Thermal Emissions (~40%)**: Achieving the decomposition temperature of approximately 900°C — and the clinkering temperature of 1450°C required to form the calcium silicate phases that give cement its strength — requires combustion of fossil fuels (coal, petcoke, natural gas) or electricity.

The essential insight behind Sublime Systems is that the process emissions are the larger problem and cannot be addressed by switching to renewable heat sources or electrifying kilns. A fundamentally different chemistry is required.

## Sublime's Electrochemical Process

Sublime Systems has developed an electrochemical pathway to produce calcium silicate cement — the same reactive phases that give Portland cement its binding properties — without decomposing limestone and without high-temperature kilns.

### Process Overview

The Sublime process operates through an electrochemical pH-swing methodology:

**Step 1 — Calcium Extraction (Acidic Step)**
A calcium-bearing mineral feedstock (limestone, calcium silicate minerals, or industrial alkaline wastes) is dissolved in an acidic aqueous solution within an electrochemical cell. The applied current drives protons (H⁺) to the feedstock, where they react to liberate calcium ions (Ca²⁺) into solution. This step operates at ambient temperature and pressure.

The anodic half-reaction generates acidity:
    H₂O → ½ O₂ + 2 H⁺ + 2 e⁻

The acidic environment dissolves calcium from the feedstock:
    CaCO₃ + 2 H⁺ → Ca²⁺ + H₂O + CO₂  *(if limestone feedstock)*
    or
    CaSiO₃ + 2 H⁺ → Ca²⁺ + SiO₂ + H₂O  *(if calcium silicate feedstock)*

**Step 2 — Calcium Precipitation (Basic Step)**
The calcium-rich solution is transferred to a second electrochemical compartment where water reduction generates hydroxide ions (OH⁻), raising the pH:
    2 H₂O + 2 e⁻ → H₂ + 2 OH⁻

Under basic conditions, dissolved calcium reacts with dissolved silica to precipitate calcium silicate hydrates — the same compounds that provide strength in Portland cement:
    x Ca²⁺ + y SiO₂(aq) + z OH⁻ → CaₓSiO_y(OH)₂ₓ₋₂y · nH₂O (precipitate)

**Step 3 — Drying and Blending**
The precipitated calcium silicate product is filtered, washed, and dried. It is then blended with supplementary cementitious materials as needed to produce the final cement product. No high-temperature processing is required.

## Electrolyzer Technology

Sublime Systems has developed a proprietary membrane-based electrolyzer designed for the specific requirements of the calcium extraction and precipitation process. Key characteristics:

- **Membrane selection**: Ion-exchange membranes that selectively permit proton or hydroxide transport while preventing mixing of acidic and basic streams, critical for process efficiency
- **Electrode materials**: Catalysts optimized for water oxidation (oxygen evolution) and water reduction (hydrogen evolution) in the specific electrolyte chemistries used
- **Cell design**: Flow-through or flow-by configurations that provide high surface area for calcium dissolution and efficient mass transport
- **Scaling approach**: Similar to PEM and alkaline water electrolyzers, Sublime's electrolyzer stacks can be scaled by adding cells in parallel

The hydrogen byproduct from the cathodic (basic) step is a valuable revenue stream. Depending on scale, hydrogen can be compressed for sale, used for on-site thermal needs, or fed to fuel cells for electricity generation to reduce net grid consumption.

## Feedstock Flexibility

Sublime's process can utilize a wide range of calcium-bearing feedstocks:
- **Limestone (CaCO₃)**: The conventional cement feedstock; when used in the Sublime process, CO₂ is released at ambient conditions as a concentrated stream suitable for capture or utilization
- **Calcium silicate minerals (wollastonite, anorthite, etc.)**: These feedstocks contain no carbonate, so no process CO₂ is generated
- **Industrial alkaline wastes**: Steel slag, coal fly ash, red mud (bauxite residue), and demolition concrete all contain reactive calcium that can be extracted electrochemically, creating a circular economy pathway
- **Basalt and mafic rocks**: Abundant silicate rocks that contain calcium; the electrochemical process can extract calcium from these minerals, though dissolution kinetics are slower than for limestone

The ability to use non-carbonate feedstocks is the key to achieving true zero-CO₂ cement production. When calcium silicate minerals or industrial wastes serve as the calcium source, no CO₂ is released from the chemistry of the process.

## Energy Consumption

The electrochemical process consumes electricity for:
1. Water splitting to generate the protons (anode) and hydroxide ions (cathode) that drive the pH swing
2. Pumping and electrolyte circulation
3. Product drying (low-temperature)

Published literature indicates electrical energy consumption in the range of 2–4 MWh per tonne of cement produced, depending on feedstock reactivity, electrolyzer efficiency, and drying requirements. This is lower than the combined thermal and electrical energy consumption of conventional cement production (approximately 3–4 GJ thermal + 100–150 kWh electrical per tonne of clinker, equivalent to approximately 0.9–1.2 MWh of primary energy) when accounting for the conversion efficiency from primary energy to electricity.

The hydrogen byproduct (approximately 20–50 kg per tonne of cement, depending on feedstock and process conditions) represents an energy credit of approximately 0.5–1.5 MWh per tonne of cement if used for electricity generation.
