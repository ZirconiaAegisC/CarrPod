# Sublime Systems — Hydrogen Co-Product Economics and Integration

## The Hydrogen Byproduct Opportunity

One of the most economically significant features of Sublime Systems' electrochemical cement process is the co-production of hydrogen at the cathode. For every tonne of cement produced, approximately 20–50 kilograms of hydrogen are generated, depending on process configuration and feedstock chemistry. This hydrogen byproduct transforms the economic and strategic calculus of Sublime cement production.

## Hydrogen Production Chemistry

Hydrogen is produced at the cathode of the electrochemical cell via the hydrogen evolution reaction:

    2 H₂O + 2 e⁻ → H₂ + 2 OH⁻

This reaction is the same fundamental chemistry used in alkaline and PEM water electrolyzers — the dominant technologies for green hydrogen production. In effect, Sublime's process is an electrolyzer that produces cement as its primary product and hydrogen as a co-product.

### Hydrogen Production Rate

The quantity of hydrogen produced per tonne of cement depends on:
- The calcium-to-silicon ratio of the cement product (higher Ca/Si requires more calcium transport, driving more current and more hydrogen)
- The Faraday efficiency of the process (the fraction of current that goes to the desired reactions versus side reactions)
- The specific process configuration and feedstock

At a production rate of 30 kg H₂ per tonne of cement (a mid-range assumption):
- A 250,000 t/yr Sublime cement plant produces ~7,500 tonnes of hydrogen annually
- At a green hydrogen price of $3–5/kg, this represents $22.5–37.5 million in annual revenue
- Per tonne of cement, the hydrogen credit is $90–150 (at $3–5/kg H₂), significantly offsetting electricity and other operating costs

## Hydrogen Utilization Options

### Option 1: Sale to Merchant Hydrogen Market

Selling hydrogen to external buyers provides the simplest revenue stream:
- Industrial hydrogen consumers: Oil refineries (hydrotreating, hydrocracking), ammonia production, chemical manufacturing, metals processing, glass manufacturing
- Emerging hydrogen markets: Fuel cell vehicle refueling stations, power generation (hydrogen turbines, fuel cells), steelmaking (hydrogen-DRI)
- Revenue: Market price minus compression, storage, and transport costs

### Option 2: On-Site Thermal Utilization

Hydrogen can be combusted on-site for thermal needs:
- Product drying (the cement product must be dried after precipitation)
- Feedstock preheating or calcination (if a thermal step is added)
- Building heating and facility utilities
- Revenue equivalence: Displacement of purchased natural gas or electricity for thermal needs

### Option 3: On-Site Electricity Generation

Hydrogen can be converted to electricity via fuel cells:
- PEM fuel cells: 50–60% electrical efficiency; waste heat can be used for drying
- Solid oxide fuel cells: 55–65% electrical efficiency; higher-grade waste heat
- Revenue equivalence: Displacement of purchased grid electricity; reduces net electricity consumption by 10–20%

### Option 4: Integration with CO₂ Utilization

If Sublime operates with limestone feedstock and captures the concentrated CO₂ stream, hydrogen can be combined with captured CO₂ to produce:
- Synthetic methane (methanation): CO₂ + 4 H₂ → CH₄ + 2 H₂O
- Methanol: CO₂ + 3 H₂ → CH₃OH + H₂O
- Synthetic jet fuel or diesel (Fischer-Tropsch): CO + 2 H₂ → –(CH₂)– + H₂O

These power-to-X pathways add capital complexity but can produce high-value products and sequester CO₂ in long-lived materials or fuels.

## Hydrogen Market Context

### Current Hydrogen Market

The global hydrogen market is approximately 95 million tonnes per year, dominated by "grey" hydrogen produced from natural gas via steam methane reforming (SMR) with CO₂ vented to the atmosphere:
- Grey hydrogen cost: $1–2/kg (dependent on natural gas price)
- Blue hydrogen (SMR + CCS) cost: $2–3/kg
- Green hydrogen (electrolysis with renewable electricity) cost: $3–6/kg (declining)

### Green Hydrogen Cost Trajectory

The cost of green hydrogen is projected to decline substantially through 2030:
- DOE Hydrogen Shot target: $1/kg by 2031
- European Hydrogen Backbone target: €2–3/kg by 2030
- Driving factors: Declining electrolyzer capital costs (learning rates of 15–20% per doubling of cumulative capacity), declining renewable electricity costs, manufacturing scale-up

Sublime's hydrogen co-product competes in the green hydrogen market. Its cost advantage is that the capital cost of electrolysis is shared with cement production — the electrolyzer is already paid for by the cement product. This means Sublime's marginal cost of hydrogen production is essentially the electricity cost, which at 55 kWh/kg H₂ and $30/MWh is approximately $1.65/kg — competitive with grey hydrogen even without any green premium.

## Strategic Implications

The hydrogen co-product transforms Sublime's strategic positioning:

1. **Economic resilience**: Even if cement prices are low, hydrogen revenue provides a floor for plant economics

2. **Geographic flexibility**: Plants can be sited near hydrogen demand centers as well as cement demand centers, potentially improving logistics economics

3. **Revenue diversification**: A single plant generates revenue from two distinct markets (cement and hydrogen), reducing exposure to cyclical downturns in either

4. **Partnership optionality**: Potential partnerships with hydrogen offtakers (industrial gas companies, ammonia producers, refineries) in addition to cement partners

5. **Policy support**: The hydrogen co-product qualifies Sublime plants for hydrogen production incentives (e.g., IRA Section 45V clean hydrogen production tax credit of up to $3/kg), further improving economics
