# Glossary of Technical Terms

**Document Control:** ACS-TyLynn-BFP-07-002 | Revision 1.0 | July 2026
**Classification:** Confidential — Client Business Information

---

## A

**AASHTO** — American Association of State Highway and Transportation Officials. The standards-setting body for highway and bridge design, construction, and maintenance in the United States. Publishes the LRFD Bridge Design Specifications, Manual for Bridge Evaluation, and the 2026 Guidelines for Duplex Coating Systems.

**Abrasive Blast Cleaning** — Surface preparation method in which abrasive media (steel grit, aluminum oxide, coal slag, etc.) are propelled at high velocity against a steel surface to remove existing coatings, mill scale, and corrosion products. Graded by cleanliness levels per SSPC/NACE joint standards (e.g., SP 10 Near-White, SP 5 White Metal).

**AC Impedance Spectroscopy** — An electrochemical technique used to evaluate the condition of protective coatings and the corrosion state of reinforced concrete by measuring the impedance response of the system to an applied alternating current across a range of frequencies.

**Adaptive Management** — A structured, iterative decision-making process that uses monitoring data to adjust management actions over time, particularly valuable for long-lived infrastructure where initial design assumptions may not perfectly predict actual performance.

**AMPP** — Association for Materials Protection and Performance. Formed in 2021 by the merger of NACE International (National Association of Corrosion Engineers) and SSPC (The Society for Protective Coatings). The principal professional organization for corrosion engineering and protective coatings in the United States.

**Anode** — The electrode in a corrosion cell or cathodic protection system at which oxidation (corrosion) occurs. In cathodic protection, sacrificial anodes (zinc, aluminum, magnesium) are consumed over time to protect the structure; impressed current anodes (MMO titanium, graphite, high-silicon cast iron) are not consumed and are powered by an external DC source.

**ASTM International** — Formerly the American Society for Testing and Materials. Publishes consensus technical standards for materials, products, systems, and services, including critical standards for corrosion assessment (ASTM D610, ASTM D4417) and coating evaluation.

---

## B

**BATA** — Bay Area Toll Authority. A division of the Metropolitan Transportation Commission (MTC) responsible for the administration of toll revenues from the seven state-owned toll bridges in the San Francisco Bay Area. BATA programs and funds the Capital Improvement Program (CIP) for the toll bridge fleet.

**Bridge Information Modeling (BrIM)** — The application of Building Information Modeling (BIM) principles to bridge structures, creating a digital representation of physical and functional characteristics that serves as a shared knowledge resource throughout the asset life cycle.

**Bridge Investment Program (BIP)** — A competitive grant program established under the Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act (IIJA, 2021) administered by FHWA. Provides funding for bridge replacement, rehabilitation, preservation, and protection projects. Large Bridge Project grants (over $100 million) are available for nationally significant bridges.

---

## C

**C3/C4/C5 Environmental Classification** — ISO 12944-2 categories for atmospheric corrosivity:
- **C3 (Medium):** Urban and industrial atmospheres with moderate sulfur dioxide pollution; coastal areas with low salinity.
- **C4 (High):** Industrial areas and coastal areas with moderate salinity.
- **C5 (Very High):** Industrial areas with high humidity and aggressive atmosphere; coastal and offshore areas with high salinity.
- **C5-M (Marine):** Specifically for marine environments with very high corrosivity, including splash zones and salt-laden atmospheres.

**Cal/OSHA** — California Division of Occupational Safety and Health. The state agency responsible for workplace safety regulations, including the 2025 amendments to Title 8, Section 1532.1 governing lead exposure during bridge painting and coating removal operations.

**Caltrans** — California Department of Transportation. The state agency responsible for the design, construction, maintenance, and operation of the California state highway system, including the seven state-owned Bay Area toll bridges. Key divisions relevant to this program: Division of Engineering Services (DES), Division of Maintenance, Structure Maintenance and Investigations (SM&I), and Materials Engineering and Testing Services (METS).

**Cathodic Protection (CP)** — An electrochemical technique for controlling the corrosion of a metal surface by making it the cathode of an electrochemical cell. Two principal methods:
- **Galvanic Cathodic Protection (GCP):** Uses a more electronegative sacrificial metal (zinc, aluminum, magnesium) that corrodes in preference to the protected structure.
- **Impressed Current Cathodic Protection (ICCP):** Uses an external DC power source (transformer-rectifier) and inert anodes (MMO titanium) to drive a protective current onto the structure.

**CIP** — Capital Improvement Program. BATA's rolling multi-year program of major rehabilitation and replacement projects for the Bay Area toll bridge fleet.

**Coating Disbondment** — The loss of adhesion between a protective coating and the substrate, often driven by cathodic reactions at the coating-steel interface, moisture ingress, or mechanical damage. A primary failure mode for organic coatings on steel.

**Corrosion Rate** — The rate at which a metal deteriorates in a specific environment, typically expressed in micrometers per year (μm/yr) of thickness loss or mils per year (mpy). Measured electrochemically using linear polarization resistance (LPR) or determined by section loss measurement over time.

---

## D

**DDO** — Design Deviation Order. A formal engineering directive issued when a structural element is found to deviate from its design assumptions, typically due to deterioration, damage, or changes in loading conditions. A DDO triggers an engineering evaluation and may require load posting, repair, or replacement.

**Design Life** — The period of time for which a structure or component is designed to perform its intended function without major repair or rehabilitation. The SF-Oakland East Span was designed for a 150-year service life, the longest explicit design life of any major U.S. bridge structure.

**Duplex Coating System** — A two-layer corrosion protection system consisting of a metallic zinc layer applied to the steel substrate (by hot-dip galvanizing or thermal spray), followed by an organic coating system (epoxy intermediate and polyurethane topcoat). The zinc layer provides both barrier protection and galvanic (cathodic) protection at coating defects, while the organic topcoat extends the life of the zinc layer. Duplex systems typically provide 1.5 to 2.5 times the service life of the sum of the individual coating systems applied separately.

---

## E

**Electrochemical Potential** — The voltage difference between a metal electrode and a reference electrode (typically copper/copper sulfate, CSE, for soil and concrete; silver/silver chloride, Ag/AgCl, for marine environments). Used to assess corrosion risk and verify cathodic protection effectiveness.

**Embedded Reference Electrode** — A sensor permanently installed within a concrete structure to measure the electrochemical potential of the reinforcing steel relative to a known reference. Essential for long-term ICCP system monitoring and performance verification.

**Environmental Product Declaration (EPD)** — A standardized document (per ISO 14025 and EN 15804) that quantifies the environmental impact of a product based on life-cycle assessment. Increasingly required for construction materials on publicly funded projects.

**Epoxy-Coated Reinforcing Steel (ECR)** — Steel reinforcing bars coated with a fusion-bonded epoxy layer to provide a barrier against chloride ingress and corrosion initiation. Widely used in bridge decks and marine structures, though effectiveness depends on coating integrity during handling and placement.

---

## F

**Faying Surface** — The contact surface between two structural elements joined by bolting or riveting, as in a steel truss bridge gusset plate connection. Faying surfaces are particularly vulnerable to crevice corrosion and require special surface preparation and coating attention.

**FHWA** — Federal Highway Administration. The U.S. Department of Transportation agency that provides financial and technical support to state and local governments for the construction, preservation, and improvement of the National Highway System. Administers the Bridge Formula Program (BFP), Bridge Investment Program (BIP), and other federal-aid highway programs.

**Fracture-Critical Member (FCM)** — A steel tension member or tension component of a member whose failure would be expected to result in the collapse of the bridge. FCMs require special inspection procedures (arms-length, hands-on) at intervals not to exceed 24 months per the National Bridge Inspection Standards (NBIS).

---

## G

**Galvanic Series** — A ranking of metals and alloys in order of their electrochemical nobility (corrosion potential) in a given environment. Metals higher in the series (more active, e.g., zinc, magnesium) will corrode preferentially when galvanically coupled to metals lower in the series (more noble, e.g., steel, copper).

**Galvanizing** — The process of applying a zinc coating to steel by immersion in a bath of molten zinc (hot-dip galvanizing) or by electrodeposition. Provides both barrier and galvanic protection. Hot-dip galvanizing is generally limited to components that can fit within the zinc bath dimensions.

**GWP** — Global Warming Potential. A measure of the greenhouse gas emissions associated with a product, process, or activity over its life cycle, expressed in kilograms of carbon dioxide equivalent (kg CO₂-eq). An increasingly important metric in infrastructure material selection.

---

## H

**Half-Cell Potential** — The electrochemical potential of reinforcing steel measured against a reference electrode (typically copper/copper sulfate), used per ASTM C876 to assess the probability of active corrosion in reinforced concrete structures.

---

## I

**ICCP** — See Impressed Current Cathodic Protection under Cathodic Protection (CP).

**IIJA** — Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act. The 2021 U.S. federal legislation (Public Law 117-58) that authorized approximately $1.2 trillion in infrastructure spending, including the Bridge Formula Program and Bridge Investment Program administered by FHWA.

**ISO 12944** — International Organization for Standardization standard series (Parts 1–9) for "Corrosion Protection of Steel Structures by Protective Paint Systems." The most comprehensive international standard for specifying, applying, and evaluating protective coating systems on structural steel.

**ISO 2063** — International standard for thermal spraying of zinc, aluminum, and their alloys for corrosion protection. Specifies requirements for the thermal spray process, coating thickness, adhesion, and quality control.

---

## L

**LCCA** — Life-Cycle Cost Analysis. An economic evaluation method that accounts for all costs associated with an asset over its entire life cycle: initial capital costs, operation and maintenance costs, rehabilitation costs, and end-of-life disposal costs. All future costs are discounted to present value using a specified discount rate to enable comparison of alternatives with different cost profiles over time.

**Lead-Based Paint (LBP)** — Paint containing lead compounds as pigments or drying agents. Ubiquitous on steel bridges constructed before approximately 1980. Removal of lead-based paint is regulated by OSHA (29 CFR 1926.62) and Cal/OSHA (Title 8, Section 1532.1), requiring worker protection, containment, air monitoring, and hazardous waste disposal.

**Linear Polarization Resistance (LPR)** — An electrochemical technique for measuring the instantaneous corrosion rate of reinforcing steel in concrete by applying a small voltage perturbation and measuring the resulting current response. Used for corrosion monitoring and ICCP system performance verification.

---

## M

**Metallizing** — A term used interchangeably with "thermal spraying" in the bridge industry, referring specifically to the application of molten zinc, aluminum, or zinc-aluminum alloy to a prepared steel surface for corrosion protection.

**MMO Anode** — Mixed Metal Oxide anode. A titanium substrate coated with a mixture of precious metal oxides (typically iridium, ruthenium, and tantalum oxides) used as the anode material in impressed current cathodic protection systems. MMO anodes have very low consumption rates and long service lives (50+ years in properly designed systems).

**MTC** — Metropolitan Transportation Commission. The transportation planning, financing, and coordinating agency for the nine-county San Francisco Bay Area. MTC serves as both the regional metropolitan planning organization (MPO) and the Bay Area Toll Authority (BATA).

---

## N

**NBIS** — National Bridge Inspection Standards. Federal regulations (23 CFR Part 650, Subpart C) that establish requirements for the inspection, evaluation, and documentation of highway bridges in the United States. NBIS specifies inspection types, frequencies, personnel qualifications, and the 0–9 condition rating system used for bridge element assessment.

**NBI** — National Bridge Inventory. The FHWA-maintained database of all bridges on public roads in the United States. Contains NBIS inspection data, including condition ratings, for over 618,000 bridges.

---

## P

**P3** — Public-Private Partnership. A contractual arrangement between a public agency and a private-sector entity in which the private party assumes substantial financial, technical, and operational risk in the delivery of a public infrastructure project or service.

**Polarization** — The change in electrode potential caused by the flow of current to or from an electrode. In cathodic protection, the goal is to polarize the steel to a potential at which the corrosion rate is negligible (typically −850 mV vs. CSE, or a 100 mV polarization shift per NACE SP0169).

**Potentiostat** — An electronic instrument that controls the voltage difference between a working electrode and a reference electrode while measuring the current flow. Used in laboratory and field corrosion testing, including ICCP system evaluation.

---

## R

**Reference Electrode** — A stable electrode with a known, constant electrochemical potential used as a reference point for measuring the potential of other electrodes. Common types: copper/copper sulfate (CSE), silver/silver chloride (Ag/AgCl), and saturated calomel (SCE).

**Reinforcing Steel (Rebar)** — Steel bars or mesh embedded within concrete to provide tensile strength. In marine environments, chloride ingress through the concrete cover initiates corrosion of the reinforcing steel, leading to concrete cracking, spalling, and loss of structural capacity.

**Remaining Service Life (RSL)** — The estimated period of time for which a bridge element can continue to perform its intended function at the required level of reliability, given its current condition and the projected deterioration rate.

**Rust Grade** — The ASTM D610 rating system for evaluating the degree of rusting on painted steel surfaces, expressed on a 0–10 scale where 10 represents no rusting and 0 represents complete surface rusting.

---

## S

**Sacrificial Anode** — See Galvanic Cathodic Protection under Cathodic Protection (CP).

**SAS** — Self-Anchored Suspension Bridge. A suspension bridge design in which the main cables are anchored to the ends of the stiffening deck rather than to massive external anchorages. The SF-Oakland East Span is the world's longest self-anchored suspension bridge, with a single tower and a single main cable.

**Shear Link Beam** — A replaceable structural steel element designed to yield in shear during a major earthquake, dissipating seismic energy and protecting the primary structural elements from damage. Shear link beams are critical components of the seismic energy dissipation system on seismically retrofitted and new-design bridges, including the SF-Oakland East Span.

**Stray Current** — Electrical current flowing through paths other than the intended circuit. In cathodic protection, stray current from DC transit systems, welding operations, or other CP systems can cause unintended corrosion on adjacent structures.

**SSPC** — The Society for Protective Coatings (now part of AMPP). Historically published surface preparation and coating application standards widely used in the bridge industry, including the SSPC-SP series of abrasive blast cleaning standards.

---

## T

**Thermal Spray Zinc (TSZ)** — A corrosion protection process in which zinc wire or powder is fed into a spray gun, melted by an oxy-fuel flame or electric arc, and propelled by compressed air onto a prepared steel surface. The molten zinc particles flatten and solidify on impact, forming a dense, adherent metallic coating. TSZ provides both barrier and galvanic protection. When sealed and topcoated with an organic coating system, it forms a "TSZ duplex" coating system with a 50-to-75-year service life to first maintenance in C3/C4 environments.

**Transformer-Rectifier (T/R)** — An electrical device that converts alternating current (AC) line power to direct current (DC) at a controlled voltage for impressed current cathodic protection systems. Modern T/R units include remote monitoring capability via cellular or satellite telemetry.

**TRB** — Transportation Research Board. A division of the National Academies of Sciences, Engineering, and Medicine that promotes innovation and progress in transportation through research. Publishes the NCHRP (National Cooperative Highway Research Program) report series and convenes the TRB Annual Meeting.

---

## Z

**Zinc Coating Thickness** — The thickness of the applied zinc layer in a duplex or metallized coating system, typically measured in micrometers (μm) or mils (1 mil = 25.4 μm). ISO 2063 specifies minimum zinc thickness requirements by environmental classification: typically 100 μm for C3, 120 μm for C4, and 150 μm for C5 environments.

**Zinc-Aluminum Alloy Coating** — A thermal spray coating using a zinc-aluminum alloy wire (typically 85% Zn / 15% Al, or 50% Zn / 50% Al) instead of pure zinc. The aluminum addition provides increased hardness, improved high-temperature performance, and altered galvanic protection characteristics compared to pure zinc.
