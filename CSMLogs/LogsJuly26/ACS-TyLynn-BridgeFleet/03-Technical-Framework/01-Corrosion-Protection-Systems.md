## Executive Summary

This technical framework presents a comprehensive analysis of corrosion protection methodologies for steel and concrete bridge assets operating in aggressive marine environments. The document evaluates coating systems, cathodic protection technologies, and duplex coating economics with specific reference to Bay Area toll bridge conditions. The analysis draws upon empirical performance data from Norwegian, Oregon, and Florida transportation authorities, combined with lifecycle cost modeling calibrated to ISO 12944 environmental classifications. The recommended approach centers on transitioning from conventional alkyd and epoxy maintenance cycles to thermal spray zinc duplex systems, yielding service life extensions from 20–25 years to 50–100 years depending on environmental severity, while capturing substantial reductions in whole-life cost through the elimination of multiple intermediate maintenance interventions.

## Section 1: The Corrosion Problem

### 1.1 National Economic Impact

Corrosion of transportation infrastructure represents one of the most significant deferred maintenance liabilities confronting state departments of transportation. The AMPP (Association for Materials Protection and Performance, formerly NACE International) estimates that corrosion costs US bridges $13.6 billion annually in direct maintenance expenditures. This figure encompasses coating removal and reapplication, structural steel replacement, concrete patch repair, cathodic protection system installation and monitoring, and associated engineering oversight.

Indirect costs substantially exceed direct maintenance expenditures. When a bridge lane closes for coating operations, traffic diversion generates measurable economic losses through increased travel time, fuel consumption, vehicle operating costs, and lost productivity. Modeling conducted by the Federal Highway Administration (FHWA) indicates that user delay costs during major bridge rehabilitation can range from $1,500 to $15,000 per lane-mile per day depending on traffic volumes, with urban corridors typically falling at the upper end of this spectrum. For a major coating project requiring 18–24 months of lane closures — typical for a large suspension or truss bridge — the cumulative economic impact on users can reach tens of millions of dollars, frequently exceeding the direct construction cost of the coating work itself.

### 1.2 Environmental Classification

The Bay Area bridge fleet operates under environmental conditions that rank among the most aggressive in North America for steel corrosion. Under the ISO 12944-2 classification system for atmospheric corrosivity, the region's bridges fall predominantly into categories C4 (high corrosivity) and C5-M (very high corrosivity — marine). Key environmental factors include:

- **Salt fog exposure**: Prevailing onshore winds carry chloride-laden aerosols across the Bay, with chloride deposition rates measured at 60–300 mg Cl⁻/m²/day in near-shore zones, corresponding to ISO corrosivity category C5.
- **Tidal splash zones**: Structural elements within 3 meters of mean high water experience alternating wet-dry cycling with concentrated chloride solutions, creating localized conditions that can exceed C5 classification.
- **De-icing salt exposure**: Approach structures and lower deck elevations on bridges such as the Bay Bridge East Span and Richmond–San Rafael Bridge experience seasonal exposure to sodium chloride and calcium chloride de-icing compounds, accelerating corrosion at expansion joints, bearing seats, and drainage discharge points.
- **Atmospheric pollutants**: Industrial emissions and vehicular exhaust contribute sulfur dioxide and nitrogen oxide deposition, which acidify surface moisture films and accelerate both uniform corrosion and pitting rates on unprotected steel.

### 1.3 Traditional Coating Performance

Conventional alkyd-based coating systems, widely applied to bridge steel through the 1980s, exhibit a service life of 9 to 13 years in moderate to severe environments before significant coating breakdown necessitates full removal and reapplication. Failure modes include:

1. **Undercutting corrosion**: Moisture penetration at holidays and mechanical damage sites propagates beneath the coating film, causing adhesion loss and blistering over progressively larger areas.
2. **Saponification**: The alkaline environment generated at cathodic sites beneath intact alkyd films chemically attacks the ester bonds in the binder, softening the coating and accelerating disbondment.
3. **Chalking and UV degradation**: Surface resin degradation from ultraviolet exposure produces a friable chalk layer that, while not directly compromising barrier properties, indicates progressive binder deterioration.
4. **Edge retraction**: Coating films thin at sharp edges and corners due to surface tension effects during application, creating preferred sites for corrosion initiation.

The lifecycle consequence is that a bridge protected with alkyd coatings requires complete coating removal and replacement approximately every 12 years, with intermediate spot repairs at 6- to 8-year intervals, generating a recurring cost and traffic disruption cycle that compounds over the structure's design life.

### 2.2 Detailed System Descriptions

#### 2.2.1 Epoxy Zinc-Rich Systems (Current Industry Standard)

The conventional three-coat epoxy system consists of an inorganic or organic zinc-rich primer (65–85% zinc dust by weight in the dried film), an epoxy intermediate coat providing barrier properties and build, and an aliphatic polyurethane topcoat for color retention and UV resistance. Total dry film thickness (DFT) typically ranges from 10 to 16 mils (250–400 μm). Surface preparation requires SSPC-SP 10 (near-white metal blast cleaning) with a minimum 2.5 mil (63 μm) angular anchor profile.

Performance in C4-C5 environments reaches 15 to 20 years when application quality is strictly controlled. However, field conditions on bridge structures frequently compromise coating integrity: moisture condensation during overnight application windows, embedded chlorides in pitted steel that survive blast cleaning, and thickness variations at complex connection geometries all contribute to premature failure at 10 to 15 years in the most aggressive marine exposures.

#### 2.2.2 Thermal Spray Zinc (TSZ)

Thermal spray zinc application — also termed metallizing or wire arc spraying — deposits molten zinc droplets onto a prepared steel surface using either electric arc or combustion flame as the heat source. The process produces a coating 8 to 14 mils (200–350 μm) thick with adhesion exceeding 1,000 psi (6.9 MPa), compared to 300–500 psi for conventional liquid coatings. The zinc serves as both a barrier layer and a galvanic sacrificial anode: at any coating discontinuity, the zinc corrodes preferentially to the steel substrate, preventing undercutting propagation.

TSZ has been applied to bridge structures since 1965, with the earliest installations now exceeding 55 years of service without requiring recoating. The Norwegian Public Roads Administration (NPRA) has standardized TSZ duplex for all major bridge steel since the 1970s, accumulating performance data across diverse coastal, fjord, and inland environments.

Key operational parameters for TSZ application include:
- Surface preparation to SSPC-SP 5 (white metal blast) with 3.0–4.0 mil angular profile
- Application within 4 hours of blast completion to prevent flash rusting
- Zinc wire conforming to ASTM B833 or ISO 14919 (minimum 99.9% purity)
- Arc spray parameters: 24–32V, 100–300A, standoff distance 150–250 mm
- Sealant application (thin-film penetrating sealer) within 24 hours of spray completion to fill interconnected porosity

#### 2.2.3 TSZ Duplex Systems

The duplex concept combines TSZ with an organic topcoat system (typically epoxy intermediate plus polyurethane or polysiloxane finish) applied over the sealed metallized surface. The zinc substrate provides galvanic protection at any points where the organic topcoat is breached, while the topcoat dramatically reduces the zinc consumption rate by shielding it from direct environmental exposure. The synergistic effect extends the total system life far beyond either component alone.

NPRA inspection records for 62 TSZ duplex bridges, encompassing structures from 1965 through present, demonstrate the following coating performance index (CPI) values — defined as the years to first major maintenance (5% or greater coating breakdown):

| ISO 12944 Category | CPI (Years) | Equivalent Environment Example |
|-------------------|-------------|-------------------------------|
| C2 (Low) | 100+ | Rural inland, low humidity |
| C3 (Medium) | 60–80 | Urban inland, temperate coastal |
| C4 (High) | 40–50 | Industrial coastal, saline estuary |
| C5-M (Very High Marine) | 25–35 | Surf zone, salt spray, offshore |

For Bay Area bridge applications in C4-C5 environments, TSZ duplex systems project to a 35- to 50-year service life before requiring significant maintenance intervention, compared to 15 to 20 years for the current epoxy standard and 9 to 13 years for legacy alkyd.

#### 2.2.4 Nano-Enhanced Coating Technologies

Pasquick polyaspartic coating technology, developed by Covestro (formerly Bayer MaterialScience), reduces the conventional three-coat system to two coats by eliminating the separate primer layer. The polyaspartic chemistry enables a single formulation to achieve both adhesion and barrier functions while curing approximately four times faster than conventional epoxy-polyurethane systems. This acceleration substantially reduces application windows and weather-related downtime, with full cure achievable in 2 to 4 hours versus 12 to 24 hours for standard epoxies.

Graphene-enhanced coatings represent an emerging class of protective systems that incorporate graphene nanoplatelets at loadings of 0.1–2.0% by weight into epoxy, polyurethane, or polysiloxane binder systems. The two-dimensional carbon lattice creates a highly tortuous diffusion path for water, oxygen, and chloride ions, dramatically reducing permeation rates. Independently measured surface roughness values of 38 to 70 μm for graphene-modified epoxy coatings — versus industry standard values of 120 to 300 μm for conventional marine coatings — correlate with reduced fouling adhesion, enabling biocide-free foul-release performance. The nanoscale reinforcement also improves abrasion resistance, impact toughness, and flexibility without increasing film thickness.

### 2.3 Cost Modeling Methodology

Lifecycle cost comparisons for coating systems employ net present value (NPV) analysis over a standard 75-year evaluation period with discount rates of 3% and 7% (consistent with OMB Circular A-94 guidance for public infrastructure). Cost elements include:

- **C₀**: Initial surface preparation and coating application
- **Cⱼ**: Maintenance interventions at year j (spot repair, partial recoat, full removal and replacement)
- **Cₐ**: Annualized inspection and condition monitoring
- **Cᵤ**: User delay costs during each intervention (calculated from traffic modeling)
- **Cₑ**: Environmental compliance costs (containment, hazardous waste disposal)

The cost factors for maintenance operations relative to initial application cost (C) are:
- Spot repair (minor): 0.2C to 0.3C
- Repair (moderate, 5–15% area): 0.4C to 0.6C
- Maintenance coating (full overcoat): 0.8C to 1.0C
- Full removal and replacement recoating: 1.5C to 1.8C

The dominant lifecycle cost driver is the frequency of major recoating interventions, each of which incurs not only the direct coating cost but also full scaffolding, containment, abrasive blasting, and traffic control costs that frequently equal or exceed the coating material and labor costs themselves.

## Section 3: Cathodic Protection for Concrete

### 3.1 Technology Overview

Impressed current cathodic protection (ICCP) is the established electrochemical method for arresting chloride-induced reinforcement corrosion in concrete bridge structures. A low-voltage direct current is applied between an inert anode installed on the concrete surface and the embedded reinforcing steel (which functions as the cathode). The imposed current polarizes the steel to a potential at which corrosion is thermodynamically or kinetically suppressed, typically −700 mV to −1,100 mV versus a copper/copper sulfate reference electrode (CSE), depending on the protection criterion employed.

### 3.2 TSZ Anodes for ICCP

Thermal spray zinc serves a dual role in ICCP applications: it functions as the impressed current anode during active protection and reverts to sacrificial anode behavior if the impressed current system experiences an interruption. This inherent redundancy is critical for bridge applications where power supply continuity cannot be guaranteed and where access for anode replacement is prohibitive.

The Oregon Department of Transportation (ODOT) has developed the most extensive North American database on TSZ anode performance for bridge ICCP. Since 1998, ODOT has installed TSZ ICCP anodes on 15 historic reinforced concrete arch bridges along the Oregon coast, selected for their architectural significance and high replacement cost. Key program metrics include:

- **Design anode life**: 25 years at a current density of 1.0–2.0 mA/ft² (10.8–21.5 mA/m²)
- **Protection criterion**: 100 mV polarization decay over 4 hours (NACE SP0290)
- **Bridge service life extension**: Structures originally designed for 50-year service life now projected to reach 120+ years through combined ICCP and structural monitoring
- **Remote monitoring**: Reference electrodes and depolarization measurements captured at quarterly intervals via telemetry, enabling trend analysis and proactive current adjustment

### 3.3 Comparative Anode Costs

Cost data for concrete ICCP anode systems, adjusted from 1992 procurement benchmarks to current (2026) estimated values:

| Anode System | Installed Cost (1992, $/ft²) | Estimated Current Installed Cost (2026, $/ft²) | Typical Service Life (years) |
|-------------|------------------------------|-----------------------------------------------|------------------------------|
| Arc-Sprayed Sacrificial Zinc | $3.34 | $8–15 | 15–25 |
| Titanium Mesh (MMO-coated) ICCP | $21.87 | $45–65 | 50–75 |
| Conductive Coating (Carbon-filled) | $12.34 | $25–35 | 15–20 |
| TSZ Zinc ICCP (Combined) | $4.50–6.00 (1998) | $15–22 | 25–30 |

The arc-sprayed TSZ anode offers the most favorable cost-to-performance ratio for ICCP applications on concrete bridge elements, particularly for geometrically complex surfaces (arched soffits, beam-column joints, curved fascia) where mesh anode installation labor escalates disproportionately.

### 3.4 Case Studies

#### 3.4.1 Oregon Coastal Bridge Program

Beginning in 1998, ODOT initiated a systematic ICCP program addressing 120+ reinforced concrete bridges along US Highway 101, where chloride exposure from Pacific Ocean spray had caused widespread reinforcement corrosion. The program budgeted $150 million over a 15-year implementation horizon, prioritizing bridges based on condition rating, traffic volume, and structural redundancy. Operational experience has demonstrated that 5–10% cost savings are achievable through optimized current output adjustments based on seasonal variations in concrete resistivity and chloride concentration profiles, rather than constant-current operation throughout the year.

#### 3.4.2 Howard Frankland Bridge, Florida

The Howard Frankland Bridge (I-275) crossing Tampa Bay provides a long-term case study in CP economics. Over a 30-year monitoring period, the Florida Department of Transportation (FDOT) invested approximately $15 million in incremental cathodic protection system installations — including conductive coating anodes, titanium mesh segments, and TSZ anode expansions — to address progressive reinforcement corrosion in the bridge's precast concrete piles and deck soffits. Independent lifecycle cost analysis concluded that this $15 million CP investment avoided an $865 million bridge replacement that would have been necessitated by unchecked corrosion deterioration within the same timeframe. The benefit-to-cost ratio of approximately 57:1 illustrates the economic leverage achieved when electrochemical protection is applied proactively rather than reactively.

#### 3.4.3 Crescent Beach Bridge, Florida

The Crescent Beach Bridge (SR 206) near St. Augustine, Florida, received a $3.9 million comprehensive ICCP system installation in the mid-1990s, addressing extensive chloride-induced delamination and spalling in the reinforced concrete arch ribs and pier caps. Post-installation monitoring over 25+ years has documented arrest of active corrosion, with depolarization measurements consistently exceeding the 100 mV criterion. The structure, which was approaching a condition rating that would have triggered replacement planning, has now been extended to 40+ years of additional service beyond the CP installation date, with no major structural concrete repairs required in the protected zones.

### 3.5 Corrosion Monitoring and Forecasting

Continuous monitoring of ICCP system performance employs embedded silver/silver chloride (Ag/AgCl) and manganese dioxide (MnO₂) reference electrodes installed at representative locations during anode application. Data collection at quarterly to semi-annual intervals tracks:

1. **Instant-off potential**: The polarized potential of the reinforcing steel immediately after interrupting the protection current, indicating the electrochemical condition at the steel-concrete interface.
2. **Depolarization rate**: The 4-hour, 24-hour, and 72-hour potential decay after current interruption, providing a direct measure of corrosion activity. The 100 mV decay over 4 hours is the most widely accepted protection criterion (NACE SP0290-2007).
3. **Circuit resistance trends**: Increasing resistance indicates anode deterioration, connection degradation, or concrete drying — each requiring different operational responses.

Degradation forecasting frameworks integrate these electrochemical measurements with chloride diffusion modeling (Fick's second law with time-dependent diffusion coefficients) and concrete resistivity data to project remaining service life and optimize current output schedules.

## Section 4: Duplex Coating Economics

### 4.1 Lifecycle Cost Framework

The economic case for TSZ duplex coating systems rests on the principle that the incremental initial investment — typically 50% to 80% above a conventional three-coat epoxy system — is multiplied by the avoided cost of one or more complete recoating cycles over the structure's remaining service life. When scaffolding, containment, and traffic management costs are incorporated, the financial tipping point occurs when a duplex system avoids even a single maintenance recoating event.

Define the net present value differential between coating systems A and B as:

ΔNPV = Σₜ₌₀ᵗ⁼ᵀ [Cᵦ(ᵗ) − Cₐ(ᵗ)] × (1 + r)⁻ᵗ

where T is the evaluation period, r is the discount rate, and C(t) represents the sum of direct construction costs and user delay costs at each intervention year t. For a 75-year evaluation at 3% discount rate, typical results show:

| Environment | Epoxy 3-Coat NPV (Indexed) | TSZ Duplex NPV (Indexed) | NPV Savings |
|------------|---------------------------|-------------------------|-------------|
| C3 (Medium) | 100 | 58 | 42% |
| C4 (High) | 100 | 65 | 35% |
| C5-M (Very High Marine) | 100 | 82 | 18% |

These figures account for the epoxy system requiring recoating at 20, 40, and 60 years in C3, at 15, 30, 45, and 60 years in C4, and at 12, 24, 36, 48, and 60 years in C5.

### 4.2 Social Cost Integration

User delay costs (UDC) during bridge maintenance closures constitute a significant component of the total economic calculus. The FHWA Work Zone Road User Costs methodology quantifies these impacts through:

- **Additional travel time**: Value of time at $18–$35 per vehicle-hour for passenger cars, $35–$90 for commercial trucks (2026 adjusted values).
- **Additional vehicle operating costs**: Fuel, maintenance, and depreciation attributable to detour routing and congestion delay.
- **Crash cost differentials**: Work zone crash rate multipliers.
- **Emission costs**: Additional CO₂, NOₓ, and particulate emissions from congestion and detour mileage.

For a major toll bridge such as the Richmond–San Rafael Bridge (ADT approximately 80,000 vehicles), a 12-month lane closure for recoating can generate user delay costs of $25–$60 million, comparable to or exceeding the direct coating project cost. When these social costs are incorporated into the NPV calculation, duplex systems that eliminate even one future recoating event generate benefit-to-cost ratios exceeding 3:1 relative to conventional epoxy maintenance cycles.

### 4.3 Norwegian Precedent

The Norwegian Public Roads Administration (NPRA) has accumulated the world's most comprehensive dataset on long-term TSZ duplex performance. Beginning with the 1965 construction of the Tromsø Bridge — a 1,036-meter cantilever span crossing the Tromsøysundet strait at 69°N latitude — NPRA adopted metallizing plus organic topcoat as the standard steel protection specification for all major bridge structures. The decision was driven by:

1. **Access constraints**: Many Norwegian bridges span deep fjords where scaffolding erection is exceptionally costly and weather-restricted.
2. **Maintenance philosophy**: NPRA explicitly adopted a "build it and leave it" approach, accepting higher initial cost to minimize future interventions.
3. **Operational experience**: Early adoptions demonstrated that TSZ duplex systems applied in the 1960s and 1970s remained in serviceable condition through the 2000s without requiring recoating — a 35–45 year performance record that validated the lifecycle cost model.

Current NPRA specifications require thermal spray zinc (minimum 100 μm) with epoxy/polyurethane topcoat (minimum 150 μm total) for all structural steel in C3 and above environments, with the explicit objective of achieving 50+ years to first major maintenance.

## Section 5: Bay Area Application

### 5.1 Current Program Status

The Bay Area Toll Authority (BATA) Capital Improvement Program (CIP) allocates approximately $500 million for structural steel coating work across the seven state-owned toll bridges over the current multi-year programming horizon. This expenditure reflects the legacy coating inventory — predominantly alkyd and early epoxy systems applied during the 1980s and 1990s — reaching the end of their effective service lives simultaneously across multiple structures.

Key project status:

- **Richmond–San Rafael Bridge**: $68 million comprehensive steel coating project completed June 2024. The project addressed the cantilever truss spans and approach structures with a conventional three-coat epoxy zinc-rich system. Given the structure's C4-C5 marine exposure, the applied system projects to a 20- to 25-year interval before the next major coating intervention.

- **Carquinez Bridge**: Paint project scheduled for fiscal year 2027/28. Condition assessments have documented deterioration of the existing coating system, particularly at truss connections, gusset plates, and lower chord members where moisture entrapment accelerates corrosion. The project represents a decision point where conversion to duplex TSZ could be evaluated against the lifecycle economics presented in this framework.

- **San Francisco–Oakland Bay Bridge West Span**: Major coating project programmed for fiscal year 2024/25, with costs materially impacted by new California Department of Public Health (CDPH) lead exposure regulations that impose stricter containment, air monitoring, and worker protection requirements during abrasive blasting of lead-based coatings. The additional compliance cost — estimated at 15% to 25% above historical coating project unit costs — further strengthens the economic case for coating systems that minimize the number of future blasting operations.

- **San Mateo–Hayward Bridge**: Concrete rehabilitation program comprising $45 million Phase 1 and $118 million Phase 2, addressing chloride-induced reinforcement corrosion in the bridge's reinforced concrete approach spans and pier elements. The scope includes concrete removal and replacement, localized cathodic protection, and waterproofing membrane installation.

### 5.2 Strategic Recommendation

The central recommendation of this framework is the systematic conversion of the Bay Area toll bridge coating maintenance program from conventional epoxy recoating cycles to TSZ duplex systems applied at each scheduled major intervention. The rationale follows directly from the data presented in Sections 1 through 4:

1. **Each epoxy recoating represents a missed opportunity**: When a bridge is fully scaffolded, contained, and blast-cleaned — incurring the full mobilization, traffic control, and environmental compliance costs — the incremental cost of applying a TSZ duplex system versus a conventional epoxy system is approximately 25% to 40% of the total project cost. This incremental investment eliminates at least one and possibly two future recoating cycles, each of which would independently cost 100% to 150% of the current project cost in inflation-adjusted terms.

2. **The lead abatement regulatory trajectory increases future intervention costs**: California's progressively stricter lead exposure standards make each future abrasive blasting operation more expensive than the last. Duplex coating systems that extend the interval between blasting events from 20 years to 50+ years reduce the number of future lead compliance events from 3–4 down to 0–1 over the bridge's remaining service life.

3. **User delay cost avoidance dominates the economic analysis**: At a conservative estimate of $30 million in user delay costs per major recoating event for a high-volume Bay Area toll bridge, avoiding even one future recoating cycle recovers more than the entire incremental cost of upgrading to duplex TSZ across multiple structures.

4. **The San Mateo–Hayward concrete program illustrates the parallel opportunity**: Cathodic protection investments of $163 million in the current program, if extended to comprehensive ICCP coverage using TSZ anodes across all chloride-contaminated elements, could extend the structure's reinforced concrete service life from the design 50 years to 100+ years, with monitoring data providing the empirical basis for informed capital planning rather than reactive repair.

### 5.3 Implementation Pathway

A phased conversion strategy would begin with the Carquinez Bridge paint project (FY27/28) as a pilot TSZ duplex application, leveraging the project's existing mobilization and containment infrastructure. Performance monitoring over the subsequent 5-year period would validate the lifecycle cost model with structure-specific data, building the technical and procurement foundation for adoption of duplex TSZ as the BATA standard specification for all future steel coating interventions across the remaining bridge fleet.
