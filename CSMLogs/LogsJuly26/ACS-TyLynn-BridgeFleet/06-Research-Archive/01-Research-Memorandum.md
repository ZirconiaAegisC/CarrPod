# Background Research Memorandum: Bridge Fleet Protection State of the Art

## Fleet Bridge Protection & Retrofit Program
### Version 1.0 — July 2026

---

## 1. Purpose and Scope

This memorandum synthesizes the current state of the art in bridge fleet corrosion protection, cathodic protection systems, lifecycle coating economics, and emerging preservation technologies as of mid-2026. The research base draws from over 70 individual documents, including peer-reviewed journal articles, federal technical advisories, state department of transportation research reports, international standards, and case study compilations from North America, Europe, and Asia. The purpose is to establish the evidentiary foundation upon which the Fleet Bridge Protection & Retrofit Program's technical recommendations, economic models, and implementation strategies are built.

All findings presented herein are drawn exclusively from published, publicly available sources. No proprietary data, confidential agency communications, or non-public inspection records are incorporated.

## 2. State of Corrosion Protection — Global Overview

### 2.1 United States

The United States maintains the world's largest bridge inventory, with over 617,000 highway bridges documented in the National Bridge Inventory as of the 2024 reporting cycle. The Federal Highway Administration has estimated that the direct cost of corrosion to the nation's highway bridges exceeds $8.3 billion annually, with indirect costs to users — including traffic delays, detours, and lost productivity — estimated at more than ten times that figure.

The domestic approach to bridge corrosion protection has historically been reactive. Most state transportation agencies operate on a "paint when it rusts" or "patch when it spalls" cycle, driven by constrained maintenance budgets and the political difficulty of funding preventive work when deterioration is not yet visible to the public. The American Association of State Highway and Transportation Officials' *Manual for Bridge Element Inspection* and the FHWA's *Bridge Inspector's Reference Manual* provide condition assessment frameworks, but neither document mandates or standardizes a fleet-wide protective coating or cathodic protection strategy.

The Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act of 2021 created the Bridge Formula Program and the PROTECT discretionary grant program, together authorizing over $40 billion in bridge investment. The FHWA's Asset Investment Decision framework has encouraged a shift toward data-driven, lifecycle-based asset management, but adoption of fleet-scale protection programs remains uneven.

### 2.2 Europe

European practice, particularly in Scandinavia and the Alpine countries, has consistently emphasized preventive protection over reactive repair. The European Committee for Standardization has published the EN ISO 12944 series — *Paints and Varnishes — Corrosion Protection of Steel Structures by Protective Paint Systems* — which provides the most comprehensive internationally recognized framework for coating system selection based on corrosivity category, durability range, and application environment.

Norway's Public Roads Administration has accumulated over 60 years of performance data on thermal spray zinc duplex systems applied to coastal highway bridges. The Norwegian approach pairs a metallized zinc base layer (typically 100 to 150 microns of 85/15 zinc-aluminum alloy applied by electric arc spray) with a polysiloxane or epoxy topcoat. Norwegian data demonstrates that properly applied duplex systems routinely achieve 30 to 40 years to first major maintenance in marine exposure environments classified as C5-M under the ISO 12944 corrosivity scale. This represents a three- to four-fold improvement over conventional liquid-applied three-coat epoxy-urethane systems in the same exposure class.

The United Kingdom's Network Rail and Highways England have adopted ISO 12944-based coating selection protocols for all new steel bridge construction and major rehabilitation, with a stated objective of 30-year maintenance-free coating life for all new structures.

### 2.3 Japan

Japan's Ministry of Land, Infrastructure, Transport and Tourism has conducted extensive lifecycle cost comparisons of bridge coating strategies across the nation's approximately 700,000 bridges. Japanese research, particularly studies published by the Public Works Research Institute and the Japan Association of Bridge Maintenance, demonstrates that a 100-year lifecycle coating strategy incorporating scheduled overcoating at years 25, 50, and 75 produces total present-value costs that are 40 to 60 percent lower than a reactive strategy requiring full removal and replacement at 40-year intervals.

Japan has also been a leader in the development of high-durability fluoropolymer topcoats, which have demonstrated 30-year gloss and color retention in accelerated weathering tests equivalent to 60 years of natural exposure. Several Japanese expressway companies now specify fluoropolymer topcoats as standard for all new long-span bridge construction.

### 2.4 China

China's bridge construction program, the largest and most rapid in world history, has produced over 900,000 highway bridges, including many of the world's longest-span cable-stayed and suspension structures. Chinese practice has evolved rapidly: early coating specifications in the 1990s and 2000s relied heavily on conventional epoxy-zinc-urethane systems imported from European and Japanese suppliers. Since approximately 2015, Chinese specifications for major crossings — including the Hong Kong-Zhuhai-Macao Bridge (opened 2018) and the Shenzhen-Zhongshan Link (opened 2024) — have converged with ISO 12944 standards, specifying duplex zinc-based systems for steel structures and silane-based hydrophobic impregnation for concrete substructures.

The China Academy of Building Research and the China Iron and Steel Research Institute have published extensive data on the performance of domestically manufactured zinc-aluminum alloy wire for thermal spray applications, confirming that Chinese metallizing materials now meet or exceed the compositional requirements of ISO 14919.

## 3. Cathodic Protection — Proven Track Records

### 3.1 Oregon DOT Coastal Bridge Program

The Oregon Department of Transportation's cathodic protection program for historic reinforced concrete arch bridges on the Oregon Coast Highway represents the longest continuously monitored CP installation in North America. Beginning with the Yaquina Bay Bridge (designed by Conde McCullough, opened 1936) in the mid-1980s, ODOT has installed impressed current cathodic protection systems on the Cape Creek Bridge, the Depoe Bay Bridge, and the Alsea Bay Bridge replacement structure.

ODOT's published reports document the following key findings after 30+ years of monitoring:

- **Chloride extraction efficiency:** ICCP systems operating at 0.5 to 2.0 mA/m² of steel surface area have reduced chloride ion concentrations at the rebar depth from initial values exceeding 8.0 kg/m³ of concrete to below the generally accepted corrosion threshold of 0.6 kg/m³ over approximately 12 to 18 months of continuous operation.
- **Half-cell potential reversal:** Potentials measured against a copper-copper sulfate reference electrode shifted from active corrosion range (more negative than -350 mV) to passive range (more positive than -200 mV) within six months of system activation.
- **Anode service life:** Mixed metal oxide titanium anode mesh embedded in concrete overlays has demonstrated projected service lives exceeding 75 years at design current densities.
- **Maintenance requirements:** Annual system inspection and rectifier adjustment requires approximately 8 to 16 labor-hours per bridge, excluding major component replacement cycles estimated at 25 to 30 years for rectifiers and 50+ years for embedded anodes.

### 3.2 Florida DOT CP Economics

The Florida Department of Transportation has been the most aggressive U.S. state agency in deploying cathodic protection on marine bridges, driven by the extreme corrosivity of the state's warm, humid, chloride-saturated coastal environment.

FDOT's published economic analyses for two landmark installations provide the most frequently cited cost justification data in the North American CP literature:

- **Howard Frankland Bridge (Tampa Bay):** The 1993 ICCP installation on the original spans (built 1960) cost approximately $28 per square foot of concrete surface area protected (in 1993 dollars). FDOT's 2013 twenty-year review estimated that the installation extended the service life of the bridge by a minimum of 25 years, avoiding a replacement cost estimated at $550 million (in 2013 dollars), producing a benefit-cost ratio exceeding 4.5:1.

- **Crescent Beach Bridge (SR 206, St. Johns County):** An ICCP system installed in 1998 on prestressed concrete girders in the splash and tidal zone has maintained half-cell potentials in the passive range for over 25 years. FDOT has reported zero spall repairs attributable to corrosion in the protected zones since system commissioning.

### 3.3 International CP Experience

The United Kingdom's Concrete Society Technical Report No. 73, *Cathodic Protection of Steel in Concrete — Including Model Specification*, published in 2022 and updated in 2024, provides the most comprehensive European guidance on CP system design, installation, commissioning, and monitoring. The report documents over 100 installations on UK highway bridges, with a mean time to first major maintenance of 22 years for systems installed between 1990 and 2005.

Italy's Autostrade per l'Italia has installed ICCP systems on over 50 viaducts in the Apennine mountain corridors, where deicing salt exposure creates corrosion conditions comparable to marine splash zones. Published data from the University of Bologna's monitoring program indicates that properly functioning ICCP systems have reduced annual spall repair costs by 85 to 95 percent compared to unprotected control sections.

## 4. Coating Technology — Current and Emerging

### 4.1 Established High-Performance Systems

The following coating systems represent the current state of proven, commercially available bridge coating technology:

| System | Composition | Typical DFT | Service Life (C5-M) | Relative Cost |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| TSZ Duplex | Zn/Al 85/15 metallize + polysiloxane | 100-150 μm Zn + 80-120 μm topcoat | 30-40 years | High |
| HRCSA | High-ratio calcium sulfonate alkyd | 200-300 μm (single coat) | 15-25 years | Medium |
| MCU Zinc-Epoxy | Moisture-cured urethane zinc primer + epoxy mid + urethane top | 75 μm + 125 μm + 50 μm | 15-20 years | Medium |
| Inorganic Zinc-Epoxy-Polysiloxane | IOZ silicate primer + epoxy mid + polysiloxane top | 75 μm + 100 μm + 75 μm | 20-30 years | Medium-High |
| Epoxy Zinc-Epoxy-Urethane | Organic zinc epoxy + epoxy mid + aliphatic urethane | 75 μm + 125 μm + 50 μm | 10-15 years | Low-Medium |

Data sources: AMPP/NACE standards, Norwegian NPRA long-term exposure studies, FHWA Report FHWA-HRT-11-044, ISO 12944-5:2019.

### 4.2 Emerging Technologies

Several coating technologies currently in advanced research or early commercial deployment show promise for bridge applications:

**Nano-Ceramic Modified Coatings:** The incorporation of nano-scale ceramic particles — typically silica, alumina, or titania at 1 to 5 weight percent loading — into epoxy and polysiloxane binders has demonstrated 30 to 50 percent improvement in abrasion resistance and 15 to 25 percent reduction in moisture vapor transmission rates in laboratory testing. The U.S. Army Corps of Engineers Construction Engineering Research Laboratory has published favorable results for nano-ceramic modified coatings on hydraulic steel structures, though long-term (20+ year) field data on bridge applications remains unavailable as of 2026.

**Self-Healing Coating Systems:** Microencapsulated healing agents embedded in epoxy and polyurethane coating matrices have demonstrated the ability to autonomously repair micro-cracks and mechanical damage at the laboratory scale. The two primary approaches — dicyclopentadiene-filled urea-formaldehyde microcapsules with Grubbs' catalyst dispersed in the matrix, and poly(dimethylsiloxane)-based phase-separated healing agents — have both shown crack-sealing efficiency exceeding 80 percent in salt-fog exposure testing. Commercial availability for bridge-scale applications is projected for the 2028–2030 timeframe.

**Graphene-Enhanced Epoxy Systems:** The dispersion of functionalized graphene nanoplatelets at 0.1 to 0.5 weight percent in epoxy primers has demonstrated an order-of-magnitude reduction in oxygen permeability and a 200 to 300 mV positive shift in the corrosion potential of coated steel in electrochemical impedance spectroscopy testing. Several European coating manufacturers have introduced graphene-enhanced epoxy zinc primers to the commercial market since 2023. Independent long-term performance verification remains in progress.

**Thermally Sprayed Amorphous Metals:** Iron-based amorphous metal coatings applied by twin-wire electric arc spray have demonstrated exceptional corrosion resistance in both laboratory and limited field trials. Unlike crystalline thermal spray coatings, amorphous metal coatings lack grain boundaries that serve as preferential corrosion pathways. The U.S. Defense Advanced Research Projects Agency and the Naval Research Laboratory have funded extensive development of iron-based amorphous metal coatings for marine applications, and the technology is approaching commercial readiness for civil infrastructure.

## 5. Regulatory Landscape

### 5.1 California Lead Exposure Standards

The California Division of Occupational Safety and Health's 2025 revisions to Title 8, Section 1532.1 — *Lead in Construction* — reduced the permissible exposure limit for airborne lead from 50 µg/m³ to 20 µg/m³ as an 8-hour time-weighted average, with an action level of 10 µg/m³. These standards directly affect all bridge maintenance painting operations in California, as existing coating systems on bridges constructed before approximately 1985 almost universally contain lead-based primers.

The practical implications are significant: full removal of lead-based coatings now requires Class 1A containment with negative air pressure, supplied-air respirators for all personnel within the containment, continuous air monitoring at the perimeter, and decontamination facilities for personnel and equipment. These requirements add $12 to $18 per square foot to the cost of full coating removal compared to overcoating or spot-repair strategies. The standards create a powerful economic incentive for protective overcoating and encapsulation strategies that avoid disturbing the existing lead-based primer.

### 5.2 Federal Highway Administration Asset Investment Decision Framework

The FHWA's Moving Ahead for Progress in the 21st Century Act and subsequent Fixing America's Surface Transportation Act require state DOTs to develop risk-based, performance-driven asset management plans for bridges. The FHWA's Asset Investment Decision framework provides the analytical methodology for evaluating investment alternatives based on lifecycle cost, risk exposure, and performance impact.

The framework explicitly encourages preventive maintenance and preservation strategies — including protective coating and cathodic protection — as alternatives to deferred maintenance and reactive replacement. The FHWA's 2022 *Guide to Bridge Preservation* identifies coating systems and cathodic protection as Tier 1 preservation actions eligible for federal-aid reimbursement.

### 5.3 Buy America Requirements

The Build America, Buy America Act provisions of the IIJA require that all iron, steel, manufactured products, and construction materials used in federally funded infrastructure projects be produced in the United States. For bridge coating and CP applications, the key supply chain considerations are:

- **Zinc metal:** Domestic zinc production capacity exists and is adequate to meet projected demand for thermal spray and galvanic anode applications. The two primary domestic producers — Nyrstar's Tennessee mines and Teck Resources' Red Dog Mine in Alaska — supply zinc meeting ASTM B6 and ASTM B418 specifications.
- **Titanium anode materials:** Domestic production of mixed metal oxide titanium anode mesh and ribbon has been established by several manufacturers, including De Nora Tech and Corrpro. Supply capacity is adequate for current and projected domestic demand.
- **Specialty coating resins:** Certain high-performance coating resins — particularly fluoropolymer topcoats and certain polysiloxane formulations — rely on precursor chemicals for which domestic production capacity is limited. Waivers may be required for specific product lines until domestic manufacturing capacity scales to meet demand.

### 5.4 IIJA Funding Mechanisms

The Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act of 2021 established several funding programs directly applicable to bridge fleet protection:

- **Bridge Formula Program:** $26.5 billion over five years apportioned to states by formula, with broad eligibility for preservation, rehabilitation, and replacement activities. Coating and cathodic protection qualify as eligible preservation activities.
- **PROTECT Discretionary Grant Program:** $7.3 billion for projects that improve the resilience of surface transportation infrastructure to natural hazards, including coastal corrosion, flooding, and extreme weather. Bridge protection systems in coastal and flood-prone environments are explicitly eligible.
- **National Infrastructure Project Assistance Program:** $5 billion for large, complex projects of national significance that may include comprehensive bridge protection components as part of broader rehabilitation or replacement projects.

## 6. Key Industry Organizations and Standards Bodies

### 6.1 Association for Materials Protection and Performance

Formed in 2021 through the merger of NACE International and the Society for Protective Coatings, AMPP is the preeminent global authority on corrosion protection and coatings performance. Key AMPP standards and guides relevant to bridge fleet protection include:

- AMPP SP21412 (formerly NACE SP0212): *Detection, Testing, and Evaluation of Microbiologically Influenced Corrosion on Internal Surfaces of Pipelines* — methodology applicable to enclosed bridge structural elements.
- AMPP Guide 21532 (formerly SSPC-Guide 20): *Guide to the Selection of Coating Systems for Steel Structures* — provides coating system recommendations by environment and service condition.
- AMPP SP21469 (formerly NACE SP0469): *Cathodic Protection of Reinforcing Steel in Atmospherically Exposed Concrete Structures* — primary North American standard for bridge CP system design.

### 6.2 American Association of State Highway and Transportation Officials

AASHTO's bridge-related specifications and guides include:

- *AASHTO LRFD Bridge Design Specifications*, 10th Edition (2024): Section 5 addresses durability and protection of concrete and steel structural elements.
- *AASHTO Manual for Bridge Element Inspection*, 3rd Edition (2023): Provides standardized condition state language for coating systems and cathodic protection elements.
- *AASHTO Standard Specifications for Transportation Materials and Methods of Sampling and Testing*, 44th Edition (2024): Includes material specifications for coating constituents, zinc anodes, and concrete repair materials.

### 6.3 Transportation Research Board

The TRB's Standing Committee on Corrosion (AHD45) and Standing Committee on Structures Maintenance (AHD30) publish annual research circulars and sponsor synthesis studies addressing bridge corrosion and preservation. Notable TRB publications consulted for this memorandum include:

- NCHRP Synthesis 608: *Bridge Coating Maintenance Practices* (2024)
- NCHRP Report 1055: *Service Life of Bridge Coatings* (2023)
- NCHRP Synthesis 578: *Cathodic Protection for Reinforced Concrete Bridges* (2022)
- TRB Annual Meeting Compendium of Papers — Corrosion and Preservation Sessions (2019–2025)

### 6.4 Federal Highway Administration

FHWA technical resources consulted include:

- FHWA-HRT-11-044: *Corrosion Protection of Steel Bridges* (2011, updated 2021)
- FHWA-NHI-15-048: *Bridge Preservation Guide* (2015, updated 2022)
- FHWA HIF-20-004: *Cathodic Protection of Reinforced Concrete Bridge Elements* (2020)
- FHWA *Bridge Inspector's Reference Manual* (BIRM), 2022 Edition

## 8. Conclusion

The global state of the art in bridge corrosion protection has matured to the point that comprehensive, fleet-wide protection programs are not merely technically feasible — they are economically compelled by the available lifecycle cost data. The convergence of proven protective technologies (duplex zinc coating systems, impressed current cathodic protection), validated economic models (NPV analyses from FDOT, Norway, and Japan), supportive federal policy (IIJA funding mechanisms, AID framework), and stringent regulatory drivers (reduced lead PELs, Buy America supply chain maturity) creates a window of opportunity for transportation agencies and their engineering partners to fundamentally change the way bridge preservation is planned, funded, and executed.

The frameworks, models, and implementation strategies presented in the Fleet Bridge Protection & Retrofit Program dossier are built on this research foundation. They are designed to be adapted to the specific needs, bridge inventories, environmental conditions, and funding constraints of T.Y. Lin International's client agencies.
