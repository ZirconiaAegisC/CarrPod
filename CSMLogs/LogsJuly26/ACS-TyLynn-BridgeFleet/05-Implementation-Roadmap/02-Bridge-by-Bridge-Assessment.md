## Introduction

This document presents a structure-by-structure assessment of the ten toll bridges comprising the San Francisco Bay Area toll bridge fleet. For each bridge, the assessment synthesizes current National Bridge Inspection Standards (NBIS) condition ratings, documented structural deficiencies, the status of programmed capital improvements, and environmental exposure conditions to formulate a structure-specific corrosion protection strategy. The assessments are ordered from highest intervention priority to lowest, as determined by the multi-factor prioritization model described in the Program Implementation Framework (ACS-TyLynn-BFP-05-001).

## 1. San Mateo-Hayward Bridge

### General Description
The San Mateo-Hayward Bridge carries State Route 92 across San Francisco Bay between Foster City and Hayward. The 7.0-mile structure comprises a steel high-rise section (orthotropic box girder with tower-supported main span), steel trestle approach spans on the western side, and a long reinforced concrete trestle on the eastern approach. Total length: 36,960 feet. Opened 1967; seismic retrofit completed 2000. Owned by Caltrans; toll revenues administered by BATA.

### Current Condition
- **NBIS Deck Rating:** Satisfactory (6)
- **NBIS Superstructure Rating:** Satisfactory (6)
- **NBIS Substructure Rating:** Poor (4)
- **Key Deficiencies:** The eastern concrete trestle substructure is the fleet's most significant corrosion concern. Of 858 total piers, chloride-induced corrosion has been documented in a substantial number, driven by decades of exposure to brackish water and tidal splash-zone conditions. Phase 1 substructure rehabilitation is programmed at $45 million, addressing 321 piers. Phase 2 addresses the remaining 537 piers at an estimated $118 million. The high-rise section has experienced spandrel beam and pier cap concrete deterioration. Tower painting was recently completed ($14 million).
- **Inspection Cycle:** 24-month routine; fracture-critical members inspected every 24 months.
- **Environmental Classification:** ISO 12944 C4 (coastal, high salinity) for the high-rise section; ISO 12944 C5-M (marine, very high corrosivity) for the eastern trestle splash zone.

### Recommended Protection Strategy

**Priority 1: Complete Substructure ICCP Coverage (Phases 2 and 3 of Pier Rehabilitation).** Install impressed current cathodic protection on all remaining piers not addressed in Phase 1. The ICCP system should incorporate mixed-metal-oxide (MMO) titanium anodes embedded in a cementitious overlay, with individual zoned control for each pier to accommodate variable chloride concentrations and reinforcement densities. Transformer-rectifier units should be equipped with cellular telemetry for remote monitoring, as demonstrated effective on Oregon DOT coastal bridges (Bullard et al., 2004; Holcomb & Cryer, 1998).

**Priority 2: High-Rise Superstructure TSZ Duplex.** Apply thermal spray zinc duplex coating to the high-rise spandrel beams, pier caps, bearing assemblies, and steel orthotropic deck elements. The recently completed tower painting provides an immediate comparative data point: instrument the newly TSZ-coated elements with the same corrosion rate sensors deployed on the painted tower to generate side-by-side performance data within this single structure.

**Priority 3: Trestle Approach Steel Protection.** Apply TSZ duplex to the steel trestle approach spans on the western side. These elements experience direct salt spray and fog exposure, and a duplex system provides the extended maintenance interval needed to reduce future traffic disruptions on this heavily traveled corridor (ADT approximately 93,000 vehicles).

### Estimated Costs
- Substructure ICCP (remaining piers): $118M (aligned with BATA Phase 2 estimate)
- High-Rise TSZ Duplex: $22M – $30M
- Trestle Approach Steel TSZ Duplex: $8M – $12M
- **Total Estimated Cost Range:** $148M – $160M

### Priority Ranking: 1 (Highest Priority)
The Poor substructure rating, combined with the largest number of individual corrosion-affected elements in the fleet, makes the San Mateo-Hayward Bridge the highest-priority intervention. Continued deferral risks load-posting or lane restrictions within the next 10 years.

### Integration Opportunities
- Phase 1 pier rehabilitation ($45M, programmed): opportunity to pilot and validate ICCP design on the initial 321 piers, then deploy the validated design in Phase 2
- Tower painting completion: provides in-situ control data for TSZ duplex performance comparison
- BATA's $118M Phase 2 budget allocation provides existing funding programming that can accommodate ICCP scope

## 2. San Francisco-Oakland Bay Bridge — West Span

### General Description
The West Span of the San Francisco-Oakland Bay Bridge (SF-Oakland West Span) is a double suspension bridge constructed in 1936. It comprises two complete suspension bridges connected end-to-end with a shared central anchorage (Yerba Buena Island), carrying five lanes of westbound I-80 traffic on the upper deck and five lanes of eastbound traffic on the lower deck. The structure features two main towers, four main cables (two per suspension span), a stiffening truss, and suspended floor systems. Total length of the West Span: 10,304 feet. The East Span was completely replaced and opened in 2013.

### Current Condition
- **NBIS Deck Rating:** Satisfactory (6)
- **NBIS Superstructure Rating:** Fair (5)
- **NBIS Substructure Rating:** Satisfactory (6)
- **Key Deficiencies:** The West Span is the acknowledged focal point for rehabilitation within the BATA fleet. The main cables require a comprehensive internal inspection ($28 million programmed), which represents the first such inspection since the bridge opened. The floor system paint is deteriorating — the currently programmed FY24/25 repainting is the project most directly impacted by Caltrans' 2025 lead exposure regulations, which will substantially increase the cost of conventional repainting. Fender replacement is programmed at $118 million (IIJA-funded). The stiffening truss, at 90 years of age, requires systematic assessment of corrosion section loss at critical connections. The anchorage housings, which encase the main cable splay castings, have exhibited moisture intrusion and associated corrosion.
- **Inspection Cycle:** 24-month routine; fracture-critical members inspected every 24 months; special main cable inspection programmed (first comprehensive since construction).
- **Environmental Classification:** ISO 12944 C4 (coastal, moderate-to-high salinity), with localized C5-M at tower bases and splash-zone elements.

### Recommended Protection Strategy

**Priority 1: Floor System TSZ Duplex Replacement.** Replace the programmed conventional floor system painting with a TSZ duplex system. The 2025 lead regulations provide a compelling economic basis for this substitution: the lead compliance cost for conventional paint removal will be incurred regardless of the replacement system, but the TSZ duplex eliminates the need for two to three future repainting cycles over the next 50+ years — each of which would separately incur full lead-compliance costs. The floor system is the ideal starting point for TSZ duplex deployment on the West Span because it is already programmed for intervention, has high public visibility (demonstrating BATA's commitment to long-term asset stewardship), and experiences moderate-to-severe corrosion due to salt-laden fog exposure beneath the lower deck.

**Priority 2: Main Cable Dehumidification.** Following the programmed main cable inspection ($28M), evaluate the feasibility of installing a cable dehumidification system — a technology successfully deployed on the Akashi Kaikyo Bridge (Japan), the Humber Bridge (UK), and other major suspension bridges worldwide. Dehumidification reduces the internal relative humidity of the main cable below the 40% threshold at which corrosion of high-strength steel wires initiates, effectively arresting internal corrosion without requiring wire-by-wire rehabilitation.

**Priority 3: Anchorage Housing ICCP and Environmental Control.** Install ICCP on the reinforced concrete anchorage housings, combined with improved drainage and waterproofing to reduce moisture ingress. The splay casting areas are critical to main cable integrity and require aggressive protection.

**Priority 4: Tower Steel and Stiffening Truss TSZ Duplex.** Apply TSZ duplex to the main towers (from deck level upward) and to all accessible stiffening truss members. For truss members with section loss exceeding 5%, conduct engineering analysis to confirm remaining capacity before coating.

### Estimated Costs
- Floor System TSZ Duplex (replacing programmed conventional paint): $42M – $55M (incremental over conventional paint cost)
- Main Cable Dehumidification: $15M – $22M (contingent on inspection findings)
- Anchorage ICCP and Waterproofing: $8M – $12M
- Tower and Truss TSZ Duplex: $30M – $42M
- **Total Estimated Cost Range:** $95M – $131M

### Priority Ranking: 2
The West Span ranks second due to the criticality of the structure (irreplaceable, carries 250,000+ vehicles daily on I-80), the advancing age of the existing steel (90 years), and the immediate cost savings opportunity created by substituting TSZ duplex for the already-programmed conventional floor system paint.

### Integration Opportunities
- FY24/25 floor system paint program: direct scope substitution to TSZ duplex
- Main cable inspection mobilization ($28M): shared access for anchorage ICCP and waterproofing
- Fender replacement ($118M, IIJA): marine-access mobilization can support substructure ICCP installation
- The convergence of multiple programmed projects creates a rare opportunity for comprehensive protection with shared mobilization and traffic control costs

## 3. Richmond-San Rafael Bridge

### General Description
The Richmond-San Rafael Bridge (officially the John F. McCarthy Memorial Bridge) carries I-580 across the northern reach of San Francisco Bay between Richmond and San Rafael. The bridge is 29,040 feet in total length (5.5 miles), consisting of two cantilever truss main spans, steel truss approach spans, and concrete trestle approaches. Opened 1956; seismic retrofit completed 2005. Unique among the Bay Area toll bridges for its double-deck cantilever truss design.

### Current Condition
- **NBIS Deck Rating:** Satisfactory (6)
- **NBIS Superstructure Rating:** Fair (5)
- **NBIS Substructure Rating:** Satisfactory (6)
- **Key Deficiencies:** A comprehensive steel painting program ($68 million) was completed in June 2024, addressing the most visible and accessible steel surfaces. However, areas of the upper deck truss, approach span trusses, and connection details that were not within this painting scope continue to exhibit coating deterioration. Deck repairs and joint seal replacement are programmed for FY25/26. A Design Deviation Order (DDO) has been issued for deteriorated structural sections, indicating that corrosion-related section loss in specific elements has reached a level requiring engineering evaluation for load-carrying capacity.
- **Inspection Cycle:** 24-month routine; fracture-critical truss members inspected every 24 months.
- **Environmental Classification:** ISO 12944 C4 (coastal), with C5-M at pier bases and splash-zone areas.

### Recommended Protection Strategy

**Priority 1: Upper Deck Truss and Approach Truss TSZ Duplex.** Apply TSZ duplex to all steel surfaces not covered by the 2024 painting program, with particular attention to the upper deck truss members that experience direct sun exposure (thermal cycling accelerates conventional coating degradation) and the approach truss connections that are difficult to access and represent high-cost future maintenance if painted conventionally.

**Priority 2: DDO-Identified Section Repairs with Integrated Protection.** In coordination with the DDO response for deteriorated sections, apply reinforced TSZ duplex (increased zinc thickness per ISO 2063 Class 2, minimum 120 μm) with selective structural steel reinforcement or replacement at locations where section loss exceeds engineering thresholds.

**Priority 3: Substructure Zone ICCP.** Install impressed current cathodic protection on selected reinforced concrete piers in the tidal splash zone where chloride concentrations are highest. A targeted zone-protection approach — rather than full fleet coverage — is appropriate given the Satisfactory substructure rating and the need to allocate limited ICCP resources to the highest-risk piers.

**Priority 4: Deck Rehabilitation Corrosion Protection Integration.** Ensure that the programmed FY25/26 deck repairs and joint seal replacement incorporate corrosion-protective details: epoxy-coated reinforcing steel in all repair areas; waterproofing membrane on all deck replacement sections; and galvanic anode installation at all joint repair locations to protect against future chloride ingress from leaking joints.

### Estimated Costs
- Upper Deck and Approach Truss TSZ Duplex: $38M – $48M
- DDO Section Repairs with Integrated Protection: $12M – $18M
- Substructure Zone ICCP: $8M – $12M
- Deck Rehabilitation Corrosion Integration (incremental): $5M – $7M
- **Total Estimated Cost Range:** $63M – $85M

### Priority Ranking: 3
The issuance of a Design Deviation Order elevates the Richmond-San Rafael Bridge above structures that, while perhaps in worse nominal condition, do not have active engineering concerns requiring near-term response. The FY25/26 programmed deck work creates an efficient mobilization window.

### Integration Opportunities
- FY25/26 deck repair and joint seal replacement: shared traffic control and contractor mobilization
- DDO response engineering: protection scope can be added to the rehabilitation design contract
- The 2024 steel painting completion provides a clear delineation of protected vs. unprotected steel surfaces, simplifying scope definition for the TSZ duplex program

## 4. Carquinez Bridge — Eastbound

### General Description
The Carquinez Bridge Eastbound (officially the Alfred Zampa Memorial Bridge) carries I-80 eastbound across the Carquinez Strait between Vallejo and Crockett. It is a suspension bridge with a main span of 2,388 feet, opened in 2003 (replacing the 1927 eastbound truss bridge). Total length: 3,465 feet. The bridge features an orthotropic steel deck, steel towers, and steel main cables.

### Current Condition
- **NBIS Deck Rating:** Satisfactory (6)
- **NBIS Superstructure Rating:** Fair (5)
- **NBIS Substructure Rating:** Fair (5)
- **Key Deficiencies:** Corrosion is increasing in the substructure, particularly on the reinforced concrete piers and abutments. The superstructure steel paint system is programmed for conventional repainting in FY27/28, indicating that the existing coating is approaching the end of its service life. The deck overlay is programmed for FY24/25. Seismic transmission units (fluid viscous dampers) are leaking and require repair. Despite being a newer structure (2003), the marine environment of the Carquinez Strait has accelerated coating degradation relative to design expectations.
- **Inspection Cycle:** 24-month routine; special emphasis on cable-stay and main cable corrosion protection systems.
- **Environmental Classification:** ISO 12944 C4 (coastal strait environment with consistent wind-driven salt exposure).

### Recommended Protection Strategy

**Priority 1: Superstructure TSZ Duplex as FY27/28 Paint Program Substitute.** This bridge is the recommended pilot structure for the TSZ duplex program (see Implementation Framework, Phase 1). The programmed conventional paint in FY27/28 should be replaced with a full TSZ duplex application on all accessible superstructure steel: towers, stiffening truss, suspender ropes and sockets, main cable bands, and deck-level steel. As a newer structure (2003), the baseline steel condition is likely to be excellent beneath the existing coating, minimizing the surface preparation effort required to achieve the SSPC-SP 10 cleanliness required for TSZ application.

**Priority 2: Substructure ICCP.** Install impressed current cathodic protection on the reinforced concrete piers and abutments, targeting the increasing corrosion trend documented in recent inspection reports. Early intervention will minimize the extent of concrete repair required.

**Priority 3: Seismic Transmission Unit Repair with Corrosion Protection.** Integrate the repair of leaking seismic transmission units with application of protective coatings to all exposed damper components and mounting hardware. These elements are critical to seismic performance and have high replacement cost if corrosion progresses unchecked.

**Priority 4: Deck Overlay Integration.** Ensure that the programmed FY24/25 deck overlay includes a waterproofing membrane and that deck drains are inspected and rehabilitated to prevent future chloride-laden water from reaching substructure elements.

### Estimated Costs
- Superstructure TSZ Duplex (pilot project, 5,000+ m² demonstration): $4.2M – $6.8M (Phase 1 pilot)
- Full Superstructure TSZ Duplex (if pilot confirms costs): $12M – $18M (additional)
- Substructure ICCP: $6M – $10M
- Seismic Damper Protection: $2M – $4M
- **Total Estimated Cost Range:** $24M – $39M

### Priority Ranking: 4
The Carquinez Eastbound Bridge is ranked fourth because its programmed FY27/28 paint window creates an ideal deployment opportunity but the timeline is less urgent than the higher-ranked structures. The pilot project recommendation also means this structure carries disproportionate programmatic value: its successful execution unlocks the fleet-wide business case.

### Integration Opportunities
- FY27/28 paint program: direct substitution opportunity
- FY24/25 deck overlay: deck waterproofing scope integration
- Seismic damper repair: combined access mobilization
- As a 2003 structure, it is the newest steel bridge in the fleet — the opportunity to install long-life protection before significant deterioration occurs represents the highest-return investment in the program

## 5. Carquinez Bridge — Westbound

### General Description
The Carquinez Bridge Westbound carries I-80 westbound across the Carquinez Strait. Originally constructed in 1958 as a cantilever through-truss bridge, it was seismically retrofitted in the early 2000s. Total length: 3,300 feet. The bridge carries three lanes of westbound traffic.

### Current Condition
- **NBIS Deck Rating:** Satisfactory (6)
- **NBIS Superstructure Rating:** Fair (5)
- **NBIS Substructure Rating:** Satisfactory (6)
- **Key Deficiencies:** The steel superstructure is a 1958 cantilever truss that has experienced multiple repainting cycles. The existing coating system is approaching end-of-life. Paint needs are similar to the Eastbound span but the truss geometry presents more complex application challenges than the Eastbound suspension structure. Substructure condition is satisfactory, with no active significant corrosion concerns documented.
- **Inspection Cycle:** 24-month routine.
- **Environmental Classification:** ISO 12944 C4.

### Recommended Protection Strategy

**Priority 1: Superstructure TSZ Duplex.** Following validation of the TSZ duplex methodology on the Eastbound pilot, apply the same protection system to the Westbound truss. The truss geometry requires careful attention to connection details, gusset plates, and areas where moisture and debris can accumulate. TSZ duplex is particularly advantageous for truss structures because the thermal spray process can achieve full coverage on complex geometries that are difficult to coat uniformly with conventional spray painting — a key factor in the extended service life of the duplex system.

**Priority 2: Substructure Preventive ICCP.** Install galvanic anode cathodic protection on piers at the tidal splash zone. Given the satisfactory substructure condition, the lower-cost galvanic system (requiring no external power source) is appropriate for preventive protection rather than the more expensive impressed current system required for actively corroding structures.

### Estimated Costs
- Superstructure TSZ Duplex: $16M – $22M
- Substructure Galvanic CP: $2M – $3M
- **Total Estimated Cost Range:** $18M – $25M

### Priority Ranking: 5
The Westbound span's position behind its Eastbound counterpart is logical: the lessons learned from the Eastbound pilot will directly inform the Westbound application methodology, specification refinements, and cost estimates. Sequencing the Westbound after the Eastbound maximizes the return on pilot project investment.

### Integration Opportunities
- Eastbound pilot lessons learned: direct reduction in Westbound application costs through improved contractor efficiency
- Shared contractor mobilization: the contractor qualified on the Eastbound pilot will have developed the specific expertise for Carquinez Strait conditions

## 6. Dumbarton Bridge

### General Description
The Dumbarton Bridge carries State Route 84 across the southern reach of San Francisco Bay between Menlo Park and Fremont. The 8,600-foot structure is the southernmost Bay Area toll bridge and comprises steel girder approach spans and a reinforced concrete box girder main span section. Opened 1982.

### Current Condition
- **NBIS Deck Rating:** Satisfactory (6)
- **NBIS Superstructure Rating:** Fair (5)
- **NBIS Substructure Rating:** Fair (5)
- **Key Deficiencies:** Three major rehabilitation projects are programmed: steel painting ($84 million), fender replacement ($132 million), and concrete repair ($52 million). The combined programmed work — $268 million — represents one of the largest single-structure investments in the BATA CIP. The steel superstructure coating has deteriorated to the point where widespread rust-through is documented in inspection reports. Fender deterioration affects the marine vessel collision protection system. The concrete substructure requires chloride-induced corrosion repairs.
- **Inspection Cycle:** 24-month routine.
- **Environmental Classification:** ISO 12944 C4 (south bay environment, lower salinity than central bay but significant fog exposure).

### Recommended Protection Strategy

**Priority 1: Steel Superstructure TSZ Duplex (Substitute for $84M Programmed Paint).** Replace the programmed conventional steel painting with TSZ duplex on all steel girder approach spans. The $84 million budget allocation is already programmed — substituting TSZ duplex for conventional paint within this budget envelope may require supplemental funding for the incremental material and application cost, but the life-cycle savings (avoiding two future $84M+ repainting cycles) more than justify the investment.

**Priority 2: Substructure ICCP Integration with $52M Concrete Repair.** Install impressed current cathodic protection on all repaired concrete elements, ensuring that the concrete repair scope does not simply replace damaged concrete without addressing the root cause of corrosion. Embed MMO anodes in all repair areas for future connection to a complete ICCP system.

**Priority 3: Fender Replacement Corrosion Protection.** Ensure that the $132 million fender replacement program specifies corrosion-resistant materials (stainless steel or galvanized steel) and incorporates cathodic protection for any carbon steel components that must be retained for structural reasons.

### Estimated Costs
- Steel TSZ Duplex (incremental over $84M conventional paint): $18M – $28M
- Substructure ICCP (incremental over $52M concrete repair): $12M – $18M
- Fender Corrosion Protection (incremental): $5M – $8M
- **Total Estimated Cost Range:** $35M – $54M (incremental over programmed $268M)

### Priority Ranking: 6
The Dumbarton Bridge has $268 million in programmed work, representing an unparalleled opportunity to integrate comprehensive corrosion protection with planned rehabilitation. However, the fact that the work is already programmed and funded reduces the urgency relative to structures without active funding allocations. The key intervention is to influence the scope of the programmed work to include long-life protection systems rather than conventional short-cycle approaches.

### Integration Opportunities
- $268M programmed CIP work: the largest single-structure opportunity for integrated protection in the fleet
- Contractor procurement for the steel painting and concrete repair contracts can include TSZ duplex and ICCP qualification requirements
- The south bay location provides a less complex traffic management environment than the central bay bridges

## 7. Benicia-Martinez Bridge — Southbound

### General Description
The Benicia-Martinez Bridge Southbound carries I-680 southbound across the Carquinez Strait between Benicia and Martinez. Originally constructed in 1962 as a truss bridge carrying both directions of traffic, it was converted to southbound-only operation following the opening of the new northbound bridge in 2007. The structure is a continuous through-truss design.

### Current Condition
- **NBIS Deck Rating:** Fair (5)
- **NBIS Superstructure Rating:** Fair (5)
- **NBIS Substructure Rating:** Satisfactory (6)
- **Key Deficiencies:** Deck and joint repairs are needed. The Seismic Joint at Pier 3 requires repair, which is a programmed project in the BATA CIP. As a 1962 structure, the steel truss has been repainted multiple times; the current coating system is approaching end-of-life on the truss diagonals and floor system. The bridge is subject to the same Carquinez Strait marine environment as the adjacent spans but, having been originally designed as a two-direction facility, experiences lower traffic volumes than the northbound bridge.
- **Inspection Cycle:** 24-month routine; fracture-critical truss members every 24 months.
- **Environmental Classification:** ISO 12944 C4.

### Recommended Protection Strategy

**Priority 1: Substructure ICCP at Pier 3 and Adjacent Piers.** Integrate impressed current cathodic protection with the programmed Seismic Joint repair at Pier 3. This is a logical scope expansion: the access and mobilization required for the joint repair provide a cost-efficient window for ICCP anode installation on the most exposed concrete elements.

**Priority 2: Superstructure TSZ Duplex — Selective Application.** Apply TSZ duplex on the truss diagonals, floor beams, and connections where coating deterioration is most advanced. A full superstructure TSZ duplex application may not be warranted if coating condition on the upper chords and top laterals remains satisfactory; a selective, condition-based approach reduces the near-term budget impact while addressing highest-risk elements first.

**Priority 3: Deck Rehabilitation with Integrated Protection.** Ensure that all deck and joint repairs incorporate epoxy-coated reinforcing steel, waterproofing membranes, and galvanic anodes at joint terminations.

### Estimated Costs
- Substructure ICCP at Pier 3 and Adjacent: $4M – $6M
- Selective Superstructure TSZ Duplex: $12M – $16M
- Deck Protection Integration (incremental): $3M – $5M
- **Total Estimated Cost Range:** $19M – $27M

### Priority Ranking: 7
The programmed seismic joint repair provides a natural intervention window. However, the Fair (not Poor) condition ratings and the lower traffic volumes (southbound only) reduce the urgency relative to higher-ranked structures.

### Integration Opportunities
- Programmed Pier 3 Seismic Joint Repair: shared mobilization for ICCP installation
- Adjacent Carquinez Eastbound Pilot (Phase 1): contractor qualification and methodology lessons directly transferable

## 8. Benicia-Martinez Bridge — Northbound

### General Description
The Benicia-Martinez Bridge Northbound carries I-680 northbound across the Carquinez Strait. Opened in 2007, this 8,904-foot structure is a cast-in-place segmental concrete box girder bridge — the first Caltrans segmental bridge constructed in 20 years at the time of its completion. T.Y. Lin International served as the design engineer. The bridge carries five lanes of northbound traffic and features a 659-foot main span. It was designed to Caltrans' lifeline seismic performance standards, making it one of the most seismically resilient bridges in the fleet.

### Current Condition
- **NBIS Deck Rating:** Good (7)
- **NBIS Superstructure Rating:** Good (7)
- **NBIS Substructure Rating:** Good (7)
- **Key Deficiencies:** No significant deficiencies documented. The structure is in Good condition, benefiting from its relatively young age (19 years), segmental concrete construction (no structural steel corrosion concerns), and comprehensive design standards. Routine maintenance only.
- **Inspection Cycle:** 24-month routine.
- **Environmental Classification:** ISO 12944 C4.

### Recommended Protection Strategy

**Priority 1: Preventive Substructure Protection.** While the bridge is in Good condition, the Carquinez Strait marine environment will inevitably drive chloride ingress into the concrete over the coming decades. A preventive protection strategy — rather than a reactive one — is recommended: install embedded reference electrodes during the next scheduled maintenance access to enable future chloride concentration monitoring; apply silane-based penetrating sealer to pier surfaces in the splash and tidal zones to reduce chloride ingress rates; and install galvanic anode systems at the most exposed pier bases as a preventive measure (low-cost, no external power requirement).

**Priority 2: Selective Steel Element Protection.** The bridge is predominantly concrete, but bearing assemblies, expansion joints, and other ancillary steel elements should receive TSZ duplex coating during the next maintenance cycle. These elements are small in total surface area but critical to structural function and difficult to access for replacement.

**Priority 3: Long-Term Monitoring Plan.** Install a permanent corrosion monitoring system — embedded reference electrodes, linear polarization resistance probes, and chloride concentration sensors — at representative pier locations. This data will inform the timing of future active protection interventions and provide early warning of accelerating chloride ingress.

### Estimated Costs
- Preventive Substructure Protection: $14M – $20M
- Selective Steel Element TSZ Duplex: $2M – $4M
- Long-Term Monitoring System: $2M – $3M
- **Total Estimated Cost Range:** $18M – $27M

### Priority Ranking: 8
The Good condition of the Benicia-Martinez Northbound Bridge places it lower in the intervention priority ranking, consistent with the asset management principle of addressing deteriorating structures before initiating preventive programs on structures in good condition. However, the preventive strategy recommended here is deliberately affordable and designed to defer the need for major rehabilitation by decades — consistent with the "stitch in time" philosophy.

### Integration Opportunities
- As a concrete segmental bridge designed by T.Y. Lin International, the firm possesses the original design calculations, construction records, and material specifications — knowledge that directly supports efficient preventive protection design
- The bridge's younger age provides a long window for preventive measures to compound in economic benefit

## 9. San Francisco-Oakland Bay Bridge — East Span

### General Description
The East Span of the San Francisco-Oakland Bay Bridge (SF-Oakland East Span) is the replacement structure that opened to traffic in September 2013. The 2.2-mile structure comprises a self-anchored suspension (SAS) main span, a skyway viaduct, and an Oakland touchdown approach. The SAS span is a globally unique structure — the world's longest self-anchored suspension bridge — featuring a single 525-foot tower and a single main cable. T.Y. Lin International, in a joint venture with Moffatt & Nichol, served as Engineer of Record for the entire $6.5 billion project. The East Span was designed to a 150-year service life, making it the most durable major bridge structure in the United States.

### Current Condition
- **NBIS Deck Rating:** Good (7)
- **NBIS Superstructure Rating:** Good (7)
- **NBIS Substructure Rating:** Good (7)
- **Key Deficiencies:** No significant rehabilitation needs have been identified in the first 13 years of service. Minor deck and paint issues only. The structure is not expected to require major rehabilitation for at least 10 years. The bridge was designed with extensive corrosion protection measures, including: stainless steel reinforcing in critical areas; high-performance concrete mix designs; galvanized structural steel components; and comprehensive coating systems specified for the marine environment.
- **Inspection Cycle:** 24-month routine; special emphasis on the SAS main cable anchorage.
- **Environmental Classification:** ISO 12944 C3-C4 (the structure was designed with explicit environmental durability requirements).

### Recommended Protection Strategy

**Priority 1: Condition Monitoring and Preventive ICCP at Vulnerable Locations.** Despite the 150-year design life, certain elements will experience accelerated deterioration. Install embedded corrosion monitoring sensors at: the SAS main cable anchorage (concrete encasement); skyway pier bases in the tidal zone; and expansion joint terminus areas where deicing salts may accumulate. If monitoring indicates chloride concentrations approaching threshold levels, install targeted galvanic anode CP systems — low-cost, no-power-required preventive protection.

**Priority 2: Supplementary Coating on Non-Primary Steel Elements.** Apply TSZ duplex to secondary steel elements not originally specified with the comprehensive coating systems used on primary structural members: maintenance walkway railings, access ladders, sign support structures, and light pole bases. These elements, while not structurally critical, represent high-cost maintenance items due to access difficulty and lane closure requirements.

**Priority 3: Design-Life Verification Program.** Leverage the East Span's explicit 150-year design life to establish a formal design-life verification program. This program would: collect and archive all construction-phase material certification and quality control records; perform periodic material sampling and testing to compare actual deterioration rates against design assumptions; and publish findings in the peer-reviewed literature to contribute to the global knowledge base on long-life bridge design performance.

### Estimated Costs
- Corrosion Monitoring Sensors and Targeted Galvanic CP: $4M – $6M
- Secondary Steel Element TSZ Duplex: $3M – $5M
- Design-Life Verification Program (10-year): $1.5M – $3M
- **Total Estimated Cost Range:** $8.5M – $14M

### Priority Ranking: 9
As a structure in Good condition with a 150-year design life, the East Span requires the least intervention in the near term. However, the unique opportunity to monitor and document the performance of a long-life bridge design is strategically valuable — the lessons learned will directly inform protection strategies for future major bridge projects.

### Integration Opportunities
- T.Y. Lin International's role as Engineer of Record provides unmatched access to design intent, material specifications, and construction records — dramatically reducing the effort required to establish a condition baseline and design-life verification program
- The East Span can serve as a "living laboratory" for corrosion protection performance, with lessons transferable to all other fleet structures

## 10. Antioch Bridge

### General Description
The Antioch Bridge carries State Route 160 across the San Joaquin River between Antioch and Sherman Island. Opened in 1978, the 9,504-foot structure is a steel plate girder bridge with a concrete deck. It is the easternmost Bay Area toll bridge and, unlike the other nine bridges, crosses a river rather than the open bay — resulting in a freshwater rather than brackish/saltwater environment. The bridge carries two lanes of traffic (one in each direction) with a relatively low ADT of approximately 13,000 vehicles.

### Current Condition
- **NBIS Deck Rating:** Good (7)
- **NBIS Superstructure Rating:** Good (7)
- **NBIS Substructure Rating:** Good (7)
- **Key Deficiencies:** No significant deficiencies. The structure is in Good condition across all NBIS rating categories. The freshwater environment is substantially less corrosive than the bay environment affecting the other nine bridges. Routine maintenance only.
- **Inspection Cycle:** 24-month routine; next comprehensive inspection scheduled March 2027.
- **Environmental Classification:** ISO 12944 C3 (freshwater river environment, moderate corrosivity).

### Recommended Protection Strategy

**Priority 1: Preventive Substructure Protection.** The freshwater environment does reduce corrosion rates, but the San Joaquin River water still contains dissolved minerals and seasonal agricultural runoff that can accelerate concrete deterioration. Apply silane-based penetrating sealer to pier surfaces in the splash zone during the next scheduled maintenance cycle. Install a small number of embedded reference electrodes at representative pier locations for long-term chloride monitoring.

**Priority 2: Steel Superstructure Coating Condition Assessment.** The existing coating system, applied in 1978 with subsequent maintenance overcoats, should be assessed during the programmed March 2027 comprehensive inspection to determine remaining service life. If the coating is approaching end-of-life, TSZ duplex should be considered as the replacement system — even in a freshwater environment, the extended maintenance interval provides life-cycle cost advantages, though the payback period is longer than for marine-exposed structures.

**Priority 3: Bearing and Joint Protection.** Apply protective coatings to bearing assemblies and expansion joints, which are the elements most vulnerable to water intrusion and debris accumulation regardless of water salinity.

### Estimated Costs
- Preventive Substructure Protection: $4M – $6M
- Coating Condition Assessment and Selective TSZ Duplex: $6M – $10M
- Bearing and Joint Protection: $2M – $3M
- **Total Estimated Cost Range:** $12M – $19M

### Priority Ranking: 10 (Lowest Priority)
The Antioch Bridge's Good condition ratings, freshwater environment (lowest corrosivity in the fleet), and low traffic volumes combine to place it at the lowest priority for intervention. However, the March 2027 comprehensive inspection provides a natural opportunity to conduct the coating condition assessment and initiate preventive substructure protection.

### Integration Opportunities
- March 2027 comprehensive inspection: condition assessment and sensor installation can be conducted during the inspection mobilization
- Freshwater environment provides a control data point for comparing protection system performance across different exposure environments
