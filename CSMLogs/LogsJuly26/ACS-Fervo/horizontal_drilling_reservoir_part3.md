## Scaling the Drilling Program

The transition from Project Red's single-well-pair demonstration to Cape Station's 400 MW multi-well development represents a step change in drilling program scale. A 400 MW geothermal field, depending on well productivity, requires 40-80 well pairs — 80-160 wells to drill, complete, and connect to surface facilities. At a drilling pace of 10-20 wells per year, the drilling program spans 4-10 years, requiring sustained execution capability.

### Program Management and Logistics

Managing a multi-year, multi-well drilling program requires capabilities in logistics, supply chain management, and contractor coordination that exceed the requirements of a single-well-pair demonstration. Drill pads must be constructed, water supply for drilling and stimulation secured, produced water disposal or reinjection arranged, and the surface facilities to connect each new well pair to the power plant designed and constructed on a schedule aligned with drilling progress.

Fervo's Houston headquarters provides access to the ecosystem of drilling program managers, logistics coordinators, and supply chain professionals who have managed large-scale drilling programs in the Permian Basin and other active oil and gas plays. The company can subcontract rigs, pressure pumping services, and ancillary services from established oilfield service companies, focusing its internal resources on subsurface design, reservoir engineering, and overall program management.

### Permitting and Regulatory Timeline

Drilling on federal lands — Cape Station is located on Bureau of Land Management acreage in Utah — requires compliance with the National Environmental Policy Act (NEPA), which mandates environmental impact assessment and public comment periods. The NEPA process for a large-scale geothermal development can take 18-36 months from initial application to Record of Decision.

The permitting timeline is often the critical path for geothermal project development, not because the permitting requirements are unreasonable but because the agencies responsible for permit review (BLM, US Fish and Wildlife Service, state environmental agencies) are under-resourced relative to the volume of applications they process. Fervo's experience navigating the federal permitting process for Project Red in Nevada provides a foundation for the more extensive permitting required for Cape Station.

The DOE grant supporting Cape Station includes provisions for permitting assistance, and the project's alignment with federal clean energy goals may facilitate inter-agency coordination. However, permitting risk — delays, litigation by opponents, or requirements for additional environmental studies — remains a material consideration for the project schedule.

### Workforce Development for Drilling Operations

The drilling and completion workforce required for Cape Station — drillers, roughnecks, frac hands, equipment operators — will be drawn from the oil and gas labor pool, which has been highly mobile historically, with workers relocating between basins as drilling activity shifts. The western United States has not been a major oil and gas drilling center in recent decades, and the geothermal drilling workforce in the region is small relative to the requirements of a 400 MW development.

Fervo's workforce strategy likely involves a combination of: recruiting experienced oilfield workers from Texas, Oklahoma, and North Dakota; training local workers in drilling and completion operations; and developing a permanent workforce in Utah that can sustain operations beyond the initial construction phase. The transition from temporary construction employment to permanent operations and maintenance employment is a positive narrative for community acceptance and political support.

## Well Cost Benchmarking

Public data on geothermal well costs is limited because most geothermal development has been conducted by privately held companies or regulated utilities that are not required to disclose detailed cost data. However, enough information is available from DOE-funded demonstration projects, industry conferences, and academic research to construct reasonable cost benchmarks.

### Current Well Cost Estimates

A typical EGS well pair — one injection well and one production well, each with a vertical section from surface to the target formation and a horizontal lateral within the formation — is estimated to cost $15-25 million at current pricing, inclusive of drilling, casing, cementing, completion (stimulation), and wellhead equipment. This estimate is based on data from DOE-funded EGS demonstration projects, including the FORGE (Frontier Observatory for Research in Geothermal Energy) project in Utah, with adjustments for the cost reductions that Fervo has reportedly achieved through its oilfield technology approach.

The vertical section of an EGS well typically accounts for 30-40% of total well cost, with the horizontal lateral accounting for 50-60% and completion (stimulation) accounting for 10-15%. This cost distribution highlights the importance of horizontal drilling efficiency improvements: a 10% reduction in horizontal drilling cost translates to a 5-6% reduction in total well cost.

### Cost Reduction Drivers

The primary drivers of well cost reduction — faster drilling (more feet per day), longer bit life (fewer trips to change bits), and reduced non-productive time (equipment failures, wellbore instability) — are operational improvements rather than technological breakthroughs. The cumulative effect of these operational improvements, applied across a multi-well drilling program, is a well-characterized learning curve that Fervo can reasonably expect to capture.

Secondary cost drivers include supply chain optimization — negotiating volume discounts on casing, wellheads, and stimulation materials — and design standardization — defining a "factory well" design that is repeated across the field with minimal customization, enabling learning to accumulate faster than if each well were individually engineered.

### The $45/MWh Target

Achieving the DOE's $45/MWh target for EGS requires reducing total project costs — not just well costs — by approximately 50-60% from current levels. This reduction must come from a combination of well cost reduction, power plant cost reduction, and improvement in well productivity (more megawatts per well pair). Fervo has not publicly committed to a timeline for achieving $45/MWh, but the company's participation in the DOE grant program implies alignment with the 2035 target.

The trajectory from current costs to $45/MWh is not linear: initial cost reductions from operational learning are likely to be larger than later reductions as the technology matures. Whether the industry can achieve the full cost reduction target depends on sustained investment in EGS development — both private and public — that generates the cumulative production volume required to drive the learning curve.
