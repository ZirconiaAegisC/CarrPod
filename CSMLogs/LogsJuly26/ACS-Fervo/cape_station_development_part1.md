## Cape Station: The Flagship Project

Cape Station, Fervo Energy's 400-megawatt geothermal development in southwestern Utah, represents the largest EGS project under construction globally and the defining test of whether Enhanced Geothermal Systems can scale to utility-relevant capacity. The project is located on land administered by the Bureau of Land Management in Beaver County, Utah, a region with high geothermal gradient and proximity to existing transmission infrastructure serving the Los Angeles and Las Vegas electricity markets.

### Project Overview

The 400 MW capacity target places Cape Station in the category of major power generation infrastructure. For context, 400 MW is approximately the capacity of a mid-sized combined-cycle natural gas plant, sufficient to power roughly 300,000 homes. It exceeds the total installed capacity of any single conventional geothermal field in the United States.

The project is being developed in phases, with the initial phase targeting 100-150 MW of capacity, ramping to the full 400 MW over a development timeline of 5-8 years. The phased approach allows Fervo to incorporate learning from early wells into later phases, reducing well costs and improving productivity as the development progresses.

### Site Selection Rationale

The Beaver County location was selected based on several favorable characteristics. The geothermal gradient in the area — approximately 40-60°C per kilometer, significantly above the regional average — reduces the drilling depth required to reach target temperatures, lowering well costs. The target formation — granitic basement rock at depths of 2,000-4,000 meters — is well-characterized from oil and gas exploration wells drilled in the region in the 1970s and 1980s, reducing geological uncertainty.

Proximity to transmission infrastructure was a key factor. The Intermountain Power Project transmission corridor, which carries electricity from Utah to Southern California, passes within 20 miles of the Cape Station site. Interconnection to this corridor provides access to the California electricity market, where geothermal power commands a premium due to the state's Renewable Portfolio Standard and its requirement for round-the-clock carbon-free electricity.

The BLM land designation is also relevant. Federal lands in the western United States have been the primary location for geothermal development because the BLM has a statutory mandate to manage for multiple uses, including energy development, and because federal geothermal lease terms provide a predictable regulatory framework. Obtaining BLM approval for a geothermal development is a multi-year process but one with a defined pathway and established precedent.

### Development Timeline and Milestones

The Cape Station development timeline, based on Fervo's public disclosures and permitting documents, follows a phased schedule:

- **Phase 1 (2024-2026):** Exploration drilling, well testing, and initial production wells. Construction of the first 50-100 MW power block. First electricity generation targeted for 2026-2027.

- **Phase 2 (2026-2028):** Expansion drilling to support additional power blocks. Construction of transmission interconnection. Ramp-up to 200-250 MW of total capacity.

- **Phase 3 (2028-2030):** Full-field development. Drilling of remaining well pairs. Construction of final power blocks to reach the 400 MW capacity target.

This timeline is ambitious and assumes that permitting, drilling operations, and power plant construction proceed according to plan. Delays in any of these activities would extend the timeline, but the phased approach provides flexibility to adjust the pace of investment based on the performance of early wells and the evolution of power market conditions.

### Capital Investment Requirements

The total capital investment for a 400 MW geothermal field is substantial. Benchmarking against the DOE's cost targets suggests a total project cost of $1.5-2.5 billion, with drilling and completion accounting for $750 million to $1.5 billion of the total, depending on well costs and productivity.

This capital requirement exceeds Fervo's balance sheet capacity and will require project financing — a combination of debt (typically 60-70% of project cost, secured by off-take agreements with creditworthy counterparties) and equity (30-40%, contributed by Fervo and project equity partners). The Google power purchase agreement, discussed in a separate dossier, provides the offtake anchor for initial project financing.

The availability of project finance for EGS is uncertain because commercial banks and institutional investors have limited experience with geothermal projects and none with utility-scale EGS. The DOE's Loan Programs Office, which has the authority to provide loan guarantees for innovative energy projects under Title XVII of the Energy Policy Act, is a potential source of debt financing for Cape Station. A DOE loan guarantee would substantially de-risk the project for commercial lenders and could be the catalyst that unlocks private capital for EGS project finance.
