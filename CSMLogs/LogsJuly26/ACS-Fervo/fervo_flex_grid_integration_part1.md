## Beyond Baseload: The Flexible Geothermal Opportunity

Geothermal power has historically been operated as baseload generation — producing at a constant output level 24 hours per day — because the marginal cost of generation is near zero (the "fuel" is free heat from the Earth) and the revenue from selling every megawatt-hour produced exceeds the negligible cost of producing it. Fervo Energy's FervoFlex technology challenges this operational paradigm by enabling geothermal plants to vary their output in response to grid conditions — ramping up when electricity prices are high and curtailing when prices are low or when renewable generation is abundant.

### The Value of Dispatchability

The electricity grid of the future — dominated by variable wind and solar generation — requires flexible resources that can respond to changes in net load (total demand minus variable renewable generation). The "duck curve," first observed in California and now appearing in electricity markets globally, illustrates the challenge: solar generation depresses net load during midday hours, then net load ramps sharply in the evening as solar output declines and demand peaks. The grid requires resources that can ramp up to meet this evening peak and ramp down during the midday solar surplus.

Battery storage is the technology most commonly deployed to provide this flexibility, but batteries have limitations: they can only shift energy over hours, not days or weeks; they degrade with cycling; and their cost, while declining, remains significant. Natural gas peaking plants provide flexibility but produce carbon emissions. FervoFlex offers an alternative: a carbon-free resource that can provide flexibility over any timescale — hourly, daily, seasonally — without degradation or emissions.

### The Physics of Flexible Geothermal Operation

The concept of flexible geothermal operation is based on a simple physical principle: the geothermal reservoir is a thermal battery. When the power plant reduces its output — extracting less heat from the produced geothermal fluid — the reservoir temperature recovers through conduction of heat from the surrounding rock. The "stored" thermal energy can be accessed later by increasing extraction.

In practice, flexible operation is implemented by varying the flow rate of the production pump. Reducing the production flow rate reduces the mass flow of hot water to the power plant, reducing electricity output. The water that is not produced remains in the reservoir, heating up through conduction. When electricity prices increase, the production flow rate is increased, and the plant ramps to higher output.

The ramp rate of a geothermal plant — the speed at which it can increase or decrease output — is limited by thermal stresses in the power plant equipment and by the hydraulic response of the reservoir to changes in production rate. Fervo has not disclosed the ramp rates achievable with FervoFlex, but geothermal plants in New Zealand and Iceland have demonstrated the ability to ramp at rates of 5-10% of capacity per minute, which is comparable to natural gas-fired plants and substantially faster than coal or nuclear.

### Revenue Optimization Through Load-Following

In electricity markets with high renewable penetration — California, Texas, the United Kingdom — wholesale electricity prices exhibit significant intra-day and seasonal variation. During midday hours, when solar generation is abundant, prices can fall to zero or become negative (generators must pay to inject electricity into the grid). During evening peak hours, when solar generation declines and demand remains high, prices can spike to $100-200/MWh or higher.

A flexible geothermal plant can optimize its revenue by shifting production from low-price periods to high-price periods. The plant operates at reduced output during midday solar surplus, preserving reservoir heat, and ramps to full output during evening peak pricing. The revenue uplift from this operational strategy, relative to constant-output baseload operation, depends on the magnitude and predictability of price spreads between low-price and high-price periods.

Analysis of California wholesale electricity prices suggests that a flexible geothermal plant could achieve revenue uplifts of 10-25% relative to constant-output operation, depending on the specific hourly price profile, the achievable ramp rate, and the depth of turndown (the minimum sustainable output level as a percentage of full capacity). This revenue uplift provides an economic incentive for flexible operation that is independent of any policy support or capacity market payments.

### Grid Reliability Services

Beyond energy arbitrage, flexible geothermal resources can provide ancillary services to the grid — services that maintain grid frequency, voltage, and reliability — for which the grid operator compensates the generator. These services include frequency regulation (responding to second-by-second fluctuations in grid frequency), spinning reserves (capacity that can be brought online within 10 minutes to replace a lost generator), and non-spinning reserves (capacity available within 30 minutes).

The market for ancillary services is substantial: in the CAISO market, ancillary service procurement costs total hundreds of millions of dollars annually. A geothermal plant that can provide these services — particularly frequency regulation, which requires rapid and precise response — can earn ancillary service revenue in addition to energy revenue, further improving project economics.
