## The Physics of Engineered Geothermal Reservoirs

An Enhanced Geothermal System operates on straightforward thermodynamic principles: inject cold water into hot rock at depth, allow the water to absorb heat as it flows through an engineered fracture network, and produce hot water or steam to drive a turbine-generator at the surface. The engineering challenge lies in creating and managing the subsurface heat exchanger — the fracture network — to maximize heat extraction while minimizing parasitic losses.

### Heat Transfer Fundamentals

The heat extraction rate of an EGS reservoir is governed by three primary parameters: the rock temperature at depth, the surface area of the fracture network through which water flows, and the flow rate of water through the network. Rock temperature is a function of the geothermal gradient — the rate at which temperature increases with depth — which varies by location but averages approximately 25-30°C per kilometer in the western United States.

A well drilled to 3,000 meters (approximately 10,000 feet) in an area with a 30°C/km geothermal gradient encounters rock at approximately 115°C (assuming a surface temperature of 25°C). This temperature is sufficient for binary-cycle power generation, in which the geothermal fluid heats a secondary working fluid (typically isobutane or a refrigerant) that vaporizes at a lower temperature than water and drives a turbine. Binary-cycle plants are the standard technology for EGS applications because they can operate efficiently at the lower resource temperatures typical of EGS relative to conventional flash-steam geothermal.

At 5,000 meters (approximately 16,400 feet) with the same gradient, rock temperature reaches approximately 175°C. At these higher temperatures, the thermal-to-electric conversion efficiency improves, and the economics of the project become correspondingly more favorable. Fervo's target depth range — 3,000 to 6,000 meters — balances the drilling cost that increases with depth against the thermal efficiency that improves with temperature.

### Fracture Network Design

The fracture network is the engineered component of the EGS reservoir. In a conventional hydrothermal system, natural fractures provide the permeability that allows water circulation. In hot, dry rock targeted by EGS, the fracture network must be created through hydraulic stimulation — injecting water at high pressure to open existing natural fractures and create new ones.

The design objective is a fracture network with sufficient surface area to heat the circulating water to the target temperature, sufficient permeability to allow adequate flow rates with acceptable pumping power, and sufficient connectivity between injection and production wells without creating preferential flow paths ("short circuits") that allow injected water to reach the production well before being fully heated.

This is a multi-objective optimization problem that draws directly on the reservoir engineering methods developed for unconventional oil and gas. The same physics that governs fluid flow through hydraulically fractured shale reservoirs — Darcy's law for flow through porous media, fracture mechanics for stimulation design, tracer tests for reservoir characterization — applies to the EGS context with the substitution of heat transfer for hydrocarbon recovery.

### Thermal Lifetime and Reservoir Management

An EGS reservoir has a finite thermal lifetime: as heat is extracted, the rock mass surrounding the fracture network cools, and the produced water temperature declines over time. The rate of thermal decline depends on the heat extraction rate relative to the size of the reservoir and the rate at which heat is conducted from the surrounding rock mass into the fracture zone.

Reservoir management strategies can extend thermal lifetime. Periods of reduced production allow the rock mass to reheat through conduction from the surrounding formation. Rotating production among multiple well pairs in a field allows individual reservoirs to recover thermally while maintaining overall field output. These strategies have analogs in geothermal fields that have been operating for decades (the Geysers in California, Larderello in Italy) and are well-understood by geothermal reservoir engineers.

The thermal lifetime question has implications for project finance. A geothermal power plant is a long-lived asset — typically 30-40 years — and project lenders require confidence that the resource will sustain production over the debt term. Conventional hydrothermal projects benefit from decades of operational data demonstrating resource longevity. EGS projects lack this operational history, and the thermal decline behavior of engineered reservoirs must be characterized through modeling and relatively short operational experience. This introduces a risk premium into project financing that will decline as the EGS industry accumulates operational data.

## Comparison with Conventional Geothermal

The distinction between EGS and conventional geothermal is fundamental to understanding Fervo's market opportunity. Conventional geothermal taps natural hydrothermal systems — reservoirs of hot water or steam that exist in permeable rock formations. These resources are geographically constrained: in the United States, commercial hydrothermal resources are concentrated in California (the Geysers, Imperial Valley), Nevada, and a few locations in other western states.

The total installed capacity of conventional geothermal in the United States is approximately 3.7 gigawatts — less than 0.5% of total US electricity generation capacity. The resource is not limited by the availability of heat but by the availability of co-located heat, water, and permeability. EGS decouples the permeability requirement from the heat and water requirements, expanding the addressable resource by orders of magnitude.

The DOE estimates that EGS could support 60-90 gigawatts of installed geothermal capacity in the United States by 2050 — a 15-25 fold increase over current geothermal capacity. Achieving this scale requires reducing the cost of EGS development from current levels to a target of $45 per megawatt-hour by 2035, as articulated in the DOE's Enhanced Geothermal Shot initiative.
