    DOMESTIC CRITICAL MINERALS INDEPENDENCE ACT OF 2026
    House of Representatives, 119th Congress, 2nd Session

================================================================================

I.  PURPOSE AND SCOPE

    The Domestic Critical Minerals Independence Act of 2026 (DCMIA)
    establishes a comprehensive, whole-of-government framework to
    achieve domestic lithium and critical minerals supply chain
    independence by calendar year 2035. The Act recognizes that the
    United States currently imports approximately ninety-seven percent
    (97%) of its lithium requirements, that the People's Republic of
    China controls over sixty-five percent (65%) of global lithium
    processing and refining capacity, and that this strategic
    vulnerability constitutes an unacceptable threat to national
    security, economic competitiveness, and the clean energy
    transition.

    The Act authorizes a total of fifty billion dollars
    ($50,000,000,000) in appropriations, loan guarantees, tax
    incentives, and direct investment over a ten-year period, divided
    across ten titles addressing the entire lithium and critical
    minerals value chain from extraction through processing,
    manufacturing, recycling, and strategic stockpiling.

II. STRATEGIC NATIONAL CONTEXT

    The United States faces a critical juncture in its energy and
    industrial policy. Domestic demand for lithium is projected to
    increase by more than 3,000 percent (3,000%) by 2040, driven by
    electric vehicle manufacturing, grid-scale battery storage, and
    defense applications. The domestic lithium processing sector
    currently accounts for approximately three percent (3%) of global
    production, concentrated almost entirely in a single facility at
    Silver Peak, Nevada, operated by Albemarle Corporation.

    The Smackover Formation in southern Arkansas represents the
    largest known lithium brine resource in North America, with
    estimated recoverable reserves exceeding four million metric tons
    of lithium carbonate equivalent (LCE). The Thacker Pass deposit
    in Humboldt County, Nevada, holds an estimated 13.7 million metric
    tons of LCE, representing the largest known sedimentary lithium
    resource in the United States.

    The Inflation Reduction Act of 2022 established domestic content
    requirements for electric vehicle battery components, creating
    substantial regulatory incentives for domestic mineral supply.
    However, these statutory requirements cannot be fully realized
    without a corresponding expansion of domestic extraction and
    processing capacity. The DCMIA bridges this gap.

III. KEY PROVISIONS SUMMARY

    TITLE I — DOMESTIC LITHIUM MINING
        Expedited federal permitting for lithium mining projects;
        establishment of a uniform mining royalty framework; codified
        environmental protection and reclamation standards; mandatory
        tribal consultation and community benefit agreements.

    TITLE II — DIRECT LITHIUM EXTRACTION (DLE) TECHNOLOGY
        Five billion dollars ($5,000,000,000) in DLE research and
        development funding; dedicated Arkansas Smackover Formation
        development program; Salton Sea geothermal lithium recovery
        demonstration projects; Great Salt Lake mineral extraction
        feasibility and environmental impact assessment.

    TITLE III — PROCESSING AND MANUFACTURING INFRASTRUCTURE
        Fifteen billion dollars ($15,000,000,000) in loans and grants
        for domestic lithium hydroxide conversion plants, cathode
        active material and anode active material manufacturing
        facilities; establishment of a Strategic Processing Reserve
        to maintain minimum processing capacity during market
        disruptions.

    TITLE IV — BATTERY RECYCLING AND CIRCULAR ECONOMY
        National battery collection and reverse logistics
        infrastructure; advanced recycling technology grants for
        electro-extraction and hydrometallurgical processes;
        minimum recycled content requirements for new battery
        manufacturing; spent lithium battery export controls and
        domestic processing requirements.

    TITLE V — STRATEGIC INTERNATIONAL PARTNERSHIPS
        Bilateral critical minerals agreements with Australia, Canada,
        Chile, Argentina, and the Democratic Republic of the Congo;
        Western Hemisphere Minerals Partnership framework;
        diversification of supply away from PRC-controlled sources.

    TITLE VI — RESEARCH AND DEVELOPMENT
        Next-generation battery chemistry research including silicon
        anode, solid-state electrolyte, sodium-ion, sodium-metal,
        Prussian blue analog, and lithium-sulfur technologies;
        advanced manufacturing process development; critical
        materials substitution research.

    TITLE VII — FUNDING AND TAX INCENTIVES
        Fifty billion dollars ($50,000,000,000) total authorization;
        extension of Section 45X Advanced Manufacturing Production
        Credit for domestic critical mineral processing; investment
        tax credit for qualifying processing and recycling
        facilities; expansion of Department of Energy Title XVII
        loan guarantee programs.

    TITLE VIII — PERMITTING REFORM
        Expansion of FAST-41 covered projects to include critical
        mineral mining and processing; NEPA review modernization;
        state-federal permitting coordination framework; binding
        permitting decision timelines with accountability mechanisms.

    TITLE IX — NATIONAL STRATEGIC LITHIUM STOCKPILE
        Establishment of a National Lithium Stockpile under Defense
        Production Act Title III authority; minimum six-month
        emergency supply target; market stabilization release
        authority; domestic purchase preference requirements.

    TITLE X — IMPLEMENTATION AND OVERSIGHT
        Critical Minerals Supply Chain Office within the Department
        of Energy; annual reporting to Congress; Inspector General
        oversight; ten-year sunset with reauthorization review.

IV. ECONOMIC AND NATIONAL SECURITY IMPACT

    The DCMIA is projected to create approximately 85,000 direct
    domestic jobs in mining, processing, manufacturing, and recycling
    sectors, with an additional 150,000 indirect and induced jobs.
    The Act will reduce annual lithium import expenditures by an
    estimated twelve billion dollars ($12,000,000,000) by 2035.
    Full domestic supply chain independence is projected to reduce
    battery pack costs by fifteen to twenty-two percent (15-22%)
    relative to current import-dependent pricing, accelerating
    electric vehicle adoption and grid storage deployment.

    From a national security perspective, the DCMIA eliminates a
    critical single-point-of-failure in the defense industrial base.
    Lithium is essential to all modern military battery systems,
    including portable power, unmanned systems, submarine propulsion,
    and directed energy weapons. Dependence on adversarial nations
    for this mineral constitutes a direct threat to military
    readiness and operational capability.

V.  COMPLIANCE WITH EXISTING LAW

    This Act shall be implemented in a manner consistent with the
    National Environmental Policy Act (42 U.S.C. 4321 et seq.), the
    Clean Water Act (33 U.S.C. 1251 et seq.), the Clean Air Act
    (42 U.S.C. 7401 et seq.), the Endangered Species Act (16 U.S.C.
    1531 et seq.), the Mining Law of 1872 (30 U.S.C. 22 et seq.),
    the Defense Production Act of 1950 (50 U.S.C. 4501 et seq.), and
    all applicable tribal sovereignty and consultation requirements.
