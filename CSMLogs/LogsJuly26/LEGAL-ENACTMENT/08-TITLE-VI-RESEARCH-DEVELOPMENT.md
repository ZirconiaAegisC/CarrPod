    SEC. 601. NEXT-GENERATION BATTERY CHEMISTRY RESEARCH.

        (a) ESTABLISHMENT OF PROGRAM. — The Secretary of Energy,
        acting through the Office of Energy Efficiency and Renewable
        Energy and the Advanced Research Projects Agency-Energy
        (ARPA-E), shall establish a Next-Generation Battery Chemistry
        Research Program.

        (b) RESEARCH PRIORITIES. — The Program shall fund research,
        development, and demonstration of the following battery
        chemistry platforms:

            (1) SILICON ANODE LITHIUM-ION BATTERIES. — Research to
                increase silicon content in graphite-silicon composite
                anodes above fifty percent (50%) by mass, stabilize
                the solid-electrolyte interphase during volume
                expansion cycling, and reduce capacity fade to less
                than ten percent (10%) over one thousand (1,000)
                charge-discharge cycles. Target energy density:
                not less than four hundred (400) watt-hours per
                kilogram at the cell level by 2030.

            (2) SOLID-STATE ELECTROLYTE BATTERIES. — Research across
                multiple solid electrolyte chemistries, including:
                    (A) Oxide-based solid electrolytes (LLZO,
                        LATP, LLTO);
                    (B) Sulfide-based solid electrolytes (LGPS,
                        argyrodite-type);
                    (C) Polymer-based solid electrolytes;
                    (D) Thin-film solid electrolytes.
                Target: commercial-scale manufacturing demonstration
                by 2028, with not more than fifty dollars ($50) per
                kilowatt-hour electrolyte cost at scale.

            (3) SODIUM-ION BATTERIES. — Research to develop sodium-ion
                battery chemistries using Prussian blue analog
                cathodes and hard carbon anodes as a lithium-free
                alternative for grid storage and low-cost electric
                vehicle applications. Target energy density: not
                less than one hundred sixty (160) watt-hours per
                kilogram by 2028.

            (4) SODIUM-METAL BATTERIES. — Research to enable stable
                sodium metal anode cycling through advanced
                electrolyte formulations, artificial solid-electrolyte
                interphase layers, and three-dimensional current
                collector architectures.

            (5) LITHIUM-SULFUR BATTERIES. — Research to address the
                polysulfide shuttle effect, improve sulfur cathode
                utilization above eighty percent (80%), and achieve
                cycle life exceeding five hundred (500) cycles.
                Target gravimetric energy density: not less than
                five hundred (500) watt-hours per kilogram.

            (6) LITHIUM-AIR AND METAL-AIR CHEMISTRIES. — Fundamental
                research into oxygen reduction and evolution catalysts,
                electrolyte stability in the presence of reactive
                oxygen species, and lithium metal anode protection
                strategies.

        (c) FUNDING. — There is authorized to be appropriated to the
        Secretary of Energy three billion dollars ($3,000,000,000)
        for the Next-Generation Battery Chemistry Research Program
        for fiscal years 2027 through 2036, of which:

            (1) Not less than one billion dollars ($1,000,000,000)
                shall be allocated for solid-state battery research
                and manufacturing process development;

            (2) Not less than seven hundred fifty million dollars
                ($750,000,000) shall be allocated for silicon anode
                and advanced anode research;

            (3) Not less than five hundred million dollars
                ($500,000,000) shall be allocated for sodium-based
                battery research;

            (4) Remaining amounts shall be allocated across lithium-
                sulfur, metal-air, and other emerging chemistries.

    SEC. 602. ADVANCED MANUFACTURING PROCESS DEVELOPMENT.

        (a) PROCESS DEVELOPMENT PROGRAM. — The Secretary of Energy
        shall establish an Advanced Manufacturing Process Development
        Program to support:

            (1) Development of continuous manufacturing processes
                for cathode active material synthesis replacing
                batch co-precipitation methods;

            (2) Dry electrode coating processes that eliminate
                toxic N-methyl-2-pyrrolidone (NMP) solvent from
                electrode manufacturing, reducing both capital and
                operating costs;

            (3) Advanced electrolyte formulation and high-throughput
                screening for novel electrolyte-solvent combinations;

            (4) In-line metrology and quality control systems using
                machine vision, X-ray diffraction, and laser-induced
                breakdown spectroscopy for real-time process
                monitoring and defect detection;

            (5) Cell-to-pack and cell-to-chassis manufacturing
                architectures that eliminate module-level components,
                increasing pack-level energy density and reducing
                manufacturing cost.

        (b) MANUFACTURING DEMONSTRATION FACILITY. — The Secretary
        shall fund the construction and operation of an Advanced
        Battery Manufacturing Demonstration Facility, to be located
        at a national laboratory, providing shared-access pilot
        manufacturing lines for:

            (1) Cathode active material synthesis at scale of up
                to one thousand (1,000) metric tons per year;

            (2) Electrode coating and calendaring;

            (3) Cell assembly, formation, and aging;

            (4) Cell and pack testing under automotive and grid
                storage duty cycles.

        (c) FUNDING. — There is authorized to be appropriated to the
        Secretary of Energy one billion five hundred million dollars
        ($1,500,000,000) for the Advanced Manufacturing Process
        Development Program for fiscal years 2027 through 2036.

    SEC. 603. CRITICAL MATERIALS SUBSTITUTION PROGRAM.

        (a) PROGRAM ESTABLISHMENT. — The Secretary of Energy shall
        establish a Critical Materials Substitution Research Program
        to develop battery chemistries and technologies that reduce
        or eliminate dependence on cobalt, nickel, graphite, and
        other critical materials for which the United States is
        import-dependent.

        (b) RESEARCH AREAS. — The Program shall support:

            (1) Cobalt-free cathode active materials, including
                high-nickel NMA (nickel-manganese-aluminum) and
                disordered rocksalt cathodes;

            (2) Nickel-free cathode chemistries, including advanced
                lithium iron phosphate (LFP) and lithium manganese
                iron phosphate (LMFP) with improved energy density;

            (3) Synthetic graphite alternatives, including hard
                carbon from biomass precursors and silicon-dominant
                anodes;

            (4) Sodium-based chemistries as a complete lithium
                substitution technology for stationary storage
                applications.

        (c) FUNDING. — There is authorized to be appropriated to the
        Secretary of Energy five hundred million dollars ($500,000,000)
        for the Critical Materials Substitution Research Program for
        fiscal years 2027 through 2036.

    SEC. 604. NATIONAL LABORATORIES COORDINATION.

        (a) CRITICAL MINERALS RESEARCH COORDINATION OFFICE. — The
        Secretary of Energy shall establish, within the Office of
        Science, a Critical Minerals Research Coordination Office
        to coordinate and integrate critical mineral research
        activities across:

            (1) Argonne National Laboratory (battery chemistry and
                materials characterization);

            (2) Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory (advanced
                light source, molecular foundry);

            (3) Oak Ridge National Laboratory (materials synthesis,
                neutron scattering);

            (4) National Renewable Energy Laboratory (battery
                performance and lifetime modeling);

            (5) Idaho National Laboratory (battery testing and
                recycling process development);

            (6) Pacific Northwest National Laboratory (grid storage
                applications);

            (7) Sandia National Laboratories (materials science,
                reliability engineering);

            (8) Lawrence Livermore National Laboratory (computational
                materials science, machine learning for materials
                discovery).

        (b) INDUSTRY ACCESS. — The Coordination Office shall establish
        streamlined mechanisms for private-sector access to national
        laboratory facilities, expertise, and intellectual property
        for critical mineral and battery technology development,
        including cooperative research and development agreements
        (CRADAs), strategic partnership projects, and user facility
        agreements.

        (c) FUNDING. — There is authorized to be appropriated to the
        Secretary of Energy two hundred fifty million dollars
        ($250,000,000) for the Critical Minerals Research Coordination
        Office for fiscal years 2027 through 2036.
