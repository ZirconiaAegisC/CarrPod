    SEC. 501. BILATERAL CRITICAL MINERALS AGREEMENTS.

        (a) AUTHORIZATION. — The President is authorized, through the
        Secretary of State and the United States Trade Representative,
        to negotiate and enter into bilateral Critical Minerals
        Agreements with allied and partner nations for the purpose
        of securing diversified, reliable, and ethically sourced
        supplies of lithium and other critical minerals.

        (b) ELIGIBLE NATIONS. — Priority for Critical Minerals
        Agreement negotiations shall be given to:

            (1) AUSTRALIA. — The Commonwealth of Australia, as the
                world's largest lithium producer, with a negotiated
                agreement to:
                    (A) Secure long-term lithium spodumene
                        concentrate supply contracts for domestic
                        processing;
                    (B) Establish joint processing ventures with
                        Australian lithium producers;
                    (C) Coordinate critical minerals stockpile
                        policies;
                    (D) Harmonize environmental and labor standards
                        for lithium mining and processing.

            (2) CANADA. — Canada, with specific provisions for:
                    (A) The Quebec-Arkansas Critical Minerals
                        Corridor, establishing an integrated
                        lithium supply chain from Canadian mines
                        to United States processing facilities;
                    (B) Joint development of the James Bay and
                        Whabouchi lithium districts;
                    (C) Cross-border permitting coordination for
                        critical mineral projects;
                    (D) Harmonized battery recycling and circular
                        economy standards.

            (3) CHILE AND ARGENTINA. — The Republic of Chile and the
                Argentine Republic, with provisions for:
                    (A) Direct Lithium Extraction technology
                        transfer and joint development programs;
                    (B) United States investment in lithium brine
                        development in the Lithium Triangle;
                    (C) Environmental sustainability standards for
                        Salar brine extraction;
                    (D) Diversification of lithium supply away from
                        PRC-controlled supply chains.

        (c) AGREEMENT REQUIREMENTS. — Each Critical Minerals Agreement
        shall include:

            (1) Binding commitments on transparency of mineral
                concession awards and royalty payments;

            (2) Environmental and labor protection provisions
                consistent with International Labour Organization
                core conventions;

            (3) Provisions for dispute resolution through
                international arbitration or other mutually
                agreed mechanisms;

            (4) Reporting requirements for both parties on mineral
                production, exports, and pricing;

            (5) Restrictions on export of critical minerals to
                nations designated as foreign adversaries by the
                Secretary of Commerce.

        (d) REPORT TO CONGRESS. — Not later than one hundred eighty
        (180) days after the date of enactment of this Act, and
        annually thereafter, the Secretary of State and the United
        States Trade Representative shall submit to Congress a
        report on the status of Critical Minerals Agreement
        negotiations and implementation.

    SEC. 502. WESTERN HEMISPHERE MINERALS PARTNERSHIP.

        (a) ESTABLISHMENT. — The Secretary of State, in coordination
        with the Secretary of Commerce, the Secretary of Energy, and
        the United States Trade Representative, shall establish a
        Western Hemisphere Minerals Partnership with the governments
        of Canada, Mexico, Chile, Argentina, Bolivia, Peru, Brazil,
        and such other Western Hemisphere nations as may elect to
        participate.

        (b) OBJECTIVES. — The Partnership shall pursue:

            (1) Harmonization of critical mineral resource reporting
                standards and geological survey methodologies across
                the Western Hemisphere;

            (2) Joint investment in critical mineral exploration,
                extraction, and processing infrastructure;

            (3) Coordination of critical mineral stockpile policies
                and emergency response mechanisms;

            (4) Development of common environmental sustainability
                standards for lithium brine extraction in high-
                altitude salar environments;

            (5) Coordination of policies regarding the participation
                of foreign state-owned enterprises in critical mineral
                supply chains within the Western Hemisphere.

        (c) FUNDING. — There is authorized to be appropriated to the
        Secretary of State two hundred fifty million dollars
        ($250,000,000) for the establishment and operation of the
        Western Hemisphere Minerals Partnership for fiscal years
        2027 through 2036.

    SEC. 503. AFRICAN CRITICAL MINERALS ENGAGEMENT.

        (a) FINDINGS. — The Congress finds that:

            (1) The Democratic Republic of the Congo produces
                approximately seventy percent (70%) of global
                cobalt supply, with a substantial portion of
                production controlled by Chinese state-owned
                enterprises through long-term offtake agreements;

            (2) Zimbabwe, Namibia, Mali, and Ghana possess
                significant lithium resources, with Chinese
                enterprises holding controlling stakes in the
                majority of active lithium development projects
                in those nations;

            (3) United States engagement in African critical
                mineral development must prioritize transparency,
                labor rights, environmental protection, and
                community benefit in contrast to extractive
                models employed by strategic competitors.

        (b) ENGAGEMENT STRATEGY. — The Secretary of State shall:

            (1) Establish a Critical Minerals Technical Assistance
                Program for African partner nations, providing
                geological survey support, mining regulatory
                framework development, and environmental
                monitoring capacity building;

            (2) Provide financing through the United States
                International Development Finance Corporation
                (DFC) for critical mineral projects in Africa that
                meet high environmental, social, and governance
                standards;

            (3) Negotiate bilateral investment treaties with African
                nations that include specific provisions protecting
                United States investor interests in critical mineral
                projects.

        (c) FUNDING. — There is authorized to be appropriated five
        hundred million dollars ($500,000,000) for the African
        Critical Minerals Engagement program for fiscal years 2027
        through 2036.

    SEC. 504. INDO-PACIFIC MINERALS SECURITY FRAMEWORK.

        (a) EXPANSION. — The Secretary of State shall expand the
        Minerals Security Partnership, in coordination with the
        governments of Australia, Canada, Finland, France, Germany,
        Japan, the Republic of Korea, Sweden, the United Kingdom, and
        the European Commission, to include:

            (1) Joint investment funds for critical mineral projects
                in partner nations;

            (2) Coordinated stockpile policies to reduce collective
                vulnerability to supply disruptions;

            (3) Shared infrastructure investment in mineral processing
                facilities located in partner nations;

            (4) Common standards for responsible mineral sourcing and
                supply chain due diligence.

        (b) PARTICIPATION RESTRICTIONS. — Membership in the Minerals
        Security Partnership and access to Framework-sponsored
        financing shall not be available to any nation designated as
        a foreign adversary or to any entity owned or controlled by
        such a nation.

        (c) ANNUAL REPORT. — The Secretary of State shall submit an
        annual report to Congress on Minerals Security Partnership
        activities, including:

            (1) Projects funded and under development;

            (2) Aggregate mineral production capacity secured;

            (3) Changes in PRC market share of critical mineral
                processing across key segments;

            (4) Recommendations for additional partnership actions.
