================================================================================
               TITLE IV — BATTERY RECYCLING AND CIRCULAR ECONOMY
================================================================================

    SEC. 401. NATIONAL BATTERY COLLECTION INFRASTRUCTURE.

        (a) FINDINGS. — The Congress finds that:

            (1) Fewer than five percent (5%) of lithium-ion batteries
                in the United States are currently recycled, with the
                vast majority disposed of in municipal solid waste
                landfills, representing both an environmental hazard
                and a substantial loss of recoverable critical
                materials with an estimated value exceeding two
                billion dollars ($2,000,000,000) annually;

            (2) Battery recycling can recover up to ninety-five
                percent (95%) of lithium, cobalt, nickel, manganese,
                and graphite content through modern hydrometallurgical
                and pyrometallurgical processes, with advanced
                electro-extraction technologies achieving lithium
                recovery rates exceeding ninety-seven percent (97%);

            (3) End-of-life electric vehicle batteries represent an
                increasingly significant domestic resource, with an
                estimated three hundred forty thousand (340,000)
                metric tons of spent batteries reaching end-of-life
                annually by 2035.

        (b) NATIONAL COLLECTION NETWORK. —

            (1) ESTABLISHMENT. — The Administrator of the
                Environmental Protection Agency, in coordination
                with the Secretary of Energy and the Secretary of
                Transportation, shall establish a National Battery
                Collection and Reverse Logistics Network.

            (2) COMPONENTS. — The Network shall include:
                    (A) A minimum of one (1) certified battery
                        collection point in each county with a
                        population exceeding one hundred thousand
                        (100,000) residents;
                    (B) Regional battery consolidation and
                        sorting centers in each of the ten (10)
                        Environmental Protection Agency regions;
                    (C) A standardized battery labeling and tracking
                        system for end-of-life management;
                    (D) A public education campaign regarding
                        battery recycling requirements and options.

            (3) PRODUCER RESPONSIBILITY. — As a condition of sale
                in the United States, manufacturers of lithium-ion
                batteries with capacity exceeding one kilowatt-hour
                (1 kWh) shall participate in a producer-funded
                collection and recycling program approved by the
                Environmental Protection Agency.

        (c) FUNDING. — There is authorized to be appropriated to the
        Environmental Protection Agency one billion five hundred
        million dollars ($1,500,000,000) for the establishment and
        operation of the National Battery Collection and Reverse
        Logistics Network for fiscal years 2027 through 2031.

    SEC. 402. ADVANCED RECYCLING TECHNOLOGY GRANTS.

        (a) GRANT PROGRAM. — The Secretary of Energy shall establish
        a competitive grant program for advanced battery recycling
        technology research, development, and demonstration.

        (b) ELIGIBLE TECHNOLOGIES. — Grants under this section shall
        support the following technology pathways:

            (1) HYDROMETALLURGICAL PROCESSES. — Processes using
                aqueous chemistry for selective metal recovery,
                including leaching with organic and mineral acids,
                solvent extraction, ion exchange, and selective
                precipitation;

            (2) ELECTRO-EXTRACTION. — Electrochemical processes for
                direct recovery of lithium, cobalt, nickel, and
                manganese from battery black mass without intermediate
                chemical precipitation steps;

            (3) DIRECT CATHODE RECYCLING. — Processes that recover
                cathode active material in a form suitable for direct
                reuse in new battery manufacturing without complete
                chemical breakdown and re-synthesis;

            (4) MECHANICAL PRE-TREATMENT. — Advanced shredding,
                sorting, and separation technologies to produce
                high-purity black mass streams with minimized
                cross-contamination;

            (5) PROCESS INTENSIFICATION. — Continuous-flow recycling
                processes, modular recycling systems, and process
                integration technologies that reduce capital and
                operating costs of recycling facilities.

        (c) DEMONSTRATION FACILITIES. — Not less than one billion
        dollars ($1,000,000,000) of amounts authorized under this
        section shall be allocated for the construction and operation
        of not fewer than five (5) commercial-scale demonstration
        recycling facilities, each with processing capacity of not
        less than ten thousand (10,000) metric tons of battery
        material per year, to be operational not later than
        December 31, 2032.

        (d) FUNDING. — There is authorized to be appropriated to the
        Secretary of Energy two billion five hundred million dollars
        ($2,500,000,000) for the grant program established under this
        section for fiscal years 2027 through 2036.

    SEC. 403. RECYCLED CONTENT MANDATES.

        (a) BATTERY MANUFACTURING REQUIREMENTS. —

            (1) Effective January 1, 2032, all lithium-ion batteries
                sold in the United States for electric vehicle
                applications with a capacity exceeding one
                kilowatt-hour (1 kWh) shall contain not less than
                the following minimum percentages of recycled
                content by mass of critical material:

                    (A) LITHIUM. — Not less than ten percent (10%)
                        recycled lithium content, increasing to
                        twenty percent (20%) effective January 1,
                        2035, and thirty percent (30%) effective
                        January 1, 2040;

                    (B) COBALT. — Not less than twenty-five percent
                        (25%) recycled cobalt content, increasing to
                        fifty percent (50%) effective January 1,
                        2035;

                    (C) NICKEL. — Not less than fifteen percent (15%)
                        recycled nickel content, increasing to
                        thirty percent (30%) effective January 1,
                        2035;

                    (D) GRAPHITE. — Not less than ten percent (10%)
                        recycled graphite content, increasing to
                        twenty-five percent (25%) effective January
                        1, 2035.

            (2) The Secretary of Energy, in consultation with the
                Administrator of the Environmental Protection Agency,
                shall establish chain-of-custody documentation and
                third-party verification requirements for recycled
                content compliance.

        (b) WAIVER AUTHORITY. — The Secretary of Energy may grant a
        temporary waiver of recycled content requirements for a
        period not exceeding two (2) years upon a finding that
        sufficient recycled material supply is not available at
        commercially reasonable prices due to factors beyond the
        control of the applicant.

        (c) REPORTING. — Battery manufacturers shall annually report
        recycled content data to the Secretary of Energy, who shall
        publish an annual Recycled Content Compliance Report.

    SEC. 404. SPENT BATTERY EXPORT CONTROLS.

        (a) EXPORT RESTRICTIONS. —

            (1) Effective January 1, 2029, spent lithium-ion
                batteries and battery-derived black mass containing
                lithium, cobalt, nickel, manganese, or graphite shall
                be classified as a Strategic Secondary Material under
                this Act.

            (2) Export of Strategic Secondary Materials shall be
                subject to licensing by the Department of Commerce,
                Bureau of Industry and Security, under procedures
                substantially similar to those established for
                exports of defense articles under the Arms Export
                Control Act (22 U.S.C. 2751 et seq.).

            (3) Licenses for export of Strategic Secondary Materials
                shall not be granted unless the applicant demonstrates
                that the material cannot be domestically processed
                at commercially reasonable terms within one hundred
                eighty (180) days.

        (b) EXCEPTIONS. — Export restrictions shall not apply to:

            (1) Integrated battery packs returned to foreign original
                equipment manufacturers under warranty or recall
                programs;

            (2) Materials exported to nations with which the United
                States has entered into a bilateral Critical Minerals
                Agreement under Title V of this Act;

            (3) Exports of five hundred (500) kilograms or less per
                calendar year for research and development purposes.

        (c) ENFORCEMENT. — Violation of export restrictions under
        this section shall be subject to civil penalties of not more
        than the greater of two hundred fifty thousand dollars
        ($250,000) or twice the market value of the exported
        material per violation, and criminal penalties for knowing
        and willful violations of not more than one million dollars
        ($1,000,000) and imprisonment of not more than ten (10) years.
