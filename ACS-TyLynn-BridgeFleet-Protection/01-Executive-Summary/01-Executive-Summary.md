5. **San Mateo-Hayward Bridge** (trestle with high-rise section) — opened 1967

BATA's Fiscal Year 2024–2033 Capital Improvement Plan (CIP) represents a $2.3 billion commitment to preserving and enhancing these critical assets. ### The Corrosion Challenge: Economics, Safety, and Sustainability

Corrosion of steel and reinforced concrete bridge elements is the single largest maintenance cost driver for transportation infrastructure in the United States. The direct cost of corrosion to US highway bridges is estimated at $13.6 billion annually, a figure that encompasses structural steel repainting, concrete rehabilitation, deck replacement, and traffic disruption costs. For coastal and marine environments such as San Francisco Bay, the corrosion burden is disproportionately severe due to the combined effects of salt-laden atmosphere, tidal splash zones, fog condensation cycles, and chloride ion penetration into reinforced concrete.

Traditional protective coating systems — predominantly multi-coat organic paint systems applied over abrasive-blasted steel — have served the industry for decades but carry fundamental lifecycle limitations. A conventional paint system on a marine bridge typically requires full removal and replacement every 15 to 25 years, with spot repairs occurring at 5- to 10-year intervals. The Richmond-San Rafael Bridge painting project, completed in 2024 at a cost of $68 million, illustrates the scale of expenditure required for a single repainting cycle on a single structure. Extrapolated across the ten-structure Bay Area fleet over a 75-year horizon, assuming three to four repaint cycles per structure, the cumulative cost of conventional painting approaches $1.5 billion to $2.0 billion in present-value terms — excluding the indirect costs of traffic management, containment, environmental compliance, and worker safety.

Each repainting cycle also imposes significant non-financial burdens. Abrasive blasting of existing coatings — many of which contain lead-based primers applied before the 1978 ban on lead paint — generates hazardous waste requiring full containment, transport, and disposal under stringent EPA and Cal/OSHA regulations. Workers must operate in Level C protective equipment within negative-pressure enclosures. Traffic lane closures disrupt the 1.3 million daily vehicle crossings, with economic productivity losses estimated at $2 million to $5 million per day for a major Bay Area crossing. Each cycle of blasting, containment, and repainting imposes an environmental footprint that is increasingly difficult to reconcile with California's ambitious climate and sustainability targets.

The fundamental question confronting bridge asset managers is whether continuing the cycle of 15- to 25-year paint maintenance is economically rational when alternative protective systems offering 60- to 100-plus-year service lives are commercially available, technically mature, and demonstrably superior in lifecycle cost analyses.

### Strategic Framework: Five Pillars of Fleet Protection

This dossier presents a strategic framework organized around five interdependent pillars, each of which is explored in depth in subsequent sections of this document. The framework is designed to be implemented as a phased, fleet-wide program that optimizes resource allocation across multiple structures while minimizing traffic disruption through coordinated scheduling of inspections, protection application, and monitoring activities.

#### Pillar 1: Fleet-Wide Condition Assessment and Prioritization

Effective fleet management begins with accurate, current, and predictive condition data. Advanced material degradation analytics — applied through proprietary modeling frameworks — enable a systematic, multi-factor assessment of every structural element across the fleet. These frameworks integrate corrosion severity indices, chloride exposure mapping, seismic fragility curves, traffic criticality scoring, and remaining service life projections to produce a prioritized intervention matrix. Rather than treating each bridge as an isolated asset, the fleet-wide approach identifies common failure modes, groups structures by similar exposure conditions, and schedules interventions to achieve economies of scale in contractor mobilization, containment setup, and material procurement.

The output of this assessment is a dynamic, data-driven prioritization model that ranks every structural element by urgency of intervention, assigns recommended protection systems based on exposure environment, projects the cost of deferred action, and identifies opportunities for bundled procurement across multiple structures. This approach transforms bridge preservation from a reactive, structure-by-structure maintenance function into a proactive, portfolio-level asset management discipline.

#### Pillar 2: Duplex Coating Systems — Thermal-Sprayed Zinc with Organic Topcoat

Thermal-sprayed zinc (TSZ) duplex coating systems — consisting of a thermally applied metallic zinc layer (typically 99.9% pure zinc, applied by arc-spray or flame-spray process) sealed with an organic topcoat — represent the most significant advance in structural steel corrosion protection since the introduction of inorganic zinc-rich primers. Unlike conventional paint systems that function as passive barriers and degrade progressively from the moment of application, TSZ duplex systems provide active cathodic protection: the zinc layer sacrificially corrodes to protect the underlying steel, effectively eliminating underfilm corrosion propagation at scratches, holidays, and impact damage sites.

The performance record of TSZ duplex systems is extraordinary and well-documented. The Norwegian Public Roads Administration has applied TSZ duplex coatings to coastal and fjord-crossing bridges since 1965. Norwegian bridge inventories indicate that structures protected with TSZ duplex systems have required only a single maintenance event — typically a topcoat refresh — over 50 years of continuous service in some of the world's most aggressive marine environments. This compares to three to four complete repainting cycles for conventionally coated structures over the same period. The Norwegian experience, corroborated by independent studies from Japan, the United Kingdom, and the United States (FHWA, NACE International, and SSPC), establishes that TSZ duplex systems can reasonably be expected to deliver 60 to 100-plus years of primary corrosion protection in marine and industrial environments classified as ISO 12944 categories C4 (high corrosivity) and C5 (very high corrosivity).

The economic implications for the Bay Area toll bridge fleet are transformative. Replacing the 15- to 25-year paint maintenance cycle with a 60- to 100-plus-year duplex coating cycle eliminates two to three complete repainting events per structure over the design life, reducing direct coating costs by an estimated 40% to 60% in net present value terms. The avoidance of repeated containment, hazardous waste disposal, and traffic disruption adds further economic benefit that, when fully quantified, positions TSZ duplex systems as the dominant economic choice for any bridge structure with a remaining service life exceeding 30 years.

#### Pillar 3: Cathodic Protection for Concrete Substructures

Reinforced and prestressed concrete bridge substructures in marine environments are vulnerable to chloride-induced corrosion of embedded reinforcing steel — a deterioration mechanism that is particularly aggressive in the tidal splash and spray zones characteristic of San Francisco Bay crossings. Once chloride concentrations at the depth of reinforcing steel exceed the corrosion threshold (typically 0.05% to 0.10% by weight of concrete for prestressed elements and 0.20% to 0.40% for conventionally reinforced elements), active corrosion initiates, and the resulting expansive rust products cause concrete cracking, spalling, delamination, and progressive loss of structural capacity.

Cathodic protection (CP) is the only proven technology for halting chloride-induced corrosion of reinforcing steel in concrete without complete removal and replacement of chloride-contaminated material. Two principal CP approaches are applicable to bridge substructures:

**Impressed Current Cathodic Protection (ICCP)** applies a small direct current between an inert anode (typically mixed metal oxide-coated titanium mesh, ribbon, or probe anodes) embedded in or attached to the concrete surface and the reinforcing steel cathode. The impressed current polarizes the steel to a potential at which corrosion is thermodynamically impossible. ICCP systems are inherently controllable, monitorable, and adaptable to changing conditions, and modern systems incorporate remote monitoring capabilities that substantially reduce inspection labor costs.

**Galvanic Cathodic Protection (GCP)** — also called sacrificial anode CP — utilizes metals that are naturally anodic to steel (zinc, aluminum-zinc-indium alloys) placed in electrical continuity with the reinforcing steel. The galvanic potential difference drives a protective current without requiring external power. GCP systems are simpler to install and maintain than ICCP but deliver lower current densities and are generally best suited to lower-resistivity environments or as supplemental protection in conjunction with ICCP for critical elements.

The Oregon Department of Transportation (ODOT) has protected more than 15 historic coastal bridges using TSZ anodes configured as impressed current CP anodes. The Yaquina Bay Bridge (Newport, Oregon, 1936), the Astoria-Megler Bridge (1966), and the Depoe Bay Bridge (1927) — all reinforced concrete arch and beam structures in aggressive Pacific Ocean coastal environments — have been successfully protected using these systems. ODOT's monitoring data indicates that impressed current CP applied to these structures has effectively halted active corrosion, with depolarization measurements consistently exceeding the NACE SP0290 100 mV criterion. The projected service life extension for these protected structures is 50 to 75 years beyond their unprotected condition.

A particularly instructive case for the Bay Area is the TSZ anode ICCP system developed and standardized by ODOT. In this configuration, thermal-sprayed zinc — the same material used in duplex coating systems for structural steel — is applied directly to the concrete surface to serve as a distributed impressed current anode. This achieves the dual benefit of providing cathodic protection to the embedded reinforcing steel while simultaneously acting as a protective coating for the concrete surface against further chloride ingress. The combination of active corrosion control and passive chloride barrier creates an integrated protection system with a projected functional life of 30 to 40 years for the anode — at which point a simple reapplication of the zinc layer restores full protection without requiring concrete removal or structural intervention.

Cost comparisons are compelling. Cathodic protection installation costs range from approximately $12 to $52 per square foot of protected concrete surface area, depending on system complexity, anode configuration, and access conditions. By contrast, full structural replacement of a major bridge — as exemplified by the $865 million Howard Frankland Bridge replacement in Florida — represents a cost that is between 50 and 500 times higher than the cost of CP protection, depending on the scale of intervention. For the San Mateo-Hayward Bridge substructure, where replacement is physically and politically infeasible given the structure's role in the regional transportation network, cathodic protection represents not merely a cost-effective choice but the only viable long-term preservation strategy.

#### Pillar 4: Seismic Resilience and Structural Hardening

The San Francisco Bay Area is one of the most seismically active regions in the United States, situated at the boundary of the Pacific and North American tectonic plates and traversed by the San Andreas, Hayward, Calaveras, Rodgers Creek, and San Gregorio fault systems. The United States Geological Survey assigns a 72% probability of a magnitude 6.7 or greater earthquake striking the Bay Area within the 30-year period from 2014 to 2043. The economic consequences of a major seismic event disabling one or more of the Bay Area's toll bridges would be catastrophic — measured not merely in repair costs but in the paralysis of a regional economy with a GDP exceeding $1 trillion.

TYLin's institutional expertise in seismic bridge engineering is among the most distinguished in the world. The firm's innovations — including shear link beams for energy dissipation, hinge pipe beams for controlled structural articulation, and the self-anchored suspension design deployed on the new Bay Bridge East Span — represent state-of-the-art approaches to seismic resilience. The California Department of Transportation's $9 billion Toll Bridge Seismic Retrofit Program, initiated following the 1989 Loma Prieta earthquake and encompassing the seismic hardening of all seven state-owned toll bridges, stands as a landmark achievement in infrastructure protection and a powerful economic argument for proactive investment in seismic resilience.

The Loma Prieta earthquake of 17 October 1989 — magnitude 6.9, epicentered in the Santa Cruz Mountains approximately 60 miles south of San Francisco — caused a 50-foot section of the San Francisco-Oakland Bay Bridge's upper deck to collapse onto the lower deck, resulting in one fatality and the closure of the bridge for one month. The direct repair cost was approximately $30 million, but the economic impact of the closure was estimated at $300 million to $500 million in lost productivity and commerce. The event catalyzed the Toll Bridge Seismic Retrofit Program and fundamentally changed the engineering community's understanding of the vulnerability of major bridge structures to near-field ground motions.

The application of advanced seismic protection to the Bay Area toll bridge fleet should build on and extend the principles established in the retrofit program. While the major retrofit projects substantially improved the seismic performance of the primary structural systems, the full integration of corrosion protection with seismic hardening — ensuring that seismic retrofit elements are themselves protected against corrosion degradation — represents an opportunity for lifecycle optimization. Seismic isolation bearings, energy dissipation devices, and structural steel retrofit elements exposed to marine atmospheres require the same level of corrosion protection as the primary structure. A fleet-wide approach that coordinates corrosion protection with seismic retrofit activities avoids the inefficiency of protecting new elements separately from the existing structure and ensures that the full investment in seismic resilience is preserved against environmental degradation.

#### Pillar 5: Lifecycle Economics — Making the Investment Case

The economic argument for transitioning from conventional paint-based corrosion protection to advanced metallic coating and cathodic protection systems rests on rigorous lifecycle cost analysis (LCCA) supported by decades of international performance data. BATA's own Toll Bridge Asset Management Plan explicitly endorses the principle that early, higher initial investment prevents substantially higher future costs — the "Reduce Backlog" scenario proved significantly more cost-effective than "Status Quo" or "Defer Maintenance" alternatives in BATA's modeling.

A comprehensive lifecycle cost comparison of protective coating strategies over a 100-year analysis period — the standard horizon for major bridge infrastructure — reveals a clear hierarchy of economic performance:

| Protection Strategy | Expected Service Life | Cycles Over 100 Years | Relative 100-Year NPV |
|---------------------|----------------------|----------------------|----------------------|
| Hot-Dip Galvanizing | 35–70 years (C1–C3) | 1–3 | 1.0 (baseline) |
| Conventional Paint (IOZ/Epoxy/Urethane) | 15–25 years | 4–7 | 2.5–3.5× |
| Thermal-Sprayed Zinc (unsealed) | 30–50 years | 2–3 | 1.5–2.0× |
| TSZ Duplex (TSZ + Organic Topcoat) | 60–100+ years | 1 | 1.2–1.5× |

*Source: Comparative analysis based on NACE International, ISO 14713, Norwegian Road Administration, and FHWA published data. NPV = Net Present Value, normalized to hot-dip galvanizing baseline. Analysis assumes 4% discount rate, C4–C5 marine exposure environment, and includes direct application costs, traffic management, containment, waste disposal, and periodic inspection.*

In low-corrosivity environments (ISO categories C1–C3, corresponding to rural, urban, and mild industrial atmospheres), hot-dip galvanizing — the complete immersion of fabricated steel elements in molten zinc — consistently demonstrates the lowest 100-year life cycle cost. However, hot-dip galvanizing is inherently limited by the physical dimensions of galvanizing kettles and is applicable only to new construction or the complete replacement of structural elements. For existing in-situ bridge structures, TSZ and TSZ duplex systems are the practical metallic coating alternatives.

In C4 (high corrosivity — industrial and coastal) and C5 (very high corrosivity — marine, high salinity) environments, which characterize the entire San Francisco Bay bridge fleet, TSZ duplex systems dominate lifecycle cost comparisons. The Japanese Ministry of Land, Infrastructure, Transport and Tourism has published extensive comparative analyses confirming the superiority of metal coating systems in severe marine corrosion environments despite their higher initial application cost. The Japanese studies, conducted across bridges in the Seto Inland Sea and along the Pacific coast of Honshu, demonstrate that the higher initial cost of TSZ duplex application (approximately 1.5× to 2.5× the cost of a conventional three-coat paint system) is recovered within the first maintenance cycle — typically within 20 to 25 years — and yields net savings of 40% to 60% over the full 100-year analysis period.

For the Bay Area toll bridge fleet, the implication is clear: the $500 million allocated in BATA's current CIP for structural steel painting across all bridges could, if strategically redirected toward TSZ duplex systems on the highest-priority structures, reduce the 75-year cumulative coating cost from an estimated $1.5 billion to $2.0 billion to approximately $700 million to $900 million — a savings of $600 million to $1.1 billion in present-value terms. This represents a return on the incremental investment in advanced coating technology that exceeds 300% over the analysis period.

### The Solution Framework: A Systematic, Fleet-Wide Approach

The framework presented in this dossier is designed to be comprehensive, phased, and adaptable. It is grounded in the following principles:

**Systematic over Reactive.** Rather than responding to individual structure deterioration as it reaches critical thresholds, the fleet-wide approach employs proprietary modeling frameworks to forecast deterioration trajectories, identify optimal intervention timing, and coordinate activities across multiple structures to capture economies of scale.

**Lifecycle over First-Cost.** Protection system selection is driven by 100-year lifecycle cost analysis that fully accounts for direct application costs, traffic management, containment, environmental compliance, inspection, and the cost of deferred intervention. Higher initial investment in advanced protection systems is economically justified when the avoided cost of future maintenance cycles is properly quantified.

**Protection over Repair.** The most cost-effective strategy is to prevent corrosion from initiating or advancing, rather than repairing the structural damage caused by corrosion. Advanced material degradation analytics enable the identification of structures and elements that are approaching corrosion thresholds, allowing protective interventions to be applied before structural repair becomes necessary.

**Integration over Isolation.** Corrosion protection, seismic resilience, and structural health monitoring are interdependent lifecycle management functions. Coordinating these activities — applying cathodic protection in conjunction with seismic retrofit, installing monitoring sensors during coating application, scheduling inspections to feed condition data into deterioration models — reduces total program cost and improves outcomes compared to managing each function in isolation.

### Summary of Key Findings

1. **The corrosion threat is economically material.** At $13.6 billion annually nationwide, corrosion is the single largest maintenance cost for US highway bridges. For the Bay Area toll bridge fleet, the 75-year cost of continuing conventional paint-based protection is estimated at $1.5 billion to $2.0 billion in present-value terms.

2. **Advanced protection systems are technically mature and economically superior.** TSZ duplex coating systems provide 60 to 100-plus years of corrosion protection in marine environments, compared to 15 to 25 years for conventional paint. Norwegian bridges protected with TSZ duplex systems have required only one maintenance event in 50 years. Lifecycle cost savings of 40% to 60% over a 100-year analysis period are supported by international data.

3. **Cathodic protection is essential for concrete substructures.** The San Mateo-Hayward Bridge substructure — rated "Poor" and requiring over $163 million in remediation — represents the most urgent application for impressed current cathodic protection. Oregon DOT's successful protection of 15-plus historic coastal bridges using TSZ anode ICCP systems provides a proven technical pathway.

4. **Seismic and corrosion protection must be integrated.** The $9 billion Caltrans Toll Bridge Seismic Retrofit Program demonstrated the value of proactive seismic hardening. Ensuring that seismic retrofit elements are protected from corrosion — through the same advanced coating and CP systems applied to primary structural elements — preserves the full value of the seismic investment.

5. **BATA's own analysis supports early, higher investment.** The TBAMP "Reduce Backlog" scenario is explicitly identified as the most cost-effective long-term strategy. The current $2.3 billion, 10-year CIP provides the funding mechanism to transition from reactive paint maintenance to proactive fleet protection.

6. **TYLin is uniquely positioned to lead this transformation.** The firm's institutional knowledge of Bay Area bridges, its established relationships with Caltrans and BATA, its technical leadership in bridge engineering, and its global platform through Sidara create an opportunity that no competitor can match. Establishing a Fleet Bridge Protection Program as a formal service offering would differentiate TYLin in a market where bridge preservation spending is projected to grow substantially over the coming decades as the nation's bridge inventory continues to age.
