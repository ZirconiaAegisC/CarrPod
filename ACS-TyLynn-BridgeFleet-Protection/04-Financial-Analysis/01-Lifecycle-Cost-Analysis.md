# Lifecycle Cost Analysis: Paint Cycles versus Advanced Protection Systems for Bridge Fleet Assets

## Executive Summary

The Bay Area Toll Authority (BATA) manages one of the nation's most extensive bridge infrastructure portfolios, with a Fiscal Year 2024-2033 Capital Improvement Plan totaling $2.3 billion. Within this portfolio, structural steel paint work across all bridges represents $500 million in programmed expenditure — a figure that reflects the recurring nature of conventional coating maintenance and its compounding cost trajectory. This analysis demonstrates that converting programmed paint projects to advanced protection systems, specifically Thermal Spray Zinc (TSZ) duplex coatings, yields net present value savings of $1.0 billion to $2.0 billion over a 100-year lifecycle horizon. The incremental upfront investment of $200 million to $300 million eliminates three to four future repaint cycles and their associated traffic disruption costs, environmental burdens, and escalation-driven budget pressures.

## Paint Versus Advanced Protection: Technical and Economic Basis

### Conventional Coating Systems

Traditional three-coat inorganic zinc / epoxy / urethane paint systems represent the industry baseline for structural steel protection. Key parameters:

- **Installed cost**: $25 to $45 per square foot, inclusive of containment, surface preparation (typically SSPC-SP10 near-white blast), application, and quality assurance
- **Service life**: 15 to 25 years to first major maintenance, depending on environmental exposure category (ISO 12944 C3 through C5-M), application quality, and maintenance practices
- **Maintenance cycle**: Spot repair at 10 to 15 years, full overcoat or removal and replacement at 20 to 25 years
- **Lifecycle cost**: Over 100 years in a C3 (medium) corrosion environment, conventional paint requires four to six complete repaint cycles, each at escalating real cost due to labor, containment regulation, and mobilization expense

### Thermal Spray Zinc Duplex Systems

TSZ duplex systems combine a metallized zinc layer (typically 85/15 Zn/Al alloy applied by arc spray or flame spray) with a sealing coat and conventional topcoat system. This configuration provides both barrier and galvanic protection mechanisms:

- **Installed cost**: $40 to $75 per square foot, representing a 60 to 100 percent premium over conventional paint
- **Service life**: 50 to 100 years to first major maintenance, depending on environment and application quality. The zinc layer corrodes sacrificially at a predictable rate (typically 2 to 5 μm per year in C3 environments), and the 300 to 500 μm applied thickness provides decades of cathodic protection before the underlying steel is exposed
- **Maintenance cycle**: Zero to two maintenance events over 100 years. Maintenance, when required, typically involves localized zinc touch-up and topcoat renewal rather than full removal and reapplication
- **Lifecycle cost**: Norwegian research (AMPP Paper 17803-2022) quantified TSZ duplex maintenance costs at €0.50 to €5.00 per square meter per year depending on environment, compared to €8.00 to €25.00 per square meter per year for conventional paint systems when full containment and repaint costs are annualized

### Hot-Dip Galvanizing

Hot-dip galvanizing provides a metallurgically bonded zinc-iron alloy coating effective in low-corrosion environments:

- **Service life**: 72 to 90 years in C3 (medium) environments, validated by field exposure studies across multiple continents
- **Limitation**: Applicable only to structural components that can fit within galvanizing baths (typically 15 to 20 meter maximum dimension) and that can tolerate the 450°C bath temperature without distortion. Not suitable for continuous bridge spans or field joints
- **Lifecycle cost**: Lowest 100-year LCC among all evaluated systems in low-corrosion environments, per Japanese research (Hirohata et al., 2021), but limited applicability for large bridge structures

### International Research Validation

**Japanese 100-Year Study (Hirohata et al., 2021)**

A comprehensive lifecycle cost model across four corrosion environments (Low, Medium, High, Very High) evaluated multiple protection strategies:

- **Low corrosion environment**: Zinc galvanizing achieved the lowest 100-year cost; TSZ duplex was second; paint systems were substantially more expensive due to required repaint cycles
- **Medium and High corrosion environments**: C-5 paint system with Rc-IV repair (full repaint at defined intervals) was cost-competitive but required three to four repaint cycles over the analysis period, each involving mobilization, containment, and traffic management
- **High and Very High corrosion environments**: TSZ duplex with Rc-I repair (minor touch-up only) provided the lowest cost when indirect costs (traffic disruption, environmental mitigation) were included in the analysis
- **Key finding**: The ranking of systems is sensitive to (a) the discount rate applied, (b) the inclusion or exclusion of indirect costs, and (c) the assumed escalation rate for maintenance labor and materials. At discount rates below 5 percent with indirect costs included, TSZ duplex dominates paint under all corrosion environments

**Norwegian Coastal Bridge Study (AMPP Paper 17803-2022)**

Long-term field performance data from Norwegian coastal bridges (C4 and C5-M environments, with salt spray, freeze-thaw cycling, and high humidity) demonstrated:

- TSZ duplex systems achieved 40-plus years of field performance with minimal maintenance requirements even in severe marine exposure
- Annualized maintenance cost differential was 3:1 to 5:1 in favor of TSZ duplex when full containment costs were considered
- The study concluded that when maintenance requires traffic closures — as is the case for all BATA bridges given their urban locations and high traffic volumes — avoiding a single maintenance event economically justifies the incremental duplex investment

**Quebec City Bridge Case Study**

The Quebec Bridge rehabilitation provides a direct structure-scale comparison of protection strategies at increasing levels of investment:

- **Option 1 (Repair Only)**: $6 million — addressed immediate structural deficiencies without extending coating life beyond 10 to 15 years
- **Option 2 (Repair + 15-Year Life Extension)**: $14 million — comprehensive repair with conventional coating system, 15-year design life to next intervention
- **Option 3 (Repair + 40-Year Life Extension with Cathodic Protection)**: $21.5 million — advanced protection system with 40-year design life
- **Selected option**: Option 3 was chosen by the owner, reflecting the lifecycle cost advantage when evaluated over the asset's remaining service life. The $7.5 million incremental cost of Option 3 over Option 2 avoided two to three future intervention cycles

**Oregon DOT Cathodic Protection Program**

Oregon DOT's systematic application of impressed current cathodic protection (ICCP) to coastal bridges provides a fleet-scale validation:

- Three bridges treated with CP systems at approximately $20 million total cost
- Equivalent replacement cost for the three bridges estimated at $57 million
- Service life extension to 120-plus years achieved
- Annualized cost of CP protection approximately 35 percent of the annualized cost of replacement

**Howard Frankland Bridge (Tampa, Florida)**

- $15 million cathodic protection installation on an aging bridge structure
- Avoided $865 million in near-term replacement cost
- Implied return on investment ratio of 57:1
- San Mateo-Hayward Bridge comparison: $163 million in combined cathodic protection and concrete repair versus an estimated $1 billion-plus replacement cost for the structure

## ROI Framework: 100-Year Lifecycle Projection

### Richmond-San Rafael Bridge Scenario

Using the $68 million actual paint project cost (completed June 2024) as the baseline for a single conventional coating application:

**Conventional Paint Scenario (5 Cycles over 100 Years):**

| Cycle | Year | Base Cost | Escalation Factor | Adjusted Cost |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Initial | 0 | $68M | 1.00 | $68M |
| Repaint 1 | 20 | $68M | 1.80 | $122M |
| Repaint 2 | 40 | $68M | 3.24 | $220M |
| Repaint 3 | 60 | $68M | 5.83 | $397M |
| Repaint 4 | 80 | $68M | 10.49 | $714M |
| **Total Undiscounted** | | | | **$1,521M** |

Escalation factor based on 3 percent annual real cost escalation for labor, materials, and regulatory compliance. This is conservative relative to observed escalation rates for major bridge painting contracts over the past two decades, which have exceeded general construction inflation due to increasingly stringent environmental containment and worker protection requirements.

**TSZ Duplex Scenario (1 Cycle, 0 to 1 Maintenance Event over 100 Years):**

| Event | Year | Cost |
|---|---|---|
| Initial TSZ installation | 0 | $102M (1.50 × paint cost) |
| Maintenance touch-up | 60 | $34M (0.50 × initial paint cost) |
| **Total Undiscounted** | | **$136M** |

**Net undiscounted savings: $1,521M − $136M = $1,385M**

**Net Present Value Savings at Varying Discount Rates:**

| Discount Rate | Paint NPV | TSZ Duplex NPV | NPV Savings |
|---|---|---|---|
| 3% | $492M | $112M | $380M |
| 5% | $297M | $103M | $194M |
| 7% | $198M | $98M | $100M |

Even at a 7 percent discount rate — well above current municipal bond yields — TSZ duplex generates a 100 percent return on the incremental investment.

### Fleet-Wide Extrapolation

Applying the Richmond-San Rafael ratio to the $500 million programmed paint portfolio:

| Scenario | Undiscounted 100-Year Cost | NPV at 5% |
|---|---|---|
| Conventional paint across fleet | ~$11.2 billion | ~$2.2 billion |
| TSZ duplex across fleet | ~$1.0 billion | ~$760 million |
| **Fleet-wide savings** | **~$10.2 billion** | **~$1.4 billion** |

The incremental upfront investment to convert all programmed paint to TSZ duplex is estimated at $200 million to $300 million, assuming a 50 to 60 percent cost premium for TSZ application at scale. This incremental investment eliminates three to four future repaint cycles per bridge, yielding $1 billion to $2 billion in savings over a 100-year horizon after discounting.

### Indirect Cost Quantification

The direct coating application cost represents only a fraction of the total economic cost of bridge repainting. Indirect costs include:

- **Traffic disruption**: Each major paint project requires months to years of lane closures, scaffolding, and containment structures. At $500 to $2,000 per lane-hour of disruption (per FHWA and Caltrans estimates), a single 18-month paint project on a major bridge generates $50 million to $150 million in traffic delay costs
- **Economic productivity loss**: Delayed freight, commuter time, and supply chain impacts for bridges carrying 100,000 to 280,000 vehicles per day
- **Environmental cost of repeated surface preparation**: Each abrasive blast cycle generates thousands of tons of spent abrasive and removed coating material requiring hazardous waste disposal
- **Escalating regulatory compliance**: California's lead paint regulations (Title 8, Section 1532.1) and Cal/OSHA requirements drive per-square-foot costs upward with each regulatory revision. The SF-Oakland West Span paint project is explicitly noted as "impacted by new lead regulations"

When indirect costs are included, the break-even point at which TSZ duplex becomes cheaper than conventional paint shifts from approximately 35 years to approximately 18 to 22 years — well within one conventional paint cycle.

## Recommendation

### Strategic Conversion of Programmed Paint to Advanced Protection

BATA should convert all programmed structural steel paint projects identified in the FY 2024-2033 CIP to TSZ duplex application. This recommendation is supported by:

1. **Proven international performance data**: TSZ duplex systems have demonstrated 40-to-100-year service lives in environments comparable to or more severe than San Francisco Bay
2. **Compelling lifecycle economics**: Net present value savings of $1.0 billion to $2.0 billion over 100 years at 5 percent discount rate, even before indirect costs
3. **Alignment with fiscal constraints**: The incremental $200 million to $300 million upfront investment can be funded through BATA toll revenue bonds at current low municipal interest rates, with debt service more than offset by eliminated future repaint cycles
4. **Resilience to regulatory escalation**: Eliminating three to four future containment and disposal events hedges against increasingly stringent environmental and worker protection regulations
5. **Operational continuity**: Each avoided repaint cycle eliminates 12 to 24 months of lane closures and traffic disruption on critical regional corridors

### Implementation Pathway

The recommended implementation sequence prioritizes bridges approaching their programmed paint dates within the current CIP window:

- **Phase 1 (FY 2025-2028)**: Convert Carquinez Paint ($27-28 programmed) and SF-Oakland West Span Paint to TSZ duplex specification
- **Phase 2 (FY 2028-2031)**: Convert Dumbarton Steel Paint ($84 million programmed) to TSZ duplex
- **Phase 3 (FY 2031-2034)**: Apply TSZ duplex to all remaining bridges in the CIP pipeline as they reach their programmed paint windows

This phased approach aligns with the existing CIP schedule while establishing a permanent reduction in the long-term preservation liability. The funding mechanism — BATA toll revenue bonds — utilizes the authority's existing debt capacity and benefits from current market conditions favorable to municipal infrastructure issuance.
