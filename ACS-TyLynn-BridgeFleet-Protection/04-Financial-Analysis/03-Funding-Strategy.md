# Funding and Procurement Strategy for Bridge Fleet Protection

## Strategic Context

The Bay Area Toll Authority's FY 2024-2033 Capital Improvement Plan allocates $2.3 billion across bridge preservation, technology modernization, and routine maintenance. Within this framework, structural steel coating work represents $500 million in programmed expenditure — a figure that, under conventional procurement approaches, locks BATA into a recurring liability of escalating repaint costs for decades. Converting this programmed expenditure to advanced protection systems requires a deliberate funding and procurement strategy that addresses the incremental upfront cost while capturing the long-term lifecycle savings.

## Toll Revenue Framework

### Current Toll Revenue Structure

BATA's revenue base derives from toll collections across seven state-owned bridges in the San Francisco Bay Area. The toll structure applies uniform rates with collection at designated plazas:

- **Current base toll**: $7.00 for two-axle vehicles (effective January 1, 2022)
- **Annual revenue**: Approximately $800 million in gross toll revenue across the bridge system
- **Revenue allocation**: Debt service obligations consume approximately 40 percent of toll revenue; operations and maintenance consume 25 percent; capital projects receive the remaining 35 percent

### Toll Increase Authorization and Timing

The 2026 toll increase, authorized through the Regional Measure 3 (RM3) ballot initiative process and California Streets and Highways Code Section 30921, is programmed specifically to address the structural funding gap between programmed preservation work and available revenue:

- **Revenue impact**: Each $1.00 toll increase generates approximately $120 million in additional annual revenue across the seven-bridge system
- **Preservation gap**: The gap between programmed preservation needs ($1.2 billion over ten years, averaging $120 million annually) and available capital revenue is funded through a combination of toll increases, bond issuances, and federal formula funds
- **Revenue loss timing**: Every year of delay in implementing the programmed toll increase permanently forfeits approximately $120 million in revenue that cannot be recaptured through future rates

### Bonding Capacity

BATA maintains an active debt program backed by toll revenue pledges. Current outstanding debt:

| Issuance | Amount | Year | Purpose |
|---|---|---|---|
| Toll Revenue Bonds, Series 2021 | $360 million | 2021 | Bridge rehabilitation and seismic retrofit |
| Toll Revenue Bonds, Series 2024 | $200 million | 2024 | Capital program acceleration |
| **Total Outstanding** | **$560 million** | | |

BATA's debt service coverage ratio, credit rating (AA- from S&P and Aa3 from Moody's as of most recent issuance), and pledged revenue stream provide capacity for additional bond issuances to fund the incremental cost of advanced protection system conversion. Financing $600 million in additional debt at current municipal rates generates approximately $690 million in total debt service over a 30-year amortization — a cost that is substantially offset by eliminating future repaint cycles that would similarly require debt financing.

## Federal Funding Mechanisms

### IIJA Bridge Formula Funds

The Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act (Public Law 117-58) authorized $27.5 billion in bridge formula funding over five fiscal years (2022-2026), representing the single largest dedicated bridge investment since the construction of the Interstate Highway System. California's allocation from the Bridge Formula Program:

- **FY 2022-2026 total**: Approximately $4.2 billion in combined formula and discretionary bridge funds to California
- **BATA allocation**: $118 million in IIJA bridge formula funds already programmed for Bay Bridge fender replacement and structural protection
- **Eligibility**: Bridge preservation, rehabilitation, and replacement activities — including protective coating systems that extend service life — qualify for IIJA formula fund reimbursement at up to 80 percent federal share for Interstate System bridges and 80 percent for off-system bridges

The $118 million Bay Bridge fender allocation demonstrates successful navigation of the IIJA application and obligation process. The same mechanism can fund advanced coating applications on all BATA bridges that carry federal-aid highway routes, which includes all seven state-owned toll bridges.

### FHWA Accelerated Innovation Deployment Program

The AID program (formerly the Technology and Innovation Deployment Program under 23 U.S.C. 503) provides incentive funding for deployment of proven but underutilized innovations:

- **Total program authorization**: $500 million nationally (FY 2022-2026 under IIJA)
- **Maximum award per project**: No statutory cap; typical awards range from $5 million to $25 million
- **Previous Bay Area deployment**: Over $80 million in AID and predecessor program funds have been deployed for new technologies in California, including accelerated bridge construction methods, high-performance materials, and advanced corrosion protection systems
- **Eligibility**: TSZ duplex coating constitutes a proven innovation — mature in Europe, Japan, and the United Kingdom — that qualifies for AID demonstration deployment funding. The program specifically supports technologies that reduce lifecycle costs and minimize traffic disruption

AID application for TSZ duplex deployment on the Carquinez or Dumbarton bridge would establish a California demonstration project that validates the technology for broader fleet-wide application while recovering 50 to 80 percent of the incremental cost through federal reimbursement.

### State Transportation Improvement Program and State Highway Operation and Protection Program

California's STIP and SHOPP provide complementary funding streams:

- **SHOPP**: Focused on preservation and rehabilitation of existing state highway assets, including bridges. SHOPP funds approximately $4.1 billion annually statewide for pavement and bridge preservation. BATA bridges, as part of the state highway system, are eligible for SHOPP funding for coating projects that extend service life
- **STIP**: Provides capacity expansion and major rehabilitation funding. Advanced protection systems that constitute a "rehabilitation" rather than "routine maintenance" activity — defined as work that extends service life by more than ten years — qualify for STIP funding

### Regional Measures RM1, RM2, and RM3

The Regional Measure framework represents a uniquely Bay Area mechanism that has provided dedicated transportation funding for decades:

- **RM1 (1988)**: $2.4 billion program for bridge seismic retrofit and transit expansion; largely completed
- **RM2 (2004)**: $4.0 billion program including bridge improvements and toll system modernization
- **RM3 (2018)**: $4.45 billion program approved by Bay Area voters, including $370 million for bridge rehabilitation and preservation

RM3 funds specifically designated for bridge rehabilitation can be programmed toward advanced coating applications that extend bridge service life. The political and voter accountability framework of RM3 — requiring demonstrated project delivery and measurable outcomes — aligns with the documented performance and lifecycle cost advantage of TSZ duplex systems.

## Public-Private Partnership Potential

### P3 Structures for Fleet-Wide Programs

Public-private partnerships offer an alternative delivery mechanism that can accelerate deployment while transferring performance risk to the private sector:

- **Design-Build-Finance-Operate-Maintain (DBFOM)**: A private consortium finances the upfront TSZ duplex installation across multiple bridges and is compensated through availability payments from BATA over a 30-to-50-year concession period. The consortium's return depends on meeting performance specifications for coating integrity and structural condition — aligning private incentives with long-life coating outcomes
- **Energy Savings Performance Contract analog**: Adapted from the federal ESPC program, a "Corrosion Protection Performance Contract" structure would allow BATA to contract for advanced coating application with repayment from the verified savings in eliminated repaint cycles. The contractor guarantees coating performance over a defined period, bearing the cost of any premature failure and maintenance
- **Availability payment model**: BATA makes fixed periodic payments to the private partner contingent on the bridge coating system meeting defined condition thresholds (e.g., less than 1 percent rust-through, adhesion above specified minimum, zinc thickness remaining above minimum threshold). This transfers technical performance risk to the entity best positioned to manage it

### Procurement Economies of Scale

Bundled procurement across the BATA fleet captures economies of scale that reduce the unit cost of advanced coating application:

- **Equipment mobilization**: Arc spray equipment, containment systems, and quality control apparatus can move sequentially across bridges, eliminating repeated mobilization and demobilization costs that can represent 10 to 15 percent of individual project budgets
- **Specialized workforce**: TSZ application requires certified thermal spray operators. A fleet-wide program justifies investment in a stable, trained workforce rather than project-by-project recruitment
- **Material procurement**: Zinc wire for arc spray application, purchased in fleet-scale quantities, can achieve 10 to 20 percent volume discounts relative to individual project procurement
- **Quality assurance infrastructure**: A standardized QA/QC program — destructive testing, adhesion pull-off testing, holiday detection, and zinc thickness measurement — can be deployed across the fleet with shared protocols and personnel

The model projects that bundled procurement reduces the TSZ duplex cost premium from approximately 60 percent over paint (for individual project procurement) to approximately 40 to 45 percent (for fleet-wide bundled procurement), improving the NPV advantage by $50 million to $100 million fleet-wide.

## Performance-Based Contracting

### Warranty Specifications

Conventional bridge painting contracts typically carry a one-year workmanship warranty with no performance warranty for coating longevity. This structure creates no contractor incentive to maximize service life. Performance-based contracting transforms this dynamic:

**Recommended warranty specification for TSZ duplex procurement:**

- **Five-year comprehensive warranty**: Full contractor responsibility for any coating defect or failure within five years of project acceptance, including mobilization, containment, surface preparation, and reapplication at no cost to BATA
- **Ten-year zinc thickness warranty**: Contractor warrants that zinc thickness remains above 50 percent of specified minimum at any measured point after ten years. Below threshold, contractor performs localized touch-up at no cost
- **Fifteen-year rust-through warranty**: Less than 0.1 percent of surface area exhibiting rust-through (ASTM D610 Rust Grade 7 or better) at 15 years. Failure triggers negotiated remediation at reduced cost
- **Warranty bond requirement**: 5 percent of contract value held as warranty surety bond for the duration of the warranty period, ensuring contractor financial capacity to address failures

These specifications align contractor incentives with BATA's lifecycle cost objectives: the contractor who applies the highest-quality coating benefits from reduced warranty claims, while BATA benefits from extended service life regardless of which specific contractor performs the work.

### Incentive Structures

Beyond warranty requirements, explicit incentive payments can further align outcomes:

- **Early completion bonus**: Daily incentive payment for completion ahead of schedule, recognizing the traffic disruption cost avoided
- **Quality metric bonuses**: Payment multipliers for exceeding specified adhesion, thickness uniformity, and surface profile thresholds
- **Lifecycle cost sharing**: A portion of verified lifecycle cost savings, measured against a baseline conventional paint projection, shared with the contractor as a success fee at defined milestones (year 10, year 20, year 30)

## Recommended Funding Architecture

### Dedicated Budget Line Item

The most consequential recommendation is structural: create a dedicated "Bridge Fleet Protection Program" budget line item within the BATA Capital Improvement Plan, separate from the general "Bridge Preservation" category. This line item would:

- Aggregate all coating, painting, cathodic protection, and corrosion mitigation expenditures across the fleet into a single programmatic category
- Apply a unified lifecycle cost analysis framework to all protection decisions
- Track the long-term preservation liability as a managed program rather than a series of independent projects
- Provide transparency to BATA oversight bodies and the public on the total cost of bridge corrosion management

### Phased Funding Recommendation

| Phase | Period | Scope | Funding Sources | Estimated Cost |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Phase 1 | FY 2025-2028 | Carquinez and SF-Oakland West Span TSZ conversion | BATA toll bonds (60%), IIJA bridge formula (25%), AID demonstration (15%) | $80-120M |
| Phase 2 | FY 2028-2031 | Dumbarton steel paint TSZ conversion, plus fender CP integration | BATA toll bonds (50%), SHOPP (30%), RM3 (20%) | $160-220M |
| Phase 3 | FY 2031-2034 | Remaining fleet-wide conversion; bundled procurement contract | BATA toll bonds (40%), STIP (25%), SHOPP (20%), federal discretionary (15%) | $200-300M |
| **Total Program** | **FY 2025-2034** | **Complete fleet conversion** | **Blended funding stack** | **$440-640M** |

The $440 million to $640 million total program cost — of which $200 million to $300 million represents the incremental cost above conventional paint — compares favorably against the $500 million already programmed for conventional paint alone over the same period. The incremental investment of $200 million to $300 million, funded through existing revenue mechanisms and additional borrowing at current low interest rates, eliminates $1.0 billion to $2.0 billion in future repaint liability over the 100-year analysis horizon.

### Debt Financing Analysis

The incremental $200 million to $300 million in additional borrowing, amortized over 30 years at 4.0 to 5.5 percent municipal rates, generates annual debt service of $12 million to $20 million. This compares to avoided future repaint costs — each of which would require $100 million to $300 million in inflation-adjusted expenditure — making the debt service obligation substantially smaller than the liability it eliminates. The debt service coverage ratio remains well above BATA's bond covenant requirements, and the credit rating agencies have indicated that investments demonstrably reducing long-term liabilities are viewed favorably in credit assessments.

## Procurement Implementation

The recommended procurement strategy combines immediate specification changes with longer-term structural reforms:

1. **Immediate**: Issue a policy directive requiring lifecycle cost analysis with a minimum 75-year analysis period for all bridge coating specification decisions, effective with the next advertised contract
2. **Near-term (FY 2025-2026)**: Develop and advertise a TSZ duplex specification for the Carquinez Paint project, incorporating the performance warranty framework described above
3. **Mid-term (FY 2026-2028)**: Conduct a bundled procurement market sounding with qualified TSZ contractors to establish pricing, capacity, and interest for fleet-wide bundling
4. **Long-term (FY 2028+)**: Implement fleet-wide bundled procurement with performance-based contracting, capturing scale economies and transferring technical performance risk to qualified private-sector partners

This phased approach respects BATA's existing contractual commitments and CIP schedules while establishing the institutional, financial, and procurement infrastructure for systematic conversion to advanced protection systems across the bridge fleet.
