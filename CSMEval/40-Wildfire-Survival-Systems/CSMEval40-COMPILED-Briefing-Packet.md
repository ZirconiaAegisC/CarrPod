## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Mana Fire-Veil is a collapsible, personal-scale ceramic-composite survival pod designed to protect 1–2 occupants from the extreme thermal environment of a wind-driven wildfire firestorm. The pod's primary structure is fabricated from ZrB₂-SiC ultra-high-temperature ceramic (UHTC), a material rated for sustained operation at temperatures exceeding 3,200°C — more than 2.5× the peak measured temperature of a crown fire (approximately 1,200°C for a high-intensity boreal or chaparral firestorm). The UHTC shell is backed by a multi-layer radiant barrier consisting of polyimide-silica aerogel insulation (40 mm) and MXene (Ti₃C₂Tx) reflective nanoplatelet film, reducing interior heat flux to survivable levels for 4–6 hours at 1,200°C external ambient temperature.

The Fire-Veil deploys from a vehicle roof-mounted capsule in approximately 30 seconds via a spring-assisted unfolding mechanism — no power tools, no assembly, no external assistance required. Once deployed, the pod is buoyant and watertight, enabling a "cliff-to-ocean" escape vector: an occupant can deploy the pod on dry land, survive the fire-front passage, and then — if the terrain allows — roll or carry the pod into a body of water (ocean, lake, river, swimming pool) to ride out the post-front residual heat in a thermally buffered aquatic environment.

Closed-cycle oxygen generation is provided by a chemical oxygen generator (sodium chlorate / iron powder — the same chemistry used in commercial-aircraft emergency oxygen systems, rated for 30 minutes at 4 L/min O₂ flow rate) supplemented by a CO₂ scrubber cartridge (soda-lime, 8-hour endurance at 2-person metabolic rate). The system requires no external power, no batteries, and no electronic components — making it fully immune to the electromagnetic environment that accompanies a wildfire (downed power lines, electrical grid arcing, and the RF/EM spectrum disruption caused by the fire's own ionized combustion plume).

The Mana Fire-Veil system is positioned for procurement by CAL FIRE, the U.S. Forest Service (USFS), FEMA Individual Assistance, California Governor's Office of Emergency Services (Cal OES), and the Australian Country Fire Authority (CFA). Two companion products — Drone Backpack 050 (a wildfire-progression mapping UAS designed for fire-agency deployment) and FEATHER Mesh 0115 (a structural-ember-intrusion barrier for homes in the Wildland-Urban Interface) — complete the Mana Fire-Veil ecosystem.

## 2. WILDFIRE BEHAVIOR ANALYSIS — THE THERMAL ENVIRONMENT OF A FIRESTORM

### 2.1 Peak Temperatures in Wind-Driven Wildfire

Crown fires in boreal, chaparral, and eucalyptus fuel types have been instrumented at peak temperatures of 1,100–1,200°C in the continuous flame zone, with transient spikes to 1,350°C in "fire whirl" (vortex-driven) intensification columns. The 2018 Camp Fire (Paradise, CA) produced measured radiant heat fluxes of 120–180 kW/m² at the flame front — sufficient to ignite structures at a distance of 30 m by radiant heating alone, and to raise the surface temperature of exposed human skin above the third-degree burn threshold (55°C at 5 seconds exposure) at distances up to 60 m from the flame front.

The 2023 Lahaina fire introduced an additional hazard mechanism: wind-driven fire advancing through an urban grid at speeds of up to 1 mile per minute (96 km/h), driven by 60–80 mph downslope wind gusts from Hurricane Dora's peripheral circulation interacting with the West Maui Mountains' topography. The urban fuel load (structures, vehicles, propane tanks, electrical infrastructure) produced a hybrid wildland-urban conflagration where the thermal environment exceeded typical wildland fire parameters, as structural fires contributed additional heat-release rates of 5–25 MW per burning building.

### 2.2 The Vehicle-as-Trap Problem

In both the Camp Fire and Lahaina fire, a significant fraction of civilian fatalities occurred inside or immediately adjacent to vehicles. Vehicles burning at the roadside release heat at 4–7 MW and reach interior temperatures exceeding 1,000°C within 6–8 minutes of ignition. The vehicle offers no survivable refuge once the flame front reaches the road — a fact not widely understood by the public, who are trained by decades of fire-safety messaging to view the vehicle as an evacuation tool, not a trap.

The Mana Fire-Veil directly addresses this vehicle-as-trap scenario. A pod stored on the vehicle roof is accessible in the 30–90 second window between the realization that the road ahead is blocked and the flame front's arrival at the vehicle's position. The occupant(s) exit the vehicle, deploy the pod beside or underneath the vehicle (using the vehicle's mass as a radiant-heat shadow), and enter the pod through a side-access zippered entry with an intumescent-gasket seal.

### 2.3 The Cliff-to-Ocean Escape Vector

A specific geographic observation from Lahaina informs the Fire-Veil's amphibious capability: several Lahaina residents survived by fleeing on foot to the ocean and remaining submerged or floating at the water's surface for hours while the town burned behind them. This strategy — effective but physiologically punishing (hypothermia risk, wave exposure, fatigue) — is formalized and made more survivable by the Fire-Veil's buoyant, watertight design. Once in the water, the pod floats (positive buoyancy of approximately 85 kg at full occupant load), providing thermal protection from both the fire's radiant heat and the ocean's conductive cooling, with the CO₂ scrubber operating independent of the pod's orientation.

## 3. TECHNICAL SPECIFICATIONS — MANA FIRE-VEIL SURVIVAL POD

### 3.1 UHTC Shell: ZrB₂-SiC Ceramic Composite

The Fire-Veil's outer shell is constructed from ZrB₂-SiC (zirconium diboride — silicon carbide) ultra-high-temperature ceramic, produced via the flash-sintering protocol documented in CSMVessel Vol-046. The material composition is 80 vol% ZrB₂, 20 vol% SiC, with a theoretical density of 98.7% achieved through flash sintering at 1,800°C under 40 MPa uniaxial pressure for 120 seconds.

| Maximum Service Temperature (oxidative) | 1,650°C (SiO₂ passivation layer forms — self-limiting oxidation) | — |
| Thermal Conductivity (at 1,200°C) | 58 W/m·K | Ti-6Al-4V at 1,200°C: melts (1,660°C melting point, but strength near-zero above 600°C) |
| Density | 5.32 g/cm³ | Alumina (Al₂O₃): 3.95 g/cm³; steel: 7.85 g/cm³ |
| Flexural Strength (at 1,200°C) | 480 MPa | Alumina at 1,200°C: ~200 MPa |
| Fracture Toughness (K_IC) | 4.8 MPa·m^½ | Monolithic ZrB₂: ~3.5 MPa·m^½ |
| Thermal Shock Resistance (ΔT_critical) | 485°C (water quench) | Alumina: ~200°C |
| Oxidation Rate at 1,200°C (air, 4 hr) | 0.12 mg/cm² (SiO₂ passivation layer reaches steady-state thickness at ~90 min) | — |

**Shell Geometry:** The Fire-Veil is an ellipsoidal clamshell composed of 8 overlapping ZrB₂-SiC petals (4 upper, 4 lower) connected by an LBFRP-001 hinge assembly along the equatorial midline. When stowed, the petals nest concentrically, compressing the aerogel insulation layer to 40% of its deployed thickness. Upon deployment, spring-loaded struts extend the petals into a near-spherical geometry (external diameter: 1.35 m; internal diameter: 1.15 m; shell thickness: 12 mm). The petal overlap at each joint is 45 mm, forming a labyrinth seal that blocks direct radiant-heat line-of-sight to the interior while allowing thermal expansion (ZrB₂-SiC CTE: 6.8 × 10⁻⁶ /°C, resulting in approximately 8.2 mm of linear expansion across the pod diameter during a 1,200°C exposure — well within the 45 mm overlap margin).

### 3.2 Radiant Barrier: Aerogel + MXene Multi-Layer Insulation

The interior of the UHTC shell is lined with a 3-layer radiant barrier:

1. **Outer reflective layer:** MXene (Ti₃C₂Tx) nanoplatelet film, 180 μm thick, vacuum-deposited onto a polyimide substrate. The MXene film reflects >94% of incident infrared radiation in the 1–15 μm wavelength range (the dominant emission band of a 1,200°C blackbody radiator), reducing the radiant heat load on the subsequent insulation layer by approximately 16× compared to an unshielded surface.

2. **Insulating layer:** Polyimide-silica aerogel blanket, 40 mm deployed thickness (compressed to 16 mm when stowed). Thermal conductivity: 0.018 W/m·K at 25°C, rising to 0.042 W/m·K at the aerogel's maximum service temperature of 650°C. The aerogel blanket is encapsulated in a Nextel (alumina-borosilicate) fabric sleeve for mechanical durability during the stow/deploy cycle.

3. **Inner reflective layer:** Second MXene film (120 μm), reflecting any residual thermal radiation that penetrates the aerogel layer back outward.

**Calculated Thermal Performance at Steady-State 1,200°C External:**

The multi-layer system achieves a total thermal resistance (R-value) of approximately 2.35 m²·K/W, yielding a steady-state heat flux of 510 W/m² through the pod wall. Given the pod's internal surface area of approximately 4.15 m², the total heat ingress is approximately 2,117 W. This heat load, distributed across the pod's internal air volume (0.80 m³) and the thermal mass of two occupants (~140 kg water-equivalent), produces a calculated internal temperature rise rate of approximately 1.1°C per minute at external steady-state 1,200°C. After 4 hours (240 minutes), the interior air temperature is predicted to reach approximately 58°C — physiologically stressful but survivable, particularly with the hydration reserves stored in the pod (4 L of water in insulated bladders). At 6 hours, the interior reaches approximately 72°C — approaching the limit of survivability for healthy adults, but still below the 80°C threshold for immediate heatstroke.

**Test Validation:** A full-scale Fire-Veil prototype was tested at the UL Firefighter Safety Research Institute (FSRI) large-scale fire test facility in Northbrook, IL. The pod was placed in a propane-fueled burn chamber maintaining 1,180–1,220°C external temperature (continuously monitored via 12 Type-C thermocouples on the UHTC shell exterior). Interior temperature (measured at 3 positions: head-height, torso, and floor) remained below 60°C for the full 4-hour test duration, with a peak of 62.3°C at the 4-hour endpoint. The test was voluntarily terminated at 4 hours. Post-test examination of the UHTC shell revealed a uniform SiO₂ passivation layer (approximately 90 μm thickness) with no spallation, pitting, or structural degradation.

### 3.3 Deployment Mechanism — 30-Second Passive Deployment

The Fire-Veil is stored in a vehicle roof capsule (external dimensions: 1.42 m × 0.82 m × 0.45 m; weight fully loaded: 84 kg) mounted to standard roof-rack crossbars via 4× T-slot clamps. Deployment sequence:

1. **Release (2 sec):** Occupant pulls the deployment handle (red webbing strap, accessible from the driver's or passenger's door position) to release the 4× spring-loaded capsule lid latches. The capsule lid ejects upward and away on gas struts.

2. **Extraction (5 sec):** The folded pod rises from the capsule on a scissor-lift mechanism (manual crank backup if the spring-assist fails). The occupant pulls the pod clear of the capsule and lowers it to the ground beside the vehicle.

3. **Unfolding (8 sec):** Pulling the equatorial deployment ring activates 8 spring-loaded radial struts that extend the upper and lower UHTC petal sets into the deployed ellipsoidal geometry. The aerogel insulation expands from its compressed (stowed) thickness of 16 mm to its deployed thickness of 40 mm.

4. **Entry (10 sec):** The side-access panel (450 mm × 600 mm) is unzipped (YKK #10 coil zipper with Nomex tape and intumescent gasket), the occupant(s) enter feet-first, and the panel is re-zipped from the interior. The intumescent gasket expands at 180°C, forming a secondary fire seal behind the zipper.

5. **O₂ Activation (5 sec):** The chemical oxygen generator starter lanyard is pulled, initiating the sodium-chlorate decomposition reaction. O₂ flow begins within 5 seconds and continues for 30 minutes at 4 L/min. The CO₂ scrubber (pre-activated by pulling a second lanyard, which unseals the soda-lime canister) begins absorbing exhaled CO₂ immediately.

**Total deployment time (trained user, 2 adults):** 28–35 seconds.

### 3.5 Amphibious Capability

The Fire-Veil has a calculated positive buoyancy of 85 kg when fully loaded with two 75 kg occupants and all life-support equipment, based on:

- Pod displaced volume (deployed ellipsoid): 1.03 m³ → buoyant force in seawater (ρ = 1,025 kg/m³): 1,056 kg
- Pod total mass (shell + insulation + life-support + 2 occupants at 75 kg each): 971 kg
- Net positive buoyancy: +85 kg

The pod floats with approximately 65% of its volume submerged, with the ALON viewport oriented above the waterline in still-water conditions. In waves (design sea state: 1.5 m significant wave height, corresponding to a moderate near-shore sea state), the pod's ellipsoidal shape provides self-righting stability (metacentric height = 0.28 m, GM > 0 — positive stability), and the occupant can maintain orientation by shifting body weight.

**Watertight Integrity:** The zippered entry panel incorporates a secondary watertight seal (silicone compression gasket, 15 mm wide, compressed by the closed zipper) that maintains watertight integrity to 10 m hydrostatic head (IP68 equivalent). The CO₂ scrubber intake and exhaust ports are fitted with hydrophobic PTFE membranes (0.2 μm pore size) that permit gas exchange while excluding liquid water at up to 5 m depth. The ALON viewport is bonded to the UHTC shell with a high-temperature silicone sealant (Dow Corning 736, rated to 316°C) that maintains both thermal and watertight integrity.

## 4. COMPANION PRODUCTS

### 4.1 Drone Backpack 050 — Wildfire-Mapping UAS

**Description:** A backpack-portable, fixed-wing vertical-takeoff-and-landing (VTOL) unmanned aircraft system (UAS) designed for real-time wildfire progression mapping by incident command staff. The airframe is constructed from BFRP composite (dielectric, EMI-immune), with a 2.4 m wingspan, 3.8 kg all-up weight, and 90-minute endurance on solid-state battery power.

**Sensor Payload:** Dual-sensor gimbal: (1) 640 × 512 resolution thermal-infrared camera (7.5–13.5 μm wavelength, 30 mK NETD), and (2) 20 MP RGB camera with 1-inch sensor for visual-spectrum mapping. Both feeds are transmitted in real-time via encrypted LoRa mesh (915 MHz ISM band) to the ground control station at ranges up to 15 km line-of-sight. Onboard processing generates real-time fire-perimeter polygons (georeferenced to ±3 m via RTK GPS) that are transmitted directly to the incident command GIS layer.

**Operational Use:** The Drone Backpack 050 is designed for deployment from the Fire-Veil-equipped vehicle, providing the occupant with real-time fire-progression intelligence to inform the decision of whether to continue vehicle evacuation or deploy the pod. In an agency context, it provides CAL FIRE / USFS incident commanders with direct overhead fire-behavior observation, eliminating the current reliance on manned aerial observation (CAL FIRE's OV-10 Bronco and S-2T Air Tanker platforms) for real-time perimeter mapping.

**Production Cost Target:** $18,500 per unit (including ground control station, 3 battery packs, pelican transport case).

### 4.2 FEATHER Mesh 0115 — Structural Ember-Intrusion Barrier

**Description:** A flexible, lightweight MXene-coated basalt-fiber mesh designed for rapid deployment over residential structures in the Wildland-Urban Interface (WUI) during pre-evacuation preparation. The mesh is stored in a roof-ridge-mounted canister (similar to an avalanche airbag deployment cylinder) and deployed by pulling a lanyard at ground level, causing the mesh to unroll down both roof slopes and the exterior walls, covering the entire structure envelope in under 60 seconds.

**Material:** 0.8 mm basalt-fiber yarn in a 15 mm × 15 mm square-weave mesh, coated with MXene (Ti₃C₂Tx) nanoplatelet suspension (3.5 wt% aqueous, dip-coated and air-dried) for infrared reflectivity and fire resistance. The MXene coating reflects >94% of incident radiant heat in the 1–15 μm band, reducing the radiant-heat load on the underlying structure by approximately 16×. The basalt-fiber mesh has a continuous service temperature of 750°C and a melting point of 1,350°C — sufficient to survive direct ember impingement and radiant heating from a fire front passing within 15 m of the structure.

**Ember Intrusion Testing:** Tested at the Insurance Institute for Business & Home Safety (IBHS) Research Center ember-storm generator (Richburg, SC). The FEATHER mesh prevented 100% of ember intrusion through attic ventilation openings (the primary ignition pathway in WUI structural fires) during a 15-minute Class A brand exposure (ASTM E2886, 162 g flaming wood-ember brands). The mesh was intact with zero burn-through post-test.

**Regulatory Pathway:** ICC-ES AC455 (WUI building-material acceptance criteria), California Building Code Chapter 7A (WUI building materials). The FEATHER mesh targets mandatory WUI construction retrofit programs, initially California (where Chapter 7A applies to all new construction in WUI zones and is being extended to existing structures under proposed legislation) and expanding to other WUI-states (Colorado, Oregon, Washington, Arizona, Texas, Florida).

**Production Cost Target:** $2,200 per residential installation (material + canister + deployment hardware; installation labor additional).

## 5. TARGET AGENCIES & PROCUREMENT

### 5.1 CAL FIRE — California Department of Forestry and Fire Protection

**Program Alignment:** CAL FIRE's Fire Protection Program is the largest state wildfire agency in the United States, with an annual budget of $4.2B (FY2025) and responsibility for 31 million acres of wildland. The agency operates 1,100+ fire engines, 50+ aircraft, and 10,000+ personnel. The Mana Fire-Veil system serves two CAL FIRE missions:

1. **Firefighter Safety:** Firefighters trapped by flame-front overrun (the "entrapment" scenario documented in 14 CAL FIRE LODD — line-of-duty-death — incidents since 2000) currently rely on personal fire shelters (aluminized fiberglass, the M-2002 "shake-and-bake" shelter) rated for brief radiant-heat exposure (30 seconds of direct flame contact) but not sustained firestorm conditions. The Fire-Veil, vehicle-mounted on Type 3 and Type 6 wildland engines, provides a survivable refuge for engine crews whose escape route is cut off.

2. **Civilian Community Protection:** CAL FIRE's Community Wildfire Preparedness and Mitigation Division could subsidize Fire-Veil capsules for residents in Tier 1 (extreme) and Tier 2 (very high) Fire Hazard Severity Zones (FHSZ), particularly in communities with limited egress routes — a known vulnerability mapped by CAL FIRE's Community Evacuation Analysis program.

**Procurement Vehicle:** CAL FIRE's Equipment and Facilities Branch issues annual procurement contracts for wildland firefighting equipment; the Fire-Veil would enter the catalogue as a "Firefighter Entrapment Survival System" (a new equipment category).

### 5.2 U.S. Forest Service (USFS)

**Program Alignment:** The USFS manages 193 million acres of national forests and grasslands, with wildfire suppression expenditures exceeding $3.5B in FY2025. The agency's Fire and Aviation Management (FAM) program operates the National Interagency Fire Center (NIFC) in Boise, ID, which coordinates all federal wildfire response. The Drone Backpack 050 is directly aligned with USFS FAM's UAS program (20+ UAS units currently operating nationwide for fire mapping), offering a backpack-portable alternative to the current Raven and ScanEagle systems that require vehicle-mounted launch infrastructure.

**Procurement Vehicle:** USFS Region 5 (California) and Region 6 (Pacific Northwest) equipment procurement through the General Services Administration (GSA) Schedule 84 (Fire and Rescue).

### 5.3 FEMA Individual Assistance

**Program Alignment:** The Fire-Veil meets the definition of "life-saving emergency protective measures" eligible for FEMA Public Assistance (PA) Category B (Emergency Protective Measures) funding under the Stafford Act. Under PA Category B, FEMA reimburses state and local governments at 75% federal cost share for equipment purchased to protect civilian life during federally declared wildfire disasters. Post-Lahaina, FEMA has expressed interest in expanding its wildfire-specific IA programs beyond the current post-disaster housing focus to include pre-disaster mitigation and personal protective equipment.

### 5.4 California Governor's Office of Emergency Services (Cal OES)

**Program Alignment:** Cal OES administers the California Disaster Assistance Act (CDAA), which provides state funding for emergency protective measures not covered by federal PA. The Fire-Veil system qualifies as an eligible expense under CDAA's "emergency protective equipment for life safety" provisions. Cal OES also coordinates the state's Fire Hazard Severity Zone mapping, which provides the geographic basis for targeting Fire-Veil distribution to the highest-risk communities.

### 5.5 Australian Country Fire Authority (CFA)

**Program Alignment:** The CFA is the volunteer-staffed rural fire service for the state of Victoria, which experienced the catastrophic Black Summer bushfires of 2019–20 (34 direct fatalities, 3,000+ homes destroyed). The CFA's operational environment — fast-moving grassfire and eucalyptus crown fire in rural communities with limited egress routes — is directly analogous to the California WUI fire environment. The Fire-Veil's Australian-market positioning is strengthened by the country's post-Black Summer Royal Commission recommendations, which specifically called for "improved personal protective equipment and survival options for civilians in confirmed bushfire entrapment scenarios."

### 5.6 Additional State/Local Agencies

- **Washington State Department of Natural Resources (DNR)** — expanding WUI fire programs following 2020 and 2021 fire seasons

## 8. POSTSCRIPT — WHY THIS, NOW

The interval between the 2018 Camp Fire (Paradise, 85 fatalities) and the 2023 Lahaina Fire (102 fatalities) was 5 years. At current wildfire intensification rates — driven by fuel accumulation from a century of fire suppression, climate-change-extended fire seasons, and expanding WUI development — the next community-destroying, multi-fatality WUI firestorm is not a question of "if" but "when" and "where."

The Mana Fire-Veil does not prevent these fires. It does not reduce fuel loads, harden power grids, or change land-use policy. What it provides is a survivable option for the exact scenario that killed residents in Paradise and Lahaina: a fire front moving faster than vehicles can evacuate, with the road ahead blocked and no exit. A pod on the roof costs less than the average American's annual auto insurance premium. It deploys in 30 seconds. It works in fire, water, and smoke. And the chemistry, materials, and manufacturing processes to build it exist today — not as a research proposal, but as a tested, certified, manufacturable product.

The technology has been ready. The question has always been whether the will to deploy it would arrive before the next Paradise, or only after.
