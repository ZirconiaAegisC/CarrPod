REF:           CSMEval30-B

NFPA 99 and Joint Commission emergency-power requirements are genuinely
good standards — for the outages they were designed around. They were not
written with a twelve-month transformer-replacement scenario in mind,
because that scenario wasn't the design assumption available at the time.
That's not a flaw in the standard. It's a gap between the standard and a
newer piece of information.

                 NFPA 99 WAS WRITTEN FOR A DIFFERENT KIND OF OUTAGE
                 CSMEval30-B | June 2026 | v1.0
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

PURPOSE
Establish the mismatch between current hospital backup-power design
assumptions and true Carrington-event outage timescales, and why this
sector's stakes place it at the highest urgency tier in this outreach
series.

KEY FACTS
• Ventilators, dialysis, surgical suites, and cardiac monitoring/
  defibrillation equipment all depend on continuous power — converting a
  power outage into a direct, individual safety-of-life risk almost
  immediately, unlike most other facility types.
• Current hospital backup-generator design, per Joint Commission/NFPA 99
  requirements, is generally sized for outages measured in hours to a few
  days.
• A true Carrington event's grid impact implies extended, transformer-
  replacement-timescale regional outages (see CSMEval11's Lloyd's of London
  scenario data: transformer replacement lead times of up to 12 months).
• This sector directly intersects with active clinical-practice content
  (defibrillation strategy, diagnostic vibrometry) referenced in the
  medical-device sector's own briefing (CSMEval26) — meaning the CSM
  research base here serves both facilities engineering and clinical
  practice simultaneously.

RISKS / CONCERNS IF UNADDRESSED
• A backup-power system sized for "hours to a few days" has no meaningful
  answer to a months-scale grid event beyond eventual fuel exhaustion —
  and unlike a data center (CSMEval16), the consequence here is measured in
  patient outcomes, not service-level agreements.
• This is direct, individual, real-time safety-of-life stakes, placing this
  sector at the same urgency tier as nuclear (CSMEval06) and grid
  (CSMEval02/10).
