A land-based facility that loses power still has a "outside" to evacuate to.
A vessel mid-voyage doesn't have that option in the same way — which is
exactly why this sector's risk case reads differently than any onshore
facility's.

                 EVERYTHING A COASTAL CITY NEEDS, WITH NOWHERE TO WALK TO
                 CSMEval14-B | June 2026 | v1.0
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

PURPOSE
Establish why maritime GIC exposure combines infrastructure-scale
consequence (independent power/water/medical systems, thousands of people)
with a uniquely limited evacuation option compared to onshore facilities.

KEY FACTS
• A large cruise vessel independently generates its own power, produces its
  own water, and maintains onboard medical facilities and food storage — a
  self-sufficient infrastructure profile comparable to a small coastal city.
• A steel hull in seawater is an efficient GIC current sink — the same
  physics that makes bridges and pipelines GIC-exposed, applied to a vessel
  that, unlike a bridge, is also carrying its full population at the moment
  of exposure.
• Classification societies (DNV, ABS, Lloyd's Register) are the technical-
  standards gatekeepers for any hull/systems redesign — meaning any
  hardening pathway must eventually clear a classification review, not just
  an operator's internal engineering sign-off.
• The CSM maritime research corpus is, by volume, one of the largest and
  most mature product lines in the entire repository, built around a real
  named anchor case study rather than a hypothetical vessel.

RISKS / CONCERNS IF UNADDRESSED
• Mid-voyage grid-down conditions remove the "evacuate to shore
  infrastructure" option available to most onshore facilities facing the
  same hazard.
• Classification-society review timelines mean hardening pathways not
  already in a standards conversation face a longer runway than onshore
  retrofits.

NEXT STEP

channels best suited to extending the existing fleet-wide retrofit
