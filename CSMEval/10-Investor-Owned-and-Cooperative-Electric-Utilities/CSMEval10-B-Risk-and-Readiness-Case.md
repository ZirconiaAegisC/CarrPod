One number, first, before anything else: twelve months. That's the
transformer replacement lead time Lloyd's of London's modeling hinges on for
the worst-case scenario. Not twelve days. Not twelve weeks. Twelve months,
during which the thing your customers need most is exactly the thing you
can't quickly buy more of.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
                 THE TWELVE-MONTH NUMBER
                 CSMEval10-B | June 2026 | v1.0
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

PURPOSE
Quantify the financial exposure a true Carrington-class event represents to
utility capital planning, and why probability-reduction hardware has an
outsized value proposition relative to its cost.

KEY FACTS
• Lloyd's of London's North American grid scenario places the cost of a true
  Carrington-level event at $0.6–2.6 trillion, hinging directly on transformer
  replacement lead times of up to 12 months.
• TPL-007/EOP-010 compliance is an assessment/operating-procedure obligation
  — it plans around consequences, but does not itself reduce the probability
  of transformer damage.
• Physical hardening (structure- and perimeter-level GIC path removal) is one
  of the few available levers that reduces probability rather than just
  managing consequence.
• The May 2024 Gannon Storm already produced isolated transformer and
  equipment stress industry-wide during a G3-to-G5 event — a preview at a
  fraction of full Carrington-class severity.

RISKS / CONCERNS IF UNADDRESSED
• A 12-month replacement lead time doesn't shrink because a rate case is
  inconvenient — the physics and the supply chain are indifferent to budget
  cycles.
• Capital deferred past this planning cycle carries forward the full
  pre-hardening probability into the next solar maximum window.
