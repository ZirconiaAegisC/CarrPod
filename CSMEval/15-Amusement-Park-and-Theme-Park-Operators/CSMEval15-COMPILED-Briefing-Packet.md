SUBJECT:       Three Risk Factors, Rarely Stacked This Densely Anywhere Else
REF:           CSMEval15-B

Most venues have one or two of these risk factors. Major theme parks tend to
have all three, stacked, in the same footprint — which is worth naming
plainly rather than assuming it's obvious.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
                 THREE RISK FACTORS, RARELY STACKED THIS DENSELY
                 CSMEval15-B | June 2026 | v1.0
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

PURPOSE
Name the three risk factors this sector combines more densely than most
other venue types, and why that combination warrants a dedicated hardening
case study rather than a generic facilities briefing.

KEY FACTS
• Large numbers of the public occupy a fixed location for extended periods —
  a crowd-density profile with real evacuation-planning implications during
  a grid-down event.
• Ride systems are simultaneously electro-mechanically complex (motor-driven
  attractions, control electronics) and structurally massive (steel
  coasters) — a dual electrical/structural GIC exposure most facilities
  don't combine.
• Many major parks sit on coastal or floodplain-adjacent land — adding a
  second, independent hazard (flood) that compounds rather than replaces
  the GIC exposure.
• Project Aegis-K's Knott's Berry Farm redesign explicitly combines
  Carrington-event GIC hardening with amphibious/flood-risk redesign in one
  methodology — proof the combined-hazard approach is achievable, not just
  theoretically necessary.

RISKS / CONCERNS IF UNADDRESSED
• Ride-safety engineering that addresses structural and electrical risk
  separately may miss failure modes that only appear when both hazards
  occur together (e.g., a flood event during or shortly after a geomagnetic
  storm).
• Guest evacuation planning without a grid-down-specific scenario may
  underestimate real evacuation timelines.

This sector has an unusually strong single-venue concentration — one expo
reaches most of the operators, ride manufacturers, and inspection firms that
matter, all at once.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
                 IAAPA EXPO IS THE WHOLE SECTOR, IN ONE WEEK
                 CSMEval15-C | June 2026 | v1.0
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

PURPOSE
Identify the primary and secondary channels for introducing Project Aegis-K
to theme park operators, ride manufacturers, and safety-certification firms.

PRIMARY VENUE — IAAPA EXPO (ANNUAL, TYPICALLY NOVEMBER)
The primary global industry trade show; the single best distribution event
for this entire sector, reaching operators, ride manufacturers, and
inspection firms in one place, one week a year.

STANDARDS CHANNEL — ASTM F24 COMMITTEE
The American Society for Testing and Materials' F24 Committee on Amusement
Rides and Devices — the correct technical-standards body for any
ride-support-structure materials conversation (BFRP rebar, composite
monopoles).

REGULATORY NOTE
State-level ride-safety regulatory agencies vary by state — relevant for any
operator-specific compliance conversation, but not a single national channel
the way IAAPA and ASTM F24 are.

SUGGESTED SEQUENCING
1. IAAPA Expo exhibitor/technical-session presence (broadest, highest-
   leverage single event).
2. ASTM F24 Committee engagement in parallel (standards credibility for the
   structural-materials angle specifically).
3. Direct facilities-engineering-team follow-up with operators who show
   interest at IAAPA.
