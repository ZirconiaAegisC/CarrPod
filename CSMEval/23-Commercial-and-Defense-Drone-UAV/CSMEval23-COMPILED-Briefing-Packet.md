================================================================================

Two things are true about drones that don't usually get said in the same
sentence: they're GPS-dependent the same way farm equipment is, and in
emergency-response contexts, they're increasingly asked to be load-bearing
for an actual human being. Most consumer/commercial platforms were designed
for the first fact, not the second.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
                 GPS-DEPENDENT AND, SOMETIMES, LOAD-BEARING FOR A HUMAN LIFE
                 CSMEval23-B | June 2026 | v1.0
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

PURPOSE
Establish why GPS dependency and emergency-response load-bearing use cases
both deserve more explicit design attention across this sector than typical
UAS roadmaps currently give them.

KEY FACTS
• Drones are GPS-dependent in a way directly analogous to the precision-
  agriculture equipment that visibly malfunctioned during the May 2024
  Gannon Storm — the same GNSS-disruption exposure, on a different
  platform.
• Emergency-response and evacuation use cases (the Aegis-Ascension backpack
  concept specifically) place drones in a load-bearing-for-human-life role
  most consumer/commercial platforms were never designed for.
• A range of entry points exists across this sector, from hobbyist/
  university platforms (the sub-250g ASB-1) to emergency-response-grade
  heavy-lift systems (Aegis-Ascension) — meaning the GPS-resilience
  conversation is relevant at every scale, not just the high end.

RISKS / CONCERNS IF UNADDRESSED
• UAS platforms without GPS-degradation contingency inherit the same
  failure mode already observed in a real 2024 event, at a moment when
  drone-based emergency response is becoming more, not less, common.
• Emergency-response drone programs that don't explicitly account for
  load-bearing-for-human-life engineering requirements may be adapting a
  delivery-drone design philosophy to a use case it wasn't built for.

One expo reaches most of this sector's commercial and defense manufacturers.
Two smaller, more specific channels reach the regulatory and academic
audiences the first one doesn't.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
                 ONE BIG ROOM, TWO SMALLER ONES
                 CSMEval23-C | June 2026 | v1.0
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

PURPOSE
Identify the primary trade show and secondary regulatory/academic channels
for introducing the three UAS concepts to their respective audiences.

PRIMARY VENUE — AUVSI XPONENTIAL
The Association for Uncrewed Vehicle Systems International's XPONENTIAL is
the industry's largest trade show — the right venue for both the
Archimedes Screw propulsion concept and the Aegis-Ascension backpack,
reaching commercial, defense, and emergency-response manufacturers in one
place.

REGULATORY CHANNEL — FAA UAS INTEGRATION OFFICE
Relevant for any platform requiring airspace-integration discussion,
particularly the emergency-response heavy-lift use case.

ACADEMIC CHANNEL — AIAA (FOR THE WIND-ENERGY-HARVESTING/PHYSICS RESEARCH)
The correct venue for the sub-250g ASB-1 research platform and its
underlying autorotation physics research — reaches university mechatronics
programs more effectively than a commercial trade show would.

TIMING NOTE
The emergency-response evacuation angle (Aegis-Ascension) is strongest
immediately following any high-profile natural disaster news cycle — worth
holding that specific concept's outreach for the right moment rather than
leading with it unconditionally.
