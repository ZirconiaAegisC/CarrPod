A GPS glitch in a city is an inconvenience — there's a street sign, another
driver, or a cell signal to fall back on. A GPS glitch sixty miles down a
forest service road, with no cell signal and no one else around, is a
genuinely different problem. That's the specific stakes-raising factor this
briefing wants to name.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
                 NO FALLBACK INFRASTRUCTURE CHANGES THE MATH
                 CSMEval34-B | June 2026 | v1.0
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

PURPOSE
Establish why GPS/GNSS resilience carries higher real-world stakes for
overlanding, RV, and vanlife travel than for typical urban/suburban driving.

KEY FACTS
• This community already lives with heavy reliance on GPS/GNSS navigation
  in remote areas with no fallback infrastructure — no street signs, no
  reliable cell signal, often no other travelers nearby.
• The May 2024 Gannon Storm's GNSS degradation directly affected
  precision-agriculture autosteer systems — the same underlying GPS-
  dependency profile shared by overlanding navigation units.
• Deliberately independent power and climate systems (common in serious
  overlanding/vanlife builds) already parallel the Aegis Home appliance
  philosophy at vehicle scale — meaning adoption here requires less
  behavior change than in most other consumer contexts.
• Extended vehicle-cabin occupancy (multi-day or multi-week trips) raises
  the relevance of cabin vibration/comfort research beyond what a typical
  daily commute would justify.

RISKS / CONCERNS IF UNADDRESSED
• A GPS failure in a remote area lacks the fallback options (asking a
  bystander, checking a nearby cell tower's coverage, using a well-marked
  street grid) available in urban/suburban contexts.
• Builds that already prioritize independent power/climate systems but skip
  navigation redundancy have addressed only part of their actual
  remote-travel risk profile.

Identify the trade show, industry association, and club-network channels
best suited to reaching the overlanding, RV, and vanlife community.

PRIMARY VENUE — OVERLAND EXPO
The major annual trade show/gathering for the overlanding community; engage

INDUSTRY CHANNEL — RV INDUSTRY ASSOCIATION (RVIA)

consistent with the content-driven distribution pattern also seen in
