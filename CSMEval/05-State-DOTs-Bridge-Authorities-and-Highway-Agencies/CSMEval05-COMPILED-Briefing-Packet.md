Nobody trained as a bridge engineer signed up to also become an electrical
engineer, and that's a completely reasonable career boundary to have set for
yourself. Unfortunately, physics does not respect career boundaries: a
continuous steel girder behaves like a very long, very expensive antenna
during a geomagnetic event, whether or not "antenna" was ever in your job
description. Good news — we already did the electrical-engineering part.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
                 YOUR BRIDGE IS AN ANTENNA
                 CSMEval05-A | June 2026 | v1.0
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

PURPOSE
Summarize CSM structural hardening options addressing GIC/telluric-current
exposure in long-span steel bridge structures — a risk category not
traditionally modeled in structural (as opposed to power-systems) engineering.

1. DIELECTRIC CITADEL PROTOCOL (GLOBAL BRIDGE AUDIT METHODOLOGY)
   A GIC + directed-energy load-case audit methodology already applied to
   four named metro bridge inventories (Bay Area, London, Pittsburgh,
   Seattle) and one global structural engineering firm. Same methodology,
   ready to extend to additional jurisdictions.

2. BASALT-FIBER-REINFORCED-POLYMER (BFRP) REBAR
   Non-conductive rebar substitute for new-build and retrofit concrete
   structures — removes the embedded-steel GIC current path at the material
   level rather than the structure level.

3. AEGIS GEOPOLYMER CONCRETE
   Dielectric concrete formulation proposed for critical structural pours
   where a fully non-conductive structural envelope is the design goal.

4. AEGIS POWER LINE MONOPOLE (COMPOSITE STRUCTURAL PRECEDENT)
   Not a bridge component itself, but a proof point: a non-metallic
   structural member already engineered to hold real load in a GIC-exposed
   application, relevant precedent for any bridge-hardware retrofit
   discussion.

WHAT MAKES THIS DIFFERENT FROM A COLD PITCH
This isn't a hypothetical methodology looking for a first customer. Four
metro audits already exist. The ask is simply whether your bridge inventory
would like to be the fifth.

Here's the thing nobody tells you in engineering school: entire hazard
categories can fall between two disciplines' filing cabinets for decades,
not because anyone was careless, but because "whose desk is this on" is a
genuinely hard question when the hazard is electromagnetic and the asset is
structural. GIC/telluric-current analysis grew up entirely inside power-
systems engineering. Bridge engineering grew up entirely inside structural
and materials science. Nobody was wrong. Nobody was covering this specific
gap, either.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
                 WHY NOBODY MODELED THIS UNTIL NOW
                 CSMEval05-B | June 2026 | v1.0
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

PURPOSE
Explain the disciplinary gap that left long-span bridge GIC exposure
unmodeled, and why current conditions make closing that gap timely.

KEY FACTS
• Long, continuous steel structures (girders, cables, rebar networks)
  concentrate telluric/GIC current the same way transmission lines do —
  a well-understood physics principle in power engineering, historically
  applied only to power infrastructure, not structural infrastructure.
• A worked LA-area case example (from the companion evacuation-protocol
  research) modeled an amplified geoelectric field of 43 V/km across a 2 km
  bridge span under G5 storm conditions, producing enough induced current to
  burn off expansion-joint lubrication within roughly six hours, seizing
  joints and inducing thermal-cycling deck cracking.
• Solar Cycle 25's confirmed SSN ~137 peak (vs. ~115 forecast) raises the
  probability of the G4/G5 conditions this analysis assumes.
• Four existing CSM metro bridge audits (Bay Area, London, Pittsburgh,
  Seattle) already demonstrate the methodology is transferable, not
  bespoke-per-city from scratch.

RISKS / CONCERNS IF UNADDRESSED
• Capital planning cycles that don't include GIC/telluric load cases will
  continue to omit a real, physically-grounded failure mode from asset
  condition assessments.
• Post-storm bridge closures compound evacuation-timing risk already
  documented in the emergency-management sector's own briefing series —
  this sector's inaction has downstream effects on other sectors' plans.

PURPOSE
Propose extending the existing Dielectric Citadel bridge-audit methodology to
a new jurisdiction, and identify the technical venues that support that
introduction.

PATHWAY 1 — CAPITAL PROGRAM / TRANSPORTATION IMPROVEMENT PROGRAM (TIP) CYCLE
Most state DOTs run 4-6 year TIP windows for asset-management planning — the
natural point to add a GIC/telluric load-case review to an existing bridge
condition assessment, without creating a new planning process.

PATHWAY 2 — POST-STORM TECHNICAL COURTESY BRIEFING
Outreach timed to follow any publicized G4+ NOAA space-weather alert tends to
land better than a cold pitch during a quiet news cycle — not because the
content changes, but because the audience's attention does.

PATHWAY 3 — AASHTO / ASCE TECHNICAL COMMITTEE PRESENTATION
Presenting the existing four-city methodology at an AASHTO Committee on
Bridges and Structures session or an ASCE Structural Engineering Institute
event reaches multiple jurisdictions' technical staff in one venue, rather
than one relationship at a time.

cities, would your bridge inventory be interested in a fifth" — addressed to
