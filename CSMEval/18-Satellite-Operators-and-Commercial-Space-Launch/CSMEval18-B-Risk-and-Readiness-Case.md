PURPOSE
Establish that satellite/space-weather exposure is already a priced,
underwritten risk category, not a speculative future concern, and why that
matters for this sector's own risk posture.

KEY FACTS
• The May 2024 Gannon Storm expanded Earth's upper atmosphere enough to
  increase orbital drag on low-Earth-orbit satellites, prompting NASA's
  ICESat-2 mission to enter safe mode and complicating ESA space-debris
  tracking.
• Lloyd's of London underwrites nearly a third of all global space risk —
  meaning this sector's insurers are already pricing this exposure today,
  not as a future scenario.
• Concept-stage research (humanoid robot hardening, alternative launch-assist
  actuators) represents exactly the kind of moonshot materials/actuation work
  aerospace and space advanced-concepts divisions maintain internal review
  processes to evaluate.

WHY THIS MATTERS TO OPERATORS SPECIFICALLY
An operator whose own insurer is already pricing space-weather exposure has
a direct, practical interest in any research that could eventually reduce
that exposure — even concept-stage research, because early positioning ahead
of an eventual underwriting-credit conversation has real value.
