Positive Train Control was, correctly, one of the biggest safety investments
your industry has made in a generation. It also quietly added a GNSS-timing
dependency that didn't exist in the older systems it replaced — worth
naming plainly, not as a criticism of PTC, but as an honest accounting of
what changed.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
                 YOUR PTC INVESTMENT DESERVES A MATCHING HARDENING INVESTMENT
                 CSMEval20-B | June 2026 | v1.0
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

PURPOSE
Make the operator-specific capital case for pairing PTC/signaling investment
with GIC/GNSS-disruption hardening, building on the federal-regulatory case
already presented in CSMEval07.

KEY FACTS
• Continuous-welded rail is a long-conductor GIC pathway with the same
  physics as a transmission line — a structural/electrical exposure that
  predates PTC but compounds with it.
• Modern PTC systems add a GNSS/timing dependency that mirrors the
  GPS-disruption risk documented in the May 2024 Gannon Storm's effect on
  farm equipment.
• The actual capital-budget decisions for catenary, signaling, and
  rolling-stock hardening are made by operating companies themselves, not by
  FRA — meaning this conversation has to happen here, not just at the
  federal level.
• Short line and regional freight railroads, commuter authorities, and urban
  light rail/metro operators each carry this exposure at their own scale,
  independent of Class I freight's larger footprint.

RISKS / CONCERNS IF UNADDRESSED
• PTC's safety benefits under normal conditions don't offset a GNSS-
  disruption scenario the system wasn't originally designed against.
• Capital committees that treat PTC as a completed safety investment, full
  stop, may not recognize the follow-on hardening decision as connected to
  the same original safety case.
