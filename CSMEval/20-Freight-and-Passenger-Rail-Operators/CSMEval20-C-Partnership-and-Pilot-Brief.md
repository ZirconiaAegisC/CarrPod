Freight and passenger rail have different owners, different capital
processes, and mostly different trade associations — so let's split the
channel the same way the industry already splits itself.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
                 FREIGHT AND PASSENGER SPLIT, ON PURPOSE
                 CSMEval20-C | June 2026 | v1.0
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

PURPOSE
Match outreach channel to freight versus passenger/transit ownership
structure.

FREIGHT CHANNEL — ASSOCIATION OF AMERICAN RAILROADS (AAR)
Freight rail's standards and research body; the correct venue for Class I
and short-line freight capital-planning audiences.

PASSENGER/TRANSIT CHANNEL — APTA RAIL CONFERENCE
Reaches commuter rail authorities and urban light rail/metro operators in
one venue, the same venue referenced for the federal-side outreach in
CSMEval07 — worth noting that a single conference presence can serve both
this dossier and that one.

CROSS-BORDER CHANNEL
Railway Association of Canada — relevant for North American cross-border
operators whose networks span both countries.

SUGGESTED SEQUENCING
1. Identify whether the target operator is primarily freight or
   passenger/transit, and lead with AAR or APTA accordingly.
2. For operators with mixed freight/passenger exposure (some Class I
   railroads host both), engage both channels rather than choosing one.
3. Layer in Railway Association of Canada engagement for any cross-border
   operator.

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
