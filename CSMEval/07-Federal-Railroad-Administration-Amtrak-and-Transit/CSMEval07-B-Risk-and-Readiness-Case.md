Here's the counterintuitive part: modernizing a rail network — more Positive
Train Control, more automated signaling, more GPS-referenced positioning —
makes the system safer under normal conditions and more exposed under GIC
conditions, simultaneously. Not a reason to stop modernizing. A reason to
harden while you do it, instead of after.

                 THE RISK THAT GROWS AS THE SYSTEM GETS SMARTER
                 CSMEval07-B | June 2026 | v1.0
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

PURPOSE
Explain why rail signaling's GIC exposure carries a real-time safety-of-life
dimension distinct from the structural-integrity risk profile affecting
bridges and highways, and why that risk grows with electronic modernization.

KEY FACTS
• Continuous-welded rail and overhead catenary systems form long,
  continuous, low-resistance conductive networks — the same underlying
  physics as transmission-line and bridge GIC exposure.
• Unlike structural damage, a signaling failure can be silent — a system that
  reports incorrect train position/occupancy rather than visibly failing.
• The May 2024 Gannon Storm produced documented GPS/GNSS degradation across
  the U.S. Midwest lasting up to two days — a direct precedent for
  GPS-referenced Positive Train Control systems losing positional accuracy
  during a geomagnetic event.
• Solar Cycle 25's confirmed SSN ~137 peak keeps this exposure live for the
  remainder of the current solar maximum period.

RISKS / CONCERNS IF UNADDRESSED
• PTC and automated signaling investment, without a parallel GIC-hardening
  and GPS-redundancy investment, increases electronic dependency without a
  matching increase in electromagnetic resilience.
• A real-time safety-of-life framing (versus the structural-integrity framing
  used for bridges) means the cost of being wrong here is measured
  differently than a closed route — it's measured in whether the system knows
  the truth about where trains are.
