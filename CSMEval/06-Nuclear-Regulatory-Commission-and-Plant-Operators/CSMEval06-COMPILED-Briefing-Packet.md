                 NON-CONDUCTIVE SWITCHYARD & BALANCE-OF-PLANT HARDENING
                 CSMEval06-A | June 2026 | v1.0
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

PURPOSE
Summarize three CSM hardening components targeting the specific offsite/
onsite transformer and auxiliary-system GIC exposure documented in the
historical U.S. nuclear plant GMD incident record.

1. SWITCHYARD PERIMETER HARDENING
   Composite (non-metallic) monopoles and dielectric perimeter fencing
   (CSMFAB0105, CSMFAB0104) applied to switchyard structures — addressing
   the structure-level GIC path independent of transformer-level mitigation
   already covered by plant-specific GIC studies.

2. NON-CONDUCTIVE AUXILIARY WIRING AND MOTOR DESIGN
   GIC-safe wiring and piezoelectric/pneumatic motor design concepts
   (CSMFAB013, CSMFAB020) for auxiliary systems — targeting balance-of-plant
   equipment that sits outside primary safety-system design-basis
   requirements but still represents an availability and equipment-protection
   concern.

3. CHROMATIC FIELD MONITOR
   Passive, low-power GIC/EMF area monitor (CSMFAB021) for switchyard and
   site-perimeter deployment — a lower-cost complement to full magnetometer-
   grade instrumentation, intended for denser deployment across a plant
   fleet.

WHAT THIS IS NOT
This is not a safety-system redesign proposal, and nothing here is presented
as a substitute for any NRC-required design-basis analysis. This is
balance-of-plant and switchyard hardware positioned to reduce the specific
transformer/auxiliary-equipment failure mode already documented in NERC's own
historical GMD-impact record.

                 FOUR PLANTS, THREE SOLAR CYCLES, ONE REPEATING PATTERN
                 CSMEval06-B | June 2026 | v1.0
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

PURPOSE
Present the documented historical record of GMD-related electrical incidents
at U.S. nuclear plants, and why Solar Cycle 25's confirmed peak returns the
fleet to the exposure window this record describes.

KEY FACTS — THE HISTORICAL RECORD
• Maine Yankee — transformer damage/failure events in March 1989 and again in
  April 1991 (two separate solar cycles' worth of recurrence at the same
  site).
• Salem Unit 1 and Salem Unit 2 — transformer-related incidents in March and
  September 1989.
• Seabrook — a vibration-related event in November 1998.
• Hope Creek — downrated to 55% power in July 2000.
• This spans three solar cycles (roughly Cycles 22 through 23), meaning the
  pattern is not a single-event anomaly — it is a repeating exposure that
  correlates with geomagnetic storm activity across multiple solar maxima.
• Solar Cycle 25's confirmed SSN ~137 peak (NOAA SWPC, Q1 2026) places current
  conditions back inside the activity range associated with this historical
  record.
• Nuclear plants carry the lowest acceptable tolerance for GIC-related
  electrical anomaly of any sector in this outreach series: a loss-of-offsite-
  power event initiates emergency diesel generator dependency and, in
  worst-case cascades, core-cooling risk.

WHY THIS RECORD MATTERS NOW, SPECIFICALLY
• A four-incident, three-cycle historical record is exactly the kind of
  pattern that regulatory and industry technical-exchange venues (NRC RIC,
  EPRI Nuclear Sector) are built to review and act on.
• This is not a hypothetical hardening pitch — it is a hardware response to
  an already-documented failure mode.

                 PRESENTING THE RECORD AND THE RESPONSE TOGETHER
                 CSMEval06-C | June 2026 | v1.0
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

PURPOSE
Identify the primary and secondary venues for presenting the historical GMD
incident record (Briefing B) alongside the hardening response (Briefing A) to
the nuclear regulatory and operator community.

PRIMARY VENUE — NRC REGULATORY INFORMATION CONFERENCE (RIC)
The annual, primary NRC/industry technical-exchange venue. This is the venue
where a documented historical failure pattern and a corresponding hardware
response belong in the same room, in front of both regulators and operators
simultaneously.

SECONDARY VENUES
• Nuclear Energy Institute (NEI) — industry trade association; engage via
  NEI's regulatory-affairs working groups, particularly any group already
  focused on infrastructure resilience.
• EPRI Nuclear Sector — parallel to EPRI's grid-side GMD/SUNBURST program,
  with a nuclear-specific electrical-systems research track; a natural
  technical-validation partner given EPRI's existing GIC monitoring role on
  the grid side.
• American Nuclear Society (ANS) Annual Meeting — broader technical/academic
  audience, useful for peer credibility alongside the more operator-focused
  RIC and NEI channels.
• Institute of Nuclear Power Operations (INPO) — industry self-regulatory
  body; engage via INPO's technical-advisor channel for electrical/grid
  interface topics.

SUGGESTED SEQUENCING
1. EPRI Nuclear Sector (technical validation first — this strengthens
   everything that follows).
2. NRC RIC (regulatory + operator audience together, the highest-value single
   venue).
3. NEI and INPO (industry-adoption and best-practice channels, in parallel).
