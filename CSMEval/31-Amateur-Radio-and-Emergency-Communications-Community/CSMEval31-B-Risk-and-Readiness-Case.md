Most technically sophisticated audiences in this outreach series are also,
professionally, somewhat removed from the actual moment a disaster happens
— they research it, regulate it, or insure it. This community does something
different: when normal infrastructure fails, ARES/RACES operators are
activated, in person, that same day.

PURPOSE
Establish why this community combines technical sophistication with direct
operational relevance more than any other audience in this outreach series.

KEY FACTS
• HF propagation is directly affected by the same ionospheric disturbances
  that degrade GPS — amateur radio operators track this as routine
  operational knowledge via NOAA's R-scale, not as novel information.
• ARES (Amateur Radio Emergency Service) and RACES (Radio Amateur Civil
  Emergency Service) are the volunteer emergency-communications backbone
  that activates when normal infrastructure fails — the exact scenario the
  CSM corpus is built around.
• Direct overlap exists with county-level emergency management structures
  already addressed in CSMEval01 and CSMEval08 — many ARES/RACES volunteers
  are embedded with county EOC teams.
• This is, in practice, the most technically sophisticated and most
  naturally receptive general-public audience for CSM research of any group
  in this outreach series.

WHY THIS MATTERS TO THE REST OF THIS OUTREACH SERIES
A community that both understands the physics and shows up during the
actual event is a uniquely valuable feedback channel for every hardware and
protocol concept elsewhere in this corpus — not just a distribution target.

NEXT STEP
