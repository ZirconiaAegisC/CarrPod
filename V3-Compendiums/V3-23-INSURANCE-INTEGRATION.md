## 2.0 ACTUARIAL VALIDATION (INS-02)

### 2.1 GIC Loss Curves — Methodology

The actuarial model uses a compound Poisson-Gamma process to model GMD event frequency and severity:

**Event Frequency:** Solar cycle data (NOAA SWPC, 1755-2026) yields a G5 (Extreme) event rate of approximately 4 per century, G4 (Severe) events at 10 per century, and G3 (Strong) events at 20 per century. The exceedance probability curve for a Carrington-magnitude event (Dst < -850 nT) is approximately 12% per century — or 0.12% per year. At that annual probability, a $2.4 trillion loss has an annual expected value of $2.88 billion — approximately equal to the current global property catastrophe reinsurance premium for all perils combined.

**Williams Translation:** "A Carrington event has about a 0.12% chance of happening in any given year. That doesn't sound like much. But 0.12% times $2.4 trillion = $2.88 billion in expected annual losses. That's roughly what the entire global reinsurance industry collects in property cat premiums every year. In other words, GMD risk alone equals all other natural catastrophe risk combined — and nobody is pricing it."

### 2.3 Correlation Modeling — The "Everything Everywhere All At Once" Problem

Standard insurance portfolio diversification relies on uncorrelated risks — a hurricane in Florida doesn't cause an earthquake in California. GMD violates this assumption:

| Catastrophe Type | Geographic Correlation | Line-of-Business Correlation | Diversification Possible? |
|---|---|---|---|
| Hurricane | Regional (100-500 km) | Property + BI + Auto | Yes — geographically diversify |
| Earthquake | Regional (50-200 km) | Property + BI + Fire Following | Yes — geographically diversify |
| Pandemic | Global (correlated across all regions) | Life + Health + BI + WC | Some — reinsurance and government backstops |
| **Carrington GMD** | **Continental to hemispheric** | **Property + BI + Auto + Marine + Aviation + Life + Health + Crop + Cyber** | **NO — EVERY LINE TRIGGERS SIMULTANEOUSLY** |

This correlation structure means that a Carrington event is the one scenario where insurance industry solvency is genuinely at risk — not for individual carriers but for the global industry as a whole, because reinsurance capital would be overwhelmed by simultaneous claims across all lines and all geographies.

That frees up hundreds of millions in capital that they can deploy for new business. That's real money. That's the kind of thing CFOs notice."
