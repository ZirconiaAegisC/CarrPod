# STT-MRAM: Technology Architecture & Scaling

## Physics of Spin-Transfer Torque

Spin-Transfer Torque MRAM (STT-MRAM) represents a fundamentally
different write mechanism from Toggle MRAM, enabling scalability to
gigabit densities and sub-20nm process nodes. Instead of using
magnetic fields generated by external write lines, STT-MRAM exploits
the spin-transfer torque effect: a spin-polarized current passing
directly through the magnetic tunnel junction (MTJ) exerts a torque
on the free layer magnetization, switching it between parallel and
anti-parallel states.

The direction of switching depends on the polarity of the current.
When electrons flow from the pinned (reference) layer to the free
layer, the spin-polarized current aligns the free layer parallel to
the reference layer. Reversing the current direction switches the
free layer to the anti-parallel state. The current required for
switching (the critical current, Ic) is proportional to the MTJ
cross-sectional area, an essential property for scalability: as
the cell shrinks, the write current decreases proportionally.

This current-driven mechanism fundamentally solves the scaling
limitation of field-switched Toggle MRAM. At 28nm and below, STT-MRAM
write currents are in the range of 30–80 µA per cell, making dense
arrays feasible without excessive IR drop or electromigration
concerns in the write path.

## Perpendicular Magnetic Anisotropy (PMA)

All commercial STT-MRAM from Everspin employs perpendicular magnetic
anisotropy (PMA), where the magnetization of both the free and
reference layers is oriented out of the plane of the film. PMA
provides two decisive advantages over in-plane anisotropy for
scaled cells:

1. **Higher thermal stability**: The energy barrier (Δ) for a PMA
   cell is proportional to interfacial perpendicular anisotropy
   energy rather than shape anisotropy. At small diameters (below
   approximately 40 nm), interfacial anisotropy remains strong
   while shape anisotropy of in-plane cells collapses.

2. **Reduced switching current**: The critical switching current
   for PMA cells is lower than for in-plane cells at equivalent
   thermal stability, enabling lower write energy and reducing
   the size and drive strength required for the access transistor.

The PMA stack is engineered at the interface between the CoFeB
free layer and the MgO tunnel barrier. The interfacial anisotropy
energy density (Ki) arises from hybridization of Fe 3d and O 2p
orbitals at the CoFeB/MgO interface. Careful control of the CoFeB
thickness (typically 1.2–1.8 nm) and the MgO crystallinity during
post-deposition annealing are critical to achieving both high TMR
(150–200%) and adequate thermal stability.

## Cell Architecture and Array Design

The STT-MRAM bit cell uses a 1T-1MTJ architecture, identical in
topology to the Toggle MRAM cell but with fundamentally different
write operation:

- **Read**: The word line activates the access transistor; a small
  sense current (typically 5–15 µA) flows through the MTJ; a sense
  amplifier compares the resulting voltage against a reference to
  determine the stored state.

- **Write**: The word line activates the access transistor; a write
  current of 40–80 µA flows through the MTJ from source line to bit
  line (for one polarity) or bit line to source line (for the
  opposite polarity). The write pulse duration is typically 10–50 ns.

A critical design consideration for STT-MRAM arrays is the read
disturb margin. Because the read current also flows through the MTJ,
it must be kept below the threshold that could inadvertently switch
the free layer. This is managed through:
- Careful selection of read bias voltage (typically 0.1–0.2V)
- Statistical modeling of the switching probability distribution
  versus applied voltage
- Error correction coding (ECC) to handle any disturb-induced errors

## MTJ Process Integration

The STT-MRAM MTJ stack is integrated into the back-end-of-line (BEOL)
of the CMOS process flow, typically between Metal-4 and Metal-5 (or
higher metal levels depending on the foundry's interconnect scheme).
Key process steps include:

1. **Bottom electrode deposition**: Ta/TaN or Ti/TiN layers providing
   electrical contact and a smooth template for MTJ growth.

2. **MTJ stack deposition**: Sequential physical vapor deposition (PVD)
   of the reference layer synthetic antiferromagnet, MgO tunnel
   barrier, CoFeB free layer, and capping layer. All layers are
   deposited in a single vacuum sequence to prevent interface
   contamination.

3. **Magnetic annealing**: Post-deposition anneal at 300–400°C in a
   magnetic field (typically 1 Tesla) to set the reference layer
   magnetization and crystallize the CoFeB/MgO interface.

4. **MTJ pillar etch**: Reactive ion etching (RIE) to define the MTJ
   pillar, typically 30–80 nm in diameter depending on the technology
   node. This is the most challenging step: the MTJ materials are
   difficult to etch, conductively re-deposited sidewall material
   can cause electrical shorts, and ion bombardment can degrade the
   magnetic properties of the exposed free layer sidewall.

5. **Encapsulation**: Immediate deposition of a dielectric encapsulation
   layer (typically SiN or SiCN) after etch to protect the MTJ
   sidewalls from oxidation and moisture.

6. **Top electrode and via**: Metallization to connect the top of the
   MTJ pillar to the bit line.

## Scaling Roadmap

Everspin's STT-MRAM scaling roadmap targets:

| Node     | MTJ Diam. | Bit Cell | Density | Status            |
|----------|-----------|----------|---------|-------------------|
| 28nm     | ~60 nm    | ~0.06 µm²| 1Gb     | Production (TSMC)  |
| 22nm     | ~45 nm    | ~0.04 µm²| 2-4Gb   | Development        |
| 16nm FinFET | ~35 nm | ~0.025 µm²| 4-8Gb  | Qualification      |

The 16nm FinFET node at TSMC represents the first STT-MRAM integration
on a FinFET logic platform, which presents additional integration
challenges due to the taller transistor topography and the tighter
thermal budget constraints of advanced CMOS nodes.

## ECC and Reliability Management

STT-MRAM arrays at gigabit densities incorporate on-die error
correction coding (ECC), typically single-error-correct, double-error-
detect (SECDED) using Hamming codes, to manage the random error rate
arising from:

- Write errors (insufficient write current due to statistical
  variations in MTJ switching threshold)
- Read disturb errors
- Retention errors (thermally activated reversals in cells with
  weak PMA)
- Endurance-related degradation

Everspin's published endurance specifications for STT-MRAM products
are 10^6 to 10^8 write cycles, significantly lower than Toggle MRAM's
unlimited endurance. This reflects both the more demanding physics of
STT switching (higher current density through the MgO barrier) and
the market requirements of the target applications — data center
and embedded systems rarely require more than 10^6–10^7 write cycles
over a product's lifetime in a single memory location.
