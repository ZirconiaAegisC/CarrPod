# Sublime Systems — Competitive Intelligence: Low-Carbon Cement Landscape

## The Cement Decarbonization Challenge

Cement decarbonization is widely considered more challenging than power generation or transportation decarbonization because the majority of emissions arise from the fundamental chemistry of limestone decomposition rather than from energy consumption. This process-emissions challenge means that switching to renewable heat sources addresses only ~40% of cement CO₂ emissions. The remaining ~60% requires either (a) carbon capture and storage, (b) alternative raw materials that do not contain carbonate, or (c) fundamentally different chemistry — Sublime's approach.

## Direct Competitors

### Brimstone (Oakland, California)

Brimstone is developing a process that produces cement from calcium silicate rocks (basalt, etc.) rather than limestone:

**Technology**: Thermochemical processing of calcium silicate rocks to extract calcium and produce supplementary cementitious materials. The process claims to be carbon-negative because the silicate rocks passively absorb CO₂ during processing, and the residual silicate minerals can be used as supplementary cementitious materials.

**Comparative Assessment**:
| Dimension | Sublime Systems | Brimstone |
|-----------|----------------|-----------|
| Core Process | Electrochemical | Thermochemical |
| Temperature | Ambient (<100°C) | High (kiln-based) |
| Feedstock | Flexible (limestone or silicates) | Calcium silicate rocks |
| Technology Maturity | Pilot (250 t/yr) | Laboratory/Pilot |
| Funding | $200M+ | ~$60M |
| Key Partners | CRH, SCG | Undisclosed |
| Product | Calcium silicate cement | Blended cement |
| Carbon Claim | 90%+ reduction or net-zero | Carbon-negative |

**Analysis**: Brimstone's carbon-negative claim is compelling but depends on passive CO₂ absorption by processed rock residues — a phenomenon that occurs on geological timescales in nature and whose kinetics in an industrial process context are not fully validated. Sublime's electrochemical approach has the advantage of operating at ambient conditions (no kiln) and of having achieved pilot-scale production (250 t/yr vs. Brimstone's smaller scale).

### Fortera (San Jose, California)

Fortera has commercialized a process that converts limestone to a reactive calcium silicate cement using a lower-temperature kiln coupled with CO₂ capture:

**Technology**: Limestone is calcined at moderate temperature to produce reactive CaO, which is then reacted with CO₂ (captured from the kiln off-gas) and silica to produce a cementitious material (ReAct® technology).

**Comparative Assessment**:
| Dimension | Sublime Systems | Fortera |
|-----------|----------------|-----------|
| Core Process | Electrochemical | Thermochemical + CO₂ mineralization |
| Temperature | Ambient | Moderate (kiln, lower than Portland) |
| CO₂ Emissions | 0–5% (process) | ~60% reduction vs. Portland |
| Technology Maturity | Pilot | Commercial demo (Redding, CA plant) |
| Commercial Scale | Pre-commercial | 15,000 t/yr (demo) |

**Analysis**: Fortera is further along the commercialization path, with a demonstration plant operating in Redding, California producing cement sold under the ReAct brand. However, Fortera's technology still requires a kiln (albeit at lower temperature) and achieves a lower CO₂ reduction (~60%) compared to Sublime's 90%+. These technologies address different market segments and may be complementary rather than directly competitive.

### CarbonCure Technologies (Halifax, Nova Scotia)

CarbonCure injects captured CO₂ into fresh concrete, where it mineralizes and becomes permanently sequestered while also improving concrete strength:

**Technology**: CO₂ injection into ready-mix concrete during mixing. The CO₂ reacts with calcium ions in the cement paste to form nano-scale calcium carbonate particles that strengthen the concrete matrix.

**Comparative Assessment**: CarbonCure addresses the concrete stage rather than the cement production stage, meaning it reduces the net carbon footprint of concrete rather than the embodied carbon of the cement itself. It is complementary to Sublime's technology — low-carbon Sublime cement combined with CarbonCure's CO₂ injection could produce exceptionally low-carbon or carbon-negative concrete.

## Carbon Capture Competitors

Multiple companies are developing carbon capture technologies specifically for cement kilns. These are indirect competitors to Sublime — they represent the incumbent industry's preferred decarbonization pathway:

| Company | Capture Technology | CO₂ Reduction | Cost ($/tCO₂) |
|---------|-------------------|--------------|----------------|
| Carbon Clean | Amine-based post-combustion | 90% | $40–60 |
| Svante | Solid sorbent | 90% | $50–75 |
| Calix (LEILAC) | Direct separation (calciner) | 90%+ | $30–50 |
| Aker Carbon Capture | Amine-based | 90% | $45–65 |
| Capsol Technologies | Hot potassium carbonate | 90% | $40–55 |

Carbon capture addresses the emissions of conventional cement production without changing the fundamental process chemistry. The total cost of cement with carbon capture is estimated at $100–200 per tonne above current production costs, depending on capture technology, energy costs, and CO₂ transport/storage costs.

Sublime's electrochemical process eliminates emissions at the source rather than capturing them, avoiding the ongoing operating costs and infrastructure requirements of carbon capture and storage.

## Supplementary Cementitious Materials

SCMs — materials that partially replace Portland cement in concrete — are a widely deployed decarbonization strategy:

| SCM | Global Availability (Mt/yr) | CO₂ Reduction Potential |
|-----|---------------------------|------------------------|
| Ground granulated blast furnace slag (GGBFS) | ~300 | High (waste product) |
| Fly ash (coal combustion) | ~500 | High (waste product) |
| Natural pozzolans | Abundant | Moderate |
| Calcined clay (metakaolin) | Abundant | Moderate |
| Limestone powder | Abundant | Low-Moderate |

The challenge with SCMs is that the largest-volume SCMs — blast furnace slag and coal fly ash — are byproducts of industries (steelmaking via blast furnace, coal-fired power generation) that are themselves being decarbonized. As blast furnaces are replaced with lower-emissions technologies and coal power plants are retired, the supply of these SCMs will decline, potentially creating supply gaps that low-carbon cements like Sublime's can fill.

### Key Competitive Advantages

1. **Highest CO₂ reduction among scalable technologies**: 90%+ reduction without requiring carbon capture infrastructure or declining industrial waste feedstocks
2. **Ambient-temperature operation**: Eliminates the massive capital and operating costs of high-temperature kilns
3. **Feedstock flexibility**: Can use limestone (with concentrated CO₂ capture) or calcium silicates (zero CO₂) — flexibility across geographies and regulatory environments
4. **Drop-in product**: ASTM-certified cement that can be used in existing concrete production infrastructure without changes to construction practices
5. **Hydrogen co-product**: Revenue stream that improves overall process economics

### Key Competitive Vulnerabilities

1. **Technology maturity gap**: Carbon capture and LC3 are further along the commercialization curve
2. **Electricity cost sensitivity**: Cost-competitiveness depends on low-cost electricity
3. **Electrolyzer supply chain**: Commercial-scale deployment requires electrolyzer manufacturing capacity that does not currently exist
4. **Incumbent resistance**: The cement industry has invested heavily in carbon capture as its primary decarbonization pathway and may resist alternative approaches
