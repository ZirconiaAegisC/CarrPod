# Sublime Systems — Technology Risk Assessment

## Executive Summary

Sublime Systems' electrochemical cement technology offers a fundamentally new pathway to decarbonize one of the world's most emissions-intensive industries. However, the transition from pilot-scale production (250 tonnes/year) to commercial-scale deployment (25,000–500,000+ tonnes/year) introduces technology, operational, and market risks that must be carefully evaluated. This document assesses the principal risks facing the company through commercialization.

## Risk Category 1: Electrolyzer Scale-Up

### Risk Description
The electrolyzer stack is the core unit operation of the Sublime process, and scaling it from pilot to commercial dimensions is the single greatest technology risk. Challenges include:

- **Electrode area scaling**: Pilot cells with electrode areas of 0.1–1.0 m² must be scaled to commercial stacks with total electrode areas of tens to hundreds of square meters
- **Current distribution**: Ensuring uniform current density across larger electrodes to prevent hot spots, uneven pH generation, and accelerated membrane degradation
- **Electrolyte flow distribution**: Maintaining uniform flow across larger electrode surfaces to ensure consistent calcium dissolution and precipitation
- **Stack assembly and sealing**: Commercial stacks must maintain gas-tight seals, electrical connections, and structural integrity across hundreds or thousands of cells in series/parallel configurations
- **Manufacturing quality control**: Consistent production of membranes, electrodes, and cell components at commercial volumes with tight specifications

### Risk Severity: HIGH
Electrolyzer scale-up risk is the most significant technology risk, as it directly determines whether the process can achieve commercial production rates, energy efficiency, and operating costs.

### Mitigation Status
- Pilot electrolyzer testing at Holyoke facility provides operational data for scale-up modeling
- Sublime benefits from the broader electrolyzer industry ecosystem — PEM and alkaline electrolyzer manufacturers (for hydrogen production) have invested billions in electrolyzer manufacturing scale-up, creating supply chains for membranes, electrodes, and cell components that Sublime can leverage
- Modular stack architecture allows incremental scale-up

### Residual Risk
Commercial-scale electrolyzer performance (current efficiency, membrane lifetime, calcium transport efficiency) remains unvalidated. If commercial stacks fail to achieve target performance, process economics could be significantly worse than projected.

## Risk Category 2: Product Consistency and Quality

### Risk Description
Cement is a commodity product where consistency is paramount. Ready-mix concrete producers, precast manufacturers, and construction contractors depend on predictable setting times, strength development, and workability. Variability in Sublime cement properties could create adoption barriers:

- **Feedstock variability**: Calcium content, impurity levels, and reactivity of feedstocks can vary between sources and even within a single quarry. This variability could affect calcium extraction efficiency, precipitation kinetics, and product composition.
- **Process variability**: Electrochemical process parameters (current density, pH, temperature, residence time) can drift, affecting product characteristics.
- **Product consistency across batches**: Ensuring that cement from different production campaigns has consistent performance requires robust quality control and potentially blending of production batches.

### Risk Severity: MEDIUM-HIGH
Product quality risk is manageable through rigorous quality control, but lapses in consistency during early commercial production could create reputational damage that is difficult to overcome.

### Mitigation Status
- ASTM certification testing at Holyoke provides baseline performance data
- Statistical process control systems for monitoring key process parameters
- Product blending systems to homogenize production output
- Ongoing testing with concrete producers to validate real-world performance

### Residual Risk
The number of production campaigns at Holyoke (250 t/yr facility) is limited. The statistical basis for product consistency is smaller than ideal. Early commercial production may experience variability that narrows as operational experience accumulates.

## Risk Category 3: Feedstock Supply Chain

### Risk Description
Sublime's process can utilize multiple calcium-bearing feedstocks, but the supply chain for each feedstock type presents distinct challenges:

- **Limestone**: Abundant and low-cost, but its use in the Sublime process generates a concentrated CO₂ stream that must be captured and stored/used to achieve >90% emissions reduction. This requires co-location with CO₂ transport and storage infrastructure.
- **Calcium silicate minerals (wollastonite)**: Zero CO₂ from chemistry, but wollastonite deposits are geographically limited and current global production is only ~1 million tonnes/year — insufficient for cement-scale production. Developing new wollastonite mining operations would require significant investment and permitting.
- **Industrial alkaline wastes (steel slag, fly ash, etc.)**: An attractive circular economy feedstock, but supply volumes are limited and declining (as steel decarbonizes and coal power retires). Waste composition is variable, complicating process control.
- **Basalt and mafic rocks**: Abundant globally, but dissolution kinetics are slower than for limestone or wollastonite, potentially reducing throughput and increasing energy consumption per tonne of cement.

### Risk Severity: MEDIUM
Feedstock availability is not an existential risk — limestone is globally abundant — but the choice of feedstock affects process economics, CO₂ emissions, and geographic deployment options.

### Mitigation Status
- Testing of multiple feedstocks at Holyoke facility
- Partnerships with mining companies for calcium silicate resource development
- Process optimization for lower-reactivity feedstocks (basalt)
- CO₂ capture system development for concentrated CO₂ stream from limestone feedstock

### Residual Risk
The optimal feedstock strategy for commercial deployment is not yet defined. Limestone with CO₂ capture may be the most practical near-term pathway, but this adds cost and infrastructure requirements. Calcium silicate feedstocks offer true zero-CO₂ cement but require developing new mining supply chains.

## Risk Category 4: Electricity Cost Sensitivity

### Risk Description
Like Boston Metal, Sublime's process economics are fundamentally driven by electricity costs. At 2–4 MWh per tonne of cement:

- Each $10/MWh change in electricity cost changes the cost of cement by $20–40 per tonne
- The hydrogen by-product credit provides a partial hedge (hydrogen value is also correlated with electricity costs in electrolytic hydrogen production)

### Risk Severity: MEDIUM
This is a market risk rather than a technology risk. Sublime cement will be most competitive in regions with low-cost renewable electricity and carbon pricing.

### Mitigation Status
- Site selection targeting low-cost electricity regions for initial deployment
- Hydrogen co-product revenue provides partial hedge against electricity costs
- Process optimization to reduce electricity consumption per tonne of cement
- Potential for grid services revenue through flexible electrolyzer operation

## Risk Category 5: Market Adoption and Competition

### Risk Description
The cement industry is conservative, risk-averse, and operates on thin margins. Adoption of a radically different cement production technology faces several barriers:

- **Incumbent technology inertia**: The industry has invested billions in existing kiln-based infrastructure and is developing carbon capture as its preferred decarbonization pathway
- **Specification risk**: Architects, engineers, and contractors may be reluctant to specify a novel cement for structural applications without long-term performance data
- **Liability concerns**: Construction defect litigation creates strong disincentives for using novel materials
- **Competing technologies**: Carbon capture, LC3, and alternative binders may capture market share before Sublime achieves commercial scale
- **Cement standards evolution**: While Sublime has achieved ASTM certification, building code acceptance and procurement specification inclusion require ongoing engagement

### Risk Severity: MEDIUM
Market adoption risk is significant but manageable through strategic partnerships (CRH, SCG), ASTM certification, and targeted deployment in premium low-carbon cement markets where customers are actively seeking low-embodied-carbon solutions.

### Mitigation Status
- Strategic partnerships with world's largest cement producers (CRH, SCG) providing market access
- ASTM C595 and C1157 certification enabling specification under existing standards
- Customer demonstration projects with leading concrete producers
- Long-term durability testing program
