## 7.1 Crew Dragon: Operational Manned Spaceflight

### 7.1.1 Vehicle Overview

Crew Dragon (Dragon 2) is the first commercially developed spacecraft to carry astronauts to orbit and the International Space Station. It operates under NASA's Commercial Crew Program (CCP) and has completed 14 crewed missions through mid-2026, including NASA crew rotations, Axiom Space private astronaut missions, and the Polaris Program.

| Parameter | Specification |
|---|---|
| Crew capacity | 4 (NASA); up to 7 (maximum) |
| Launch mass | ~12,000 kg |
| Diameter | 4 m |
| Height | 8.1 m (with trunk) |
| Launch vehicle | Falcon 9 Block 5 |
| Docking | Autonomous (NASA Docking System) |
| Propulsion | 16 Draco thrusters (attitude); 8 SuperDraco (launch abort) |
| Reusability | Refurbishable; 5+ flights per capsule demonstrated |

### 7.1.2 Commercial Crew Contract Value

NASA's Commercial Crew Transportation Capability (CCtCap) contracts with SpaceX total approximately $8.6 billion across the original CCP award and subsequent extensions through 2030, covering both crew rotation missions and Boeing's delayed Starliner backup capacity, which SpaceX has absorbed.

### 7.1.3 Polaris Program

The Polaris Program, privately funded by Jared Isaacman, has conducted two missions on Crew Dragon: Polaris Dawn (September 2024), which achieved the first commercial spacewalk and highest Earth orbit since Apollo (1,400 km apogee), and a subsequent mission. The program serves as a development testbed for Starship EVA suit and life support technologies.

## 7.2 Cargo Dragon (CRS-2)

Cargo Dragon operates under NASA's second Commercial Resupply Services (CRS-2) contract, delivering pressurized and unpressurized cargo to the ISS. Cargo Dragon 2 features automated docking (vs. the original Dragon's berthing), increased payload capacity, and water-landing recovery. SpaceX has completed over 30 cargo resupply missions to the ISS through mid-2026.

## 7.3 Artemis Program and Starship Human Landing System (HLS)

### 7.3.1 HLS Contract

In April 2021, NASA selected SpaceX's Starship Human Landing System for the Artemis III lunar landing mission, with a contract value of approximately **$2.9 billion**. A subsequent Option B award in November 2022 added approximately **$1.15 billion** for a second landing demonstration (Artemis IV), bringing the total HLS contract value to approximately **$4.05 billion**.

### 7.3.2 HLS Architecture

The Starship HLS variant is a modified Starship upper stage optimized for lunar operations:

- No aerodynamic surfaces or heat shield (no atmospheric reentry required)
- Dedicated landing thrusters mounted high on the hull to minimize lunar regolith plume effects
- Solar arrays for extended lunar surface stay (>30 days)
- Elevator for crew and cargo transfer to the lunar surface
- Life support for 4 crew members
- Docking with NASA's Orion spacecraft in Near-Rectilinear Halo Orbit (NRHO)
- Requires in-orbit propellant transfer from tanker Starship variants (~8–12 tanker launches per lunar mission)

### 7.3.3 Artemis Mission Timeline

| Mission | Target | Role | Status |
|---|---|---|---|
| Artemis II | Sep 2026 | Crewed lunar flyby (Orion) | Planned |
| Artemis III | Mid-2027 | Crewed lunar landing (Starship HLS) | In development |
| Artemis IV | 2028 | Second crewed landing | Planned |
| Uncrewed HLS Demo | 2026–2027 | Uncrewed lunar landing test | In development |

### 7.3.4 Critical Path Dependencies

- **Orbital propellant transfer demonstration:** Must be proven at scale before HLS mission certification
- **Tanker launch cadence:** 8–12 launches per lunar mission requires rapid Starship turnaround
- **SLS/Orion integration:** HLS is dependent on NASA's SLS Block 1B schedule, which has experienced significant delays
- **Spacesuit development:** NASA's xEMU and Axiom Space suits must be flight-ready and compatible with HLS

## 7.4 National Security and Defense Programs

### 7.4.1 National Security Space Launch (NSSL)

SpaceX was certified for NSSL Phase 2 in 2020 and has launched numerous classified national security payloads aboard Falcon 9 and Falcon Heavy. The NSSL Phase 3 procurement (2024) includes SpaceX as the primary provider in Lane 1 (commercially procured) and a competitor in Lane 2 (mission-assured), with an estimated contract ceiling of $5.6 billion across both lanes through 2029.

### 7.4.2 Golden Dome Program

The **Golden Dome** contract, awarded by the Pentagon in early 2026, represents a $2 billion program for 600 Starshield-class satellites integrated with missile warning, tracking, and kill-assessment sensors. The program contributes to the DoD's transition from exquisite, high-cost missile warning satellites (SBIRS, Next-Gen OPIR) to a proliferated LEO architecture that is more resilient against anti-satellite threats.

### 7.4.3 Starshield Classified Architecture

Beyond Golden Dome, Starshield encompasses a broader classified portfolio including signals intelligence (SIGINT) payloads, communications relay for special operations forces, and synthetic aperture radar (SAR) imaging. Exact contract values and satellite counts for these programs remain classified, but publicly available budget documents indicate cumulative DoD obligations to SpaceX exceeding $3 billion across all Starshield contracts through FY2026.

## 7.5 Mars and Interplanetary Ambitions

### 7.5.1 Starship as Mars Transportation

Starship remains the central vehicle for SpaceX's stated Mars colonization objective. While no specific funded Mars mission exists as of mid-2026, SpaceX has stated internally that an uncrewed Mars cargo mission is feasible within the 2028 transfer window, contingent on successful orbital refueling demonstration.

### 7.5.2 Life Support and ISRU

SpaceX is developing closed-loop life support systems and in-situ resource utilization (ISRU) technologies — specifically, the Sabatier process for methalox propellant production from Martian atmospheric CO₂ and subsurface water ice — as prerequisites for crewed Mars missions. No external funding has been publicly disclosed for these efforts, which are funded internally.
