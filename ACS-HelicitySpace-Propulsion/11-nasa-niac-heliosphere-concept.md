## 1. NIAC Program Context

The NASA Innovative Advanced Concepts (NIAC) program funds early-stage,
high-payoff technology concepts that could transform future NASA missions.
NIAC awards are competitive and prioritize ideas that are technically credible
but not yet ready for conventional program funding — exactly the position that
magnetized-target fusion propulsion occupies in 2025.

Helicity Space received a NIAC Phase I contract (contract number
80NSSC25K7612, fiscal year 2025) valued at $175,000 for a concept study titled
"Helicity Drive for Rapid Heliosphere Exploration." Phase I NIAC awards are
9-month studies intended to establish the technical feasibility and mission
benefit of a concept; Phase II awards (up to $600,000) fund 2-year follow-on
development.

## 3. Study Objective

The NIAC study addresses the question: "Can a Helicity Drive propulsion system
deliver a scientific payload to the heliosphere — the boundary where the solar
wind meets the interstellar medium — and at what speed and transit time?"

The heliosphere boundary is located at approximately 120 astronomical units (AU)
from the Sun. The Voyager 1 and Voyager 2 spacecraft, launched in 1977, crossed
this boundary in 2012 and 2018 respectively — transit times of roughly 35–40
years using chemical propulsion and gravity assists. The NIAC study proposes a
dedicated heliosphere mission with transit times on the order of 5–10 years,
made possible by the Helicity Drive's high specific impulse and high delta-V
capability.

---

## 4. Science Rationale

A dedicated heliosphere mission would address fundamental questions in
heliophysics and astrophysics:

- What is the three-dimensional structure of the heliopause?
- How does the solar magnetic field interact with the interstellar magnetic
  field at the boundary?
- What is the composition and energy spectrum of galactic cosmic rays before
  they are modulated by the heliosphere?
- How do pickup ions and energetic neutral atoms (ENAs) behave in the
  heliosheath — the region of compressed solar wind just inside the
  heliopause?

The IBEX (Interstellar Boundary Explorer) and Cassini missions have provided
remote ENA imaging of the heliosphere, but in-situ measurements at multiple
locations along the boundary have never been performed. A high-delta-V
propulsion system enables a multi-point survey that would resolve the
heliosphere's structure in unprecedented detail.

## 5. Concept of Operations

The NIAC study considers a spacecraft architecture consisting of:

1. **Launch segment**: launch to low Earth orbit (LEO) or geostationary transfer
   orbit (GTO) on a conventional launch vehicle (Falcon Heavy, Vulcan Centaur,
   or similar heavy-lift class)
2. **Spiral-out phase**: the Helicity Drive performs a low-thrust spiral from
   the initial orbit to Earth escape, requiring several months
3. **Interplanetary cruise phase**: continuous or pulsed thrust accelerates the
   spacecraft to a heliocentric velocity of 10–30 AU/year, far exceeding the
   Voyager spacecraft's ~3.6 AU/year
4. **Heliosphere survey phase**: the spacecraft performs a fly-through of the
   heliopause, heliosheath, and termination shock regions, collecting in-situ
   plasma, magnetic field, and energetic particle data
5. **Interstellar cruise phase** (optional): the spacecraft continues into the
   local interstellar medium, providing the first in-situ measurements of the
   interstellar plasma beyond the heliopause

## 7. Relationship to Terrestrial Fusion

The NIAC study explicitly addresses a common objection to fusion propulsion:
that it must wait for terrestrial fusion energy to achieve breakeven. The
Helicity Drive concept argues that because the propulsion system operates as
an open magnetic nozzle — exhausting plasma for thrust rather than confining
it for energy production — it can produce useful propulsive output at fusion
gains well below unity. The NIAC contract represents NASA's recognition that
this argument is worth investigating at the concept-study level.
