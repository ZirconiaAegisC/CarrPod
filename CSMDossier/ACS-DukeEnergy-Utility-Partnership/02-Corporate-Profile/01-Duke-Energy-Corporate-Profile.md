## 3. GIC RISK ANALYSIS

**Key Factors:**
- Appalachian geology in NC/SC/KY service territory: igneous/metamorphic bedrock, 1,000-3,000 ohm-m soil resistivity — 5-10x GIC amplification vs. sedimentary basins
- Long east-west transmission corridors in Carolinas: 500+ km conductor length maximizes line-integrated GIC voltage
- Nuclear plant substations (Oconee, McGuire, Catawba, Brunswick, Harris, Robinson): these substations serve NRC-licensed facilities where transformer failure has cascading safety implications beyond grid reliability
- Florida service territory: peninsular location enhances coastal effect GIC coupling through conductive seawater

## 4. REGULATORY CONTEXT

**NERC TPL-007-4:** Duke Energy, as a registered transmission planner, is subject to this standard requiring GMD vulnerability assessment. Duke has performed the required thermal impact assessments but has not yet implemented mitigation.

**State PUC Landscape:**
- North Carolina Utilities Commission: progressive on grid modernization; has approved Duke's grid improvement plans in prior rate cases
- Florida Public Service Commission: reliability-focused; receptive to hardening investments with demonstrated storm-hardening benefit (hurricane experience provides precedent)
- Indiana, Ohio, Kentucky, South Carolina: varied regulatory environments where GMD-specific investment may be novel

**Rate-Base Treatment:** Transmission investments that enhance reliability are generally eligible for CWIP (Construction Work in Progress) rate-base treatment, improving utility cash flow during deployment.
