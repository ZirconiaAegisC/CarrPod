## Technology Platform

### Thermal Battery Architecture
The Antora thermal battery is built around three integrated subsystems:

**Subsystem 1: Carbon Block Thermal Storage**
- Solid carbon (graphite) blocks heated to 1,800°C using resistive electrical heating elements
- Carbon selected for its extreme thermal stability, high heat capacity, and low cost
- Blocks operate in an inert (argon) atmosphere to prevent oxidation
- Insulated container maintains thermal efficiency >90% over 24+ hours

**Subsystem 2: Resistive Heating System**
- Electricity from renewable sources (solar, wind) passed through resistive elements
- Converts electrical energy to thermal energy with near-100% efficiency
- Recharge time: 4-6 hours from full discharge
- Designed for daily charge/discharge cycles aligned with solar generation profiles

**Subsystem 3: Thermophotovoltaic (TPV) Power Generation**
- Specialized photovoltaic cells that convert radiant heat (infrared light) directly to electricity
- Cells face the glowing hot carbon blocks, absorbing thermal radiation and generating DC power
- Solid-state conversion: no turbines, no moving parts, no working fluids
- Demonstrated world-record TPV conversion efficiency (>40% of usable spectrum)

### Key Technical Advantages
1. **Ultra-High Temperature**: 1,800°C storage temperature enables high-grade industrial heat that replaces natural gas burners
2. **Dual Output**: Deliver heat directly (for industrial processes) AND convert to electricity (for power applications) from the same storage medium
3. **No Moving Parts**: Solid-state TPV power generation eliminates maintenance-intensive turbines, generators, or heat exchangers
4. **24/7 Carbon-Free**: Stores renewable electricity when abundant, delivers heat and power on demand regardless of time of day
5. **Modular and Scalable**: Containerized system can be deployed at individual industrial facilities, scaled by adding modules

### Competitive Moat
Antora's moat rests on three pillars: (1) proprietary TPV cell technology achieving world-record efficiencies, (2) integrated system design optimized for industrial heat applications where competing long-duration storage technologies (lithium-ion, flow batteries) cannot operate, and (3) first-mover advantage in thermal battery deployment with the first commercial installation in California (2024).

## Market Context

### Industrial Heat: The Overlooked Emissions Source
Industrial process heat accounts for approximately 20% of global CO₂ emissions, more than transportation (16%) and comparable to electricity generation (25%). Most industrial heat is produced by burning fossil fuels (natural gas, coal) directly in boilers, furnaces, and kilns. Decarbonizing industrial heat requires technologies that can deliver high temperatures (often >1,000°C) from carbon-free sources.

### The Thermal Battery Opportunity
Thermal batteries address a market that other clean energy technologies cannot serve:
- **Batteries (lithium-ion)**: Cannot deliver high-temperature heat; designed for electricity at ambient temperature
- **Green hydrogen**: Can produce high-temperature heat when burned, but suffers from poor round-trip efficiency (<40%) and high cost
- **Concentrated solar thermal**: Delivers high temperature but is intermittent and geographically constrained
- **Nuclear**: Produces high-temperature heat but faces cost, timeline, and public acceptance challenges

### Addressable Market
- **US industrial heat demand**: ~10 quadrillion BTU/year (approximately 3,000 TWh-thermal)
- **Temperature range >500°C**: ~40% of industrial heat demand
- **Initial target segments**: Cement, steel, chemicals, glass, ceramics
- **TAM estimate**: $20-50B/year in US; $100-200B/year globally for industrial heat decarbonization
