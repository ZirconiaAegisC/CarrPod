# 05 — Magnesium Byproduct Chemistry & Utilization

## MgO Source in Basalt

Basalt and related mafic rocks contain 5–15 wt% MgO, primarily hosted in:

- **Olivine** (forsterite Mg2SiO4 — fayalite Fe2SiO4 solid solution): MgO 50–57%
- **Pyroxenes** (diopside CaMgSi2O6, enstatite MgSiO3): MgO 18–25%
- **Amphiboles** (hornblende): MgO 10–16%
- **Serpentine** (Mg3Si2O5(OH)4): MgO 40–43% (hydrated)

During the clinkering process at 1,350–1,450°C, these magnesium silicates either:
- Dissolve into the clinker liquid phase (limited MgO solubility: ~2% in alite)
- Precipitate as **periclase (MgO)** crystals
- Remain in kiln dust or are separated during raw meal preparation

## Periclase Byproduct Properties

| Property | Value |
|----------|-------|
| Mineral name | Periclase |
| Formula | MgO |
| Crystal system | Cubic (halite structure) |
| Density | 3.58 g/cm³ |
| Melting point | 2,852°C |
| Solubility in water | 0.0086 g/100 mL (slightly soluble, forms Mg(OH)2) |
| Surface area (post-grinding) | 5–25 m²/g (BET) |

The periclase produced in Brimstone's process is distinct from dead-burned or
fused magnesia in that it is **highly reactive** — a consequence of lower formation
temperature compared to refractory-grade MgO — making it ideal for carbonation.

## Carbonation Kinetics

The rate-limiting step for MgO carbonation is **CO2 diffusion through the carbonate
product layer**. Kinetics follow a shrinking-core model:

    dα/dt = k · f(α) · pCO2 · exp(−Ea/RT)

Where:
- α = extent of carbonation (0–1)
- k = rate constant
- pCO2 = partial pressure of CO2 (~420 ppm ambient, enhanced in engineered cells)
- Ea = activation energy (~40–60 kJ/mol for MgO carbonation)

Under ambient conditions, near-complete carbonation of fine MgO powder (<100 μm)
occurs within 6–12 months. Particle size is the dominant control — grinding to <45 μm
reduces required residence time to 3–6 months.

## Byproduct Utilization Pathways

### 1. Permanent Sequestration (Primary)
Carbonated MgCO3 is landfilled or used as low-grade fill. This is the default pathway
and the basis for the carbon-negative claim.

### 2. Supplementary Cementitious Material (SCM)
MgO can serve as an expansive additive or SCM, though this sacrifices the carbonation
credit since the MgO would be incorporated into concrete rather than carbonated.

### 3. Soil Amendment
Magnesium carbonate is a benign soil additive that can remediate magnesium-deficient
agricultural soils. MgCO3 acts as a slow-release liming agent without the rapid pH
spike of CaO or Ca(OH)2.

### 4. Construction Materials
Carbonated MgO can be used as:
- Lightweight aggregate filler
- Road sub-base material
- Mine backfill
- Erosion control berms

### 5. Magnesium Metal Precursor (Long-term)
MgCO3 can be calcined back to MgO and reduced to magnesium metal via the Pidgeon
process or electrolytic routes, though the energy economics are challenging and
this is not Brimstone's near-term focus.

## Environmental Safety

MgO and MgCO3 are non-toxic, non-hazardous materials. MgO is classified as a mild
irritant to skin and eyes (OSHA hazard category 2); MgCO3 is Generally Recognized
as Safe (GRAS) by the FDA. Handling protocols are standard for mineral processing
facilities — dust suppression, PPE, and contained conveyance.

## Mass Balance Summary (per metric ton of clinker)

```
INPUT:
  Basalt feedstock:                ~7,000 kg
    - MgO content (10%):           ~700 kg

PROCESS:
  MgO reporting to clinker:        ~100–140 kg (dissolved or as periclase)
  MgO in kiln dust (recycled):     ~50–100 kg
  MgO in separated byproduct:      ~460–550 kg

CARBONATION:
  Stoichiometric CO2 capacity:     ~500–600 kg CO2
  Achieved carbonation (80%):      ~400–480 kg CO2 sequestered
```
