## Material Cost Trajectory

The economics of carbon fiber composite adoption in volume manufacturing are dominated by the cost of the raw material inputs. Carbon fiber prices have declined substantially over the past two decades — from approximately $30-40 per kilogram in the early 2000s to $15-25 per kilogram for industrial-grade 50K tow today — but remain an order of magnitude higher than aluminum ($2-3/kg) and two orders of magnitude higher than steel ($0.50-1.00/kg).

### Carbon Fiber Capacity Expansion

The global carbon fiber industry is investing in significant capacity expansion driven by demand from wind energy (turbine blades), pressure vessels (hydrogen storage), and automotive lightweighting. Major producers including Toray, Teijin, SGL, and Mitsubishi Chemical have announced capacity additions totaling over 100,000 metric tons of new nameplate capacity by 2028. This capacity expansion, combined with process improvements in precursor (polyacrylonitrile) production and oxidation/carbonization furnace technology, is expected to drive continued price reductions.

A sustained decline in carbon fiber prices to $10-12 per kilogram would significantly improve the economics of Additive Molding for automotive and consumer applications. At current fiber prices, the material cost of a carbon/PEEK component is typically 4-6 times the material cost of an aluminum equivalent. At $10/kg fiber prices, this ratio would narrow to 2-3 times, making the weight reduction value proposition more compelling for mid-market automotive applications.

### Thermoplastic Matrix Economics

The thermoplastic matrix materials used by Arris — PEEK, PEKK, and PAEK — are higher-cost than the commodity engineering thermoplastics (polypropylene, nylon 6/6) used in automotive interior and under-hood applications. PEEK resin prices range from $80-120 per kilogram, reflecting the material's high-temperature capability, chemical resistance, and the relatively small scale of PEEK production relative to commodity thermoplastics.

The cost of the matrix material is less significant than the fiber cost in determining total material cost because the fiber volume fraction is 55-65%, meaning resin constitutes 35-45% of the part by weight. At $100/kg PEEK and $20/kg carbon fiber, the blended material cost is approximately $50-60 per kilogram — dominated by fiber cost but with a meaningful resin contribution.

### Recycling and Circular Economy

The recyclability of thermoplastic composites provides a potential economic offset to high material cost. Unlike thermoset composites, which degrade during recycling (the cross-linked epoxy cannot be re-melted, so the best available recycling processes recover carbon fiber but destroy the matrix), thermoplastic composites can theoretically be re-ground and re-processed into new components.

The practical economics of composite recycling remain uncertain. Recovered carbon fiber has shorter fiber length than virgin fiber and is downgraded from continuous to short-fiber applications, reducing its value. The cost of collection, sorting, size reduction, and re-compounding must be subtracted from the recovered material value. However, as regulatory pressure on end-of-life vehicle recyclability increases — particularly in the European Union — the inherent recyclability of thermoplastic composites may become a competitive advantage distinct from weight reduction.

## Automotive Qualification Requirements

The automotive industry imposes qualification requirements that are different in character from aerospace but no less demanding. Automotive qualification focuses on production-part approval process (PPAP), which requires the supplier to demonstrate that its manufacturing process is capable of consistently producing parts within specification at the quoted production rate.

### Performance Validation

Automotive structural components must survive a battery of validation tests designed to simulate the abuse a vehicle experiences over its service life: vibration, thermal cycling (-40°C to +85°C for interior components, wider ranges for under-hood), chemical exposure (fuels, oils, brake fluid, road salt), and mechanical overload. Composite components introduce failure modes — delamination, fiber-matrix debonding — that are not present in metal components and that require specific test methods to characterize.

### Production Rate Capability

A critical element of automotive qualification is the run-at-rate demonstration: the supplier must operate its production process at the quoted cycle time for an extended period (typically 300 consecutive parts) and demonstrate that all parts meet specification. This is a high bar for a process like Additive Molding that is still early in its production deployment, but it is non-negotiable for automotive Tier 1 supply.

The requirement for run-at-rate capability drives the capital investment timing for Arris. The company must have production cells qualified and operating at production-representative rates before it can win production contracts, but it cannot justify the capital expenditure for multiple production cells without production contracts. This chicken-and-egg problem is typically resolved through a combination of customer-funded development programs, advance purchase commitments, and risk-sharing arrangements.

## Consumer Durables and Sporting Goods

Between the high-value aerospace market and the high-volume automotive market lies a middle ground of consumer durable goods where Additive Molding's economics may be most immediately compelling. Sporting goods — bicycle frames and components, golf club shafts, tennis rackets, hockey sticks — have been early adopters of carbon fiber composites and represent a market where weight reduction commands a consumer price premium.

The bicycle industry is a particularly relevant case study. High-end bicycle frames manufactured from carbon fiber composites sell for $2,000-5,000 (frame only) and are produced in volumes of 1,000-10,000 units annually per model — volumes well-aligned with the throughput of a single Additive Molding cell. The industry is characterized by rapid model cycles (annual updates), high design content, and a customer base willing to pay for performance. These attributes map well to a technology that offers design flexibility and structural optimization.

Lugged bicycle construction — where individual tubes are joined by external lugs — represents a component architecture that maps naturally to Additive Molding: the lugs are geometrically complex, structural components that would benefit from fiber path optimization and would be expensive to produce by any method other than machining or Additive Molding.

Other sporting goods categories where composite adoption has been limited by manufacturing cost — snowboard bindings, protective equipment, prosthetic limbs and orthotics — represent incremental addressable markets where the technology could establish a production track record while aerospace and automotive qualifications progress.
