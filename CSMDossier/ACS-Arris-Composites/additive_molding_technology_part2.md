## Process Architecture: One-Step Manufacturing

The defining characteristic of Arris Composites' Additive Molding platform is the compression of an entire manufacturing workflow into a single, automated process step. In conventional composites manufacturing, producing a structural carbon fiber component follows a sequential chain: material cutting, ply stacking, debulking, bagging, autoclave cure, demolding, and finishing. Each step requires manual intervention, independent quality checks, and introduces the potential for process drift.

Additive Molding eliminates the distinction between fiber placement, consolidation, and final forming. The thermoplastic matrix serves dual purpose: it acts as the binder during additive fiber deposition and as the final structural matrix after compression molding. This dual functionality removes the need for a separate infusion or wet-out step that characterizes thermoset composite processing.

## Software-Driven Fiber Path Optimization

A critical differentiator in the Arris technology stack is the proprietary software layer that determines fiber orientation. The software ingests a CAD model of the target part along with the expected mechanical loading conditions. It then computes optimal fiber trajectories — the three-dimensional paths along which continuous carbon fiber tows should be deposited to maximize stiffness and strength in the directions that matter.

This computational approach replaces the heuristic methods that historically governed composite laminate design. Traditional quasi-isotropic layup schedules — 0°/90°/±45° orientations repeated symmetrically — represent a compromise: they provide adequate strength in all directions but optimal strength in none. The Arris software instead produces a true orthotropic optimization, placing fiber where stress analysis indicates it will carry load.

The result is a part that uses less material while achieving higher structural performance. For aerospace brackets and structural fittings, this translates to components that are not only lighter than their metal counterparts but also stiffer and stronger in the specific loading directions that govern certification.

## Thermoplastic Advantages Over Thermoset Systems

The choice of thermoplastic matrix materials over traditional epoxy thermosets is fundamental to the Additive Molding value proposition. Thermoplastics offer several advantages that collectively enable the process economics:

**Cycle Time.** Thermoplastic consolidation is a physical process: heat, press, cool, eject. Total cycle times range from 90 seconds to 5 minutes depending on part thickness. Thermoset cure cycles, by contrast, are chemical processes that typically require 2-6 hours in an autoclave at elevated temperature and pressure.

**Shelf Life.** Thermoset prepreg materials have limited out-time and require refrigerated storage and transport. Thermoplastic prepreg is indefinitely stable at room temperature. This eliminates cold-chain logistics costs and the risk of material expiration.

**Recyclability.** Thermoplastic matrices can be re-melted and reprocessed. While practical recycling pathways for carbon fiber composites remain under development, the inherent recyclability of thermoplastics provides a sustainability narrative that aligns with customer ESG requirements.

**Toughness.** Thermoplastics exhibit superior impact resistance and damage tolerance compared to brittle epoxy systems. This property is particularly valuable for aerospace applications where foreign object damage and tool-drop scenarios are design requirements.

**Joining.** Thermoplastic components can be welded — a capability not available with thermoset composites. Ultrasonic, induction, and resistance welding techniques enable assembly of complex structures from individually molded sub-components without the weight penalty of mechanical fasteners.

## Material Portfolio

Beyond the baseline carbon fiber/PEEK system, Arris has developed formulations spanning multiple reinforcement and matrix combinations. The material portfolio addresses different points on the cost-performance curve:

- **Carbon fiber / PEEK:** Maximum performance for aerospace primary structure and defense applications. Operating temperature up to 250°C continuous. Highest material cost.
- **Carbon fiber / PAEK:** Intermediate performance, lower cost than PEEK. Suitable for secondary aerospace structure and high-end automotive.
- **Glass fiber / engineering nylon:** Consumer electronics and lower-cost industrial applications where carbon fiber's stiffness premium is not required.
- **Hybrid laminates:** Combining carbon and glass fiber plies within a single part to optimize cost while maintaining stiffness targets in critical regions.

The breadth of the material portfolio suggests that Arris intends to serve a range of price points rather than competing exclusively at the high end of the composites market.

## Quality Assurance Integration

The Additive Molding process incorporates in-line quality monitoring as a native feature of the manufacturing cell. Because the fiber placement is software-controlled, every tow trajectory is recorded digitally. This generates a complete digital twin of each manufactured part — a record of exactly where every fiber strand was placed, at what tension, and under what thermal conditions.

This digital pedigree addresses a pain point in composites manufacturing: the difficulty of non-destructive inspection. Traditional laminate inspection relies on ultrasonic C-scan or X-ray CT, which are slow, expensive, and not feasible for 100% inspection in production volumes. The digital process record provides a statistical basis for quality assurance that is more compatible with automotive and consumer electronics production rates.

## Manufacturing Cell Economics

Each Additive Molding cell is a self-contained production unit comprising fiber creels, deposition end-effectors, a compression press, and control electronics. The company's disclosed target is for a single cell to produce hundreds of thousands of parts per year — throughput comparable to plastic injection molding but with the mechanical properties of aerospace-grade structural composites.

The capital cost per cell has not been publicly disclosed, but the economic model hinges on the ratio of cell throughput to capital expenditure. If a cell can produce at rates approaching injection molding — thousands of parts per day — the amortized capital cost per part becomes competitive with die-cast metal alternatives, while offering weight savings that injection-molded short-fiber composites cannot match.
