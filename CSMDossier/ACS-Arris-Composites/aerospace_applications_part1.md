## Strategic Significance of Aerospace Market Access

Aerospace represents the most demanding — and potentially the most lucrative — market for Arris Composites' Additive Molding technology. The industry's relentless pursuit of weight reduction, its willingness to pay a premium for certified performance, and its long product lifecycles create an environment where Arris's technology advantages align naturally with customer requirements.

The strategic importance of aerospace to Arris is underscored by the presence of Boeing HorizonX — the aerospace giant's venture capital arm — among the company's investors. Corporate venture investment by an aerospace prime signals both validation of the technology's relevance to aerospace applications and the establishment of a relationship that could facilitate qualification and adoption within Boeing's supply chain.

## The Aerospace Weight Imperative

Every kilogram removed from an aircraft structure translates to measurable fuel savings over the operating life of the airframe. For a narrow-body aircraft flying 3,000 flight hours annually over a 25-year service life, each kilogram of weight reduction saves approximately 3,000 to 4,000 kilograms of jet fuel. At prevailing fuel prices, this represents $2,000 to $4,000 in lifetime fuel cost savings per kilogram of weight reduction.

This arithmetic creates an unusually high willingness-to-pay for weight-saving technologies in aerospace relative to other industries. An aerospace bracket that replaces a 1-kilogram aluminum component with a 0.5-kilogram composite equivalent might command a price premium of $500 to $1,000 — multiples of the material and manufacturing cost — because the operator's fuel savings over the life of the aircraft far exceed the incremental component cost.

The same weight-to-value calculus applies in defense aerospace, where weight savings translate to increased payload, range, or loiter time rather than fuel cost. For an unmanned aerial vehicle, reducing structural weight by 20% can increase endurance by a comparable percentage, directly enhancing mission capability.

## Types of Aerospace Components Addressable by Additive Molding

The aerospace industry contains thousands of individual part numbers that fit the profile for Additive Molding: structural components currently manufactured from machined metal that have complex geometries, moderate production volumes (hundreds to tens of thousands annually), and weight reduction value that justifies the composite material cost premium.

### Brackets and Structural Fittings

Brackets represent the most immediate addressable market. A single commercial aircraft contains between 5,000 and 10,000 brackets — components that connect systems (hydraulic lines, electrical harnesses, environmental control ducts) to airframe structure. The vast majority of these brackets are machined from aluminum, with typical weights ranging from 50 grams to 2 kilograms.

The bracket market is attractive for several reasons. Brackets are geometrically complex, making them poor candidates for automated fiber placement but good candidates for Additive Molding. They are produced in quantities that are large enough to justify tooling investment but small enough that machining remains the dominant process (injection molding tooling amortization requires higher volumes). And they are typically secondary structure — failure of a single bracket does not compromise flight safety — which simplifies the certification pathway relative to primary structure.

### Drone and Unmanned Aerial Vehicle Structures

The unmanned aerial vehicle market represents a particularly promising application for Additive Molding. UAV airframes are produced in quantities (hundreds to thousands annually) that align with the throughput of a single Additive Molding cell. Weight is at a premium because it directly determines endurance. And the regulatory environment for UAVs is less prescriptive than for crewed aircraft, reducing the certification burden.

Arris has disclosed work with drone manufacturers, though specific customers have not been publicly identified. The US Army partnership for lightweighting soldier systems suggests that defense UAV applications are within the company's addressable scope.

### Interior Components

Aircraft interior components — seat structures, galley components, overhead bin fittings — represent a market segment where composite adoption is already established but where Arris's technology could displace incumbent processes. Interior components have less stringent certification requirements than airframe structure but still benefit from weight reduction and must meet flammability, smoke, and toxicity standards.

The volumes for interior components are higher than for structural brackets — tens of thousands of units annually per aircraft program — making them attractive from a manufacturing throughput perspective. However, the cost sensitivity of interior components is also higher, as airlines purchasing interiors are price-conscious buyers operating on thin margins.

## Boeing HorizonX: The Strategic Investor Relationship

Boeing's investment in Arris through its HorizonX Ventures arm carries significance beyond the capital provided. Boeing operates one of the world's most rigorous supplier qualification systems, with detailed requirements for material traceability, process control, and quality assurance. Having a Boeing entity as an investor provides Arris with a channel to understand and address these qualification requirements during product development rather than after the fact.

The Boeing relationship also provides market intelligence. Boeing's product development teams can identify specific part families — across the 737, 787, and future aircraft programs — where composite substitution would deliver the greatest weight savings per unit of engineering effort. This directed feedback loop accelerates the path from technology demonstration to production adoption.

However, the Boeing relationship also introduces complexity. Airbus, Boeing's primary competitor, may be reluctant to qualify a supplier in which its rival holds an equity stake. Arris will need to manage this competitive dynamic carefully, potentially through firewall arrangements that separate customer-facing teams and protect proprietary customer data.
