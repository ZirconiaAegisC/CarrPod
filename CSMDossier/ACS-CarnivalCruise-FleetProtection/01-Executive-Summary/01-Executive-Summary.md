## 1. The Corporate Context

Carnival Corporation & plc stands as the largest global cruise operator, commanding a fleet of 94 vessels across 272,480 lower berths spanning eight distinguished brands: Carnival Cruise Line, Princess Cruises, Holland America Line, Seabourn, AIDA Cruises, Costa Cruises, P&O Cruises, and Cunard. In fiscal year 2025, the corporation achieved record revenues of $26.6 billion with operating income of $4.5 billion — a 25% year-over-year increase — and delivered its highest adjusted return on invested capital in nineteen years. The corporation has reduced total debt by more than $10 billion from its pandemic-era peak. Seven new vessels are on order through 2033 as the corporation continues measured, disciplined capacity growth.

In a strategic milestone during 2025, Carnival extended the depreciable useful lives of its vessels from 30 years to 35 years, reflecting confidence in enhanced asset management practices, rigorous maintenance protocols, and improved construction standards of modern cruise ships. This accounting adjustment — which reduced annual depreciation expense by approximately $300 million — signals a fundamental shift in how the corporation views vessel lifespan. Ships are living longer. The operational mandate is to ensure they are capable of doing so.

Simultaneously, Carnival is executing the unification of its dual-listed company structure into a single NYSE-listed entity, Carnival Corporation Ltd., with reincorporation to Bermuda expected to complete in the second quarter of 2026. This simplification reduces administrative complexity and positions the enterprise for greater capital allocation flexibility.

The macro environment makes this moment uniquely opportune. The global cruise refurbishment and maintenance market is projected to reach $9 billion by 2033, driven by aging fleets worldwide and increasing regulatory pressure on vessel efficiency. The International Maritime Organization's Carbon Intensity Indicator framework entered enforcement in 2024, imposing progressively tightening emissions thresholds that will penalize inefficient vessels. The European Union's Emissions Trading System now includes maritime shipping. These regulatory currents converge with Carnival's own strategic objectives of decarbonization, cost leadership, and fleet modernization.

## 2. The Fuel Imperative

Fuel represents Carnival's second-largest operating expense after payroll and benefits. In fiscal year 2025, fuel expenditures totaled approximately $1.8 billion, with the fleet consuming 2.8 million metric tons of marine fuel across its global operations. This is not merely a cost center; it is the single largest controllable variable in the corporation's operating expense framework.

Carnival has already achieved measurable progress on fuel efficiency. In the first half of fiscal year 2026, fuel consumption per available lower berth day improved by 5.6% year-over-year, driven by itinerary optimization, speed management, and hull maintenance improvements. However, the magnitude of the opportunity dwarfs what has been captured to date.

Every 1% improvement in fleet-wide fuel efficiency translates to approximately $18 million in annual savings — directly to the bottom line. A 5% improvement delivers $90 million. A 10% improvement yields $180 million. If the corporation could achieve the upper range of what advanced hull technologies demonstrably deliver — a 10% to 20% improvement in propulsive efficiency — the annual savings range from $180 million to $360 million. Over a ten-year horizon at conservative discount rates, this represents $1.5 to $3.0 billion in net present value.

The relationship between hull condition and fuel consumption is governed by well-established hydrodynamic principles. A vessel operating with a fouled hull experiences exponential increases in frictional resistance. Biofilm accumulation of 1 millimeter can increase fuel consumption by 9% to 12%. Moderate calcareous fouling — barnacles, tubeworms — can increase resistance by 30% to 50%. Severe fouling can add 80% or more to the propulsive power required to maintain design speed. These are not theoretical figures; they are documented across thousands of vessel operational records, including within Carnival's own fleet.

The corollary: a clean, smooth, well-coated hull is the single most powerful lever for controlling fuel expense across the enterprise.

Beyond direct fuel costs, hull condition influences a constellation of operational variables. Fouled hulls increase voyage duration for fixed itineraries or require itinerary compression to maintain schedules, eroding guest satisfaction. They increase main engine load factors, accelerating wear on propulsion machinery and driving higher maintenance expenditure. They degrade maneuverability, creating safety margin concerns during close-quarters port operations. They increase underwater radiated noise, a growing regulatory concern with implications for marine mammal protection compliance. And they impose a direct carbon cost, measured in additional tonnes of CO2 emitted per nautical mile — a liability under both the EU Emissions Trading System and the IMO's Carbon Intensity Indicator framework. Hull optimization is, in economic terms, the gift that compounds: every dollar saved in fuel cascades into savings in engine maintenance, emissions compliance, and voyage reliability.

## 3. Proven Technology: The AQUATERRAS Case Study

Carnival Corporation does not need to speculate about the efficacy of advanced hull coatings. The corporation already possesses compelling internal evidence. Sixteen or more vessels across the Carnival fleet have been coated with Nippon Paint Marine's AQUATERRAS biocide-free hull coating system. The results are extraordinary.

The case of AIDAdiva is emblematic and, for the purposes of this dossier, dispositive. AIDAdiva was coated with AQUATERRAS in 2022 during a scheduled dry dock period. Over the subsequent three years of operation — 2022 through mid-2025 — the vessel operated without a single underwater hull cleaning event. When inspected at its next dry dock, the hull showed zero fouling. Zero. No biofilm. No slime. No macrofouling. A pristine surface after three years in warm tropical and temperate waters, without intervention.

During the pandemic-era operational pause of 2020-2021, Carnival laid up dozens of vessels in warm-water anchorages — conditions notorious for rapid and aggressive marine growth. The contrast between AQUATERRAS-coated vessels and those coated with traditional biocidal antifouling systems was stark and unambiguous. Ships with biocidal coatings fouled severely during layup, accumulating heavy calcareous growth that required extensive, costly cleanings before return to service. Ships with AQUATERRAS remained clean. This real-world, fleet-scale natural experiment validated years of laboratory testing and manufacturer claims in the most demanding conditions imaginable.

AQUATERRAS is not the only advanced coating in Carnival's existing inventory. Vessels across the fleet employ silicone-based foul-release coatings, silyl acrylate self-polishing copolymer systems, and various hybrid formulations from leading manufacturers including Hempel, PPG, Jotun, and International Paint. The issue is not absence of technology. The issue is absence of a unified strategy.

The current state of hull coating across Carnival's 94-ship fleet can be characterized as follows: coating selection is made on a per-vessel, per-dry-dock basis by individual brand technical teams, with limited cross-brand coordination or centralized lifecycle optimization. Some ships receive state-of-the-art coatings. Others receive competent but legacy antifouling formulations. The result is a heterogeneous fleet — some vessels achieving exceptional hull performance, others operating with avoidable fuel penalties at any given moment.

## 4. The Technology Landscape

The global marine coatings industry has undergone a transformation in the past decade that Carnival has not yet fully leveraged at fleet scale. The following technologies represent the state of the art and are directly applicable to the Carnival fleet.

**Silicone-Based Foul-Release Coatings.** These coatings do not kill marine organisms through biocide release. Instead, they create a low-surface-energy, non-stick surface to which organisms cannot firmly adhere. At sufficient vessel speed, water flow across the hull physically removes any weakly attached fouling. The absence of biocides eliminates environmental discharge concerns and enables dramatically longer service intervals. PPG Sigmaglide 2390, with its patented electrostatic application process, has demonstrated up to 35% reduction in greenhouse gas emissions through combined fuel savings and elimination of biocide leaching. Hempel's Hempaguard Ultima has demonstrated fuel savings of up to 21% with 160 fouling-free idle days. Jotun's SeaQuantum X200 and International Paint's Intersleek 1100SR represent mature alternatives in this category.

**Graphene-Enhanced Coatings.** GIT Coatings of Canada has pioneered graphene-based foul-release systems that achieve sub-40-micron average hull roughness — a surface profile smoother than any conventional coating technology. The GIT XGIT-FORCE system has demonstrated a 6% out-of-dock power gain in independent vessel trials, measured against baseline performance. A 38-micron hull roughness compares to an industry norm of 75 to 90 microns for conventional antifouling and 55 to 65 microns for premium silicone systems. The fuel consumption implications of this surface smoothness are substantial and compounding over the service life of the coating.

**Proactive In-Water Hull Management.** Complementing advanced coatings, in-water hull grooming technologies — autonomous or remotely operated vehicles equipped with soft brushes or water jets — can maintain coating surfaces at or near out-of-dock smoothness throughout the service period. When combined with fouling-release coatings, this approach allows vessels to operate for five years or more between dry dockings without meaningful hull fouling penalties. Hull performance monitoring through real-time sensor networks measuring shaft power, speed-through-water, and environmental conditions provides the data foundation for optimal grooming schedules and dry dock planning. These are not aspirational technologies; they are commercially deployed today.

**Ultrasonic Antifouling Systems.** Transducer arrays mounted on the interior of the hull plate generate high-frequency acoustic vibrations that create a microscopic barrier at the hull-water interface, preventing the initial settlement of fouling organisms. These systems complement coating technology by providing a secondary layer of protection and are particularly effective in niche areas — sea chests, bow thrusters, stabilizer housings — where traditional coating coverage can be inconsistent.

## 5. The Dry Dock Calculus

Fiscal year 2026 is projected to be the busiest dry dock year in Carnival Corporation's history, with more than 2,000 aggregate dry dock days scheduled across the fleet. Each dry dock represents a multi-million-dollar capital event encompassing regulatory surveys, steel work, mechanical overhauls, hotel refurbishment, and hull treatment. The single most consequential decision made at each dry dock, from a fuel economics perspective, is the selection of hull coating system.

A cruise vessel operating an average of 330 days per year, consuming approximately 80 to 120 metric tons of marine fuel per day (depending on vessel class, itinerary, and operating speed), will burn roughly 26,000 to 40,000 metric tons of fuel annually. At a blended fuel cost of $600 to $700 per metric ton, annual fuel expenditure per vessel ranges from $16 million to $28 million. A 10% fuel efficiency differential attributable to hull condition represents $1.6 million to $2.8 million per vessel per year, purely from the coating decision.

Over a five-year dry dock interval — the industry standard for major hull coating renewal — the cumulative per-vessel fuel cost differential between a premium coating and a standard system ranges from $8 million to $14 million. Across a fleet of 94 vessels phased through dry dock on a rolling 5-year cycle, the aggregate annual fuel savings opportunity from optimal coating decisions approaches $150 million to $260 million.

The extended 35-year useful life further amplifies these economics. A vessel that might have operated for 30 years under legacy assumptions — undergoing six major dry dock cycles — will now operate for 35 years through seven cycles. Each additional cycle increases the total hull coating investment required over the vessel's life, but each cycle also represents an additional opportunity to capture fuel savings through optimal coating selection. The net present value of good coating decisions rises, not falls, with extended vessel life.

## 6. The Opportunity: A Framework for Fleet-Wide Excellence

The central thesis of this dossier is straightforward and evidence-supported: Carnival Corporation should move from its current heterogeneous, brand-level, per-dry-dock approach to hull management toward a systematic, fleet-wide, lifecycle-optimized framework. The components of this framework are:

**Component 1 — Fleet-Wide Hull Condition Assessment.** A comprehensive baseline survey of the current hull coating status, condition, and performance of all 94 vessels. This assessment includes dry film thickness measurements, surface roughness profiles, diver inspection findings, shaft power versus speed-through-water curves, and fuel consumption trends normalized for itinerary and environmental conditions. The objective is a data-rich, objectively scored hull health index for every vessel in the fleet, updated on a continuous cycle.

**Component 2 — Advanced Coating Standardization.** Based on vessel class, operational profile, itinerary characteristics, and remaining service life, a structured coating selection matrix that identifies the optimal coating system for each vessel at each dry dock event. This is not a one-size-fits-all specification. Cold-water Alaska itineraries impose different requirements than year-round Caribbean deployments. Vessels with fifteen years of remaining service life warrant different economic calculus than those with twenty-five. But the selection process should be governed by a unified methodology — a common analytical framework applied consistently across brands — rather than the current decentralized decision-making model.

**Component 3 — Proactive Hull Management Program.** An operational program combining scheduled in-water hull grooming by autonomous robotic systems, continuous hull performance monitoring, and predictive intervention scheduling. The goal is to maintain hull condition at or near out-of-dock performance levels for the entire duration of the five-year dry dock interval, eliminating the fuel penalty ramp that characterizes the current operating paradigm. This component is enabled by Component 2: grooming is most effective on premium foul-release coatings and least effective on degrading biocidal systems.

**Component 4 — Integrated Dry Dock Optimization.** Incorporating hull coating decisions into the broader dry dock planning process with full economic modeling of total cost of ownership — not merely the upfront coating material and application cost, but the total five-year fuel cost, cleaning cost, downtime cost, and residual value impact. Premium coatings typically carry a 20% to 40% material cost premium over standard systems, but their total cost of ownership advantage — when fuel savings, reduced cleaning, and extended service intervals are factored — is typically 3x to 7x the upfront premium. The integrated model makes this trade-off explicit and decision-enabling.

**Component 5 — Lifecycle Economics Modeling.** A proprietary analytical framework, built on advanced performance modeling methodologies, that calculates the net present value of hull management decisions across individual vessel lifecycles and across the aggregated fleet. This model serves as the economic backbone of the program, providing the quantitative justification for investment, the tracking mechanism for returns, and the communications platform for engaging brand leadership, the Board of Directors, and external stakeholders including insurers, financiers, and regulatory agencies.

## 7. Financial Projections

The economic case for fleet-wide hull optimization rests on conservative assumptions grounded in Carnival's own operating data and publicly documented coating performance benchmarks.

**Baseline Assumptions:**
- Fleet-wide annual fuel expenditure: $1.8 billion (FY2025 actual)
- Achievable fuel savings range: 6% (conservative) to 20% (optimistic)
- Fleet-wide coating standardization phased over two five-year dry dock cycles (10 years)
- Annual in-water hull management program cost: $15 million to $25 million (fleet-wide)
- Premium coating cost premium over standard systems: $150,000 to $400,000 per vessel per dry dock
- Dry dock interval: 5 years
- Fleet size: 94 vessels (2026), increasing to approximately 100 by 2033

**Conservative Scenario (6% fuel savings):**
- Annual fuel savings at full deployment: $108 million
- Annual program cost (grooming + coating premium amortization): $28 million
- Net annual benefit: $80 million
- Cumulative 10-year net benefit: $650 million (discounted)

**Mid-Range Scenario (12% fuel savings):**
- Annual fuel savings at full deployment: $216 million
- Annual program cost: $30 million
- Net annual benefit: $186 million
- Cumulative 10-year net benefit: $1.5 billion (discounted)

**Optimistic Scenario (20% fuel savings):**
- Annual fuel savings at full deployment: $360 million
- Annual program cost: $32 million
- Net annual benefit: $328 million
- Cumulative 10-year net benefit: $2.7 billion (discounted)

These projections are intentionally conservative in their phasing. The full savings do not materialize in year one. Deployment occurs over approximately ten years, divided into two five-year dry dock cycles. During the first cycle, vessels undergoing scheduled dry docks receive premium coatings according to the coating selection matrix. Annual savings compound as an increasing proportion of the fleet operates with optimized hull surfaces. By year six, approximately 60% of the fleet is coated to the new standard. By year ten, approximately 95% coverage is achieved, excluding vessels approaching retirement. The financial projections above represent steady-state savings at full deployment, not year-one expectations.

Cash flow modeling indicates the program reaches cumulative breakeven — total investment recovered through fuel savings — in year three from program initiation, assuming the mid-range scenario. From year four onward, the program is net cash-generating on a cumulative basis. The internal rate of return on the program investment, across all three scenarios, exceeds Carnival's published weighted-average cost of capital by a factor of three to five times. This is not a marginal investment proposition with ambiguous payback. It is a high-certainty, high-return capital deployment opportunity with a rapid payback profile and returns that compound for the remaining useful life of each coated vessel.

These projections exclude secondary benefits: reduced underwater cleaning expenditure, extended dry dock intervals (where coating performance permits), lower carbon credit purchase obligations under EU ETS and future IMO market-based measures, reduced CO2 emissions against CII targets, enhanced residual vessel values, and reputational value from demonstrable environmental leadership. Including these second-order effects, the total economic benefit would exceed the fuel-savings-only projections by an estimated 20% to 30%.

## 8. Risk Factors and Mitigation

Any program of this magnitude must be evaluated against downside scenarios.

**Application Risk.** Premium coatings, particularly silicone foul-release systems, require precise surface preparation, application conditions, and curing management. A poorly applied premium coating performs worse than a well-applied standard system. Mitigation: Centralize coating application oversight through a dedicated technical team; establish application quality standards; pre-qualify shipyard application teams; implement post-application inspection protocols.

**Performance Risk.** No coating performs identically across all vessel classes, itineraries, and operating profiles. A coating that excels on a year-round Caribbean vessel may underperform on a vessel alternating between Alaska and the South Pacific. Mitigation: The coating selection matrix (Component 2) accounts for operational profile; performance is tracked through hull condition monitoring; underperforming installations are identified early and remediated.

**Vendor Concentration Risk.** Dependence on a single coating manufacturer creates supply chain vulnerability. Mitigation: Qualify at least three manufacturers per coating category; maintain competitive tender processes; secure multi-year pricing agreements with volume commitment clauses.

**Operational Disruption Risk.** Accelerated coating deployment could create dry dock scheduling conflicts. Mitigation: Phase deployment across two five-year cycles; integrate with existing dry dock schedules; avoid accelerated timelines that compromise existing maintenance programs.

**Capital Allocation Risk.** The program requires upfront investment with benefits accruing over years. Mitigation: The lifecycle economics model (Component 5) provides rigorous cost-benefit analysis; program funding is structured through operating expenditure for grooming and capital expenditure for dry dock coating applications; returns are tracked and reported quarterly to maintain executive confidence.

## 9. Competitive and Regulatory Landscape

Carnival's principal competitors — Royal Caribbean Group, Norwegian Cruise Line Holdings, and MSC Cruises — are all investing in hull optimization programs, albeit with varying degrees of commitment. Royal Caribbean has publicly reported fuel efficiency improvements from silicone coatings on its Oasis-class vessels. Norwegian has deployed hull performance monitoring across its fleet. MSC's newbuild program specifies premium coatings as standard. Carnival, by virtue of its scale — with a fleet more than twice the size of its nearest competitor — has the largest absolute opportunity and the greatest ability to amortize program costs across its asset base. First-mover disadvantage (others are already moving) is offset by scale advantage.

On the regulatory side, the IMO's Carbon Intensity Indicator framework requires annual reporting and progressive improvement. Vessels rated D for three consecutive years or rated E for one year must submit corrective action plans. By 2030, the CII threshold will require a 40% reduction in carbon intensity versus 2008 baselines — a target that favors larger, more efficient vessels but penalizes older tonnage. The EU Emissions Trading System, currently in its maritime phase-in, imposes a direct cost on CO2 emissions that will rise as free allowances are phased out. Hull optimization is among the most cost-effective regulatory compliance measures available — its CO2 abatement cost, at the fuel prices Carnival pays, is negative: the fuel savings alone repay the investment, with emissions reduction as a pure co-benefit.

## 10. Call to Action

This dossier recommends that Carnival Corporation establish a formal, resourced, and chartered **Fleet Hull Excellence Program** — a corporate-level initiative that elevates hull management from an ad-hoc, brand-level, per-dry-dock operational decision to a core strategic discipline.

Specifically, the program should:

1. Commission an immediate fleet-wide hull condition baseline assessment (Component 1), to be completed within twelve months.
2. Develop and ratify the coating selection matrix (Component 2) through a cross-functional working group including representation from each brand's technical operations leadership.
3. Initiate a pilot proactive hull management program (Component 3) on six to ten vessels spanning multiple brands and itineraries, with rigorous performance monitoring and reporting.
4. Embed hull lifecycle economics into the dry dock planning process (Component 4) effective with the 2027 dry dock schedule.
5. Construct and validate the lifecycle economics model (Component 5) as the program's quantitative backbone and primary executive communication tool.

The resources required for these initial actions are modest relative to the opportunity — an estimated $3 million to $5 million in program establishment costs, recoverable within the first year of fuel savings from the pilot program alone. The full fleet-wide deployment represents a material but financeable capital commitment whose internal rate of return, on conservative assumptions, exceeds 40%.

Carnival Corporation has demonstrated, through its financial recovery, its balance sheet discipline, its operational execution, and its fleet lifecycle extension to 35 years, that it is capable of multi-year strategic programs that reshape the economics of the business. Fleet-wide hull optimization is of that character and scale. The technology is proven. The economics are compelling. The regulatory environment is aligned. The time to act is now.

## 11. Next Steps and Immediate Actions

This dossier serves as the foundational document for the Fleet Hull Excellence Program. The following immediate actions are recommended to transition from analysis to execution:

**Within 30 Days:** Present this dossier to the Executive Leadership Team for review and endorsement. Establish the Fleet Hull Excellence Program charter, appoint the program director, and authorize initial seed funding for Components 1 and 5 (baseline assessment and economic modeling).

**Within 90 Days:** Assemble the cross-brand technical working group comprising marine engineering directors from each brand. Initiate data collection for the fleet-wide hull condition baseline. Engage with primary coating manufacturers (Nippon Paint Marine, Hempel, PPG, Jotun, International Paint, GIT Coatings) to secure technical specifications, pricing frameworks, and application support commitments.

**Within 180 Days:** Complete the draft coating selection matrix and circulate for technical review. Deliver the fleet-wide hull condition baseline report. Present the validated lifecycle economics model with brand-specific scenarios. Identify and schedule the pilot program vessels.

**Within 12 Months:** Commence the pilot program on the first tranche of vessels coinciding with their scheduled dry dock events. Establish the hull performance monitoring data pipeline. Deliver first quarterly program performance report to executive leadership.

The program director should be empowered to draw on resources from across Carnival's technical operations organization, marine engineering departments, and financial planning groups. The program should operate with the visibility and accountability of a corporate strategic initiative — not as a departmental project within a single brand or function.

The Board of Directors should be briefed on the Fleet Hull Excellence Program at the next regularly scheduled board meeting, with emphasis on the financial materiality of fuel expenditure, the proven performance of advanced coatings within the Carnival fleet, the regulatory tailwinds from CII and EU ETS, and the compelling return on investment across all modeled scenarios.
