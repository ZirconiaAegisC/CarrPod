# Manufacturing, Supply Chain & Financial Position

## Wilsonville Manufacturing Facility

### Facility Overview

ESS Inc's primary manufacturing facility is located in Wilsonville, Oregon, approximately 20 miles south of Portland in the Pacific Northwest's "Silicon Forest" technology corridor. The facility encompasses over 200,000 square feet of production, assembly, testing, and office space within a single industrial campus.

### Production Capabilities

The Wilsonville facility houses end-to-end manufacturing of iron flow battery systems:

**Electrode Manufacturing:**
- Carbon-based electrode production using proprietary coating and thermal treatment processes
- Automated quality control including resistance mapping, thickness gauging, and visual defect detection
- Target production rate: sufficient electrodes for multiple GWh of annual cell capacity

**Stack Assembly:**
- Semi-automated assembly line for cell stack construction
- Robotic placement of bipolar plates, electrodes, membranes, and flow frames
- Automated leak testing and electrical continuity verification
- Each stack undergoes a 48-hour formation cycling process before integration

**Electrolyte Preparation:**
- On-site electrolyte mixing and quality control laboratory
- Iron chloride sourcing from domestic chemical suppliers
- Proprietary additive blending to achieve target electrolyte performance characteristics
- Tank farm for bulk electrolyte storage prior to system filling

**System Integration:**
- Final assembly of Energy Warehouse containerized systems
- Integration of stacks, tanks, pumps, piping, PCS, BMS, and thermal management
- Factory acceptance testing (FAT) simulating grid-connected charge/discharge cycles
- Quality management system certified to ISO 9001:2015

### Production Capacity and Scale-Up

ESS has disclosed target production capacity milestones:

| Milestone | Annual Capacity | Status |
|-----------|----------------|--------|
| Pilot line | 100 MWh/year | Achieved 2022 |
| Phase 1 automation | 750 MWh/year | In progress 2024–2025 |
| Phase 2 automation | 2 GWh/year | Target 2026–2027 |
| Phase 3 expansion | 8+ GWh/year | Planning stage |

The step-change from pilot to Phase 1 required significant capital investment in automated equipment, including robotic material handling, automated guided vehicles (AGVs) for in-process material movement, and manufacturing execution system (MES) software for production tracking and traceability.

## Domestic Supply Chain

ESS's supply chain is almost entirely domestic, a strategic differentiator in an energy storage industry dominated by Asian supply chains:

### Raw Materials

| Material | Primary US Source | Global Supply Risk |
|----------|------------------|-------------------|
| Iron chloride | US chemical industry (byproduct of steel pickling) | None — iron is 4th most abundant element; US is 4th largest iron ore producer |
| Sodium chloride (salt) | Domestic production in multiple states | None — effectively unlimited global supply |
| Water | On-site, municipal, or well water | None |
| Carbon electrode materials | US specialty carbon manufacturers | Low — multiple qualified domestic suppliers |
| Proton-exchange membranes | US, European, and Asian suppliers | Moderate — limited number of global membrane manufacturers; ESS qualifies multiple sources |
| Structural steel | Domestic steel mills (Nucor, Steel Dynamics) | Low — US electric arc furnace production is substantial |
| Polyethylene/PVDF tanks | US plastics fabricators | Low — commodity petrochemical derivatives |
| Power electronics (PCS) | US-based inverter manufacturers (EPC Power, Dynapower) | Low-Moderate — semiconductor components (IGBTs) sourced globally |
| Pumps and valves | US industrial equipment suppliers (ITT, Flowserve, Crane) | Low — mature domestic industrial base |

### IRA 45X Domestic Content Qualification

The all-domestic supply chain positions ESS to qualify for the Section 45X Advanced Manufacturing Production Credit without concerns about foreign entity of concern (FEOC) restrictions, which limit credits for batteries containing critical minerals or components from China, Russia, North Korea, or Iran. This is a significant competitive advantage relative to lithium-ion manufacturers, many of whom source cathode active materials from China and anode graphite from China, potentially disqualifying them from full 45X credit eligibility under Treasury Department final rules.

## Financial Position

### Revenue Profile

ESS remains in the early commercial revenue stage:

- **Product revenue:** Unit sales of Energy Warehouse and Energy Center systems to utility, C&I, and government customers
- **Service revenue:** Engineering, commissioning, and long-term service agreements
- **Development revenue:** Fees for project development activities, including site evaluation, interconnection support, and permitting assistance

Revenue is recognized on a project-by-project basis, typically using percentage-of-completion accounting for larger Energy Center contracts. Given the company's early stage, quarterly revenue is lumpy, varying significantly based on the timing of customer acceptances and milestone achievements.

### Gross Margin

Current gross margins are negative at low production volumes, reflecting:

- Under-absorption of fixed manufacturing overhead across low unit volumes
- Higher component costs at low purchase quantities
- Warranty reserves established for early-production units
- Learning curve effects in stack assembly yield and electrolyte preparation

ESS's financial model projects gross margin breakeven at approximately 500–750 MWh of annual production volume, with path to 25–30% gross margins at 2+ GWh scale, driven by:
- Manufacturing automation reducing direct labor per unit
- Volume discounts on purchased components
- Yield improvement in stack assembly (reducing scrap and rework)
- 45X credits recorded as a reduction to cost of goods sold

### Operating Expenses

R&D and SG&A expenses currently dominate the income statement:

- **Research & Development:** $30–40 million annually, funding stack design improvements, electrolyte optimization, BMS development, and next-generation product engineering
- **Selling, General & Administrative:** $40–50 million annually, including sales force, marketing, executive compensation, public company compliance (SOX, SEC reporting), insurance, and facilities

### Balance Sheet Considerations

Key balance sheet items of interest:

- **Cash and equivalents:** $100–150 million (varies by quarter based on financing activity and burn rate)
- **Debt:** Limited; ESS has primarily financed through equity rather than debt, preserving balance sheet flexibility
- **Property, plant & equipment:** Growing as manufacturing facility investment continues; depreciated over 15–25 years for buildings and 7–15 years for production equipment
- **Inventory:** Increasing as the company builds finished goods and work-in-process inventory to support anticipated demand; a sudden inventory build without corresponding revenue growth would be a concerning signal
- **Accounts receivable:** Limited, given the project-based nature of revenue recognition
- **Warranty liability:** Accrued as a percentage of revenue, reflecting management's estimate of future warranty claims on deployed systems

### Path to Profitability

Analyst consensus suggests ESS will achieve EBITDA breakeven in the 2027–2028 timeframe, dependent on: successful execution of the manufacturing scale-up roadmap, conversion of the utility project pipeline into firm revenue-generating orders, realization of targeted gross margins at volume, moderation of R&D and SG&A spending growth relative to revenue growth, and full benefit of IRA 45X credits flowing through the income statement. The primary risk to this timeline is the pace of utility procurement of LDES, which depends on regulatory mandates, interconnection queue processing times, and the broader electric utility capital expenditure cycle.
