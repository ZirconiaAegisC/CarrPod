# TECO & US Army Deployments

## Tampa Electric Company (TECO)

### Utility Profile

Tampa Electric Company (TECO), a subsidiary of Emera Inc. (TSX: EMA), serves approximately 810,000 customers in west-central Florida, including the Tampa Bay metropolitan area. TECO's generation portfolio includes natural gas (approximately 70%, including combined-cycle and combustion turbine units), solar photovoltaic (growing, with 1,250+ MW planned by 2026), and coal (declining, with retirement of remaining coal units planned).

Florida's unique grid characteristics — high air-conditioning-driven peak loads, vulnerability to hurricane-induced outages, and flat terrain that precludes pumped storage hydropower — make long-duration energy storage an attractive resource option. Unlike California or the Northeast, Florida does not have a renewable portfolio standard, but utility integrated resource planning (IRP) processes and declining solar costs are driving renewable and storage procurement.

### Project Details

In 2023, TECO and ESS Inc announced a demonstration project deploying iron flow battery technology at a TECO facility in the Tampa Bay area. The project is structured as:

- **Phase 1:** Installation of an initial Energy Warehouse system (75 kW / 500 kWh) for technology evaluation
- **Evaluation criteria:** Performance under Florida's high-ambient-temperature, high-humidity conditions; operational reliability during hurricane season; round-trip efficiency at elevated temperatures; and integration with TECO's distribution grid
- **Phase 2 (contingent on Phase 1 success):** Potential scale-up to multi-MW Energy Center configuration

### Florida-Specific Validation

The TECO deployment serves as a critical environmental validation for iron flow battery technology in challenging climatic conditions:

- **High ambient temperature:** Tampa's average daily maximum temperature exceeds 32°C (90°F) for approximately 4 months per year. The aqueous electrolyte's thermal stability eliminates the need for the air conditioning systems required by lithium-ion BESS containers in similar climates
- **High humidity:** Florida's 70–80% average relative humidity tests the corrosion resistance of balance-of-plant components, including electrical connections, enclosures, and structural elements
- **Hurricane resilience:** The deployment validates the system's ability to operate as an islanded microgrid resource following grid outages caused by extreme weather events. The Energy Warehouse's 6+ hours of continuous discharge at rated power can provide critical backup to essential loads during multi-day grid restoration efforts

### Strategic Value

The TECO relationship provides ESS with:

1. **Southeast US market entry:** A reference customer in a region where LDES procurement is nascent but growing
2. **Hurricane-hardening use case validation:** Direct operational data supporting the value proposition of LDES for grid resilience
3. **Investor-owned utility (IOU) reference:** Unlike SMUD (public power), TECO is an IOU subsidiary of a publicly traded parent (Emera), providing a reference applicable to the IOU segment that represents approximately 70% of US electricity sales

## US Army Deployments

### Mission Context

The US Department of Defense (DoD) is the single largest institutional energy consumer globally, with an annual energy bill exceeding $10 billion. The Army's climate strategy, issued in 2022, establishes targets for:
- 100% carbon-pollution-free electricity for Army installations by 2030
- A microgrid at every installation by 2035
- All-electric non-tactical vehicle fleet by 2035

Energy storage is critical to achieving these goals, particularly for installations seeking to maintain operational capability during extended grid outages (the "islanding" requirement). The Army's emphasis on supply chain security, domestic manufacturing, and technology that does not rely on materials from adversarial nations aligns strongly with ESS's value proposition.

### Deployment at Fort Detrick

ESS has deployed iron flow battery systems at US Army installations, including Fort Detrick in Frederick, Maryland. Fort Detrick is home to the US Army Medical Research and Development Command, the National Cancer Institute, and multiple biodefense laboratories. The installation's critical mission — medical and biological research supporting national security — creates a high-value use case for resilient, long-duration backup power.

Specific deployment details:
- Installation of Energy Warehouse systems providing 500+ kWh of long-duration energy storage
- Integration with existing on-site solar photovoltaic generation
- Primary function: islanded microgrid support during grid outages, ensuring continuous power to critical laboratory loads
- Secondary function: peak shaving during normal grid-connected operation to reduce demand charges

### DoD-Specific Value Proposition

ESS technology aligns with several DoD-specific requirements:

1. **Supply Chain Security:** The all-iron chemistry uses exclusively domestic materials, eliminating dependence on Chinese-controlled lithium, cobalt, and graphite supply chains. The DoD has identified battery supply chain vulnerability as a critical national security risk
2. **Non-Flammability:** Iron flow batteries present zero fire risk, eliminating the need for the extensive fire suppression systems and setback distances required for lithium-ion BESS at military installations. This is particularly important for installations like Fort Detrick that house irreplaceable biological samples and research facilities
3. **Resilience Duration:** Military installations require backup power for extended durations (72+ hours in some scenarios). While lithium-ion can economically provide 2–4 hours, the iron flow battery's ability to scale energy capacity through tank size provides a cost-effective path to multi-day resilience
4. **IT/OT Security:** ESS's US-based manufacturing, software development, and remote monitoring operations satisfy DoD concerns about foreign adversary access to operational technology systems controlling critical infrastructure at military bases

### Military Procurement Pathway

ESS products can be procured by DoD entities through several mechanisms:

- **ESPC/UESC:** Energy Savings Performance Contracts and Utility Energy Service Contracts allow third-party financing of energy improvements with repayment from guaranteed energy savings
- **Direct Procurement:** Army Corps of Engineers and Naval Facilities Engineering Systems Command (NAVFAC) have direct procurement authority for energy resilience projects
- **GSA Schedule:** Inclusion on General Services Administration procurement schedules streamlines purchasing for DoD and other federal agencies

### Broader Federal Market

Beyond the Army, ESS has opportunities across the federal government:

- **Department of Energy:** National laboratories (PNNL, NREL, Sandia, INL) are both testing ESS technology and deploying it at their own facilities
- **Department of Veterans Affairs:** VA medical centers require resilient backup power for critical care facilities
- **General Services Administration:** GSA-managed federal buildings represent a distributed portfolio of commercial-scale storage opportunities
