# Verdagy — Materials Innovation

## The Materials Problem in PEM Electrolysis

Proton exchange membrane electrolysis, the dominant competing technology for green
hydrogen production, suffers from fundamental material constraints that limit its
scalability and cost reduction potential:

- **Iridium** — required as the anode catalyst, iridium is one of the rarest elements
  on Earth, produced primarily as a byproduct of platinum mining in South Africa.
  Annual global production is approximately 7-8 tonnes, and a single gigawatt of PEM
  electrolyzer capacity requires roughly 400 kg of iridium. At current production
  rates, iridium supply constraints would cap PEM electrolyzer deployment well below
  the terawatt scale required for deep decarbonization.

- **Platinum** — used as the cathode catalyst in PEM cells, platinum faces similar
  supply constraints and price volatility, trading at approximately $30,000/kg.

- **PFAS membranes** — the perfluorinated sulfonic acid membranes used in PEM
  electrolyzers (such as Nafion) are members of the PFAS chemical family, which faces
  increasing regulatory scrutiny and potential restrictions in the European Union
  and United States due to environmental persistence and health concerns.

## Verdagy's Material Strategy

Verdagy's alkaline electrolysis platform eliminates all three of these problematic
materials entirely:

| Material | PEM Electrolysis | Verdagy Alkaline | Impact |
|----------|-----------------|------------------|--------|
| Iridium | Required (anode) | **None** | Eliminates rarest element constraint |
| Platinum | Required (cathode) | **None** | Removes precious metal cost volatility |
| PFAS | Required (membrane) | **None** | Avoids regulatory risk and environmental concerns |

## Stainless Steel as Primary Structural Material

Stainless steel serves as the primary material for bipolar plates, cell frames, and
structural components in the Verdagy eDynamic system. Stainless steel is:

- **Abundant** — global production exceeds 50 million tonnes annually
- **Corrosion-resistant** — appropriate grades withstand the alkaline electrolyte
  environment for decades
- **Manufacturable** — existing forming, welding, and coating infrastructure
- **Recyclable** — end-of-life material retains value

This stands in stark contrast to titanium bipolar plates used in many PEM systems,
which are substantially more expensive and harder to manufacture.

## Low-Cost Polymer Separators

Instead of PFAS-based ion-exchange membranes, Verdagy uses porous polymer diaphragms
that provide:

- **Ionic conductivity** — enabled by the liquid electrolyte that fills the pores
- **Gas separation** — controlled porosity prevents hydrogen-oxygen mixing
- **Low cost** — commodity polymers without fluorinated chemistry
- **Regulatory compliance** — no PFAS-related restrictions

## Electrode Catalyst Development

Verdagy's electrode catalysts are based on nickel and nickel alloys — abundant metals
with established electrochemical performance in alkaline conditions. The company has
developed proprietary high-surface-area electrode architectures, likely involving:

- Raney nickel-type structures with enhanced porosity
- Alloying elements that improve catalytic activity and stability
- Advanced coating processes (thermal spray, electrodeposition) that create controlled
  microstructures optimized for gas evolution reactions

## Supply Chain Resilience

By constraining material inputs to abundant commodities — steel, nickel, potassium
hydroxide, commodity polymers — Verdagy's supply chain is inherently more resilient
than technologies dependent on critical minerals. This resilience translates into:

- **Predictable input costs** — commodity pricing with established futures markets
- **Diverse supplier base** — multiple sources on every continent
- **Geopolitical de-risking** — no dependence on single-country supply chains
- **Regulatory durability** — compliant with anticipated PFAS restrictions
