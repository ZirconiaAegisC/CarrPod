# Verdagy — Advanced Alkaline Water Electrolysis Science

## Scientific Foundations

Alkaline water electrolysis is the oldest and most industrially proven method of
electrochemical hydrogen production, with commercial deployments dating to the early
twentieth century. The fundamental science is well established: an applied voltage
splits water molecules into hydrogen and oxygen gases in a high-pH electrolyte,
typically 25-30% potassium hydroxide solution. The thermodynamic minimum voltage
for water splitting is 1.23 V at standard conditions, though practical cells operate
at 1.7-2.1 V due to kinetic overpotentials and ohmic losses.

## Overpotential Management

The gap between thermodynamic and practical operating voltage — known as overpotential
— is the central engineering challenge in electrolyzer design. Overpotential arises
from three primary sources:

1. **Activation overpotential** — the energy barrier to initiate the electrode
   reactions, governed by catalyst activity and electrode surface area
2. **Ohmic overpotential** — resistance to ion transport through the electrolyte
   and diaphragm, governed by electrolyte conductivity and separator design
3. **Concentration overpotential** — mass transport limitations as reactants are
   consumed and products accumulate near electrode surfaces

Verdagy's research program addresses all three loss mechanisms through advanced
electrode catalyst formulations, optimized diaphragm porosity and tortuosity,
and computational fluid dynamics modeling of electrolyte flow distribution across
the industry's largest cell active areas.

## Gas Crossover Management

A critical operational consideration in alkaline electrolysis is hydrogen crossover
through the diaphragm into the oxygen stream, which can create flammable gas mixtures
if not properly managed. This challenge becomes more acute at the low current densities
experienced during part-load operation, which is increasingly common as electrolyzers
are paired with variable renewable electricity sources. Verdagy's diaphragm technology
is engineered to minimize crossover across the full turndown range, enabling safe
operation even at low utilization rates that would be problematic for conventional
alkaline systems.

## Electrolyte Chemistry & Management

The potassium hydroxide electrolyte serves dual roles: it provides the high ionic
conductivity necessary for efficient charge transport, and it participates in the
electrode reactions by supplying and accepting hydroxide ions. Over time, electrolyte
can become contaminated with carbonate from atmospheric CO₂ absorption or with metal
ions from component corrosion. Verdagy's system incorporates automated electrolyte
management subsystems that monitor conductivity, pH, and impurity levels, triggering
purification or replenishment cycles as needed to maintain optimal cell performance
over the system's multi-decade operating life.

## Degradation Mechanisms

The primary degradation mechanisms in alkaline electrolyzers include:

- **Electrode deactivation** — loss of catalytic surface area through sintering,
  dissolution, or poisoning by electrolyte impurities
- **Diaphragm degradation** — changes in porosity or wettability that increase
  ionic resistance or gas crossover
- **Corrosion** — gradual material loss from cell frames, bipolar plates, and
  gas handling components

Verdagy's materials selection — notably the avoidance of precious metals and PFAS
membranes — removes several degradation pathways that affect competing technologies
while enabling component lifetimes that support the company's ambitious warranty and
performance guarantee targets.
