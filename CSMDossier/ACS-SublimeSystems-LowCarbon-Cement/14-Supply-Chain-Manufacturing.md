# Sublime Systems — Supply Chain and Manufacturing Strategy

## Overview

The transition from the Holyoke pilot facility (250 tonnes/year) to commercial-scale deployment (25,000–500,000+ tonnes/year) requires Sublime Systems to establish robust supply chains for electrolyzer components, calcium-bearing feedstocks, electrolyte chemicals, and balance-of-plant equipment. This document examines the supply chain requirements and manufacturing strategy for commercial deployment.

## Electrolyzer Supply Chain

The electrolyzer is the core unit operation of Sublime's process. Commercial deployment at scale requires a reliable, cost-effective supply chain for electrolyzer components:

### Ion-Exchange Membranes

Ion-exchange membranes (CEMs, AEMs, and/or bipolar membranes) are the most critical and technically demanding electrolyzer components:

- **Material requirements**: Chemically stable perfluorinated or hydrocarbon-based ion-exchange polymers with high ionic conductivity, selectivity (blocking unwanted ion transport), and mechanical durability
- **Current suppliers**: The established PEM water electrolysis and chlor-alkali industries have developed membrane supply chains that Sublime can leverage. Major membrane suppliers include Chemours (Nafion™), Asahi Kasei, AGC, and emerging suppliers focused on advanced hydrocarbon membranes
- **Customization requirements**: Sublime's specific electrolyte chemistry and operating conditions (acidic anolyte, calcium-rich solutions) may require membrane modifications or custom formulations beyond off-the-shelf products
- **Manufacturing scale-up**: At 250,000 t/yr cement capacity, membrane area requirements are on the order of 50,000–200,000 m² per year, depending on current density and process configuration. This is a meaningful but achievable demand relative to the existing PEM electrolyzer membrane market.

### Electrode Materials

- **Anode catalysts**: Oxygen evolution reaction (OER) catalysts must be stable under acidic, oxidizing conditions. Iridium-based catalysts (IrO₂, IrRuO₂) are the current standard for PEM water electrolysis, and Sublime's anode operates under comparable conditions.
- **Cathode catalysts**: Hydrogen evolution reaction (HER) catalysts operating under basic conditions. Nickel-based materials (Ni, NiMo, NiFe) are effective and significantly less expensive than platinum-group metals used at the anode.
- **Electrode substrates**: Titanium (anode side) and stainless steel or nickel (cathode side) substrates provide mechanical support and current collection.

**Supply risk assessment**: Iridium is one of the rarest elements in Earth's crust, with annual global production of only 7–9 tonnes. The PEM electrolyzer industry's rapid growth has created concerns about iridium supply constraints. Sublime's iridium demand per tonne of cement would need to be managed carefully, potentially through thrifting (reducing catalyst loading), recycling, or development of non-iridium anode catalysts.

### Cell Frames and Hardware

- Cell frames, bipolar plates, gaskets, and end plates
- Materials: Titanium, stainless steel, polymer composites
- Manufacturing processes: Machining, stamping, injection molding, welding
- Supply chain: Established industrial suppliers serving the PEM electrolyzer and chlor-alkali industries

### Electrolyzer Stack Assembly

Sublime faces a build-versus-buy decision for electrolyzer stack manufacturing:
- **In-house manufacturing**: Maximum IP protection, quality control, and design flexibility, but requires capital investment and manufacturing expertise outside Sublime's core competency
- **Contract manufacturing**: Leverages existing electrolyzer manufacturing capacity and expertise, but requires disclosure of proprietary designs and creates dependency on external suppliers
- **Strategic partnership**: Joint development and supply agreements with established electrolyzer manufacturers — the most likely pathway

## Calcium Feedstock Supply Chain

### Limestone
- **Availability**: Globally abundant; limestone quarries exist in virtually every region with cement production
- **Cost**: $5–15 per tonne at the quarry; transport cost dominates delivered price
- **Quality requirements**: Relatively flexible; the electrochemical process can tolerate impurities that would be problematic in kiln-based production
- **CO₂ management**: Limestone use generates concentrated CO₂ that requires capture and storage/utilization infrastructure

### Calcium Silicate Minerals (Wollastonite)
- **Availability**: Geographically limited; major deposits in the United States (New York), China, India, Mexico, and Finland
- **Current global production**: ~1 million tonnes/year (insufficient for large-scale cement production)
- **Development requirements**: New wollastonite mining and processing capacity would need to be developed for cement-scale deployment
- **Advantage**: Zero process CO₂ emissions

### Industrial Alkaline Wastes
- **Steel slag**: ~200–300 million tonnes/year globally, but declining as steel decarbonization progresses
- **Coal fly ash**: ~500 million tonnes/year globally, but declining with coal power phase-out
- **Demolition concrete**: Abundant but distributed; collection and processing costs may exceed virgin feedstock costs
- **Red mud (bauxite residue)**: ~150 million tonnes/year; geographically concentrated near alumina refineries

### Basalt and Mafic Rocks
- **Availability**: The most abundant rock type on Earth's surface; accessible on every continent
- **Challenge**: Slower dissolution kinetics than limestone or wollastonite, requiring either finer grinding (higher energy for size reduction) or longer residence time (larger reactor volume)
- **Advantage**: Zero process CO₂ emissions; virtually unlimited resource base

## Electrolyte Chemical Supply Chain

The electrolyte system requires industrial chemicals that are commodities with established global supply chains:
- Sodium chloride or potassium chloride (supporting electrolyte)
- Sodium hydroxide or potassium hydroxide (pH adjustment)
- Specific additives (proprietary) for membrane compatibility and calcium solubility

None of these materials faces significant supply constraints.

## Balance of Plant Equipment

Beyond the electrolyzer, the Sublime process requires conventional industrial equipment with established supply chains:
- Feedstock crushing and grinding (ball mills, vertical roller mills — same equipment used in conventional cement plants)
- Reactor tanks and vessels (chemical process industry standard)
- Filtration equipment (filter presses, belt filters, centrifuges)
- Drying equipment (low-temperature dryers, fluidized bed dryers)
- Material handling (conveyors, silos, pneumatic transport)
- Hydrogen compression and storage
- Electrical infrastructure (rectifiers, transformers, switchgear)

All of this equipment is available from multiple established industrial suppliers.

## Workforce Requirements

A commercial Sublime cement plant requires a workforce with skills spanning:
- Electrochemical process operations and control
- Industrial electrolyzer maintenance (membrane replacement, electrode inspection)
- Process analytical chemistry and quality control
- Cement product testing and concrete performance evaluation
- Industrial safety (hydrogen handling, chemical handling, electrical safety)
- Conventional industrial operations (maintenance, logistics, environmental compliance)

The Holyoke pilot facility serves as the training ground for the workforce that will operate commercial plants. Sublime has developed training programs covering electrochemical operations, product quality control, and safety procedures.

## Logistics and Siting Considerations

Commercial Sublime cement plants will be sited based on:
- **Electricity availability and cost**: The primary siting criterion; regions with low-cost renewable electricity are strongly preferred
- **Feedstock proximity**: Minimizing transport costs for calcium-bearing minerals
- **Cement market proximity**: Minimizing outbound logistics costs; cement is a low-value, high-weight product with limited economic transport radius (typically 200–300 km by truck)
- **CO₂ storage infrastructure**: If using limestone feedstock with CO₂ capture, proximity to geologic CO₂ storage or CO₂ utilization facilities
- **Hydrogen offtake**: If hydrogen co-product is to be sold rather than used on-site, proximity to hydrogen pipelines or industrial hydrogen consumers
