## Investment Thesis

### The Problem
Cement production accounts for approximately 8% of global CO₂ emissions — roughly 3 billion tonnes annually. Unlike power generation or transportation, where emissions come primarily from fuel combustion, approximately 60% of cement emissions are inherent to the chemistry of limestone decomposition. Even with zero-carbon heat, conventional cement production would still emit approximately 0.5 tonnes of CO₂ per tonne of cement from the chemical reaction alone.

### The Solution
Sublime Systems replaces the 1450°C limestone kiln with an electrochemical process operating at ambient temperature that extracts calcium from minerals and precipitates calcium silicate cement without decomposing limestone. The process eliminates both process emissions and thermal emissions, achieving 90%+ CO₂ reduction when powered by clean electricity. With calcium silicate feedstocks (instead of limestone), the process has zero CO₂ from chemistry.

### Why Now
Multiple converging trends create favorable conditions:
1. Expanding carbon pricing (EU CBAM, EU ETS, potential US carbon pricing) creating direct financial incentives
2. Green building standards and corporate commitments creating premium market demand
3. Declining renewable electricity and electrolyzer costs improving process economics
4. Hydrogen co-product providing economic resilience and diversifying revenue
5. ASTM certification achieved, removing standards barriers
6. Cement industry seeking decarbonization pathways beyond carbon capture

### Competitive Moats
1. **Electrochemical process at ambient temperature**: Eliminates the massive energy and capital costs of high-temperature kilns
2. **90%+ CO₂ reduction**: Best-in-class among scalable cement technologies
3. **Feedstock flexibility**: Limestone (with concentrated CO₂ capture) or calcium silicates (zero CO₂)
4. **Drop-in product**: ASTM C595/C1157 certified — works in existing concrete infrastructure
5. **Hydrogen co-product**: $60–150/t credit that transforms process economics
6. **Strategic partnerships**: CRH (world's largest cement producer) and SCG (Southeast Asia's largest)
7. **IP portfolio**: Early priority dates on foundational electrochemical cement patents from MIT

## Key Risks

### Technology Risk (HIGH)
The transition from Holyoke pilot (250 t/yr) to commercial scale (25,000–500,000 t/yr) requires electrolyzer stack scale-up that has not yet been demonstrated. Performance at commercial scale — production rate, energy efficiency, membrane lifetime, product consistency — remains unvalidated.

### Feedstock Strategy Risk (MEDIUM)
The optimal feedstock pathway is not yet resolved:
- Limestone requires CO₂ capture infrastructure (adding cost and siting constraints) to achieve high emissions reduction
- Calcium silicates offer zero-CO₂ cement but require developing new mining supply chains at scale
- Alkaline wastes offer circular economy benefits but have declining and geographically concentrated supply

### Cost Competitiveness Risk (MEDIUM)
Without carbon pricing or hydrogen revenue, Sublime cement is more expensive than conventional cement. Competitiveness depends on:
- Low electricity costs (<$30–50/MWh)
- Moderate to high carbon prices ($50–100+/tCO₂)
- Green cement premiums ($20–50/t)
- Hydrogen co-product value ($3–5/kg)

If all three revenue/cost supports are weak simultaneously, Sublime cement may struggle to achieve mainstream competitiveness.

### Adoption Risk (MEDIUM)
The cement industry is conservative and the long-term performance data for electrochemically produced cement is limited (the technology has existed for less than a decade). Adoption may be slow, particularly for structural applications where liability concerns are highest.

### Electrolyzer Supply Chain Risk (MEDIUM)
Commercial deployment at scale requires reliable supply chains for:
- Ion-exchange membranes (currently dominated by a few global suppliers)
- Iridium anode catalysts (global iridium production is only 7–9 tonnes/year)
- Electrolyzer stack assembly (competing with PEM electrolyzer industry for manufacturing capacity)

2. **Strategic acquisition**: Acquisition by a major cement producer (CRH, Holcim, Heidelberg Materials) or industrial technology company
3. **Long-term independent operation**: Continued private company with licensing and partnership revenue model

## Diligence Questions for Management

1. What is the demonstrated electrolyzer stack performance at Holyoke (current density, Faraday efficiency, energy consumption per tonne of cement), and what are the targets for commercial stacks?

2. What are the key degradation mechanisms for membranes and electrodes in the Sublime electrolyzer, and what are the demonstrated and target lifetimes?

3. What is the projected levelized cost of cement for the commercial demonstration plant, broken down by electricity, feedstock, electrolyte/membrane replacement, labor, hydrogen credit, and capital amortization?

4. What is the preferred feedstock strategy for initial commercial deployment — limestone with CO₂ capture or calcium silicates — and what are the trade-offs?

5. How does Sublime plan to source electrolyzer stacks for commercial deployment (in-house vs. contract manufacturing vs. strategic supply agreements), and what is the projected cost per megawatt of electrolyzer capacity?

6. What long-term durability data exists for Sublime cement in concrete (5+ year performance), and what is the plan for generating the multi-decade performance data that the construction industry requires?

7. What is the status of commercial discussions with potential licensees beyond CRH and SCG, and what are the key barriers to licensing?

8. How does the hydrogen co-product strategy evolve with scale — merchant sale, on-site utilization, or integration with CO₂ utilization — and what are the economics under each scenario?

9. What is the plan for reducing or eliminating iridium dependence in anode catalysts, given global iridium supply constraints?

10. How does Sublime plan to manage the transition from a technology development organization to a commercial technology company, including building the necessary commercial, manufacturing, and project execution capabilities?
