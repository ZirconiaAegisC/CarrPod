# Sublime Systems — Policy and Regulatory Landscape

## Overview of Cement Decarbonization Policy

The policy environment for cement decarbonization is evolving rapidly, driven by the recognition that cement accounts for approximately 8% of global CO₂ emissions and that the built environment is a critical sector for achieving net-zero emissions targets. This document surveys the key policies affecting Sublime Systems' market opportunity.

## European Union

### EU Emissions Trading System (EU ETS)

The EU ETS covers cement production among other industrial emitters. Key provisions:
- Free allowance phase-out: Free allowances for cement producers are being reduced, reaching zero by 2034
- Carbon price trajectory: EU ETS allowances at €60–100/tCO₂ in 2023–2025, with projections of €100–150/tCO₂ by 2030
- At €100/tCO₂, the carbon cost of conventional cement (approximately 0.6 tCO₂/t cement) is €60/t, equivalent to 50–75% of the typical cement selling price

### Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM)

The EU CBAM applies to imported cement, with full implementation from 2026:
- Importers of cement must purchase CBAM certificates corresponding to the embedded emissions
- Embedded emissions for conventional cement are approximately 0.6–0.9 tCO₂/t
- At €100/tCO₂, CBAM charges on imported conventional cement are €60–90/t
- Sublime cement with verified low embedded emissions would face minimal CBAM charges, creating a substantial competitive advantage in the EU market

### Construction Products Regulation (CPR)

The CPR governs the marketing of construction products in the EU, requiring CE marking. Key developments:
- Revised CPR (adopted 2024) includes sustainability requirements, potentially including mandatory reporting of embodied carbon
- Harmonized technical specifications (hENs) for cement are maintained by CEN, the European standardization body
- Sublime cement must achieve CE marking through compliance with relevant hENs to access the EU market

### EU Taxonomy for Sustainable Activities

The EU Taxonomy classifies economic activities as environmentally sustainable based on technical screening criteria:
- Cement production qualifies as a "transitional activity" under the taxonomy if it meets specified emissions thresholds
- The taxonomy influences access to green financing, EU funding programs, and institutional investment mandates

## United States

### Inflation Reduction Act (IRA)

The IRA includes several provisions relevant to Sublime Systems:

- **Section 45Q — Carbon Capture Tax Credit**: Enhanced credits for carbon capture ($85/tCO₂ for industrial facilities, $180/tCO₂ for direct air capture). This provision is most relevant to Sublime's carbon capture competitors but also provides potential value if Sublime operates with limestone feedstock and captures the concentrated CO₂ stream.
- **Section 48C — Qualifying Advanced Energy Project Credit**: Investment tax credits of up to 30% for facilities that reduce industrial greenhouse gas emissions, potentially applicable to Sublime cement plants.
- **Section 50161 — Advanced Industrial Facilities Deployment Program**: $5.8 billion for deployment of advanced industrial technologies reducing greenhouse gas emissions. Cement production is specifically identified as an eligible sector.
- **DOE Loan Programs Office**: Expanded authority for loans and loan guarantees for innovative clean energy technologies, including industrial decarbonization.

### Federal Buy Clean Initiative

The Federal Buy Clean Task Force promotes procurement of low-carbon construction materials for federally funded projects:
- General Services Administration (GSA) has issued interim standards for low-embodied-carbon concrete and cement
- Federal Highway Administration is developing embodied carbon standards for transportation infrastructure
- Federal procurement represents a substantial and reliable demand source for low-carbon cement

### Advanced Research Projects Agency — Energy (ARPA-E)

ARPA-E has funded early-stage research into electrochemical cement production, including Sublime Systems' foundational technology. Continued ARPA-E and DOE applied energy program funding supports ongoing technology development.

### State-Level Initiatives

- **California**: Buy Clean California Act and the state's cap-and-trade program create incentives for low-carbon cement
- **New York**: The Low Embodied Carbon Concrete Leadership Act establishes embodied carbon limits for state-funded construction
- **Colorado, Oregon, New Jersey, and others**: Buy Clean legislation enacted or under development

## Industry Commitments and Voluntary Initiatives

### Global Cement and Concrete Association (GCCA) 2050 Net Zero Roadmap

The GCCA, representing 80% of global cement production outside China, has committed to net-zero concrete by 2050. The roadmap identifies:
- Carbon capture, utilization, and storage (CCUS) as the primary decarbonization lever (36% of total reduction)
- Alternative fuels and clinker substitution as major contributors
- Novel cements and process innovations (including electrochemical production) as emerging technologies

### Climate Group ConcreteZero

ConcreteZero is a global initiative of leading businesses committing to 100% net-zero concrete by 2050, with interim targets of 30% low-emission concrete by 2025 and 50% by 2030. Members include major real estate developers, construction firms, and engineering companies, creating demand-side pull for low-carbon cement.

### First Movers Coalition

The First Movers Coalition, launched at COP26, includes commitments from major corporations to purchase near-zero-emissions cement, creating an offtake market for early commercial production from companies like Sublime.

## Carbon Markets and Pricing Mechanisms

### Compliance Carbon Markets

| Jurisdiction | Current Carbon Price | Projected 2030 Price | Cement Carbon Cost (@ 0.6 tCO₂/t) |
|-------------|---------------------|----------------------|-----------------------------------|
| EU ETS | €60–100/tCO₂ | €100–150/tCO₂ | €60–90/t |
| UK ETS | £35–75/tCO₂ | £70–120/tCO₂ | £42–72/t |
| California | $30–40/tCO₂ | $50–80/tCO₂ | $30–48/t |
| Canada (federal backstop) | CAD $65/tCO₂ (2023) | CAD $170/tCO₂ (2030) | CAD $102/t |

At projected 2030 carbon prices, the carbon cost of conventional cement ranges from $30 to $100 per tonne, creating a substantial cost umbrella under which low-carbon alternatives can compete without requiring "green premiums."

### Voluntary Carbon Markets and Green Premiums

- Several concrete producers and construction firms have announced willingness to pay premiums of 10–30% for verified low-carbon cement
- Certification standards (ResponsibleSteel for steel, comparable standards under development for cement) provide verification frameworks
- The premium market is expected to grow as corporate net-zero commitments with scope 3 emissions coverage expand

## Policy Risk Considerations

1. **Policy reversal risk**: Changes in government (particularly in the US) could weaken climate policies, carbon pricing, and green procurement commitments

2. **Standards and certification evolution**: Changes to ASTM and EN standards, building codes, or procurement specifications could affect Sublime cement's eligibility or competitive position

3. **Carbon capture subsidies**: Generous subsidies for carbon capture (e.g., enhanced 45Q credits) could shift the competitive landscape in favor of conventional cement with CCS

4. **Carbon leakage protections**: Free allowance allocation and CBAM design could be adjusted in response to industry lobbying, potentially reducing the competitive advantage of low-carbon cement

5. **International standard harmonization**: Divergence between US (ASTM), European (EN), and Chinese (GB) standards could create market access barriers
