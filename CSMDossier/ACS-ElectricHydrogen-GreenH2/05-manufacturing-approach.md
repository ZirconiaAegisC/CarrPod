# Standardized Modular Manufacturing Approach

## The Problem with Custom Electrolyzer Projects
The electrolyzer industry has historically operated on a project-by-project, engineer-
to-order model. Each installation is uniquely designed to site specifications, with
significant custom engineering, procurement, and construction (EPC) work. This model
creates several structural problems:

1. **Non-repeatable costs**: Engineering is paid for on every project, eliminating
   the benefits of learning and repetition.
2. **Long delivery timelines**: Custom design and procurement adds 12-24 months to
   project schedules.
3. **Quality variability**: Each project introduces unique integration challenges
   and failure modes.
4. **Scaling constraints**: Engineering capacity becomes the bottleneck — you can
   only do as many projects as you have engineers.
5. **Financing difficulty**: Bespoke projects are harder for lenders to underwrite
   because each carries unique technical risk.

## Electric Hydrogen's Modular Philosophy
EH2 addresses these problems by treating the 100MW electrolyzer system as a
standardized product, not a custom project. The analogy is to how solar panel
manufacturing transformed from custom rooftop installations to standardized
commodity products.

### Productization Principles

**Standard Building Blocks**
The 100MW system is the fundamental unit. For larger projects like the Texas 300MW
facility, three 100MW blocks are deployed in parallel. Each block is identical in
design, componentry, and manufacturing process, differing only in site-specific
integration (foundations, utility connections).

Key characteristics of the standardized approach include:
- Common bill of materials across all projects
- Identical stack design regardless of end application
- Pre-engineered balance-of-plant modules
- Standardized control architecture and software
- Common spares and maintenance procedures

**Containerization**
Balance-of-plant systems — power conditioning, water treatment, gas processing,
thermal management — are pre-assembled into shipping-container-sized modules at
the Devens factory. These modules are tested and commissioned at the factory,
then shipped to project sites for final interconnection.

Benefits of containerization include:
- Reduced field labor and construction time
- Controlled factory environment for critical assembly
- Factory acceptance testing before shipment
- Standardized logistics and site preparation
- Easier permitting with pre-approved modular designs

**Configuration Flexibility**
Within the standardized framework, the system accommodates key configuration
options:
- Direct DC coupling with solar PV arrays
- AC coupling via grid interconnection
- Hybrid renewable input (solar + wind)
- Optional hydrogen compression and storage integration
- Water source flexibility (municipal, groundwater, treated wastewater)

**Software-Defined Operations**
The control system is a standardized platform that adapts to site-specific
conditions through configuration rather than code changes. Key capabilities:
- Real-time optimization of hydrogen production vs. electricity price signals
- Predictive maintenance based on operating data analytics
- Remote monitoring and diagnostics across the fleet
- Automated compliance reporting for IRA tax credit qualification

## Competitive Advantages of the Modular Approach

### Speed to Market
Standardized design cuts project development timelines from 3-5 years to 18-24
months for a 100MW installation. Factory lead time for manufacturing is the
primary schedule driver rather than custom engineering.

### Cost Predictability
Fixed bill of materials and manufacturing processes enable firm pricing rather
than cost-plus contracting. This is essential for project financing — lenders
require cost certainty.

### Scalability
Manufacturing capacity, not engineering headcount, becomes the scaling constraint.
The Devens gigafactory at 1.2 GW/year can support multiple concurrent projects
with a fixed manufacturing workforce.

### Fleet Learning
Every deployed system generates operating data that feeds back into product
improvements. Since all systems share a common design, improvements benefit
the entire installed base through software updates and component upgrades.

### Bankability
Standardized, repeatable projects are easier to finance. Lenders can underwrite
based on demonstrated performance rather than one-off engineering assessments.
This reduces cost of capital — a critical lever in capital-intensive energy
infrastructure.

## Comparison to Competitor Approaches
- **Nel Hydrogen**: Custom-engineered alkaline systems; introducing modular A-series
- **ITM Power**: Moving toward standardized modules but still project-specific
  integration
- **Plug Power**: Standardized 5MW stacks but custom project integration
- **Cummins**: Engineer-to-order model; acquiring to build manufacturing capability

EH2 is unique in prioritizing manufacturing scale over project engineering from
the company's founding — a strategic bet that the green hydrogen market will
transition from bespoke projects to standardized products.
