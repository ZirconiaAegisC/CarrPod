# Green Hydrogen Market Analysis — Global & US Outlook

## Market Overview
The global hydrogen market is approximately 95 million tonnes per annum (mtpa),
primarily used for petroleum refining (38%), ammonia production (32%), methanol
production (14%), and other industrial processes. Nearly all of this hydrogen
is "gray" — produced from unabated natural gas or coal — resulting in
approximately 900 million tonnes of CO₂ emissions annually.

Green hydrogen, produced via electrolysis powered by renewable electricity,
represents a pathway to decarbonize this existing demand and enable new
applications in sectors where direct electrification is challenging.

## Demand Drivers

### Existing Industrial Demand Decarbonization
| Sector            | Current H₂ Use (mtpa) | CO₂ Emissions (Mt/year) |
|-------------------|----------------------|--------------------------|
| Refining          | ~38                  | ~250                     |
| Ammonia           | ~32                  | ~350                     |
| Methanol          | ~7                   | ~100                     |
| Direct Reduced Iron (Steel) | ~5           | ~50                      |
| **Total**         | **~82**              | **~750**                 |

The existing industrial hydrogen market alone represents a TAM (total addressable
market) that would require ~400-500 GW of electrolyzer capacity to decarbonize
at current demand levels. This dwarfs current global electrolyzer manufacturing
capacity of less than 10 GW/year.

### New Applications
Beyond replacing gray hydrogen, green hydrogen enables decarbonization of
additional sectors:

1. **Heavy-Duty Transport**: Long-haul trucking, maritime shipping, and aviation
   are difficult to electrify with batteries due to energy density requirements.
   Hydrogen fuel cells and hydrogen-derived synthetic fuels (e-fuels) offer
   zero-emission pathways.

2. **Steel Production**: Direct reduction of iron ore using hydrogen (H₂-DRI)
   instead of coal-based blast furnaces can eliminate the majority of steelmaking
   emissions, which account for ~7% of global CO₂.

3. **Long-Duration Energy Storage**: Hydrogen can store renewable energy across
   seasons, complementing batteries' intra-day storage capability. Salt cavern
   storage provides large-scale, low-cost geological storage.

4. **High-Temperature Industrial Heat**: Processes requiring temperatures above
   200°C (cement, glass, ceramics) are challenging to electrify economically.

## Supply-Side Economics

### Cost Stack for Green Hydrogen
The levelized cost of green hydrogen (LCOH) is driven primarily by:

1. **Electricity cost**: At 55 kWh/kg system efficiency, every $10/MWh in
   electricity cost translates to $0.55/kg in hydrogen cost. Renewable power
   costs of $20-30/MWh (achievable with utility-scale solar in good US locations)
   contribute $1.10-1.65/kg.

2. **Electrolyzer capex**: Installed costs of $500-700/kW (EH2 target) amortized
   over system life contribute $0.30-0.50/kg at reasonable capacity factors.

3. **IRA 45V credit**: The $3/kg production tax credit makes US green hydrogen
   highly competitive even at higher power costs, effectively subsidizing
   the transition until electrolyzer costs and renewable power costs decline
   further.

### Cost Targets
| Component              | 2023 Baseline | 2030 Target | 2050 Target |
|------------------------|---------------|-------------|-------------|
| Electrolyzer Capex     | $700-1,400/kW | $300-500/kW | $200-300/kW |
| Renewable Power        | $25-50/MWh    | $15-25/MWh  | $10-20/MWh  |
| LCOH (unsubsidized)    | $4-7/kg       | $2-3/kg     | $1.5-2/kg   |
| LCOH (with IRA 45V)    | $1-4/kg       | <$0/kg*     | <$0/kg*     |

*With IRA 45V at $3/kg, LCOH can be negative at favorable sites before 2033
(sunset of 45V), meaning the tax credit exceeds production cost.

## US Market Position
The United States is positioned to be one of the most competitive green hydrogen
producers globally due to:

1. **Low-cost renewable resources**: Excellent solar in the Southwest, strong
   wind in the Great Plains and Texas, with capacity factors exceeding 35-45%
   for best-in-class sites.

2. **IRA incentives**: The $3/kg 45V production tax credit is the most generous
   hydrogen subsidy globally, creating a 10-year window for project development.

3. **Existing infrastructure**: Extensive natural gas pipeline network, salt
   cavern storage capacity along the Gulf Coast, and existing industrial hydrogen
   consumers provide infrastructure foundation.

4. **H2Hub program**: $7 billion allocated to regional hydrogen hubs, accelerating
   demand aggregation and infrastructure buildout.

## Risks to Market Development
- **IRA policy risk**: Repeal or modification of 45V would fundamentally alter
  US project economics.
- **Power market constraints**: Interconnection queues and transmission buildout
  pace may limit access to low-cost renewable power.
- **Offtake risk**: Industrial hydrogen consumers must be willing to sign long-
  term offtake agreements at green premiums.
- **Water constraints**: Electrolysis requires ~9 kg of water per kg of hydrogen;
  water availability in arid regions (Southwest) could constrain siting.
