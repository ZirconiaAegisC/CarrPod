## 1. STRATEGIC FRAMEWORK

### 1.1 Apple 2030 Overview

Apple 2030 is the company's comprehensive environmental sustainability strategy with the central objective of achieving carbon neutrality across Apple's entire value chain — encompassing Scope 1 (direct emissions from owned or controlled sources), Scope 2 (indirect emissions from purchased electricity, steam, heating, and cooling), and Scope 3 (all other indirect emissions occurring in the value chain, including upstream supplier emissions, downstream product use emissions, and end-of-life treatment) — by the year 2030. This commitment extends beyond Apple's own operations to the full lifecycle of every Apple product sold, including manufacturing, transportation, product use, and end-of-life materials recovery.

The Apple 2030 strategy was publicly announced in July 2020 and has been reinforced through annual Environmental Progress Reports with quantified metrics and third-party assurance. Apple achieved carbon neutrality for its global corporate operations (Scope 1 and Scope 2) in 2020, seven years ahead of the corporate operations target originally set in 2017.

### 1.2 Emissions Reduction Target

Apple has established a quantitative greenhouse gas emissions reduction target of 75% below the fiscal year 2015 baseline, measured across Scopes 1, 2, and 3 on an absolute basis (not intensity-adjusted). The target is aligned with the Paris Agreement goal of limiting global average temperature increase to well below 2°C above pre-industrial levels and pursuing efforts to limit the increase to 1.5°C. As of the most recent public disclosure (fiscal year 2025), Apple has achieved a cumulative reduction of approximately 60% from the 2015 baseline, with the remaining 15 percentage points to be achieved over the period 2026 through 2030.

The residual 25% of baseline emissions that cannot be eliminated through direct reduction measures will be addressed through high-quality, verified carbon removal credits — specifically, nature-based carbon sequestration projects (afforestation, reforestation, improved forest management, wetland and peatland restoration, regenerative agricultural soil carbon sequestration) and technology-based carbon removal (direct air capture with geological storage, bioenergy with carbon capture and storage, enhanced weathering). Apple's carbon credit procurement criteria require: additionality (the emission reduction or removal would not have occurred without carbon credit revenue), permanence (minimum 100-year carbon storage guarantee with buffer pool provision for reversal risk), verified quantification through accredited third-party validation/verification bodies under recognized standards (Verra VCS, Gold Standard, Climate Action Reserve, American Carbon Registry), and co-benefit delivery (biodiversity enhancement, water quality improvement, community economic development).

### 1.3 Governance and Accountability

Environmental performance metrics — including progress toward the Apple 2030 carbon neutrality goal, renewable electricity procurement, supplier clean energy program enrollment, recycled material content, water stewardship, and waste diversion — are reported to Apple's Board of Directors at least quarterly through the Board's Sustainability Committee. Executive compensation for senior leadership includes environmental performance modifiers. Apple's annual Environmental Progress Report is subject to limited assurance by an independent third-party auditor in accordance with ISAE 3000 (International Standard on Assurance Engagements).

## 2. RENEWABLE ENERGY

### 2.1 Corporate Operations

Apple has achieved 100% renewable electricity matching for all global corporate operations — including corporate offices, retail stores, and data centers — across 44 countries. This achievement is accomplished through a portfolio of renewable energy procurement mechanisms: direct ownership of renewable generation assets (on-site solar photovoltaic arrays, on-site fuel cells operating on renewably sourced hydrogen or biogas), long-term power purchase agreements (PPAs) with utility-scale wind and solar projects (both physical delivery and virtual PPAs settling on financial contract-for-differences terms), utility green tariff programs where available from local electric utilities, and renewable energy certificates (RECs) where direct procurement is infeasible (with a preference for bundled RECs from projects within the same electrical grid region).

Apple has facilitated the construction of over 2.5 gigawatts (GW) of new utility-scale renewable energy capacity through long-term PPAs and equity investment in renewable energy projects in the United States, Europe, and Asia-Pacific markets.

### 2.2 Supplier Clean Energy Program

The Supplier Clean Energy Program extends Apple's renewable energy commitment to manufacturing supply chain partners. The program objective is 100% renewable electricity for all Apple production by 2030, defined as renewable electricity matching for the electricity consumed in the manufacturing of Apple products, components, and materials at supplier facilities worldwide. Program participation requires suppliers to: establish a baseline electricity consumption inventory for Apple-related production, commit to 100% renewable electricity for Apple production by 2030 (with interim milestones), report annual progress including renewable electricity procurement volumes, procurement mechanisms, and renewable energy certificate retirements, and undergo third-party verification of reported data.

As of the 2025 reporting cycle, over 320 supplier facilities across 28 countries have committed to 100% renewable electricity for Apple production, representing more than 95% of Apple's direct manufacturing spend. Supplier renewable electricity procurement for Apple production exceeded 4 gigawatts of new renewable energy capacity sourced by suppliers in 2025, with cumulative supplier renewable energy investments exceeding 20 GW since program inception. Supplier facility renewable electricity procurement includes: on-site solar photovoltaic installations at supplier manufacturing campuses, utility-scale off-site wind and solar projects developed through aggregated buyer consortiums facilitated by Apple, and utility green tariff and direct-access programs where regulatory frameworks permit.

## 3. MATERIALS AND CIRCULAR ECONOMY

### 3.1 Recycled and Renewable Materials

Apple's materials strategy targets 100% recycled or renewable material content across all products, with interim goals for specific material categories:

- **Cobalt:** 100% recycled cobalt in all Apple-designed batteries, achieved as of 2025.
- **Rare Earth Elements:** 100% recycled rare earth elements in all permanent magnets across all Apple products, achieved as of 2025.
- **Tin and Gold:** 100% recycled tin in all solder alloys and 100% recycled gold in all printed circuit board surface finishes, achieved as of 2024 for tin and progressing toward 100% for gold.
- **Aluminum:** Increasing recycled content across all aluminum enclosure products. Current achievements include 100% recycled aluminum in Mac mini, Apple Watch Series 9 and later, iPad (select models), and MacBook Air (select models). The target is 100% recycled aluminum across all product enclosures by 2030.
- **Plastics:** Apple has committed to eliminating all plastics from product packaging. All current Apple product packaging is plastic-free, employing fiber-based alternatives for trays, wraps, and cushioning materials. The remaining frontier is elimination of plastic content within products themselves (connector housings, cable insulation, internal brackets) — a longer-term materials science challenge.
- **Lithium:** 95% recycled lithium in iPhone 17 Pro batteries, with a trajectory toward 100% across all product lines.
- **Tungsten, Tantalum, Steel:** Increasing recycled content targets for these materials, with progress reported annually.

### 3.2 Product Life Extension

Apple's product design philosophy emphasizes longevity and durability as foundational elements of environmental sustainability. Products are designed for extended service life through: durable materials and protective coatings (Ceramic Shield glass-ceramic, anodized and PVD-coated aluminum and titanium enclosures, IP68 water and dust resistance), long-term software support (iOS, iPadOS, and macOS updates typically provided for 5 to 7 years from initial product release, extending functional product life), repairability and serviceability (Self Service Repair program providing genuine parts, tools, and repair manuals; Apple Store and Apple Authorized Service Provider repair network exceeding 5,000 locations globally; redesigned battery attachment systems enabling safer, faster battery replacement), and trade-in and reuse programs (Apple Trade In providing monetary credit for returned devices, which are refurbished for resale where possible or responsibly recycled through Apple's Material Recovery Lab if not suitable for reuse).

### 3.3 Packaging

All Apple product packaging is now plastic-free, replacing expanded polystyrene foam, polypropylene trays, polyethylene wraps, and plastic window films with fiber-based alternatives derived from responsibly managed forests (Forest Stewardship Council certified or equivalent) or recycled fiber sources. Packaging volume reduction — achieved through product design (thinner, smaller products require less packaging) and packaging engineering (optimized insert geometry, elimination of non-functional void space) — reduces transportation-related emissions by increasing pallet and container packing density. Apple's packaging team targets continuous reduction in packaging mass and volume per product unit shipped.

## 4. WATER STEWARDSHIP

### 4.1 Corporate Water Management

Apple's corporate facilities — including corporate offices, data centers, and retail stores — employ water conservation measures including: low-flow plumbing fixtures, drought-tolerant and native-species landscaping with smart irrigation controllers, cooling tower water recycling and cycles-of-concentration optimization, and rainwater harvesting for non-potable applications where climatically and economically viable.

Data center cooling water consumption is a particular focus, as cooling tower evaporation represents the largest water use category at most Apple data center facilities. Immersion cooling technology (see Document ACS-DC-THERM-001) eliminates cooling tower water consumption entirely in immersion-cooled data halls, as the higher operating temperature of the dielectric fluid circuit enables year-round dry-cooler (air-cooled) heat rejection without evaporative water loss. For air-cooled data halls, water consumption is minimized through: elevated chilled water supply temperature setpoints to maximize waterside economizer hours, cooling tower drift eliminator upgrades to reduce drift loss, and alternative water sourcing (reclaimed municipal wastewater, harvested rainwater) where available.

### 4.2 Supplier Clean Water Program

The Supplier Clean Water Program requires Apple manufacturing suppliers to implement water stewardship practices including: facility-level water balance and metering (monthly measurement and reporting of water withdrawal by source, water consumption, and wastewater discharge by receiving body), water reuse and recycling (targeted minimum 20% water reuse rate for facilities in water-stressed basins per World Resources Institute Aqueduct tool classification), wastewater treatment to standards exceeding applicable local regulatory discharge limits, and Alliance for Water Stewardship (AWS) certification for facilities located in water-stressed regions.

As of 2025, 32 supplier facilities have achieved AWS certification, representing a combined annual water withdrawal of approximately 15 million cubic meters. Certified facilities have demonstrated measurable reductions in water withdrawal intensity (cubic meters per unit of production) and improvements in wastewater discharge quality relative to pre-certification baselines. The AWS certification process requires facilities to: conduct a catchment-level water risk assessment (physical, regulatory, and reputational water risks), engage with local stakeholders (community water users, watershed authorities, municipal water utilities), establish and report against water stewardship targets for the facility and the catchment, and undergo independent third-party audit against the AWS Standard criteria.

## 5. CARBON CREDIT STRATEGY

### 5.1 Residual Emissions Addressable Through Credits

Apple's target of 75% absolute emissions reduction from the 2015 baseline leaves approximately 25% of baseline emissions to be addressed through carbon removal credits. The residual emissions portfolio includes: process emissions from raw material extraction and processing where direct electrification or fuel switching is not technologically feasible (e.g., limestone calcination for cement production, carbon anode consumption in primary aluminum smelting), fugitive emissions from industrial processes and refrigerant leakage, agricultural emissions from bio-based material supply chains, downstream product use emissions from products in service that cannot be further reduced through energy efficiency improvements, and end-of-life treatment emissions from products that cannot be further mitigated through material recovery improvements.

### 5.2 Credit Quality Criteria

Apple's carbon credit procurement framework applies rigorous quality criteria to ensure that credits represent genuine, additional, permanent, and verified emission reductions or removals:

- **Additionality:** The emission reduction or carbon removal must be demonstrated to be additional to what would have occurred in the absence of carbon credit revenue, assessed through: investment analysis (the project internal rate of return without credit revenue is below prevailing market hurdle rates), barrier analysis (the project faces technological, institutional, or financial barriers that carbon credit revenue overcomes), and common practice analysis (the project type is not already widely deployed in the relevant jurisdiction without carbon credit incentives).
- **Permanence:** For nature-based carbon removal projects, a minimum 100-year guarantee period for carbon storage is required, with a portion of credits deposited in a buffer pool — managed by the carbon standard (Verra, Gold Standard, Climate Action Reserve, American Carbon Registry) — to compensate for potential future reversals due to wildfire, pest outbreak, land-use change, or project mismanagement. For technology-based removal, geological storage must be validated for containment integrity over a minimum 10,000-year horizon.
- **Quantification:** Emission reductions and removals must be quantified using methodologies approved under a recognized carbon crediting standard, with conservative assumptions, peer-reviewed emission factors, and statistically valid sampling protocols. Quantification is subject to validation by an accredited third-party validation/verification body (VVB) prior to credit issuance and periodic verification through the project crediting period.
- **Co-Benefits:** Projects must deliver demonstrable environmental and social co-benefits beyond carbon, including but not limited to: biodiversity enhancement (native species habitat creation or restoration), water quality and quantity improvement, soil health improvement, air quality improvement, and community economic development (employment, revenue sharing, capacity building).

### 5.3 Credit Portfolio Composition

Apple's carbon credit portfolio is composed of: nature-based removal projects (afforestation/reforestation, improved forest management, agroforestry, silvopasture, wetland and peatland restoration, blue carbon ecosystem restoration) providing approximately 60% of projected credit volume; technology-based removal projects (direct air capture with geological storage, bioenergy with carbon capture and storage, enhanced weathering and mineralization) providing approximately 25% of projected credit volume and expected to increase proportionally as technology costs decline; and, where necessary, high-quality emission reduction credits (clean cookstove distribution, landfill gas capture and utilization, industrial gas destruction) providing approximately 15% of projected credit volume as a transitional mechanism while removal project capacity scales.

## 6. REPORTING AND TRANSPARENCY

### 6.1 Annual Environmental Progress Report

Apple publishes an annual Environmental Progress Report providing: quantified year-over-year progress toward Apple 2030 goals (carbon neutrality, renewable electricity, recycled material content, water stewardship, zero waste), supplier clean energy program participation and impact metrics, product-level environmental performance (lifecycle greenhouse gas emissions per product, energy efficiency, material composition), third-party limited assurance opinion on selected environmental metrics, and forward-looking commitments and targets.

### 6.2 Regulatory Compliance

Apple's environmental reporting aligns with mandatory climate disclosure frameworks including: the United States Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) climate disclosure rule (final rule issued 2024, requiring disclosure of Scope 1 and Scope 2 greenhouse gas emissions, climate-related risks and opportunities, and climate risk governance), the European Union Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD, Directive (EU) 2022/2464, requiring detailed sustainability reporting per European Sustainability Reporting Standards), California's climate accountability legislation (SB 253 Climate Corporate Data Accountability Act and SB 261 Climate-Related Financial Risk Act), and other national and sub-national climate disclosure regulations in jurisdictions where Apple operates or whose securities laws govern Apple's securities.

### 6.3 Stakeholder Engagement

Apple engages with environmental stakeholders — including institutional investors, environmental non-governmental organizations (ENGOs), industry coalitions, academic researchers, and policymakers — to inform strategy development, benchmark performance, and contribute to collective action on climate change and environmental protection. Key engagement forums include: RE100 (corporate renewable electricity commitment initiative), the Clean Energy Buyers Alliance (CEBA) and its international affiliates, the Responsible Business Alliance (RBA) and its environmental sustainability working groups, the World Business Council for Sustainable Development (WBCSD), and the United Nations Climate Change Conference (COP) process and associated Marrakech Partnership for Global Climate Action.

## 7. PRODUCT ENERGY EFFICIENCY

### 7.1 Silicon Efficiency Leadership

Apple's custom silicon architecture — A-series for iPhone and iPad, M-series for Mac and iPad Pro, S-series for Apple Watch, H-series for AirPods, U-series for Ultra Wideband, and R-series for Apple Vision Pro — achieves industry-leading performance-per-watt through heterogeneous compute topology (performance cores, efficiency cores, GPU cores, Neural Engine, and media engines integrated on a single SoC die), unified memory architecture (eliminating data copy energy between discrete CPU and GPU memory pools), and advanced semiconductor process nodes (TSMC 3-nanometer and 2-nanometer class for current and next-generation SoCs). This silicon efficiency flows directly to reduced product-use-phase energy consumption — the largest category of lifecycle greenhouse gas emissions for most Apple products — and enables thinner, lighter product designs with smaller battery capacity requirements.

### 7.2 ENERGY STAR and Regulatory Compliance

Apple products meet or exceed ENERGY STAR program requirements for computers, displays, and imaging equipment, and comply with the California Energy Commission Appliance Efficiency Regulations, the European Union Ecodesign Directive (Directive 2009/125/EC, recast as Regulation (EU) 2024/1781), and equivalent energy efficiency regulations in all markets where Apple products are sold. Product energy efficiency is validated through testing per applicable ENERGY STAR test methods and reported on product regulatory labels and Environmental Progress Reports.

## 8. BIODIVERSITY AND LAND STEWARDSHIP

### 8.1 Data Center Site Selection

Apple data center site selection criteria include avoidance of: critical habitat for threatened or endangered species, wetlands and riparian zones, old-growth forest, and high-biodiversity-value agricultural land. Site development includes native species landscaping, pollinator habitat creation, and conservation easement establishment on adjacent undeveloped parcels where ecologically significant habitat is present.

### 8.2 Responsible Fiber Sourcing

Apple's paper and packaging fiber is sourced from responsibly managed forests — certified to Forest Stewardship Council (FSC) standards or equivalent — or from post-consumer recycled fiber sources. Apple's fiber procurement policy prohibits sourcing from: illegal logging operations, high-conservation-value forests (as defined by the HCV Resource Network), forests being converted to plantation or non-forest use, and forests in areas of social conflict. Apple has supported the conservation or improved management of over 1 million acres of working forest (predominantly in the United States and China) through partnerships with The Conservation Fund, World Wildlife Fund, and other conservation organizations.
