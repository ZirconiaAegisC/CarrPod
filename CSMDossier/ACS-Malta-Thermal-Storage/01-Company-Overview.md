# Malta Inc — Company Overview

## Corporate Identity

Malta Inc is a long-duration energy storage technology company headquartered in Cambridge, Massachusetts. The company was spun out of Google X — Alphabet's "moonshot factory" research and development laboratory — in 2018, following several years of internal development within Google X's energy and climate technology portfolio. Malta is commercializing a technology called Pumped Heat Energy Storage (PHES), which stores electricity as thermal energy in molten salt and chilled antifreeze, then reconverts it to electricity using a heat engine.

## Leadership

**Ramya Swaminathan** serves as Chief Executive Officer, appointed in 2021. Swaminathan brings extensive experience in energy infrastructure development and finance, having previously held leadership positions at major energy companies and infrastructure investment firms. Her background includes expertise in project finance, power generation asset development, and clean energy commercialization strategy.

Malta's founding team includes engineers and scientists from Google X who developed the initial PHES concept, bringing expertise in thermodynamics, turbomachinery, thermal systems engineering, and grid-scale energy systems.

## Corporate Mission

Malta's mission is to commercialize Pumped Heat Energy Storage as a cost-effective, geographically flexible alternative to pumped storage hydropower for long-duration (10–200+ hour) grid-scale energy storage. The company's thesis is that achieving deep decarbonization of the electric grid requires storage technologies capable of economically shifting energy across multi-day and weekly timescales, for which lithium-ion batteries are structurally unsuited due to their linear cost scaling with energy capacity.

## Technology Differentiation

Pumped Heat Energy Storage is fundamentally different from electrochemical batteries. Rather than storing energy in chemical bonds, PHES stores energy as thermal potential — a temperature difference between a hot reservoir (molten salt at approximately 565°C) and a cold reservoir (chilled antifreeze at approximately −60°C). The system operates on a closed-loop thermodynamic cycle:

- **Charging:** A heat pump (essentially a reversed heat engine) uses grid electricity to extract heat from the cold reservoir and pump it into the hot reservoir, creating a large temperature differential
- **Storage:** The hot and cold fluids are stored in separate, well-insulated tanks at ambient pressure, with very low self-discharge (thermal losses of <1% per day in large-scale configurations)
- **Discharging:** A heat engine (similar to a steam turbine power plant) converts the temperature difference back into electricity by extracting heat from the hot reservoir, expanding the working fluid through a turbine, and rejecting waste heat to the cold reservoir

## Key Technology Advantages

1. **Cost doesn't scale with duration:** Adding more energy storage capacity simply requires larger (or additional) molten salt and antifreeze tanks — large, low-cost cylindrical steel tanks of the type commonly used in the oil & gas and chemical industries. Power capacity (turbomachinery) is independent of energy capacity (tank size)

2. **No rare materials:** The working fluids are molten salt (nitrate salt mixtures, similar to those used in concentrated solar power plants) and a hydrocarbon-based antifreeze. Neither involves lithium, cobalt, nickel, vanadium, or rare earth elements

3. **Established supply chain:** The turbomachinery (compressors, turbines, heat exchangers) is derived from existing power generation and industrial gas turbine technology, manufactured by established OEMs including Siemens Energy, GE, and Mitsubishi Heavy Industries

4. **Geographic flexibility:** Unlike pumped storage hydropower (which requires suitable topography and hydrology) or compressed air energy storage (which requires suitable geology), PHES can be sited anywhere with a grid interconnection — no mountains, caverns, or water bodies required

5. **Long asset life:** The system has no electrochemical degradation mechanisms. Turbomachinery can be overhauled on standard maintenance intervals (5–10 years), and tanks have 30–50+ year design lives with minimal degradation

## Key Investors and Partnerships

## Product and Market Position

Malta is developing a utility-scale PHES system with a target configuration of approximately 100 MW power output and 1,000 MWh energy capacity (10-hour duration), scalable to 200+ hours by increasing tank capacity. The company has completed a detailed engineering design and is progressing toward a first-of-a-kind commercial demonstration project.

The company has engaged with multiple electric utilities and grid operators, including a publicized evaluation by ISO New England (ISO-NE) of PHES technology for the New England grid, which faces significant winter reliability challenges due to natural gas pipeline constraints and growing renewable generation.

## Regulatory and Policy Alignment

Malta's technology aligns with multiple federal and state policy drivers:

- **IRA Section 48 ITC:** Standalone energy storage qualifies for the 30% Investment Tax Credit
- **DOE LDES Earthshot:** The Department of Energy's target of reducing LDES costs by 90% to $50/MWh by 2030 creates research, development, and demonstration funding opportunities
- **State LDES mandates:** California, New York, and other states with storage procurement targets specifically value long-duration technologies
- **FERC Order 841:** Requires ISOs/RTOs to establish market rules accommodating energy storage participation, which benefits all storage technologies including PHES
