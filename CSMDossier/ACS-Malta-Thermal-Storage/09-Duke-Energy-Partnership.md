# Duke Energy Partnership & Utility Engagement

## Duke Energy Overview

Duke Energy (NYSE: DUK) is one of the largest electric utility holding companies in the United States, serving approximately 8.2 million electric customers across six states (North Carolina, South Carolina, Florida, Indiana, Ohio, and Kentucky) through its regulated utilities: Duke Energy Carolinas, Duke Energy Progress, Duke Energy Florida, Duke Energy Indiana, and Duke Energy Ohio & Kentucky. The company operates approximately 50 GW of electric generating capacity and is one of the nation's largest operators of nuclear, coal, natural gas, and hydroelectric generating assets.

Duke Energy has announced a target of net-zero carbon emissions by 2050, with interim targets of 50% reduction from 2005 levels by 2030 and 80% reduction by 2040. The company's integrated resource plans (IRPs) filed with state public utility commissions in the Carolinas, Florida, and Indiana identify long-duration energy storage as a critical enabling technology for achieving these targets while maintaining grid reliability.

## Partnership with Malta Inc

### Nature of Engagement

Duke Energy has engaged with Malta Inc to evaluate Pumped Heat Energy Storage as a potential resource for the utility's long-duration storage needs. The engagement includes:

1. **Technical Review and Due Diligence:**
   - Duke Energy engineering teams have reviewed Malta's PHES technology design, thermodynamic cycle modeling, and component specifications
   - Duke has assessed the technology's readiness level (TRL) against its internal technology qualification framework for inclusion in IRP resource portfolios
   - The utility has evaluated PHES performance characteristics (round-trip efficiency, ramp rates, part-load behavior, self-discharge rates) against its system modeling assumptions

2. **Site Feasibility Assessment:**
   - Duke Energy has identified potential host sites within its service territories for a first commercial PHES plant
   - Site selection criteria include: available land (20–50 acres), proximity to existing transmission infrastructure, avoidance of environmentally sensitive areas, and community compatibility
   - Site-specific interconnection studies are evaluating the cost and timeline for connecting a 100–200 MW PHES plant to the Duke Energy transmission system

3. **Resource Planning Integration:**
   - Duke Energy's IRP modeling teams are incorporating PHES as a candidate resource in capacity expansion models used to determine the optimal generation and storage portfolio
   - Models compare PHES against alternatives including lithium-ion batteries, pumped storage hydropower (Bad Creek expansion), hydrogen-capable combustion turbines, and new nuclear (small modular reactors)
   - PHES competes in these models on the basis of its levelized cost of storage (LCOS), locational flexibility, and operational characteristics

### Strategic Fit with Duke's Generation Portfolio

Duke Energy's generation mix in the Carolinas is characterized by:

- **Nuclear (~40% of generation):** Six operating nuclear units (Oconee 1-3, McGuire 1-2, Catawba 1) plus the retired/planned retirement units. Nuclear provides base-load, zero-carbon generation but is inflexible (cannot economically ramp to follow load)
- **Coal (~20%, declining):** Multiple coal units planned for retirement by 2035 under Duke's carbon reduction plan. Coal retirements create a need for replacement firm capacity
- **Natural Gas (~30%, growing):** Combined-cycle and combustion turbine units providing baseload and peaking capacity. Duke's IRP includes a significant buildout of new natural gas capacity, which has drawn criticism from environmental groups
- **Solar (~10%, growing rapidly):** Duke has proposed significant solar additions in compliance with North Carolina's HB 951 (2021), which requires 70% reduction in CO₂ emissions from 2005 levels by 2030 and carbon neutrality by 2050
- **Pumped Storage Hydro:** Duke operates the Bad Creek and Jocassee pumped storage facilities in South Carolina; an expansion of Bad Creek (adding 1,200 MW) is under evaluation

PHES fits into this portfolio as a long-duration storage resource that can:

- Provide firm, dispatchable capacity (4–24+ hours) to replace retiring coal units without increasing natural gas dependence
- Absorb excess solar generation during midday peaks and shift it to evening and morning demand peaks
- Provide winter reliability in a region where winter peak demand is growing and natural gas pipeline constraints (Transco, Columbia Gulf) create fuel security concerns during cold weather events
- Complement the existing pumped storage hydro fleet, providing geographic diversity (PHES does not require mountainous terrain)

### Regulatory Context

Duke Energy's ability to procure PHES capacity is governed by state regulatory processes:

- **North Carolina Utilities Commission (NCUC):** Must approve new generating resources through the IRP process and Certificate of Public Convenience and Necessity (CPCN) proceedings. The NCUC has shown willingness to approve innovative technologies when supported by strong cost-benefit analysis
- **South Carolina Public Service Commission (SCPSC):** Similar IRP and CPCN framework. South Carolina's Act 62 (2020) reformed energy regulation and created an explicit preference for least-cost resources
- **North Carolina Clean Energy Plan:** Created by Executive Order 80 (2018) and codified by HB 951 (2021), this plan emphasizes the role of storage in achieving carbon reduction targets

### Utility Partnership Model

Malta's engagement with Duke Energy follows a model common for new energy technologies:

1. **Phase 1: Technology Evaluation (current phase)**
   - Engineering review, site screening, IRP modeling
   - Duration: 1–2 years
   - Cost: Borne by both parties (Duke internal resources, Malta business development resources)

2. **Phase 2: Pre-Commercial Demonstration (next phase)**
   - A smaller-scale or first commercial-scale plant at a Duke Energy site
   - Potential cost-sharing with DOE (OCED LDES demonstrations program) or state economic development incentives
   - Operational data collection to validate performance and de-risk financing for subsequent commercial projects

3. **Phase 3: Commercial Procurement**
   - Full-scale commercial PHES plants procured through Duke's competitive resource solicitation or bilateral negotiation
   - Duke may own and operate the plants (regulated utility model) or contract with an independent power producer (IPP) through a power purchase agreement (PPA)

## Other Utility Engagement

Beyond Duke Energy, Malta has engaged with additional utilities, including:

### ISO New England (ISO-NE) Evaluation
Malta participated in ISO-NE's evaluation of long-duration energy storage technologies as resources for the New England grid, which faces unique challenges:

- **Winter reliability risk:** New England relies heavily on natural gas for both power generation and heating; during cold snaps, pipeline constraints limit gas availability for power generation, creating reliability events (e.g., the December 2017–January 2018 cold snap that resulted in emergency operating procedures)
- **Limited geographic options:** New England has limited pumped storage hydro potential due to topography and environmental constraints, making siting-flexible storage technologies particularly attractive
- **Offshore wind integration:** Massachusetts, Rhode Island, and Connecticut have committed to significant offshore wind procurement (30+ GW by 2040), creating multi-day intermittency challenges that short-duration storage cannot address

ISO-NE's evaluation provides independent technical validation that supports Malta's technology claims with other RTO/ISO planning organizations (PJM, MISO, SPP, CAISO, NYISO).

### Additional Utility Discussions
Malta has indicated engagement with other large electric utilities and independent power producers, though specific names beyond Duke Energy and ISO-NE have not been publicly disclosed. The utility engagement strategy is focused on:

- Large, vertically integrated utilities in the Southeast and Midwest (where net-zero commitments are driving LDES interest)
- IPPs developing projects for organized wholesale markets (CAISO, ERCOT, PJM)
- International utilities in markets with strong renewable integration challenges (Europe, Australia, Japan)
