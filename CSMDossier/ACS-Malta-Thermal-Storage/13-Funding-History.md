# Funding History & Capital Strategy

## Financing Rounds

Malta Inc has raised over $100 million in cumulative equity financing since its spinout from Google X in 2018. The company's funding history reflects the evolution from early-stage technology development to pre-commercial engineering and project development.

### Seed / Incubation (Pre-2018)
Prior to incorporation as an independent company, the PHES technology was developed within Google X, Alphabet's research and development laboratory. Google X provided multi-year funding for the initial concept development, proof-of-concept testing, and intellectual property creation. The resources invested during this incubation period are not reflected in Malta's post-spinout fundraising totals but represent a substantial pre-history of technical de-risking. Google X projects are typically subject to rigorous internal review (the "Foundry" process) before being approved as spinout-worthy, providing an initial quality filter.

### Series A (2018–2019)
Malta's initial venture capital round was led by Breakthrough Energy Ventures (BEV), the Bill Gates-founded climate technology investment fund. The round also included participation from other early-stage climate technology investors. The Series A funding — estimated at $20–30 million — supported:

- Technology development and engineering of the PHES thermodynamic cycle
- Subscale component testing and validation
- Initial intellectual property development and patent filings
- Building the founding engineering team in Cambridge, Massachusetts

### Series B (2020–2022)

### Government Funding

In addition to private equity, Malta has accessed government funding:

**DOE ARPA-E (Advanced Research Projects Agency-Energy):**
ARPA-E's DAYS (Duration Addition to electricitY Storage) program provided early-stage research funding for long-duration storage technologies. Malta (or the predecessor Google X team) may have received ARPA-E DAYS funding, which supports high-risk, high-reward energy storage concepts through structured milestones and technical oversight.

**DOE OCED (Office of Clean Energy Demonstrations):**
Malta has been identified as a potential recipient of funding under the Bipartisan Infrastructure Law's $505 million Long-Duration Energy Storage Demonstrations program. A Front-End Engineering Design (FEED) study award would support the detailed engineering required to advance the first commercial plant from concept to construction-ready status.

**State Economic Development Incentives:**
As Malta advances its first commercial project, state-level incentives become relevant. Potential host states may offer:
- Property tax abatements (reducing the carrying cost of the plant's assessed value)
- Investment tax credits (state-level credits supplementing the federal ITC)
- Infrastructure grants (roads, water, sewer to the project site)
- Workforce training grants (for plant operators and maintenance technicians)

## Capital Strategy and Future Funding Needs

### Path to First Commercial Plant
The first commercial PHES plant will require total capital of approximately $200–300 million (including all development, engineering, procurement, construction, and commissioning costs). Malta's capital strategy to reach this milestone includes:

1. **Existing equity:** The ~$100 million raised to date funds the company through engineering design, site selection, and initial procurement activities (long-lead turbomachinery components)

2. **DOE cost-sharing:** Cooperative agreement funding from the DOE OCED LDES demonstrations program could provide $50–100 million (typically 50% federal cost share), reducing the equity requirement

3. **Project finance debt:** Once the first plant has a power purchase agreement (PPA), interconnection agreement, and EPC contract in place, project finance debt (non-recourse or limited-recourse to Malta corporate) can fund a significant portion of construction costs. Typical debt-to-equity ratios for power generation project finance are 60:40 to 80:20, depending on technology maturity and revenue certainty

4. **Tax equity:** The 30% ITC (and potential bonus adders) can be monetized through tax equity investment, where a financial institution with sufficient tax appetite (JP Morgan, Bank of America, US Bank) invests in the project in exchange for the tax credits. Tax equity typically provides 30–40% of project capital

5. **Strategic partner equity:** Siemens Energy, Alfa Laval, or other strategic partners may invest project-level equity to demonstrate commitment, align incentives, and participate in project returns

### Corporate Capital Structure

As a private company, Malta's capital structure is not publicly disclosed. However, typical characteristics of a growth-stage clean energy technology company include:

- **Common equity:** Founder and employee shares, venture capital preferred shares (Series A, Series B), and possibly a Series C round in progress
- **Convertible notes:** Bridge financing between priced equity rounds, typically converting at a discount to the next round's price
- **Venture debt:** Loans from specialized venture debt providers (Silicon Valley Bank, Hercules Capital, Horizon Technology Finance) secured by the company's assets and intellectual property. Venture debt can extend cash runway between equity rounds but adds fixed repayment obligations
- **Government grants and cooperative agreements:** Non-dilutive funding that does not require equity in return

### Exit Strategy Considerations

As a venture-backed private company, Malta's investors require a path to liquidity (return of capital plus gain). Potential exit paths include:

1. **Initial Public Offering (IPO):** Listing on a public exchange (NYSE or NASDAQ) would provide liquidity for existing investors and access to public equity markets for future growth capital. However, IPO markets for pre-revenue clean energy technology companies have been challenging since the 2021 SPAC wave subsided. Malta would likely need to demonstrate a clear path to commercial revenue (signed PPAs, construction-start on first plant) before an IPO is viable

2. **Acquisition by a Strategic Buyer:** A major industrial company in the power generation or energy infrastructure sector could acquire Malta to internalize the PHES technology. Potential strategic acquirers include:
   - Siemens Energy (already a key partner; a natural acquirer if PHES proves commercially viable)
   - GE Vernova (GE's power generation and electrification business, with complementary turbomachinery and grid technology portfolios)
   - Mitsubishi Heavy Industries (global power generation equipment OEM with growing energy storage interests)
   - Major EPC contractors (Bechtel, Fluor, McDermott) seeking to differentiate in the energy transition construction market
   - Large electric utilities (Duke Energy, NextEra Energy, Dominion) seeking to own differentiated LDES technology

3. **Project Finance Monetization:** Rather than an IPO or acquisition, Malta could monetize its technology through a "development capital" model similar to how solar and wind developers operate: develop PHES project pipeline, secure PPAs and project financing, construct projects, and sell ownership stakes to infrastructure investors (pension funds, infrastructure funds, yieldcos) upon commercial operation, retaining a development fee and ongoing asset management fees. This generates recurring cash flow without requiring an exit event for corporate shareholders

### Capital Efficiency Considerations

Malta's capital strategy emphasizes an "asset-light" approach relative to manufacturing-centric storage companies (e.g., ESS Inc's $500 million+ investment in manufacturing automation). By partnering with existing OEMs (Siemens Energy for turbomachinery, Alfa Laval for heat exchangers) rather than building in-house manufacturing capability, Malta:

- Reduces upfront capital requirements (avoids factory construction costs)
- Leverages partners' existing factories, supply chains, and quality systems
- Converts fixed manufacturing costs into variable costs (pay per unit rather than maintaining underutilized capacity during early years)
- Shares technology risk with partners who have diversified product portfolios

The trade-off is lower potential gross margins (partners earn their margin on supplied components) and dependency on partners' continued commitment and performance. This trade-off is common in capital-intensive industries where technology developers focus on system integration and project development rather than vertically integrated manufacturing.
