# Lessons and Technology Rescue: A Post-Mortem Analysis

## Executive Summary

Gravitricity's voluntary liquidation on 1 October 2025 is a case study in the challenges
of commercialising capital-intensive, long-duration energy storage technology in a
venture-capital-funded start-up. The core technology was technically sound and validated
at demonstrator scale, but the company could not bridge from demonstrator to commercial
deployment before exhausting its financial resources. This analysis identifies the key
lessons and assesses prospects for the technology's rescue.

## Root Causes of Failure

### 1. Capital Structure Mismatch

The most fundamental cause of failure was the mismatch between capital requirements and
funding sources. Developing a first-of-a-kind 2 MW installation required approximately
£12–£15 million in project finance. A venture-funded start-up with an annual burn rate of
£2–£3 million and no revenue is inherently fragile—one failed fundraising round is fatal.

The capital structure (equity, grants, crowdfunding) was appropriate for concept and
demonstrator phases but wholly inadequate for commercial deployment. Successful energy
storage projects of the past decade have been financed by regulated utilities,
infrastructure funds, or state-backed entities with balance sheets capable of absorbing
construction risk.

### 2. The £40 Million Fundraising Gap

The 2023 fundraise represented an inflection point. The quantum suggests management
recognised the need to transition from technology development to project development—a
transition requiring an order-of-magnitude increase in capital.

The fundraising encountered a structural impediment: institutional infrastructure
investors require revenue-generating operating assets or construction-ready projects with
off-take agreements. Venture capital investors are uncomfortable with the capital intensity
and long payback periods of infrastructure. Gravitricity fell between these two categories
and satisfied neither.

### 3. The Hydrogen Pivot: Right Idea, Wrong Timing

The H2FlexiStore pivot was strategically rational: it addressed inter-seasonal storage,
leveraged core shaft engineering competencies, and generated Ofgem SIF grant income.
However, by the time H2FlexiStore produced credible outputs (mid-2024), Gravitricity's
financial position was already critical. The hydrogen storage market is structurally
further from commercialisation than gravity storage, so the pivot stretched the timeline
to revenue rather than shortening it.

### 4. The Reference Project Paradox

Investors require a commercial-scale reference project to commit capital, but building a
reference project requires capital. The Port of Leith demonstrator was technically
successful but, at 250 kW, did not constitute a reference project at infrastructure scale
(2–10 MW). The Pyhäsalmi project was intended to break this deadlock but depended on the
equity fundraising.

Potential IP acquirers face the same paradox and must address it through: balance-sheet
financing by a large corporate; government co-financing (Business Finland, EU Innovation
Fund); or a joint venture with a mine owner contributing shaft access as in-kind equity.

### 5. Technology Competition and Narrative

The emergence of Energy Vault (NYSE-listed, raised $300M+) created investor confusion.
Competitors conflated Energy Vault's above-ground crane approach with Gravitricity's
underground shaft system, and associated Gravitricity with Energy Vault's subsequent 90%
share price collapse.

Simultaneously, lithium-ion BESS costs fell approximately 80% from 2013 to 2023
(BloombergNEF), narrowing the LCOS advantage for gravity storage at short durations.
GraviStore's advantages in cycle life, degradation, and long-duration applications remain
valid, but the economic headroom narrowed as BESS costs fell.

## Technology Rescue: Pathway and Prospects

### The Technology Is Worth Saving

GraviStore's technical fundamentals—validated at demonstrator scale, independently
assessed by Imperial College London, and supported by an ABB partnership—are sound. The
technology addresses a genuine need for long-duration, high-cycle storage without
electrochemical supply chain exposure, with a credible pathway to deployment at Pyhäsalmi.

### Recommended Acquisition Strategy

For a new owner to succeed where Gravitricity failed:

1. **Balance-sheet ownership**: The acquirer must have a balance sheet capable of funding
   the first commercial project without external equity fundraising. Venture capital is
   not appropriate for this stage.

2. **Anchor project commitment**: The first project should be fully funded at
   acquisition, with a clear timeline to commercial operation. The reference project
   paradox can only be broken by committing capital.

3. **Patient timeline**: Revenue should not be expected before Year 2–3. The acquirer
   must accept this and fund corporate overheads accordingly.

4. **Shaft owner partnership**: The acquirer should partner with a mine owner who can
   contribute shaft access as in-kind equity, reducing cash requirements.

5. **Ancillary service focus**: The first project should prioritise frequency response
   and grid balancing markets, which generate higher revenue per MW than energy arbitrage
   and better leverage the sub-second response time advantage.

6. **Modular scale-up**: The first project should be 1–2 MW—sufficient as a commercial
   reference while minimising capital at risk.

### Conclusion

Gravitricity's failure is a financing failure, not a technology failure. The underlying
engineering is credible, the market need is genuine, and the Pyhäsalmi project represents
a tangible, near-term deployment opportunity. The IP sale via Hilco (deadline 25 February
2026) offers a rare opportunity to acquire a validated, patented energy storage platform
at a distressed valuation. The successful acquirer will combine patient capital with
mining engineering capability and a willingness to commit to a first-of-a-kind project.
