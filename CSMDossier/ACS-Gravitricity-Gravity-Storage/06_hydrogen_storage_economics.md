# Hydrogen Storage Economics and Market Context

## UK Hydrogen Strategy Context

The UK government published its Hydrogen Strategy in August 2021, targeting 5 GW of low-
carbon hydrogen production capacity by 2030, with an ambition of 10 GW subject to cost
reduction and market development. The strategy identifies hydrogen as critical for
decarbonising industrial clusters, heavy transport, and providing inter-seasonal energy
storage for a power system dominated by intermittent renewables.

By 2035, the UK will require approximately 10–20 TWh of inter-seasonal hydrogen storage
to balance a net-zero electricity system in which wind and solar provide 70–80% of
annual generation. Salt caverns in Cheshire and Teesside can provide perhaps 2–5 TWh.
The remaining storage deficit must be met by alternative technologies—creating a
potential market for lined rock shaft storage.

## Levelised Cost of Hydrogen Storage

Gravitricity's economic modelling for H2FlexiStore estimated a levelised cost of
hydrogen storage (LCHS) of £0.15–£0.35 per kilogram of hydrogen stored and recovered
annually, depending on shaft depth, diameter, and rock conditions. The primary cost
drivers are:

1. Shaft excavation: £5,000–£15,000 per metre of depth for mechanical excavation in
   competent rock, varying with diameter and rock hardness. Raise boring (pilot hole
   then reaming from below) is typically the most economical method for shafts of
   2–5 metres diameter.

2. Steel liner: £2,000–£5,000 per metre for the gas-tight liner, including material,
   fabrication, welding, and non-destructive testing of circumferential and longitudinal
   welds.

3. Grouting: £500–£1,500 per metre for cement grout to fill the annular space, providing
   load transfer and secondary containment.

4. Surface equipment: £5–£15 million for the electrolyser, compressor, fuel cell (or
   hydrogen turbine), and balance of plant, depending on power rating.

For a reference design of 500 metres depth, 3 metres diameter, storing 53 tonnes of
hydrogen (1.75 GWh), the total capital expenditure was estimated at £12–£25 million,
yielding an LCHS at the lower end of the range quoted above.

## Revenue Stack

H2FlexiStore was conceived to generate revenue from multiple sources:

1. Wholesale electricity arbitrage: Charging (hydrogen production) during periods of
   low or negative wholesale electricity prices, and discharging (electricity generation)
   during periods of high prices. The spark spread for hydrogen storage is fundamentally
   less favourable than for battery storage due to the lower round-trip efficiency
   (35–42% versus 85–90%), requiring a larger price differential to achieve profitability.

2. Grid balancing services: Providing long-duration reserve and contingency services to
   the Electricity System Operator (ESO), particularly during extended periods of low
   wind output (Dunkelflaute events). The 2021–2022 European gas crisis demonstrated the
   value of multi-day to multi-week storage during extreme weather events.

3. Hydrogen offtake: Direct sale of stored hydrogen to industrial consumers, transport
   applications, or injection into the gas grid (subject to blending limits, currently
   proposed at up to 20% hydrogen by volume in the UK). Direct offtake avoids the
   efficiency penalty of reconversion to electricity.

4. Constraint management: Absorbing curtailed wind generation that would otherwise be
   spilled, monetising energy that has zero or negative market value at the time of
   production.

## Development Premium

A key economic insight is that H2FlexiStore can be developed alongside GraviStore in the
same shaft—the weight travel zone occupies the upper portion of the shaft, while the
lower portion (below the deepest weight position) can be used for hydrogen storage. This
shared infrastructure concept reduces the effective cost per unit of energy storage
capacity by amortising the shaft construction or rehabilitation cost across both storage
modalities.

## Commercial Viability Assessment

Despite the theoretical economic case, H2FlexiStore faced two fundamental challenges that
prevented it from becoming a commercial lifeline for Gravitricity:

First, the hydrogen storage market in the UK remains nascent. There is no regulatory
framework for hydrogen storage business models (unlike the cap-and-floor regime
established for electricity interconnectors, or the Contracts for Difference for
generation). Without a revenue support mechanism, private capital is unlikely to
finance first-of-a-kind hydrogen storage infrastructure.

Second, the timeline for hydrogen storage revenue realisation extends well into the
2030s, when the UK hydrogen economy is expected to reach meaningful scale. For a company
burning cash in 2023–2024 and unable to raise equity, a revenue stream materialising in
2035 was not a viable survival strategy.
