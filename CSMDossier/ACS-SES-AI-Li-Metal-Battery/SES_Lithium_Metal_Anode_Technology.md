# SES AI — Lithium-Metal Anode Technology

## Fundamental Principle
Conventional lithium-ion batteries use a graphite anode (372 mAh/g theoretical capacity)
that intercalates lithium ions. A lithium-metal anode replaces graphite entirely, using
metallic lithium as the anode host. The theoretical specific capacity of lithium metal
is 3,860 mAh/g — roughly 10× that of graphite — enabling a step-change in cell-level
energy density.

## Anode Architecture
SES's lithium-metal anode is an ultra-thin lithium foil (typically 20–50 µm) deposited
on a copper current collector. During charging, lithium plates directly onto the anode
surface rather than intercalating into a host structure. This eliminates the "dead weight"
of the graphite anode, which constitutes ~15–25% of a conventional cell's volume and mass.

## The Dendrite Problem
The central challenge of lithium-metal batteries is dendrite formation: during repeated
charge/discharge cycles, lithium deposits unevenly, forming needle-like protrusions
(dendrites) that can pierce the separator, short-circuit the cell, and cause thermal
runaway. This has been the single largest barrier to lithium-metal commercialization
since the technology was first explored in the 1970s by Exxon researcher M. Stanley
Whittingham (Nobel Prize 2019).

## SES Solution — Composite Electrolyte
Rather than replacing the liquid electrolyte with a solid ceramic (as QuantumScape and
Solid Power do), SES employs a proprietary composite liquid electrolyte. This formulation
combines:
1. **High-concentration lithium salt** — a "solvent-in-salt" approach that shifts the
   solvation structure, reducing free solvent molecules available for parasitic reactions.
2. **Fluorinated co-solvents** — proprietary additives that form a stable, self-healing
   solid-electrolyte interphase (SEI) on the lithium-metal surface.
3. **Ionic liquid additives** — reduce flammability and improve thermal stability.

The composite electrolyte promotes uniform, planar lithium deposition (rather than
filamentary growth), effectively suppressing dendrite formation through electrochemical
means rather than mechanical (ceramic separator) means.

## Manufacturing Advantage
Because the electrolyte remains liquid, SES can use conventional lithium-ion manufacturing
equipment — coating, winding/stacking, electrolyte filling, formation — with minor
modifications. Solid-state battery production, by contrast, requires specialized dry-room
infrastructure, high-pressure sintering, and precision stacking that dramatically increase
capital costs. SES estimates its manufacturing CapEx per GWh to be 40–60% lower than
solid-state competitors.

## Cycle Life Performance
SES has demonstrated 800+ cycles at 80% capacity retention in pouch-cell format under
automotive-relevant conditions (0.5C charge, 1C discharge, 25°C, 2.5–4.3V). Further
improvements are targeted through electrolyte optimization and pressure management.

## Pressure Management
Lithium-metal anodes require external stack pressure (typically 50–300 kPa) to maintain
interfacial contact during cycling. SES's cell-pack integration design incorporates
spring-loaded compression plates and foam interlayers to maintain uniform pressure
distribution across the cell surface.
