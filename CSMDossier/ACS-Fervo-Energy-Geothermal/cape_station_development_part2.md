## Power Plant Configuration and Technology

The surface power generation facilities at Cape Station will use binary-cycle technology, the standard for moderate-temperature geothermal resources. The binary-cycle thermodynamic cycle transfers heat from the geothermal fluid (hot water at 150-200°C) to a secondary working fluid with a lower boiling point, which vaporizes and drives a turbine-generator. The geothermal fluid, having yielded its heat, is reinjected into the reservoir through the injection wells, creating a closed-loop water circulation system.

### Binary-Cycle Efficiency

The thermal-to-electric conversion efficiency of a binary-cycle plant is a function of the geothermal fluid inlet temperature. At 175°C (the approximate resource temperature achieved at Project Red), the first-law efficiency (electricity output divided by thermal energy input) is approximately 12-14%. This efficiency is low by the standards of fossil fuel power plants (combined-cycle natural gas plants achieve 55-60% efficiency) because the low source temperature limits the thermodynamic efficiency of the Rankine cycle.

However, efficiency is less important for geothermal economics than resource temperature and flow rate because the fuel — geothermal heat — has zero marginal cost. A geothermal plant that extracts 100 MW of thermal energy from the subsurface at 14% efficiency produces 14 MW of electricity, but the "fuel" cost is the cost of pumping water through the reservoir, which is a small fraction of revenue. The dominant costs are capital (wells and plant) rather than operating.

### Working Fluid Selection

The secondary working fluid in a binary-cycle plant — the fluid that vaporizes at a lower temperature than water and drives the turbine — is a key design variable. Common working fluids include isobutane, isopentane, and various refrigerants (R-134a, R-245fa). The selection depends on the geothermal fluid temperature and the ambient cooling temperature.

For the resource temperatures expected at Cape Station (175-200°C), isobutane or isopentane are likely candidates. These hydrocarbons have well-characterized thermodynamic properties, are commercially available, and have been used in geothermal binary plants for decades. Their flammability requires appropriate safety systems — hydrocarbon detection, ventilation, fire suppression — which are standard in geothermal plant design.

### Air-Cooled Condensers

The choice of cooling technology has significant implications for plant performance, water consumption, and cost. Binary-cycle geothermal plants can use either water-cooled condensers (which require a continuous supply of cooling water and lose a fraction of that water to evaporation) or air-cooled condensers (which use ambient air as the cooling medium and consume no water).

In the arid environment of southwestern Utah, water availability is a constraint, and Fervo has committed to using air-cooled condensers at Cape Station. Air-cooled condensers are more expensive than water-cooled systems — roughly 20-30% higher capital cost — and result in lower plant efficiency during hot weather (when the air temperature approaches the condensing temperature of the working fluid), but they eliminate water consumption for cooling, simplifying the project's water management and regulatory compliance.

The efficiency penalty of air cooling in hot weather can be mitigated by designing the plant for a higher condensing temperature, which reduces power output during the hottest hours but avoids the cost of an oversized condenser. The value of the electricity not produced during a few hundred hours per year of peak ambient temperature is typically small relative to the capital cost of sizing the condenser for those conditions.

## Transmission and Market Access

Cape Station's location near the Intermountain Power Project transmission corridor provides access to one of the most valuable electricity markets in the United States: the California Independent System Operator (CAISO) market serving Southern California. California's electricity market values geothermal power for three reasons: it qualifies under the state's Renewable Portfolio Standard (which requires 60% renewable electricity by 2030 and 100% carbon-free by 2045), it provides round-the-clock generation that complements intermittent wind and solar, and it can be contracted through long-term power purchase agreements that provide price certainty to both buyer and seller.

### Interconnection Process

Connecting a 400 MW generator to the transmission grid requires an interconnection study process administered by the transmission provider — in this case, the CAISO for deliveries into the California market, or PacifiCorp for deliveries within Utah. The interconnection study evaluates the impact of the new generation on grid stability, identifies any transmission upgrades required to accommodate the additional power flow, and estimates the cost of those upgrades.

The interconnection queue for CAISO is notoriously congested, with projects often waiting years for study results. However, geothermal projects have an advantage in the queue relative to solar and wind because they are "network resource" generators — they can be dispatched reliably and have a high capacity credit, meaning the transmission system can count on their output being available when needed. This reliability may accelerate the interconnection process relative to variable renewables.

### Power Purchase Agreement Structure

The revenue of a geothermal power plant is determined by the power purchase agreement (PPA) — the long-term contract between the generator and the electricity buyer that specifies the price to be paid for electricity over the contract term (typically 15-25 years). PPA pricing for geothermal power in the western United States has historically been in the range of $60-90/MWh, reflecting the resource's firm, dispatchable nature and its qualification for renewable energy credits.

The Google PPA for 5 MW from Cape Station — the first corporate PPA for geothermal power — is a landmark transaction that establishes a commercial precedent for corporate offtake of EGS power. The pricing of the Google PPA has not been publicly disclosed, but the transaction's significance lies less in the specific price than in the demonstration that corporate buyers — a large and growing segment of the renewable energy market — are willing to contract for geothermal power, opening a new demand channel beyond the traditional utility offtake.
