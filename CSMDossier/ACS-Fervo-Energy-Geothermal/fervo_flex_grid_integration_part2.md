## Integration with Renewable-Dominated Grids

The value proposition of flexible geothermal generation is most compelling in electricity systems with high penetration of variable renewable energy. In such systems, the midday suppression of wholesale prices by solar generation and the evening price spike as solar declines create the price spreads that flexible operation exploits. As renewable penetration increases — California targets 100% carbon-free electricity by 2045 — the value of flexible, dispatchable, carbon-free generation is expected to increase.

### The Role of Geothermal in Resource Adequacy

Grid operators maintain resource adequacy — sufficient generation capacity to meet peak demand plus a reserve margin — through capacity markets or resource adequacy requirements. Generators that can be relied upon to be available during peak demand periods receive capacity payments in addition to energy payments, compensating them for the capital cost of maintaining generation capacity.

Variable renewables (wind and solar) have low capacity credits — the percentage of nameplate capacity that the grid operator counts toward resource adequacy — because their output is weather-dependent and cannot be guaranteed during peak demand periods. A solar plant in California might have a capacity credit of 15-25%, meaning that only 15-25% of its nameplate capacity is counted toward resource adequacy requirements. A geothermal plant, by contrast, has a capacity credit approaching 100% because it can be dispatched at any time regardless of weather.

This differential in capacity credit translates to a revenue differential: a geothermal plant earns capacity payments on essentially its full nameplate capacity, while a solar plant earns capacity payments on only a fraction of its capacity. In markets with substantial capacity payments — CAISO's resource adequacy program, PJM's capacity market — this differential can be worth $10-30 per kilowatt-year, representing a significant revenue stream that is independent of energy prices.

### Grid Stability and Inertia

Conventional generators — coal, natural gas, nuclear, geothermal — provide inertia to the grid: the rotating mass of their turbines and generators resists changes in grid frequency, providing a stabilizing effect that is essential for grid reliability. Wind and solar generators, which connect to the grid through power electronics (inverters) rather than rotating machines, do not provide inertia unless specifically equipped to do so (synthetic inertia from grid-forming inverters).

As conventional generators are retired and replaced by inverter-based resources, grid inertia declines, potentially compromising frequency stability. Geothermal plants, as synchronous generators with substantial rotating mass, provide inertia that becomes increasingly valuable as the penetration of inverter-based resources increases.

The economic value of inertia is difficult to quantify in current electricity markets, which generally do not have explicit pricing for inertia provision. However, grid operators in markets with high renewable penetration — particularly ERCOT (Texas) and the Australian National Electricity Market — are developing market mechanisms to value and compensate inertia provision as a distinct grid service. If such mechanisms are adopted, geothermal plants that provide inertia would capture an additional revenue stream.

### Seasonal and Multi-Day Flexibility

The flexibility of geothermal generation extends beyond intra-day ramping to encompass seasonal and multi-day variations in output. This capability distinguishes geothermal from battery storage, which is limited to intra-day energy shifting (charge during the day, discharge during the evening), and from pumped hydro storage, which typically operates on daily or weekly cycles.

Seasonal flexibility is valuable in electricity systems with high solar penetration, where the seasonal profile of solar generation (high in summer, low in winter) does not align with the seasonal profile of electricity demand (which varies by region but often peaks in summer for cooling or winter for heating). A geothermal plant that can vary its output seasonally — producing more in winter when solar output is low and less in summer when solar is abundant — captures additional revenue from seasonal price spreads.

The operational implications of seasonal cycling on reservoir performance are less well-characterized than the implications of intra-day cycling, because most geothermal plants historically have been operated at constant output. Fervo's operational experience with Project Red and the early phases of Cape Station will provide the data required to validate reservoir performance under variable operating regimes.

## The Future of Flexible Geothermal

The FervoFlex technology positions geothermal as a complement to, rather than a competitor with, wind and solar generation. In a future electricity system dominated by low-cost variable renewables, geothermal's role is to provide the firm, flexible, carbon-free capacity that the system requires to maintain reliability — a role that batteries, hydrogen, and demand response also aspire to fill.

### Technology Development Priorities

Continued development of FervoFlex capability requires advances in several areas: reservoir models that accurately predict thermal and hydraulic response to variable production, control systems that optimize plant output in real time based on wholesale price signals, and power plant designs that accommodate frequent ramping without excessive thermal stress on equipment.

The intersection of reservoir engineering and electricity market participation — the discipline of optimizing subsurface operations to maximize revenue in wholesale electricity markets — is a domain where Fervo's Houston location and oilfield engineering culture provide a competitive advantage. The same analytical methods used to optimize oil and gas production from complex reservoirs — numerical simulation, history matching, production optimization — are applicable to geothermal reservoirs, and the workforce that has developed these skills in the oil and gas industry is available for deployment in geothermal.

### Market Structure Evolution

The value of flexible geothermal generation depends on market structures that compensate generators for the full range of services they provide — energy, capacity, ancillary services, and potentially inertia and other reliability services. In markets where these services are valued and compensated separately (such as CAISO and PJM), flexible geothermal can capture multiple revenue streams that collectively justify the investment. In markets where these services are not separately compensated (such as vertically integrated utilities in the Southeast), the value of flexibility is captured only through the energy price, which may not fully reflect the system value of flexible, dispatchable, carbon-free generation.

Market structures are evolving toward greater recognition of the value of flexible, firm, carbon-free generation. The Federal Energy Regulatory Commission (FERC) has initiated proceedings to reform transmission planning and generator interconnection processes to better accommodate the changing generation mix. State-level policies — California's resource adequacy program, New York's Zero Emission Credit program — are creating revenue mechanisms for carbon-free, dispatchable generation. These policy developments, if sustained, will enhance the economic case for FervoFlex and for EGS development more broadly.

### The 90 GW Vision

The Department of Energy's 90 GW geothermal by 2050 vision implies a fundamental transformation of the geothermal industry from a niche resource providing less than 0.5% of US electricity to a mainstream generation technology providing 8-10% of US electricity. Achieving this vision requires not only cost reduction — the DOE Earthshot target of $45/MWh — but also the demonstration that geothermal can provide the flexible, dispatchable generation that the grid of the future requires. FervoFlex is the operational capability that positions EGS to fulfill this role, making it as central to Fervo's long-term strategy as the drilling and reservoir engineering capabilities that underpin the EGS resource itself.
