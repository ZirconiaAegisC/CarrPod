## The DOE Earthshot and Cost Reduction Trajectory

The Department of Energy's Enhanced Geothermal Shot, announced in 2022, set a target of reducing the levelized cost of electricity from EGS to $45 per megawatt-hour by 2035 — an approximately 90% reduction from the cost of the first-generation EGS demonstration projects. This target is ambitious but grounded in analysis of the learning curve that the oil and gas industry achieved in reducing the cost of horizontal drilling and hydraulic fracturing over the preceding two decades.

### Learning from Unconventional Oil and Gas

The cost of drilling and completing horizontal wells in shale formations declined by approximately 60-70% between 2010 and 2020, driven by improvements in drilling efficiency (faster penetration rates, longer bit life), completion efficiency (more stages per day, higher proppant loading), and supply chain development (dedicated pressure pumping fleets, regional sand mines). These improvements were achieved through the cumulative effect of thousands of wells drilled and completed by an industry that was highly motivated to reduce costs.

Fervo's thesis is that EGS well costs will follow a similar trajectory as drilling contractors, service companies, and operators accumulate experience with the specific requirements of geothermal drilling. The challenges of geothermal drilling — higher temperatures requiring specialized downhole equipment, harder rock formations requiring more durable drill bits, and the requirement for larger-diameter wellbores to accommodate higher flow rates — are different in degree but not in kind from the challenges that the oil and gas industry has overcome.

### The Cost Stack of an EGS Project

The capital cost of an EGS power project is dominated by drilling and completion (50-60% of total project cost), followed by the surface power plant (20-25%), and balance of plant including gathering systems, injection pumps, and grid interconnection (15-25%). The dominance of drilling and completion costs means that the cost reduction trajectory of the EGS industry is primarily a function of well cost reduction.

A typical EGS well pair — one injection well and one production well — for a 5-10 MW power block costs $15-25 million at current pricing, depending on depth, geology, and regional cost factors. At these well costs, the levelized cost of electricity from EGS is in the range of $80-120/MWh — higher than wind and solar but competitive with other dispatchable, carbon-free generation technologies including nuclear and natural gas with carbon capture.

Reducing well costs to $7-12 million per pair — a 50% reduction — would bring EGS into direct cost competition with combined-cycle natural gas at $40-60/MWh, assuming continued technology improvement in binary-cycle power plant efficiency and reductions in balance-of-plant costs. This is the economic objective that Fervo and the broader EGS industry are pursuing.

### Fervo's Cost Reduction Strategy

Fervo's approach to cost reduction combines technology improvement with manufacturing discipline. On the technology side, the company is investing in: extended-reach horizontal drilling to maximize the reservoir volume accessed from a single well pair; optimized fracture stimulation designs that create more effective heat transfer surface area per unit of pumped fluid; and advanced drilling motors and drill bits capable of operating at the elevated temperatures encountered in geothermal wells.

On the manufacturing side, Fervo is applying the "factory drilling" approach pioneered in shale development: standardizing well designs, building a dedicated supply chain for geothermal-specific equipment, and developing a trained workforce that can execute drilling programs with increasing efficiency over successive wells. The factory drilling approach treats each well not as a unique engineering project but as a unit of production in a manufacturing process — an approach that has proven highly effective in reducing costs in unconventional oil and gas.

The DOE grant funding — $244 million — is directed toward demonstrating that these cost reductions can be achieved in practice at the Cape Station project. The grant serves both to reduce Fervo's capital burden during the demonstration phase and to signal government commitment to the EGS industry, which encourages private capital to participate in project financing.

## Environmental Considerations

EGS development raises environmental considerations that must be addressed for the technology to achieve broad deployment. The primary concerns relate to seismicity, water use, and land footprint.

### Induced Seismicity

Hydraulic stimulation — injecting water at high pressure to create or open fractures — induces microseismic events. The vast majority of these events are of magnitude too small to be felt at the surface (less than magnitude 2.0). However, the experience of EGS projects in Basel, Switzerland (2006) and Pohang, South Korea (2017) — where stimulation operations were linked to felt seismic events — demonstrates that induced seismicity risk must be managed proactively.

Fervo's approach incorporates real-time microseismic monitoring during stimulation operations, with pre-established "traffic light" protocols that require reduction or suspension of injection if seismic events exceed specified magnitude thresholds. This approach, adapted from the protocols developed for wastewater injection in the oil and gas industry, has been effective in managing seismicity risk at Project Red and is being implemented at Cape Station.

### Water Consumption

EGS operations consume water — primarily through evaporative losses from the cooling system at the surface power plant and through water that is permanently lost to the formation during circulation. In arid regions — including much of the western United States where the best EGS resources are located — water availability is a constraint.

Binary-cycle power plants, which Fervo deploys, use air-cooled condensers rather than evaporative cooling towers, reducing water consumption to near zero for the power generation process. The primary water demand is for drilling and stimulation operations, which require 2-5 million gallons per well — comparable to a hydraulically fractured oil or gas well — and for makeup water to replace reservoir losses during circulation. Sourcing this water sustainably in arid environments requires careful planning and engagement with local water management authorities.

### Land Footprint

Geothermal power plants have a smaller surface footprint per megawatt of capacity than any other generation technology, including solar and wind. A 400 MW geothermal field occupies approximately 5-10 square miles of surface area, compared with 20-40 square miles for a solar photovoltaic installation of equivalent capacity. The subsurface nature of the resource means that land above the reservoir can often support multiple uses — grazing, wildlife habitat — concurrent with geothermal production.

This low land footprint is a significant advantage in the western United States, where land-use conflicts have constrained the deployment of solar and wind projects. Geothermal development faces fewer siting challenges, particularly on federal lands managed by the Bureau of Land Management, which has a statutory mandate to manage for multiple uses.
