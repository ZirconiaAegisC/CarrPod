## The Drilling Revolution Applied to Geothermal

The single most important factor enabling the current generation of Enhanced Geothermal Systems is the dramatic improvement in horizontal drilling technology achieved by the oil and gas industry over the past two decades. Fervo Energy's ability to drill horizontal well sections extending 7,000 feet or more through hot, hard basement rock at commercially acceptable cost is a direct inheritance from the shale revolution.

### Horizontal Drilling: Why It Matters for Geothermal

A vertical geothermal well accesses a single point in the subsurface — the rock column directly beneath the wellhead. A horizontal well, by contrast, accesses a lateral section that can extend thousands of feet through the target formation. The ratio of reservoir contact to well cost improves by an order of magnitude when horizontal drilling is deployed.

This ratio matters because the heat extraction capacity of a well pair is proportional to the surface area of the fracture network through which water circulates. A horizontal well with a 7,000-foot lateral creates fracture surface area across that entire length, multiplying the heat extraction per well dollar by factors of 5-10 relative to a vertical well configuration.

The economics of EGS depend critically on this well productivity ratio. If a well pair costing $15 million can sustain a 5 MW power plant for 30 years, the well cost contribution to levelized cost of electricity is approximately $15/MWh — manageable within the DOE's $45/MWh target. If the same well pair can sustain only a 1 MW plant — because the reservoir contact area is insufficient — the well cost contribution rises to $75/MWh, and the project is commercially unviable. Horizontal drilling is the technology that enables the higher end of the productivity range.

### Technical Requirements for Geothermal Drilling

Geothermal drilling imposes requirements that exceed those of oil and gas drilling in several important dimensions. The temperatures encountered in geothermal wells — 175-300°C at target depth — are above the operating limits of many standard downhole tools and electronics. Drilling motors, measurement-while-drilling (MWD) systems, and logging tools must be rated for high-temperature operation or protected by active cooling.

The rock formations in geothermal environments are typically harder and more abrasive than the sedimentary basins where most oil and gas drilling occurs. Granitic basement rock has compressive strengths exceeding 200 MPa (30,000 psi), compared with 50-100 MPa for typical sandstone and shale formations. Drill bits — polycrystalline diamond compact (PDC) bits for softer formations, roller-cone bits with tungsten carbide inserts for harder rock — wear faster and require more frequent replacement, increasing non-productive time (time when the rig is not advancing the wellbore).

The wellbore architecture for geothermal production differs from oil and gas wells in the requirement for larger-diameter casing to accommodate the high flow rates — tens of thousands of barrels of water per day — required for commercial power generation. A geothermal production well might be completed with 9-5/8-inch casing, compared with 5-1/2-inch or 7-inch casing typical for oil and gas wells. Larger casing requires a larger wellbore, which increases drilling time and cost.

### Fervo's Drilling Capabilities

Fervo has not publicly disclosed detailed performance metrics for its drilling operations, but the company's progress — from Project Red's 3,250-foot lateral to Cape Station's planned multi-well development — provides indirect evidence of drilling capability improvement. The learning curve in drilling is well-characterized: for each doubling of cumulative wells drilled in a given basin, drilling costs typically decline by 10-20%. If this learning rate applies to Fervo's geothermal drilling program, the company's well costs should be declining with each successive well pair.

The company's Houston location provides a specific advantage in drilling capability: the city is the global center of petroleum engineering expertise, and Fervo can recruit from a deep pool of drilling engineers, directional drillers, and completions specialists who have spent their careers optimizing well construction in the Permian Basin, Eagle Ford, and other unconventional plays. The transfer of this expertise to geothermal applications is reportedly straightforward — the physics are the same, the equipment is largely the same, and the primary adaptation required is to the higher-temperature environment.

## Completion Design: Multi-Stage Fracturing in Hard Rock

The completion of a geothermal well — the process of creating the fracture network that will serve as the subsurface heat exchanger — is as important as the drilling of the wellbore. Fervo's approach adapts the multi-stage hydraulic fracturing techniques developed for shale gas and tight oil to the different objectives and rock mechanics of geothermal reservoirs.

### Fracture Stimulation Objectives

The objective of fracture stimulation in an EGS well is qualitatively different from stimulation in an oil or gas well. In hydrocarbon applications, the objective is to maximize the surface area of fracture in contact with hydrocarbon-saturated rock, to enable drainage of the reservoir into the wellbore. In geothermal applications, the objective is to create a connected fracture network between injection and production wells that maximizes heat transfer surface area while maintaining sufficient permeability for fluid circulation.

This difference in objectives leads to different stimulation designs. Geothermal stimulations typically use lower proppant concentrations than oil and gas stimulations — the fractures need to be held open, but not to the extent required to maintain flow of viscous hydrocarbons. The stimulation fluid — typically water with friction reducer and biocide — is designed to minimize formation damage that could reduce permeability in the near-wellbore region through which water will circulate for decades.

### Stage Spacing and Fracture Geometry

The spacing between stimulation stages — the points along the horizontal wellbore where fractures are initiated — is a key design variable. Closer spacing creates a denser fracture network with more surface area but increases stimulation cost. Wider spacing reduces cost but leaves unstimulated sections of the wellbore that contribute less to heat extraction.

Fervo's stage spacing has not been publicly disclosed but can be inferred from the design parameters typical of EGS projects. Stage spacing of 50-100 feet (15-30 meters) is typical, comparable to the spacing used in unconventional oil and gas wells. At this spacing, a 7,000-foot lateral would include 70-140 individual fracture stages.

The fracture geometry — the dimensions and orientation of each hydraulic fracture — is influenced by the in-situ stress state of the rock formation. In most tectonic settings, the minimum principal stress is horizontal, which causes hydraulic fractures to propagate in vertical planes oriented perpendicular to the minimum stress direction. This is favorable for EGS because vertical fractures provide a large heat transfer surface area in a geometry that connects effectively between injection and production wells.
