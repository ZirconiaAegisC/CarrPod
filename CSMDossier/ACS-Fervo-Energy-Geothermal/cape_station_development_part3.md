## Competitive Positioning in Geothermal Development

Fervo Energy is not alone in pursuing utility-scale EGS development. Several well-funded competitors are advancing projects that, if successful, will compete with Cape Station for capital, offtake agreements, and the limited pool of experienced geothermal drilling personnel.

### Eavor Technologies

Eavor Technologies, a Canadian company backed by BP Ventures and other investors, is developing a closed-loop geothermal technology (Eavor-Loop) that circulates a working fluid through a sealed wellbore system without formation fluid exchange. The Eavor approach eliminates the need for hydraulic stimulation and avoids induced seismicity risk but requires drilling more complex well architectures — typically multiple horizontal laterals intersecting at depth — at higher cost per unit of heat extracted.

Eavor is developing projects in Germany (Geretsried) and Canada, with plans for US development. The company's technology is differentiated from Fervo's approach and may be better suited to certain geological settings — particularly formations with low natural permeability where hydraulic stimulation is ineffective — but has not yet demonstrated commercial-scale power generation.

### Sage Geosystems

Sage Geosystems, headquartered in Houston alongside Fervo, is developing a geothermal energy storage technology that combines EGS with mechanical energy storage. The company's approach uses excess grid electricity to pump water into pressurized subsurface reservoirs, then releases the water through turbines to generate electricity when demand peaks. This energy storage value proposition complements rather than directly competes with Fervo's baseload generation model.

Sage has attracted investment from Chesapeake Energy and other oil and gas investors and is developing a pilot project in Texas. The company's focus on storage rather than baseload generation positions it in a different segment of the electricity market, but the two companies compete for the same pool of subsurface engineering talent and, to some extent, the same investor capital.

### Conventional Geothermal Developers

Established geothermal developers — Ormat Technologies, Calpine (the Geysers), Berkshire Hathaway Energy (Imperial Valley) — have decades of operational experience, existing revenue streams, and relationships with the utilities and regulators in the western US electricity markets. These companies are primarily focused on conventional hydrothermal resources but are monitoring EGS technology development and may enter the EGS market through acquisition or internal development if the technology proves commercially viable.

Ormat, in particular, is a potential competitor or acquirer for Fervo. The company operates geothermal power plants globally, manufactures binary-cycle power generation equipment in-house, and has an active technology development program. Ormat's interest in EGS has been cautious — the company has participated in DOE-funded EGS research but has not committed to commercial EGS development — reflecting the technology's early stage and the company's preference for proven resources.

## Regulatory and Political Environment

Federal policy toward geothermal energy has been supportive, with bipartisan interest in EGS driven by the technology's potential to provide firm, carbon-free electricity using domestic resources and an oilfield workforce. The DOE's Enhanced Geothermal Shot, the $244 million grant to Fervo's Cape Station project, and the inclusion of geothermal in the Investment Tax Credit provisions of the Inflation Reduction Act collectively provide a policy framework that incentivizes EGS investment.

### Investment Tax Credit

The Inflation Reduction Act restored the geothermal Investment Tax Credit at a rate of 30% for projects that begin construction before 2033, with bonus credits available for projects that meet prevailing wage and apprenticeship requirements, use domestic content, or are located in energy communities. At a 30% credit rate, a $2 billion Cape Station project would receive $600 million in tax credits, substantially improving project economics.

The tax credit monetization — converting the tax credit into cash to fund project construction — typically requires a tax equity investor: a financial institution (bank, insurance company) with sufficient tax liability to use the credits. The tax equity market for renewable energy projects is well-developed but is dominated by wind and solar transactions. Geothermal projects, with their different risk profile and longer development timelines, may face a smaller pool of potential tax equity investors.

### State-Level Support

Utah's state government has expressed support for geothermal development as an economic development opportunity, particularly in rural counties where geothermal resources are located and economic diversification is a priority. State-level support manifests in expedited permitting, workforce training programs, and infrastructure investment (roads, transmission) that reduces project costs.

The California Public Utilities Commission has directed the state's investor-owned utilities to procure 1,000 MW of zero-emission, firm generation to replace the Diablo Canyon nuclear plant, which is scheduled to close by 2030. Geothermal power from Cape Station, delivered into CAISO via the Intermountain transmission corridor, could serve a portion of this procurement requirement, providing a potentially large and creditworthy offtake market.

### Community Engagement

Large-scale energy projects in rural areas face community acceptance challenges that must be managed proactively. Drilling operations generate noise, truck traffic, and visual impact. Water use for drilling and stimulation can compete with agricultural and municipal uses. The influx of drilling crews can strain local housing and services.

Fervo's community engagement strategy for Cape Station has not been publicly detailed in depth but presumably includes: early and transparent communication with local stakeholders, hiring preference for local workers where skills align, water sourcing plans that minimize competition with existing uses, and community benefit agreements that provide tangible economic returns to the host communities in the form of property tax payments, infrastructure improvements, or direct community investment.
