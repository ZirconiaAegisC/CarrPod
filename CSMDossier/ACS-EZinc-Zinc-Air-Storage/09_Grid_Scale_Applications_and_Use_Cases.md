# e-Zinc — Grid-Scale Applications & Use Cases

## The Evolving Role of Energy Storage

Energy storage is transitioning from a niche grid service provider — primarily
frequency regulation — to a core component of electricity system planning and
operation. As renewable generation penetrations increase, storage assumes
multiple roles across the electricity value chain:

- **Generation shifting** — moving electricity from periods of high renewable
  output and low demand to periods of low renewable output and high demand
- **Capacity resource** — providing reliable capacity to meet resource adequacy
  requirements, displacing natural gas peaking plants
- **Ancillary services** — frequency regulation, spinning reserve, voltage support
- **Transmission and distribution deferral** — reducing the need for new wires
  infrastructure by absorbing generation at constrained locations
- **Behind-the-meter** — reducing customer demand charges and providing backup
  power

## Utility-Scale Deployment Model

e-Zinc's primary go-to-market strategy targets utility-scale deployments,
typically in the range of 10-100 MW with 12-100+ hours of storage duration.
These systems are integrated at the distribution or transmission level and
operated by the utility or a third-party asset manager to provide one or more
of the services described above.

## Revenue Stacking

Grid-scale storage projects typically generate revenue from multiple sources,
a strategy known as revenue stacking:

1. **Energy arbitrage** — purchasing electricity during low-price periods
   (typically midday solar or nighttime wind) and selling during high-price
   periods (evening peak, morning ramp). The revenue potential scales with
   the price spread between charge and discharge periods.

2. **Resource adequacy / capacity payments** — utilities pay for guaranteed
   capacity availability during peak demand periods. Long-duration storage
   qualifies for these payments because it can sustain output through the
   full peak demand window.

3. **Ancillary services** — frequency regulation and spinning reserve markets
   provide additional revenue, particularly for storage systems with fast
   response capability.

4. **Renewable integration** — co-located storage can capture otherwise-curtailed
   renewable generation and deliver it during higher-value periods, increasing
   the effective capacity factor and revenue of the renewable asset.

## Use Case: Solar Firming in California

California's electricity grid exemplifies the challenge that long-duration storage
addresses. The state's ambitious renewable portfolio standard (60% by 2030, 100%
carbon-free by 2045) combined with its abundant solar resource creates the
well-known "duck curve" — a deep midday net load trough followed by a steep
evening ramp. While 4-hour lithium-ion batteries can address the evening ramp,
they cannot bridge multi-day cloudy periods or the winter months when solar
output is substantially reduced. e-Zinc's 24-100 hour storage is designed to
fill this gap, providing reliable, dispatchable power regardless of weather
conditions.

## Use Case: Wind Balancing in the Great Plains

The U.S. Great Plains region — from Texas through the Dakotas — has some of the
world's best onshore wind resources. However, wind generation is inherently
variable on multi-day timescales, with periods of sustained high output
alternating with multi-day lulls. Long-duration storage co-located with wind
generation can transform variable wind output into firm, dispatchable power
that qualifies for capacity payments and can be sold under long-term power
purchase agreements with premium pricing relative to intermittent wind.

## Use Case: Industrial Load Shifting

Energy-intensive industries — including metals processing, chemical manufacturing,
and data centers — face increasing pressure to reduce their carbon footprint
while managing electricity costs. Long-duration storage enables these facilities
to:

- Shift electricity consumption to periods of high renewable output and low
  prices
- Provide behind-the-meter resilience against grid outages
- Participate in demand response programs
- Meet corporate renewable energy commitments without exposing operations to
  the intermittency of wind and solar generation
