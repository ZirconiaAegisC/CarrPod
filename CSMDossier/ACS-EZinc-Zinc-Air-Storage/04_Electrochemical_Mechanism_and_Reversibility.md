# e-Zinc — Electrochemical Mechanism & Reversibility

## The Reversibility Challenge

The central technical challenge in rechargeable zinc-air batteries — and the
reason they have not previously achieved commercial viability despite decades
of research — is achieving efficient, long-lived electrochemical reversibility.
While the fundamental discharge and charge reactions are thermodynamically
favorable, practical reversibility requires controlling several problematic
phenomena:

1. **Dendrite formation** — during charging, zinc metal can plate in needle-like
   dendritic structures that grow from the negative electrode toward the positive
   electrode, eventually causing internal short circuits
2. **Shape change** — repeated cycling causes redistribution of zinc active
   material across the electrode surface, progressively reducing the effective
   active area and capacity
3. **Passivation** — zinc oxide, the discharge product, is electrically insulating;
   if it forms as a dense, non-porous layer on the electrode surface, it can
   block further reaction and reduce accessible capacity
4. **Parasitic hydrogen evolution** — in alkaline conditions, zinc metal can
   react directly with water to produce hydrogen gas, consuming active material
   and reducing coulombic efficiency

## e-Zinc's Reversibility Solution

e-Zinc has developed proprietary electrode architectures and electrolyte
management strategies that address each of these failure mechanisms:

### Dendrite Suppression

The company's electrode design promotes uniform current distribution across the
electrode surface, eliminating the localized high-current-density regions that
initiate dendritic growth. This is achieved through engineered electrode
substrates with controlled porosity and three-dimensional current collector
structures that provide a large, uniform surface area for zinc deposition.

### Shape Change Mitigation

e-Zinc's cell architecture incorporates mechanical and electrochemical features
that constrain zinc redistribution during cycling. The use of three-dimensional
electrode structures — rather than flat planar electrodes — provides a stable
framework within which zinc deposition and dissolution occur without the gross
material migration that plagues conventional zinc electrode designs.

### Passivation Management

The cell operates under conditions — including controlled electrolyte flow and
optimized discharge depth — that prevent the formation of passivating zinc oxide
films. The electrolyte composition and circulation strategy ensure that discharge
products are deposited as porous, electrochemically accessible structures rather
than dense, insulating films.

### Hydrogen Evolution Suppression

Through careful control of electrode potential, electrolyte composition, and
operating temperature, e-Zinc minimizes the parasitic hydrogen evolution reaction.
The company has also developed proprietary electrode coatings and additives that
increase the hydrogen evolution overpotential on the zinc electrode without
compromising the desired zinc deposition and dissolution kinetics.

## Electrode Architecture

| Component | Material | Function |
|-----------|---------|----------|
| Negative current collector | Copper or copper-coated substrate | Electrical conduction, structural support |
| Zinc electrode | Porous zinc metal (discharged state: ZnO) | Active material, energy storage |
| Electrolyte | Aqueous KOH (25-30 wt%) | Ionic conduction |
| Separator | Porous polymer membrane | Prevents electrical contact, allows ion transport |
| Air cathode | Carbon-based with bifunctional catalyst | O₂ reduction (discharge) and evolution (charge) |
| Gas diffusion layer | PTFE-treated carbon paper | Allows O₂ access, prevents electrolyte leakage |

## Cycle Life Validation

Achieving >5,000 cycles — equivalent to over 13 years of daily cycling or 27
years of weekly cycling — requires rigorous validation. e-Zinc's testing
protocol includes:

- Continuous cycling at rated power and energy under controlled laboratory
  conditions
- Accelerated aging tests at elevated temperatures to project calendar life
- Partial-state-of-charge cycling to simulate real-world grid storage operation
- Post-mortem analysis of cycled cells to identify degradation mechanisms and
  validate mitigation strategies

## Coulombic & Energy Efficiency

The round-trip energy efficiency target of >70% reflects the inherent trade-offs
in metal-air battery design. The oxygen evolution reaction (during charge) has
a higher overpotential than the oxygen reduction reaction (during discharge),
creating an inherent efficiency asymmetry. e-Zinc's bifunctional air cathode
catalyst development aims to minimize this asymmetry while accepting that
zinc-air systems will operate at somewhat lower round-trip efficiency than
lithium-ion batteries — a trade-off justified by the dramatically lower capital
cost per kilowatt-hour of storage capacity.
