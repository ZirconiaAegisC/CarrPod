# ACS Group14 | BAM-2 Scale-Up: 10,000 Tons per Year

## 1. BAM-2 Strategic Significance

BAM-2 represents Group14's transition from pilot-commercial scale (BAM-1, 100 tons/yr) to genuine mass production. At 10,000 metric tons per year of SCC55, BAM-2 would be the largest silicon anode material factory in the world and would signal the silicon anode industry's maturation from boutique specialty material to commodity-scale battery component.

### Project Scope
| Parameter | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Location | Moses Lake, Washington (adjacent to BAM-1) |
| Annual Capacity | 10,000 metric tons SCC55 |
| Capital Investment | $300M+ (total project cost) |
| DOE Grant | $100M (Bipartisan Infrastructure Law — Battery Materials Processing) |
| Construction Timeline | Groundbreaking H2 2024; first production H2 2026; full ramp-up 2028 |
| Employment | 200-300 direct jobs at full capacity |
| Site Footprint | ~20,000-30,000 square meters (10-15x BAM-1) |

### Market Context for 10,000 Tons
10,000 tons of SCC55 per year represents:
- Enough material for approximately 5-10 GWh of lithium-ion cells (depending on anode formulation — 1,000-2,000 tons per GWh for 10-20% silicon blend)
- Roughly 2-3% of current global anode material demand (~500,000 tons graphite/year)
- $150-250M in annual revenue at projected SCC55 pricing ($15-25/kg)
- A meaningful but not dominant share of the projected 15% silicon anode penetration by 2030

## 2. DOE Grant: Bipartisan Infrastructure Law

### Grant Details
Group14 was awarded $100M from the U.S. Department of Energy under the Bipartisan Infrastructure Law's battery materials processing and battery manufacturing grants program. This program, administered by the DOE Office of Manufacturing and Energy Supply Chains (MESC), allocated $2.8B total across 20+ projects.

### Strategic Rationale for DOE Support
The DOE's investment in Group14 reflects several national strategic priorities:
1. **Supply chain sovereignty**: Currently >90% of graphite anode material is processed in China; silicon anode materials represent an opportunity to establish U.S. leadership
2. **Critical materials diversification**: Silicon is abundant (Earth's crust: 28% Si by mass) and not subject to the geopolitical concentration affecting graphite, cobalt, and nickel
3. **Energy transition manufacturing**: Onshoring battery materials manufacturing aligns with broader IRA and CHIPS Act industrial policy objectives
4. **Technology leadership**: Maintaining U.S. competitiveness in advanced battery materials as Chinese companies (BTR, Shanshan, Putailai) expand silicon anode production

### Grant Conditions
- Cost-share requirement: DOE funds typically cover 50% of project costs; Group14 must secure matching private capital
- Domestic manufacturing requirement: Products must be manufactured in the United States
- Davis-Bacon Act: Prevailing wage requirements for construction labor
- Reporting: Quarterly progress reports to DOE on production volumes, employment, and technology milestones
- Community benefits plan: Commitments to local workforce development, environmental justice, and community engagement

## 3. Engineering Scale-Up Challenges

### From 100 to 10,000 Tons: Two Orders of Magnitude
The scale-up from BAM-1 to BAM-2 is not simply a matter of building 100x more equipment. Critical engineering challenges include:

**Carbon Scaffold Production at Scale**:
- BAM-1 carbon production: ~150-200 tons/yr of carbon scaffold (to yield 100 tons SCC55 at 40-60% Si loading)
- BAM-2 carbon requirement: ~15,000-20,000 tons/yr of carbon scaffold
- Requires moving from batch or small-continuous furnaces to large-scale continuous carbonization lines
- Polymer precursor supply chain must scale proportionally — must secure long-term supply agreements with chemical producers

**CVD Reactor Scale**:
- BAM-1 likely uses multiple medium-scale (50-200 kg/batch) CVD reactors
- BAM-2 requires either many parallel reactors (modular approach) or much larger individual reactors
- Fluidized bed CVD (FB-CVD) is the most scalable reactor configuration for powder silicon deposition
- Group14 may transition from fixed-bed to fluidized-bed reactors for BAM-2, representing a significant process technology change

**Thermal Management**:
- Silane decomposition is exothermic; at scale, heat removal becomes a limiting factor
- Poor thermal management leads to hot spots causing silicon crystallization (>600°C) and carbon scaffold degradation
- Active cooling of CVD reactors (fluidized bed with internal heat exchange surfaces) is likely required

**Silane Supply**:
- BAM-2 at full capacity requires ~5,000-8,000 tons/yr of silane
- REC Silicon's Moses Lake facility has significant silane capacity but was idled in 2019 due to trade disputes; restart or alternative supply needed
- Alternative silane suppliers: Gelest/Mitsubishi Materials, Linde Electronics, SK Materials (potential synergy with Korea JV)

## 4. Manufacturing Cost Model

### Cost Breakdown Estimate (at BAM-2 Scale)
| Cost Element | $/kg SCC55 | % of Total |
|-------------|-----------|------------|
| Carbon precursor (polymer) | $3.00-5.00 | 15-25% |
| Silane (SiH₄) | $3.00-6.00 | 20-30% |
| Template materials (silica, etc.) | $1.00-2.00 | 5-10% |
| Electricity | $1.50-3.00 | 10-15% |
| Labor (direct + indirect) | $2.00-3.00 | 10-15% |
| Equipment depreciation | $2.00-4.00 | 15-20% |
| Quality/analytical | $0.50-1.00 | 3-5% |
| Packaging/logistics | $0.50-1.00 | 3-5% |
| **Total Manufacturing Cost** | **$13.50-25.00** | **100%** |

### Target Selling Price
- At-scale SCC55 target price: $20-30/kg
- Competitive reference: Synthetic graphite anode powder $8-15/kg; natural graphite $5-10/kg
- Value proposition: SCC55 at $20/kg with 50% higher energy density translates to ~$5-10/kWh premium at cell level, which EV OEMs accept for high-performance applications

### Cost Reduction Trajectory
- **BAM-1 (2023-2024)**: Estimated manufacturing cost $40-60/kg (low volume, process inefficiencies)
- **BAM-2 initial (2026-2027)**: Targeting $25-35/kg (scale benefits partially offset by ramp-up inefficiencies)
- **BAM-2 mature (2028+)**: Targeting $15-20/kg (full utilization, process optimization, supply chain maturity)
- **Long-term (BAM-3+)**: Targeting $12-15/kg (learning curve, next-generation process technology)

### The Moses Lake Cluster
An underappreciated dynamic: both Group14 and Sila Nano are building facilities in Moses Lake, WA. This creates a potential silicon anode manufacturing cluster with shared:
- Low-cost hydroelectric power infrastructure
- Silane supply chain (REC Silicon)
- Skilled workforce in silicon/silane chemistry
- Transportation logistics to Asian battery manufacturing hubs via Pacific Northwest ports (Seattle, Tacoma, Portland)
