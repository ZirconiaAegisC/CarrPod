# CarbonCure — CO2 Injection Chemistry

## Fundamental Reaction
CarbonCure's core chemical process involves injecting gaseous CO2 into fresh concrete during mixing. The CO2 dissolves into the aqueous phase of the concrete pore solution, forming carbonic acid (H2CO3). This transient acid species immediately dissociates into bicarbonate (HCO3-) and carbonate (CO3 2-) ions depending on the pH of the cement paste system.

## pH-Dependent Speciation
Fresh concrete has a pH of approximately 12.5–13.5 due to the presence of calcium hydroxide (portlandite) and alkali hydroxides released during cement hydration. At this high pH, dissolved CO2 exists predominantly as carbonate ions (CO3 2-), which are available to react with calcium ions in solution.

## Calcium Carbonate Precipitation
Calcium ions (Ca2+) are abundant in the pore solution from the dissolution of tricalcium silicate (C3S) and dicalcium silicate (C2S) phases during early cement hydration. The reaction pathway is:

    CO2(g)  →  CO2(aq)
    CO2(aq) + H2O  →  H2CO3
    H2CO3 + 2OH-  →  CO3 2- + 2H2O
    Ca2+ + CO3 2-  →  CaCO3(s)

The calcium carbonate precipitates as nano-scale particles (nano-CaCO3), typically in the 50–200 nanometer range, depending on local supersaturation conditions and the availability of nucleation sites.

## Reaction Kinetics
The mineralization reaction is rapid under alkaline conditions, with the majority of CO2 conversion occurring within the first 5–15 minutes after injection — the window corresponding to the concrete mixing and transportation phase before initial set. The kinetics are governed by:

1. **CO2 dissolution rate** — controlled by bubble size and mixing shear.
2. **Calcium ion availability** — a function of cement phase dissolution rates.
3. **Nucleation surface area** — existing cement particles and hydration products provide abundant heterogeneous nucleation sites.
4. **Temperature** — reaction rate increases with temperature, though the practical operating window is 5–35 °C.

## Injection Parameters
CarbonCure's system injects CO2 at a rate of approximately 0.1–0.3% by weight of cementitious material. The optimal dose depends on cement type, supplementary cementitious material (SCM) content, water-to-cement ratio, and ambient conditions. Over-injection can lead to excessive carbonation of the surface layer or reduced pH that impairs passivation of reinforcing steel, so the control system maintains strict dosage limits.

## Thermodynamic Favorability
The Gibbs free energy of calcium carbonate formation from calcium hydroxide and CO2 is approximately -62 kJ/mol at 25 °C, indicating a strongly thermodynamically favorable reaction. The equilibrium constant (Ksp for calcite = 3.36 × 10^-9) ensures near-complete precipitation of carbonate as CaCO3 under alkaline conditions.

## CO2 Permanence
Once mineralized as calcium carbonate within the concrete matrix, the CO2 is chemically bound and thermodynamically stable at ambient conditions. Decomposition of CaCO3 back to CaO and CO2 requires calcination temperatures above 825 °C — conditions never encountered during the service life of concrete in buildings or infrastructure. This qualifies CarbonCure's process as durable (permanent) carbon removal under all major carbon credit methodologies.

## Effect on Hydration
A secondary benefit of CO2 injection is accelerated cement hydration. The nano-CaCO3 particles act as nucleation sites for calcium silicate hydrate (C-S-H) gel precipitation, increasing the rate and extent of C3S hydration. This nucleation effect is responsible for the 4–6% compressive strength gain observed at equivalent cement content, enabling cement reduction while maintaining structural performance specifications.

## Compatibility with SCMs
CO2 injection is compatible with common supplementary cementitious materials including fly ash (Class C and Class F), ground granulated blast furnace slag (GGBFS), silica fume, and natural pozzolans. The presence of SCMs does not inhibit the carbonation reaction, though the calcium-to-silicate ratio of the binder system influences the equilibrium carbonate content. Blended cements with lower portland cement content may require adjusted CO2 dosage to avoid over-carbonation at exposed surfaces.

## Bubble Dynamics and Mass Transfer
The efficiency of CO2 mineralization depends critically on the gas-liquid mass transfer rate. CarbonCure's injection system produces fine CO2 bubbles (typically 0.5–2 mm diameter) through a sintered metal sparger or venturi injector. The small bubble size maximizes the gas-liquid interfacial area, accelerating CO2 dissolution. The high-shear environment of the concrete mixer — whether central pan mixer or truck drum — continuously renews the liquid film surrounding each bubble, maintaining the CO2 concentration driving force for dissolution.

Key mass transfer parameters:
- **Volumetric mass transfer coefficient (kLa):** 0.05–0.15 s^-1, depending on mixer type, drum speed, and concrete rheology.
- **Bubble residence time:** 5–20 seconds in the mixer before dissolution or surfacing.
- **Dissolution efficiency:** 60–90% of injected CO2 dissolves within the mixing window, with the remainder either dissolving during transport or reacting at the concrete surface.

## Electrochemical Considerations
The dissolution of CO2 and precipitation of CaCO3 temporarily depresses the pore solution pH by approximately 0.2–0.5 pH units, as hydroxyl ions are consumed in the conversion of carbonic acid to carbonate. This mild pH reduction is fully reversed within 15–30 minutes as continued cement hydration releases additional calcium hydroxide, restoring the typical pH of 12.5–13.5. The transient pH depression does not compromise the passivation of reinforcing steel, as the bulk pH remains well above the 9.5 threshold for steel depassivation.

## Comparison to Natural Carbonation
The natural atmospheric carbonation of concrete is a slow, diffusion-limited process that advances at approximately 1–5 mm per year in exposed concrete surfaces. This natural carbonation gradually consumes calcium hydroxide and lowers pH, eventually depassivating reinforcing steel if the carbonation front reaches the steel depth. CarbonCure's process differs fundamentally from natural carbonation in three respects:

1. **Timing:** CO2 is introduced during mixing, not decades afterward, when calcium ions are at maximum availability.
2. **Distribution:** CO2-derived CaCO3 is uniformly distributed throughout the cement paste matrix, not concentrated at the exposed surface.
3. **Consequence:** The nano-CaCO3 strengthens rather than weakens the matrix, adding nucleation sites for C-S-H formation.

## Process Optimization Variables
CarbonCure's research team continuously refines the injection process through controlled parameter variation across the 700+ plant fleet:

- **Injection timing:** Optimal injection occurs after initial water addition and wet mixing (30–60 seconds into the mixing cycle), once cement particles are suspended and calcium dissolution has begun, but before the buildup of hydration products limits CO2 access to unreacted cement surfaces.
- **Mixer filling level:** CO2 injection is most efficient at 60–80% of rated mixer capacity, providing adequate headspace for bubble dispersion without excessive gas loss.
- **Water-to-cement ratio:** Higher w/c ratios (0.45–0.55) provide more aqueous volume for CO2 dissolution but may dilute calcium ion concentration; optimal CO2 dose is weakly dependent on w/c.
- **Admixture interactions:** Common chemical admixtures — polycarboxylate ether superplasticizers, air-entraining agents, set retarders, and accelerators — do not interfere with CO2 mineralization. Calcium chloride accelerators, in fact, increase calcium availability and may enhance the carbonation reaction slightly.
