# FREYR Battery — Risk Factors

## Strategic and Technology Risks

### 1. 24M Semi-Solid Technology Scale-Up (CRITICAL)
The 24M semi-solid electrode manufacturing process has been demonstrated at the 0.05 GWh (50 MWh) pilot scale but has never been proven at commercial gigawatt-hour scale. The transition from pilot to mass production introduces risks: yield rates may be uneconomically low, throughput may not meet design specifications, cell quality and consistency may be degraded, and equipment suppliers may not have standard solutions for semi-solid processing at scale. If the technology cannot be economically scaled, FREYR's entire battery thesis collapses.

### 2. Pre-Revenue Status (HIGH)
FREYR has generated zero product revenue since its founding. All financial projections are theoretical. The company burns cash without offsetting income. In a rising-rate environment, the market's tolerance for pre-revenue industrial companies has diminished to near zero.

### 3. Strategic Incoherence
FREYR's rapid strategic shifts — Norway-first battery company → US battery + solar development company → solar manufacturing joint venture partner — have confused investors and partners. The company presents different narratives to different stakeholders: a battery play to technology investors, a solar play to IRA-focused investors, and a project developer to government lenders. Lack of focused identity undermines all constituencies.

### 4. Financing Gap (EXISTENTIAL)
Giga America Phase 1 requires ~$1.7 billion. FREYR has ~$200M in cash. Even with an optimistic $600M DOE loan and $300M strategic partner equity, a significant financing gap remains. Project finance lenders typically require binding off-take agreements, proven technology at scale, and meaningful sponsor equity — conditions FREYR cannot currently meet.

## Financial Risks

### 5. Cash Runway
At current burn rates (~$25M/quarter), FREYR has approximately 8-9 quarters of cash remaining (through late 2027). However, Giga America construction cannot begin without committed project finance. The company risks running out of cash before reaching key catalyst events (DOE loan close, solar JV production start, binding off-take).

### 6. Dilution Risk
If FREYR raises additional equity to bridge the financing gap, existing shareholders face extreme dilution. At the current market cap of ~$60M, raising even $200M would dilute existing shareholders by 75%+.

### 7. Delisting Risk
NYSE requires a minimum average closing price of $1.00 over a consecutive 30-day trading period. FREYR's sustained sub-$1 share price (post-reverse split) creates ongoing delisting risk. OTC trading would further reduce institutional interest and liquidity, making capital raising even more difficult.

### 8. IRA Dependency
FREYR's business model depends entirely on IRA Section 45X tax credits to make US-manufactured LFP cells cost-competitive with Chinese imports. If the IRA is partially repealed, modified, or if implementing regulations change unfavorably, FREYR loses its primary economic rationale. While bipartisan support for manufacturing credits exists (red states are major beneficiaries), political risk cannot be dismissed.

## Commercial Risks

### 9. No Binding Off-Take Agreements
FREYR cannot point to a single binding, large-scale offtake agreement for battery cells. Without such agreements, project finance is nearly impossible and end-market demand for FREYR's specific cell output is unvalidated.

### 10. Customer Concentration Risk
The Trina Solar JV effectively makes Trina FREYR's primary near-term commercial partner. If the JV underperforms or Trina Solar faces its own challenges (trade restrictions, financial difficulties), FREYR has no backup plan.

### 11. Chinese Competition
CATL and BYD produce LFP cells at ~$55/kWh and continue to drive costs lower through scale, automation, and vertical integration. Even with IRA credits, FREYR's target cost structure ($40-55/kWh with credits) barely matches unsubsidized Chinese production. If Chinese producers establish tariff-circumventing production in third countries (Morocco, Indonesia, Mexico), FREYR's already-thin cost advantage evaporates.

## Operational and Supply Chain Risks

### 12. Raw Material Dependency
LFP cells require lithium, iron, and phosphate. FREYR has no captive raw material supply and must purchase on the spot market or negotiate offtake. Lithium price volatility (spot prices ranged from $6,000/ton to $80,000/ton between 2020-2023) creates input cost uncertainty. Glencore (FREYR investor) could provide some raw material access but not at preferential pricing.

### 13. US Manufacturing Workforce
Coweta County, Georgia, while part of a growing manufacturing cluster, has not traditionally been a battery manufacturing hub. Recruiting and training the specialized workforce required for battery cell production may be more challenging and costly than projected. SK Battery's experience in Commerce, GA (reporting quality control challenges in early production) illustrates the difficulty of standing up a battery workforce from scratch.

### 14. Construction and Permitting Risk
Large industrial construction projects in the US routinely face delays and cost overruns due to permitting, environmental review, labor availability, and supply chain disruptions. As a small company with limited project management experience, FREYR is especially vulnerable to these risks.

## Macro and Geopolitical Risks

### 15. Interest Rate Environment
Project finance for industrial facilities depends on the interest rate environment. If long-term rates remain elevated, debt service costs make projects uneconomical and reduce lender appetite.

### 16. US-China Trade Policy Uncertainty
FREYR's competitive position depends on trade barriers limiting Chinese battery imports. Changes in tariff policy (reduction or elimination of Section 301 tariffs, for example) could undermine the domestic manufacturing premium that IRA seeks to create.
