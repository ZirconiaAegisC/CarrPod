CSMFAB critical minerals — this is the portfolio's primary geopolitical risk.

## Critical Mineral 1 — Indium (In)

**Atomic number:** 49 | **Annual world production:** ~700 metric tons (2026) | **CSMFAB use:** YInMn Blue pigment

### Supply Chain Deep Dive

**How Indium occurs in nature:**
Indium is a trace element in sphalerite (zinc sulfide) ores — typically 1-200 ppm by weight.
It is **never mined as a primary product** — always as a by-product of zinc smelting.
Global zinc production: ~13 million tons/year → ~700 tons In recovered as by-product.

**Major production steps:**
1. Zinc ore mined (galena, sphalerite): China, Peru, Australia, USA (Doe Run, Missouri)
2. Ore smelted to crude zinc → indium concentrated in flue dusts (0.5-5% In)
3. Hydrometallurgical recovery: leach flue dust → SX with D2EHPA or ionic liquid → plating
4. Refined indium: 99.99% purity

**Primary global suppliers:**
- Freeport-McMoRan (USA — from zinc operations in Arizona): ~15 t/yr
- Doe Run (Missouri, USA): ~8 t/yr — US domestic
- Korea Zinc (South Korea): ~180 t/yr — largest single producer
- Umicore (Belgium): ~60 t/yr (recycling focus)
- Various Chinese smelters: ~400 t/yr combined

**Price history and volatility:**
```
2001: $97/kg
2006: $975/kg (LCD boom)
2010: $600/kg
2016: $167/kg (oversupply)
2022: $240/kg
2024: $320/kg
2026: $290-380/kg (China export control uncertainty)
```

### Domestic US Indium Recovery Strategy

**Critical action:** CSM should position as an indium recycler, not just consumer.

**US-domestic indium recovery sources:**
1. Zinc smelter by-product streams (Missouri, Tennessee): negotiate off-stream agreement
2. Liquid crystal display (LCD) recycling: ITOs contain ~0.5 kg In/kg coating
3. CSM Phoenix Protocol In recovery from retired Aegis products

**Indium from LCD glass recycling:**
- US LCDs discarded per year: ~350 million units
- In content per TV: 0.5–2g
- Recovery potential: 50–100 tons/yr (currently mostly landfilled)
- CSM opportunity: partner with electronics recycler → capture In stream

**Estimated in-recovery cost:** $18–$35/kg (vs. $290–$380 virgin mine price)
**This makes YInMn Blue recycling the most financially compelling Phoenix Protocol component.**

### Indium Risk Mitigation Strategies (ranked by feasibility)

1. **Indium-sparing YInMn formula** — reduce In content 40-60% via Ga/Fe/Al substitution
   (Priority R&D: ~18-month development program; cost ~$280,000)

2. **Phoenix Protocol ionic liquid recovery** — recover 99.4% from each retired panel
   (Immediate action; requires ionic liquid solvent procurement)

3. **Domestic co-product agreement** with Doe Run (Missouri) or Freeport zinc operations
   (Negotiate 3-year supply contract; volume commitment for preferred pricing)

4. **CoAl2O4 substitution** for applications where Indium-free alternative acceptable
   (70-80% NIR performance vs. 85-92% for YInMn — trade-off decision per product)

5. **Strategic stockpile** — purchase 12-24 months' supply at favorable price periods
   (Working capital: $1.2M buys ~4 tons; covers portfolio production for 2+ years)

## Critical Mineral 2 — Yttrium (Y)

**Atomic number:** 39 | **Class:** Rare Earth Element (light REE group, but often processed with HREE) | **CSMFAB use:** 3Y-TZP zirconia, YInMn Blue, Si3N4 sintering aid

### Supply Chain

**Occurrence:** Yttrium is found in bastnäsite and monazite REE ores (alongside La, Ce, Pr, Nd).
Also in xenotime (yttrium-dominant REE phosphate ore) — more unusual.

**Global production:**
- China: ~85% of REE mining and essentially all REE separation
- Mountain Pass (California, MP Materials): REO concentrate exported to China for separation
- Lynas Rare Earths (Australia/Malaysia): produces separated RE oxides without China
- Energy Fuels (Utah): building US heavy REE separation capability (2024–2026)

**Yttrium oxide (Y2O3) pricing:**
- 2020: $4.50/kg
- 2022: $12/kg (REE price spike)
- 2024: $35–$50/kg (heavy REE tightening)
- 2026: $38–$55/kg

### US Domestic Yttrium Sources

**Bear Lodge (Wyoming):** Nearest-term large US heavy REE deposit with significant Y content.
Rare Element Resources — awaiting final permitting.

**Pea Ridge (Missouri):** Iron ore deposit with recoverable HREE including Y — advanced drilling.

**Round Top (Texas):** Texas Mineral Resources — large peralkaline deposit; 2% Y2O3 in deposit.
Mining schedule: 2027-2028 production begins (announced).

**Energy Fuels (White Mesa Mill, Utah):** Processing monazite sand → separating Y2O3.
Current production: ~5 tons Y/yr; target 50 tons/yr by 2027.

**Yttrium recovery from Phoenix Protocol:**
From retired YSZ ceramic parts: HCl dissolution → selective extraction with Cyanex 272 (bis(2,4,4-trimethylpentyl)phosphinic acid) → 98% Y2O3 recovery.
In-recovery cost: $22–$32/kg vs. $38–$55/kg virgin.

## Critical Mineral 3 — Cobalt (Co)

**Atomic number:** 27 | **Annual world production:** ~200,000 mt | **CSMFAB use:** CoAl2O4 pigment, SmCo magnets, LiFePO4 (minor)

### Supply Chain (Dominated by DRC)

**Democratic Republic of Congo (DRC) situation:**
- 69% of world cobalt mining (Katanga region)
- Artisanal small-scale mining: 30% of DRC output, significant humanitarian concerns
- Major mine operators: Glencore (Swiss), CMOC (Chinese), Ivanhoe Mines (Canadian)

**Price history:**
- 2018: $95,000/mt (electric vehicle boom panic)
- 2020: $38,000/mt
- 2022: $82,000/mt
- 2024: $28,000/mt (oversupply from DRC expansion)
- 2026: $32,000–$45,000/mt (normalized range)

### CSMFAB Cobalt Demand Analysis

**CoAl2O4 cobalt content:** ~14.5 wt% Co per kg CoAl2O4
Annual portfolio demand (at scale): ~220 kg Co/yr → ~1.5 metric ton CoAl2O4/yr
Annual Co cost: 220 kg × $35/kg = **$7,700/yr** — manageable

**Key insight:** CoAl2O4 uses very little cobalt relative to battery industry (LCo cathodes).
CSMFAB is NOT at risk from cobalt supply despite DRC concerns.

### US Domestic Cobalt Sources

- **Idaho Cobalt Belt:** Jervois Global (Australia/USA) — Idaho Cobalt Operations
  Opened 2023; first US primary cobalt mine in 60 years. 1,500 t/yr capacity.
- **Midwest cobalt belt (Missouri):** Cobalt in copper-cobalt sulfide deposits (Viburnum Trend)
- **Alaska:** Several Co-Ni deposits under exploration

**LiFePO4 batteries** (CSMFAB product 050, 019): Use Fe and P, not Co cathode → **cobalt-free battery chemistry** already chosen. Smart decision.

## Critical Mineral 4 — Niobium (Nb)

**Atomic number:** 41 | **Annual world production:** ~75,000 mt (Nb2O5) | **CSMFAB use:** KNbO3-BaTiO3 piezoelectric ceramics

### Supply Chain (Near-Monopoly: Brazil)

**Brazil dominates niobium production at 92%:**
- CBMM (Companhia Brasileira de Metalurgia e Mineração): 75% of world Nb supply from single mine (Araxa)
- A single Brazilian company controls the majority of world supply of a critical material

**Price (2026):** Nb2O5: $32–$55/kg | Ferroniobium (steel industry): $36–$42/kg Nb basis

**US domestic niobium:**
- Elk Creek (Nebraska): NioCorp Developments — NbC/Nb2O5 production target 2027
- Granite-hosted pegmatite deposits in several states — under-explored

**Risk level for CSMFAB:** MEDIUM — Brazil is a stable ally; no current supply disruption.
Single-source concentration is the primary risk. Mitigation: 12-month strategic inventory.

**In-house Nb recovery:** Not practical at current portfolio scale.

## Critical Mineral 5 — Tungsten (W)

**Atomic number:** 74 | **Density:** 19.3 g/cm³ (densest practical metal) | **CSMFAB use:** WO3 electrochromic, WC spheres for LRAM

### Supply Chain (China #1 at 82%)

**Global production distribution:**
- China: 72,000 mt/yr (82% of world)
- Vietnam: 4,500 mt/yr
- Russia: 2,000 mt/yr
- Portugal: 1,800 mt/yr (Panasqueira mine — Europe's significant W producer)
- USA: currently minimal (tungsten mining ceased; net importer)

**Price volatility:**
- APT (Ammonium Paratungstate) industrial standard
- 2022-2026 range: $280-$375/metric ton unit (MTU)
- 2026: $340/MTU = $34/kg W

**US domestic recovery:**
- US was #1 W producer pre-1980s; mineral rights exist in Nevada, Idaho, Colorado
- Almonty Industries (Canada) — operating Sangdong mine (South Korea); North American assets
- Proposed Nevada mines: under EIS review 2026

**CSMFAB W demand:** Small — primarily WO3 for window film and WC spheres for acoustic dampers.
Total annual demand (at scale): ~180 kg W equivalent.
**Strategic stock of 500 kg APT ($170 cost) covers 2+ years. Recommended.**

## Critical Mineral 6 — Samarium (Sm) — SmCo Magnets

**CSMFAB use:** SmCo permanent magnets in motor electromagnets (product 029, 035 control coils)
**NOT LiFePO4 — SmCo is used only in small control magnets, not primary propulsion**

### Properties of SmCo Magnets

- BHmax: 20–32 MGOe (excellent performance)
- Max operating temperature: 350°C (far exceeds NdFeB's 150°C)
- Price resistance: does not degrade in radiation (unlike NdFeB which can be bulk-demagnetized by GIC)
- **This is why SmCo is preferred in Aegis products over NdFeB — thermal margin for fire scenarios**

### Acquisition

- Electron Energy Corporation (Landisville, PA): **US domestic SmCo manufacturer** — key strategic supplier
- Arnold Magnetic Technologies (Rochester, NY): US-domestic rare earth magnet manufacturer
- TDK Magnetics (US facility): NdFeB focus but some SmCo capability
- Price: $75–$120/kg (Sm2Co17 grade B alloy)

**Critical action:** Establish exclusive supply relationship with Electron Energy Corporation before competition.
They are the only significant US domestic SmCo producer — a strategic national asset.

## Critical Mineral 7 — Titanium (Ti)

**CSMFAB use:** Primary in MXene precursor (Ti3AlC2), secondary in TiO2 components

### Global Supply and Domestic Resources

**Mineral sources:**
- Ilmenite (FeTiO3): Abundant globally — Australia, South Africa, Canada, USA (Virginia Beach heavy mineral sands)
- Rutile (TiO2): Rarer, purer — Australia, Sierra Leone, Mozambique, USA (Virginia)

**US domestic titanium:**
- **Virginia Heavy Minerals (VHM):** Major ilmenite/rutile reserve in Virginia Beach area
- **The Chemours Company (Savannah, GA):** Produces TiO2 from domestic minerals
- **Ti-rich basalt:** Eastern US basalt provinces contain recoverable Ti (synergy with basalt fiber program)

**Kroll Process (Ti metal from TiCl4):**
TiO2 + 2Cl2 + 2C → TiCl4 + 2CO → TiCl4 + 2Mg → Ti + 2MgCl2
Primary Kroll suppliers: TIMET (Titanium Metals Corp, Texas), Allegheny Technologies

**Price 2026:** TiO2 $3–$8/kg; Ti sponge $18–$28/kg; Ti powder (attrition milled) $45–$120/kg
