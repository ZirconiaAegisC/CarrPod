## Material 22 — PEEK (Polyether Ether Ketone) and PEEK CF40

**Formula:** Repeating units of ether-ether-ketone groups | **Class:** High-performance semi-crystalline thermoplastic

### 22.1 Physical Properties
- Tensile strength (neat): 100 MPa; CF40 composite: 220 MPa
- Continuous service temperature: 260°C
- Chemical resistance: excellent (all solvents, acids except concentrated H2SO4)
- Electrical resistivity: >10^15 Ω·m (neat); >10^10 Ω·m (CF40 — carbon fiber just below percolation)
- Thermal conductivity: 0.25 W/m·K (low — thermal isolator)
- Biocompatibility: FDA-approved for implants

### 22.2 How PEEK is Made

**Monomer:** 4,4'-difluorobenzophenone + hydroquinone
**Synthesis:** Nucleophilic aromatic substitution in diphenyl sulfone solvent → PEEK polymer
**Raw materials:**
- Benzene (from petroleum refining): $0.80/kg
- HF gas: $1.50/kg (fluorination step)
- Hydroquinone: $3.50–$6.00/kg (phenolic chemistry)
- K2CO3: $0.80/kg (base)

**Primary producers:**
- Victrex plc (UK) — dominant, ~70% global market share
- Evonik Vestakeep (Germany)
- Solvay KetaSpire (Belgium)
- Jilin Zhongyan (China)

### 22.3 Commercial Pricing

| Grade | Price/kg (2026) | Application |
|---|---|---|
| PEEK granules (neat) | $90–$140/kg | Machining, molding |
| PEEK rod/bar stock | $140–$210/kg | Machined parts |
| PEEK CF40 (40% CF) | $220–$320/kg | Structural composite |
| PEEK CF40 rod | $280–$380/kg | Machined structural |
| PEEK GF30 (30% GF) | $150–$220/kg | Less conductive CF replacement |
| PEEK sheet | $180–$260/kg | Flat structural |

### 22.4 In-House Production: NOT Recommended

PEEK synthesis requires specialized ether coupling reactors ($2M+) and diphenyl sulfone handling.
**Strategy: Purchase commercial PEEK and machine/mold in-house.**

**In-house PEEK machining:**
- CNC lathe/mill with carbide tooling: $25,000–$65,000
- PEEK machines cleanly with conventional tooling
- Machined part cost: material + $8–$25/part machining

**In-house injection molding of PEEK components:**
- PEEK injection molder (temperature-capable to 380°C): $45,000–$80,000
- Mold tooling: $3,000–$15,000 per design (steel mold; PEEK is abrasive)
- This reduces PEEK part cost by 40-60% vs. buying machined parts from suppliers

**Feasibility Rating: 3/5** for in-house manufacturing (buy material, add value via machining/molding)

**Purchasing strategy:** Establish volume supply agreement with Victrex (minimum 500 kg/yr commitment gets 15-25% price reduction).

**Products:** 006, 010, 012, 013, 016, 017, 019, 020, 024, 026, 028, 031, 033, 035, 038, CSMMECH 001–003, CSMIC 105, 106

## Material 23 — PEX-a (Cross-linked Polyethylene Pipe)

**Class:** HDPE cross-linked via peroxide method (a-type) | **Application:** Non-conductive water distribution

### 23.1 Properties
- Electrical resistivity: >10^12 Ω·m (complete GIC immunity for water piping)
- Service temperature: 95°C continuous (ASTM F876)
- Working pressure: 160 psi at 23°C; 80 psi at 82°C
- Memory effect: coils/bends return to shape at elevated temp — installation advantage
- Zero corrosion, -40°C freeze survivability (flexible when frozen)

### 23.2 Raw Material: Polyethylene

**Ethylene source:**
- Steam cracking of naphtha/ethane (petroleum refining by-product)
- Bio-ethylene: from bioethanol dehydration (Braskem — sugarcane feedstock, Brazil/Texas)
- HDPE price: $1.20–$1.80/kg (commodity — no in-house benefit)

**Cross-linking (Engel peroxide method for PEX-a):**
- HDPE + organic peroxide (dicumyl peroxide 0.5%) → extruded → heated chambers → cross-linking
- Degree of cross-link: >70% (ASTM F876 requirement)

### 23.3 Commercial PEX-a Pricing

|---|---|---|
| 1/2" PEX-a | $0.55–$0.75/ft | $1.80–$2.45/m |
| 3/4" PEX-a | $0.85–$1.15/ft | $2.80–$3.75/m |
| 1" PEX-a | $1.40–$1.90/ft | $4.60–$6.20/m |
| 1.5" PEX-a | $2.20–$3.00/ft | $7.20–$9.80/m |

**Domestic suppliers:** Uponor (Minnesota), Rehau (USA operations), Viega, Watts Radiant

**In-house production:** PEX-a extrusion requires $150,000–$400,000 extrusion line.
At current low pricing, commercial purchase is superior unless volume exceeds 100 km/yr.
**Feasibility Rating: 2/5** — buy commercial.

## Material 24 — PEN (Polyethylene Naphthalate) — Non-Conductive Spray Cans

**Class:** Aromatic polyester | **Key advantage over PET:** Better gas barrier, higher Tg (120°C vs. 80°C)

### 24.1 Properties
- Tensile strength: 60–90 MPa
- Oxygen barrier: 5× better than PET
- CO2 retention: ideal for pressurized aerosol cans
- Completely non-conductive: >10^14 Ω·m
- Recyclable thermoplastic (melt recycling)

### 24.2 Raw Materials

**Naphthalene dicarboxylic acid (NDC):** From naphthalene (coal tar processing or trimethylnaphthalene oxidation)
- NDC price: $4–$7/kg
**Ethylene glycol (EG):** byproduct of ethylene oxide hydration; $0.60–$1.00/kg

**Polymerization:** NDC + EG → PEN polyester (similar to PET production but different monomer)
PEN pellets: $3.50–$6.50/kg (vs. PET $1.20–$1.80/kg — 2.5-3× premium for better performance)

### 24.3 Blow Molding to Bottles/Cans

- Injection stretch blow molding (ISBM) — same as PET bottle production
- Suitable equipment: standard PET ISBM machine with temperature adjustment
- Equipment cost: $45,000–$120,000 (1-cavity to 4-cavity)
- PEN requires ~20°C higher processing temperature vs. PET

**In-house cost:** PEN pellets $4.50/kg + ISBM cycle cost $0.12/bottle = **$0.65–$0.95 per 150mL can body**
vs. commercial steel aerosol can: $0.45–$0.75 each (only for commodity volumes of millions)

**Feasibility Rating: 4/5** — using existing ISBM infrastructure (or modest capital investment)

**Products:** 018 (non-conductive spray cans)

## Material 25 — Soapstone / Steatite (Mg3Si4O10(OH)2)

**Mineral class:** Talc/steatite hydrous magnesium silicate | **Application:** Thermal mass, cooking, heating

### 25.1 Physical Properties
- Thermal mass (volumetric heat capacity): 2.22 MJ/m³·K
- Thermal conductivity: 6.5 W/m·K (spreads heat evenly — no hot spots)
- Max service temperature: 1000°C (no phase change below)
- Machinability: excellent with standard tooling (soapstone is the softest stone used industrially)
- Completely non-conductive: >10^12 Ω·m
- Emissivity: 0.91 (excellent FIR emitter for cooking)

### 25.2 Mining and Acquisition

**Primary sources:**
- **India (#1):** Rajasthan, Uttarakhand — largest global producer
- **China (#2):** Liaoning province
- **Brazil:** Minas Gerais
- **USA domestic:** Vermont (soapstone quarries active), Virginia, Massachusetts
- **Finland:** Tulikivi brand (soapstone fireplaces/ovens — proven market)

**Cost at mine/quarry:**
- Raw soapstone block: $2–$5/kg (India/China); $8–$15/kg (Vermont domestic)
- Soapstone slab (surface-finished): $40–$80/linear ft (architectural market)

### 25.3 Machining and Shaping

**Soapstone machines beautifully:**
- Standard woodworking tools work (so soft — Mohs 1-2)
- CNC waterjet cutting for precise profiles: $1–$4/linear inch
- Hand-shaping: $15–$35/hour labor

**In-house soapstone processing:**
- Angle grinders, diamond saw blades, belt sanders: $2,000–$8,000 total equipment
- In-house shaping cost: $5–$12/kg material + $8–$20/kg labor = **$13–$32/kg finished part**
- vs. buying finished soapstone products: $35-$80/kg finished

**Feasibility Rating: 5/5** — simplest material processing in portfolio. No heat, no chemistry.

**Domestic sourcing strategy:** Vermont Soapstone Company, Allegheny Gap (Virginia).
Build direct quarry relationship for 10+ ton/year volume → $4–$8/kg delivered.

**Products:** 007 (stone-bake oven), 008 (countertop warmer)

## Material 26 — GaN HEMT RF Amplifier Modules (2.45 GHz)

**Function:** Solid-state microwave source for Safe-Wave microwave oven
**Replaces:** Magnetron (1940s vacuum tube technology)

### 26.1 Performance Specifications

- Frequency: 2.45 GHz ± 50 MHz
- Output power: 100–900W (single module or combined)
- Drain efficiency: 97% (Class-F topology vs. 65-70% magnetron)
- Voltage: 28–50 V (low-voltage — no 4kV transformer needed)
- Size: 50–100 mm × 50 mm (small, modular)

### 26.2 GaN Supply Chain

**GaN on SiC wafer (preferred for RF):**
- GaN epitaxial layer grown by MOCVD on SiC substrate
- MOCVD equipment: $1M–$3M per reactor (foundry business — not in-house)
- Key suppliers: Wolfspeed (Durham NC — domestic!), MACOM, Qorvo, NXP Semiconductors

**Commercial GaN RF module pricing:**
- 100W module at 2.45 GHz: $45–$90 each (consumer volume)
- 500W combined module: $180–$280
- Automotive-qualified: $250–$450

**Domestic sourcing:** Wolfspeed (Cree spin-off) in Durham, NC — US manufacturer.
Their expansion facility (Mohawk Valley, NY, 2023) doubles US capacity.
**This is a key US-domestic supply chain for CSMFAB product 005.**

### 26.3 In-House Production: NOT Feasible

GaN RF wafer fabrication requires Class 100 cleanroom + MOCVD + ion implantation.
**Strategy: Design modular amplifier PCB using commercial GaN die or modules + custom driver electronics.**

**In-house PCB design and assembly:**
- Design custom driver/controller electronics around commercial GaN module
- PCB fabrication (outsourced): $8–$25/board
- Assembly: $5–$15/unit
- Total electronics cost: GaN module ($75) + PCB ($12) + assembly ($10) = **$97/unit electronics package**
vs. turnkey GaN microwave module: $120–$200

**Feasibility Rating: 3/5** — buy GaN RF die/module; design surrounding electronics in-house.

**Products:** 005 (Safe-Wave microwave)

## Material 28 — LiFePO4 Battery Cells

**Full name:** Lithium Iron Phosphate | **Chemistry:** Olivine structure LiFePO4 cathode

### 28.1 Why LiFePO4 Specifically

- No cobalt (eliminates DRC supply risk)
- No nickel (unlike NMC batteries)
- Thermal runaway temperature: 270°C (vs. 150°C for NMC) — fire-safe
- Cycle life: 3,000–6,000+ cycles (2-4× NMC lifetime)
- Energy density: 90–160 Wh/kg (lower than NMC but sufficient for most Aegis applications)
- Chemical stability in radiation/EM environments: excellent

### 28.2 Raw Materials

**Lithium source:**
- Spodumene (hard rock, Australia — Pilbara/Greenbushes): Li2O 7% ore → Li2CO3
- Brine (Atacama, Chile/Argentina; Clayton Valley, Nevada USA)
- Clay deposits: Thacker Pass (Nevada) — largest US deposit, active permitting
- Li2CO3 price 2026: $12–$22/kg (after 2022-2023 peak of $75/kg)

**Iron phosphate (FePO4):**
- From iron ore + phosphoric acid (phosphate rock) — both abundant and cheap
- FePO4 cost: $2–$4/kg

**Cell assembly:**
- Cathode: LiFePO4 + carbon black + PVDF binder on aluminum foil
- Anode: graphite + PVDF binder on copper foil
- Separator: polyethylene microporous membrane
- Electrolyte: LiPF6 in organic carbonate solvent

### 28.3 Commercial Pricing

| Format | Price (2026) | Supplier |
|---|---|---|
| LiFePO4 cells (cylindrical 32700) | $0.90–$1.30/Wh | Eve Energy, CATL |
| LiFePO4 cells (prismatic) | $0.85–$1.20/Wh | BYD, CATL, Gotion |
| LiFePO4 pouch | $1.10–$1.60/Wh | Amprius, Farasis |
| Domestic US (Ucore Rare Metals) | $1.80–$2.50/Wh | Premium for domestic |

**Portfolio battery demand:**
- Product 019 (forklift): 40 kWh → $8,500–$16,000 battery pack
- Product 050 (drone): 5.4 kWh → $1,150–$2,160
- Products 024, 038 (micro-batteries): 30 mAh each → $0.03–$0.05 each

### 28.4 In-House Battery Production: Future Consideration

At portfolio scale, battery assembly (not cell manufacturing) becomes viable:
- Cell procurement + module assembly + BMS design
- Equipment: automated cell stacker + spot welder + BMS circuit board
- Investment: $80,000–$200,000 for pack assembly line
- Benefit: 30-40% cost reduction vs. buying finished packs

**Feasibility Rating: 3/5** — cell assembly makes sense at scale; cell production requires >$500M.

## Material 29 — PMMA Plastic Optical Fiber (POF)

**Material:** Polymethyl methacrylate (same as Plexiglas/acrylic) | **Core diameter:** 0.5–2 mm
**Function:** Signal transmission in wiring systems; replaces copper signal wires for GIC immunity

### 29.1 Properties

- Optical bandwidth: 100 MHz·km (adequate for all sensor and control signals)
- Attenuation: 200 dB/km at 660 nm (significant — limits length to <100m without repeaters)
- Temperature range: -40°C to +85°C (adequate for vehicle and building applications)
- Bend radius: 25 mm minimum (flexible, easy installation)
- **Zero EM susceptibility — immune to all GIC, EMP, EMF**

### 29.2 Commercial Pricing

- Mitsubishi Rayon Eska (Japan): $0.45–$0.80/m
- Industrial Fiber Optics (USA): $0.55–$1.20/m
- Polymer Optics (UK): specialty $1.50–$4.00/m
- Bulk cable (1000m spools): $0.25–$0.50/m

**At portfolio volume (50 km/year estimate): $0.22–$0.40/m supply agreement**

### 29.3 In-House Drawing of PMMA Fiber

PMMA fiber drawing from MMA-grade PMMA rod → heated draw tower
Equipment: fiber draw tower (1–2m tower): $15,000–$35,000
In-house including MMA polymer: **$0.08–$0.15/m vs. $0.22–$0.80 commercial**
**Savings: 55–80%**
**Feasibility Rating: 4/5** — fiber drawing is low-complexity; MMA/PMMA readily available.

## Material 30 — Piezoelectric Materials: KNbO3-BaTiO3 and KNbO3

**Replacing:** PZT (lead zirconate titanate) — legally restricted under RoHS Annex II (EU) and increasingly regulated in US

### 30.1 KNbO3-BaTiO3 Properties (V2.0 Specification)
- d33: 285 pC/N (comparable to PZT-5H at 289 pC/N)
- Curie temperature Tc: 310°C (vs. 193°C for PZT-5H — 117°C higher thermal margin)
- Lead-free, RoHS/REACH compliant
- Dielectric constant: 600–1200

### 30.2 Raw Material Precursors

**Potassium (K2CO3):** Sylvite (KCl) mining → potash industry; $0.60/kg
**Niobium (Nb2O5):** Columbite-tantalite (coltan) ore from Brazil/DRC; $30–$55/kg Nb2O5
**Barium (BaCO3):** Witherite mining or chemical precipitation; $2.50/kg
**Titanium (TiO2):** Rutile/ilmenite mining; $3–$8/kg

**Synthesis route (solid-state reaction):**
K2CO3 + Nb2O5 → 2KNbO3 (1000°C, 4h, in air)
xKNbO3 + (1-x)BaTiO3 → solid solution → sinter to dense ceramics or press to piezo elements

**In-house synthesis cost:**
Nb2O5 ($40/kg) × 0.62 kg/kg KNbO3 + other components = ~$28–$40/kg KNbO3-BaTiO3 powder
Commercial: $180–$250/kg
**In-house savings: 75–85%**
**Feasibility Rating: 4/5** — standard solid-state synthesis; shared with ceramics program.

**Products:** Virtually all Embark products + stove, oven, water heater, plumbing, robotics
