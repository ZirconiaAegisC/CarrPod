## Material 1 — Zirconium Diboride (ZrB2)

**Formula:** ZrB2 | **Crystal:** Hexagonal (AlB2 type) | **Class:** UHTC

### 1.1 Physical and Chemical Properties
- Melting point: 3245°C (highest of common UHTCs)
- Density: 6.09 g/cm³
- Electrical resistivity: 7.8–9.2×10⁻⁸ Ω·m (mildly conductive — requires sub-percolation loading)
- Thermal conductivity: 55–65 W/m·K
- Vickers hardness: 22–23 GPa
- Crystal structure: layered, strong B-B covalent bonds + Zr-B metallic bonds

### 1.2 Raw Material Precursors

**Zirconium source:**
- Primary mineral: **Zircon (ZrSiO4)** — found in heavy mineral beach sands (Australia, South Africa, India, USA)
- Major producing countries: Australia (#1, ~35% world supply), South Africa (#2), China (#3)
- Zircon to ZrO2: Chlorination or fusion-alkali processing removes silicon → ZrO2
- ZrO2 to Zr metal: Kroll process (reduction with Mg) → then boronization OR direct synthesis

**Boron source:**
- Primary mineral: **Borax (Na2B4O7·10H2O)** — Boron, California (US Borax/Rio Tinto) and Turkey (#1 global)
- Also: **Colemanite (Ca2B6O11·5H2O)**, **Kernite** — all from evaporite deposits
- Borax to B2O3 to B metal: electrochemical reduction or carbothermal reduction at 1500°C

**ZrB2 synthesis routes:**
1. **Boro-carbothermal reduction (industrial standard):**
   ZrO2 + B2O3 + C → ZrB2 + CO2 (at 1600-1800°C in vacuum/inert atmosphere)
   — Most common commercial route; batch process in induction furnaces
2. **Direct synthesis from elements:** Zr powder + 2B → ZrB2 (SHS — Self-propagating High-temperature Synthesis)
   — Faster, less equipment, suitable for in-house at pilot scale
3. **Sol-gel precursor route (2025 advance):**
   Zirconium n-propoxide + boric acid in isopropanol → pyrolysis at 1200°C in H2
   — Produces submicron powder (<80 nm) at lower temp; lower cost than commercial powder
4. **Plasma synthesis (CO2 route, NIMS Japan 2025):**
   CO2 plasma + ZrCl4 + BCl3 → ZrB2 + CO + HCl
   — Carbon-neutral synthesis using captured CO2; emerging industrial relevance

### 1.4 In-House Synthesis — Feasibility Assessment

**SHS (Self-propagating High-temperature Synthesis) — RECOMMENDED IN-HOUSE ROUTE:**

Equipment needed:
- Inert-atmosphere reaction vessel (stainless, argon-purged): $8,000–$15,000
- Ball mill for powder mixing: $2,000–$5,000
- Sieve and particle size analysis: $3,000
- Safety enclosure (fines/fire hazard): $5,000

Process:
1. Mix Zr powder (99%, -100 mesh) + crystalline B (95%, -325 mesh) in 1:2 molar ratio
2. Cold-press into pellets at 100 MPa
3. Ignite in argon atmosphere — reaction is self-propagating (exothermic)
4. Mill product to target particle size
5. Quality check: XRD phase purity, laser diffraction particle size

**In-house cost estimate:**
- Zr powder: $45/kg (industrial grade commercial), B powder: $30/kg
- Reagent cost per kg ZrB2: ~$58–$72/kg
- Labor and energy: $35/kg
- **Total in-house: ~$95–$110/kg vs. $220–$420 commercial**
- **Savings: 50–75% compared to buying finished powder**

**Feasibility Rating: 4/5** — SHS is well-documented, low equipment cost, significant savings.
Main risk: SHS is exothermic/ignition-sensitive — requires basic safety protocols.

### 1.5 Recycled / Secondary Material Pathways

- **From Phoenix Protocol zirconia recovery:** Used Aegis-C panels → H2SO4 leach → ZrO2 precipitate → re-boronization
  Recovery efficiency: 85–92% Zr; adds $20/kg re-processing cost
- **Industrial zirconia waste:** Zirconium-containing slags from nuclear fuel processing, ceramics grinding swarf
  — Available from UNF (used nuclear fuel) facility waste streams; requires qualification

## Material 2 — Silicon Nitride (Si3N4)

**Formula:** Si3N4 | **Crystal:** alpha (amorphous/metastable) and beta (stable, hexagonal) | **Class:** Engineering ceramic

### 2.1 Physical Properties
- Melting/decomposition: 1900°C (decomposes; no true melt)
- Density: 3.17–3.21 g/cm³
- Fracture toughness (with sintering aids): 5–8 MPa·m^0.5
- Flexural strength: 700–1200 MPa (sintered grades)
- Thermal shock resistance: excellent (low alpha, high k)

### 2.2 Raw Material Precursors and Synthesis

**Silicon source:**
- **Quartzite (SiO2)** — mined in Virginia, Arkansas, Norway, Brazil
- Silica to metallurgical silicon: electric arc furnace reduction with carbon
- Si to Si3N4: two primary routes:

**Route A — Direct Nitridation (lowest cost, in-house viable):**
Si(s) + N2(g) → Si3N4 (at 1300-1400°C in N2 atmosphere)
- Uses silicon metal powder ($2.50/kg) and nitrogen gas ($0.08/m³ industrial)
- Reaction exothermic; requires temperature control
- Produces alpha-Si3N4; needs sintering aids (Y2O3, Al2O3) for densification

**Route B — Carbothermal Nitridation:**
3SiO2 + 6C + 2N2 → Si3N4 + 6CO (at 1500°C)
- Cheaper silica feedstock vs. Si metal

**Route C — Chemical Vapor Deposition (for films; not bulk):**
SiCl4 + NH3 → Si3N4 + HCl

### 2.4 In-House Synthesis

**Direct nitridation — viable at pilot scale:**
Equipment: Tube furnace (1500°C, N2 atmosphere): $12,000–$25,000
Raw materials: metallurgical Si powder $2.50/kg, N2 gas $0.08/m³
In-house cost: **$8–$14/kg** (vs. $18–$45 commercial)
**Feasibility Rating: 4/5** — straightforward chemistry; N2 atmosphere standard in ceramics labs.

### 2.5 Sintering Aids (Co-materials)

Si3N4 densification requires:
- **Yttria (Y2O3):** 2–8 wt% — see Critical Minerals (Part V)
- **Alumina (Al2O3):** 0–4 wt% — $2.50/kg technical grade (abundant)
- These form liquid phase at sintering temp → dense Y-Al-Si-N oxynitride glass phase

## Material 3 — Silicon Carbide (SiC)

**Formula:** SiC | **Crystal:** Cubic (3C-beta) or hexagonal (6H, 4H-alpha) | **Class:** Engineering ceramic

### 3.1 Physical Properties
- Melting/decomposition: 2730°C (sublimation)
- Density: 3.21 g/cm³ (sintered dense)
- Hardness: 26–28 GPa Vickers
- Thermal conductivity: 120 W/m·K (single crystal); 25-50 W/m·K (polycrystalline sintered)
- Electrical resistivity: variable, 1-10 Ω·cm (undoped); >10^12 Ω·cm (high-purity undoped)

### 3.2 Raw Materials and Synthesis

**Acheson Process (dominant industrial method since 1893):**
SiO2 + 3C → SiC + 2CO (at 2200–2500°C in graphite resistance furnace)
- Raw materials: silica sand ($0.08/kg) + petroleum coke ($0.25/kg)
- **The lowest cost advanced ceramic synthesis route in existence**
- Large-scale production: Saint-Gobain, ESK/3M, Fiven, ELK (Russia)

**Advanced powder synthesis for fine particles (for Aegis):**
- Chemical vapor deposition (CVD): SiH4 + CH4 → beta-SiC (expensive; for fibers/coatings)
- Carbothermal: SiO2 + C precursor (sol-gel route) → submicron SiC

**Feasibility Rating: 3/5** — energy intensive; viable for mid-scale operation with local power

### 3.5 Applications in CSMFAB

SiC is used primarily as:

## Material 4 — Yttria-Stabilized Zirconia (3Y-TZP; ZrO2)

**Formula:** ZrO2 + 3 mol% Y2O3 | **Class:** Transformation-toughened ceramic

### 4.1 Physical Properties
- Melting point: 2715°C (ZrO2 pure)
- Density: 6.05 g/cm³ (3Y-TZP)
- Fracture toughness: 8–12 MPa·m^0.5 (best of oxide ceramics)
- Flexural strength: 1200–1500 MPa (dental-grade)
- Hardness: 12–14 GPa
- Electrical resistivity: >10^12 Ω·m (roomtemp) — excellent GIC insulator

### 4.2 Raw Materials

**Zirconia source:** Same as ZrB2 — Zircon sand → ZrO2 via:
- Alkali fusion: ZrSiO4 + NaOH → Na2ZrO3 + Na2SiO3 → ZrO2 by acid leach
- Chlorination: ZrSiO4 + 4Cl2 → ZrCl4 → ZrO2 pyrolysis

**Yttria stabilizer source:**
- Yttrium from rare earth ores (see Part V)
- Y2O3 added at 3 mol% to stabilize tetragonal phase at room temperature
- This "metastable tetragonal" phase stress-transforms to monoclinic at crack tip → toughening

### 4.4 In-House Synthesis

**Co-precipitation route:**
ZrOCl2 (aq) + YCl3 (aq) → add NH3 → Zr-Y hydroxide co-precipitate → calcine → 3Y-TZP powder

Equipment: pH-controlled precipitation reactor ($8,000), filter press ($5,000), calcination furnace ($12,000)

In-house cost: **$25–$38/kg** (vs. $35–$140 commercial)
**Feasibility Rating: 4/5** — standard ceramic powder precipitation; well-documented.

### 4.5 Products and Applications

## Material 5 — Alumina (Al2O3) and Corundum

**Formula:** Al2O3 | **Crystal:** Rhombohedral (corundum) | **Class:** Oxide ceramic

### 5.1 Physical Properties
- Melting point: 2072°C
- Density: 3.99 g/cm³ (single crystal); 3.80–3.95 sintered
- Hardness: 18–20 GPa
- Electrical resistivity: >10^14 Ω·m

### 5.2 Raw Materials: The Most Accessible Ceramic

**Source mineral: Bauxite (Al2O3·nH2O)**
- World's most abundant ore after SiO2
- Largest reserves: Australia (33%), Vietnam (21%), Guinea (20%)
- US domestic: Arkansas, Alabama (minor production)
- Bayer process: Bauxite + NaOH → Al(OH)3 precipitate → calcine → Al2O3
- Cost of Bayer Al2O3: **$2.00–$3.50/kg** — most cost-accessible advanced ceramic

**High-purity synthesis:**
- Alcoa, Almatis: calcined technical-grade Al2O3 $3–$8/kg
- 99.99%+ purity via zone refining or Verneuil process for corundum: $25–$80/kg

### 5.3 Commercial Pricing

| Grade | Price/kg (2026) | Use |
|---|---|---|
| Technical (99%, Bayer) | $3–$6/kg | Structural, electrical insulation |
| High-purity (99.9%, calcined) | $8–$15/kg | Ceramic armor, precision parts |
| Sapphire (synthetic single crystal) | $500–$2,000/kg | Windows, wear |
| ALON (polycrystalline transparent) | $800–$1,200/kg | Transparent armor |

### 5.4 In-House Production

Producing calcined Al2O3 from bauxite requires Bayer-scale alkali plant — **not practical in-house**.
However, **purchasing technical Al2O3 at $3–$6/kg is the lowest-cost material acquisition in the portfolio** and no in-house benefit exists at this price.

ALON synthesis (for transparent armor applications): AlN + Al2O3 at 1800°C in N2 → ALON
Equipment investment: $45,000–$90,000 (pressurized hot press)
Cost differential: ALON commercial $800–$1,200/kg vs. in-house estimate $180–$280/kg
**In-house ALON: Feasibility Rating 3/5** — viable for robotics armor at scale.

**Products:** Used as sintering aid in Si3N4 and ZrO2; ceramic-coated motor windings; CSMMECH armor.

## Material 6 — MAX Phase Ceramics: Ti3AlC2 and Ti2AlC

**Formula/Class:** Mn+1AXn layered ternary carbide/nitride | Hybrid metal-ceramic

### 6.1 Physical Properties
- Density Ti3AlC2: 4.2 g/cm³
- Flexural strength: 250–350 MPa
- Fracture toughness: 5–12 MPa·m^0.5 (kinking/nonlinear elastic — no brittle fracture)
- Machinable with carbide tools (key advantage)
- Electrical conductivity: 10^6 S/m (metallic) — isolated use only for GIC immunity
- Max service temperature: 1400°C (Ti3AlC2)

### 6.2 Raw Material Precursors

Elements required: Ti + Al + C (and/or TiC)

**Titanium source:**
- Mineral: **Ilmenite (FeTiO3)** or **Rutile (TiO2)** — beach sands, Virginia, Florida, Australia
- Kroll process: TiCl4 + Mg → Ti metal (expensive; ~$20/kg)
- OR use TiO2 directly in carbothermal synthesis of MAX phase

**Aluminum source:** Alumina (Bayer) → smelted Al; $2/kg bulk aluminum metal

**Carbon source:** Graphite powder $2–$8/kg; highly available

**MAX Phase synthesis routes:**

**Route A — Solid-state reactive sintering (most practical):**
3TiC + Ti + 2Al → Ti4AlC3 (or vary stoichiometry for Ti3AlC2)
- Mix TiC, Ti, Al powders → press → heat to 1400°C in argon → MAX phase forms
- Equipment: regular box furnace + hydraulic press
- Time: 2 hours at temperature

**Route B — Titanium + aluminum + graphite:**
3Ti + 2Al + 2C → Ti3AlC2 (carbothermal, 1300-1500°C in argon)

**Route C — Hot pressing in argon:** highest density, 500 MPa + 1400°C

## Material 7 — Chemically Bonded Phosphate Ceramic (CBPC) / Geopolymer

**Class:** Inorganic polymer; cold-set ceramic; non-oxide cement-analogue

### 7.1 Composition (CBPC — Magnesium Phosphate)
- MgO (dead-burned magnesia) + KH2PO4 (potassium dihydrogen phosphate) + water
- Sets at room temperature in 15–30 minutes; no kiln required
- Compressive strength: 30–80 MPa; rises to 100+ with aggregates

**Geopolymer (alkali-activated):**
- Fly ash (class F) + NaOH (8M) + Na2SiO3 → geopolymer gel → hardens
- 78% lower CO2 vs. Portland cement; 80 MPa at 28 days

### 7.2 Raw Material Sources

**CBPC Materials:**
- MgO: magnesite (MgCO3) mining → calcination → magnesia; Turkey, China, Russia. $0.35–$0.80/kg
- KH2PO4: phosphate rock mining (Florida, Morocco) → H3PO4 + KOH neutralization. $1.20–$2.50/kg

**Geopolymer Materials:**
- Fly ash: Class F — sourced FREE or at minimal cost from coal power plants (waste product)
- Metakaolin: calcined kaolin clay ($0.55/kg); abundant worldwide
- GGBS: ground granulated blast furnace slag — steel mill byproduct ($0.18/kg)
- NaOH: Chlor-alkali process; globally available commodity ($0.45/kg 8M solution)
- Na2SiO3: silica + NaOH or by-product of silica gel production ($0.55/kg)

### 7.3 In-House Mixing — TRIVIAL

**No kiln, no special equipment for geopolymer:**
Mixer + molds → pour → ambient cure.
Equipment cost: $2,000–$8,000 (concrete-style mixing station).
In-house cost per m³: **$280–$380** (vs. Portland concrete $150–$250/m³ baseline; quality justified)

**CBPC in-house:** Same mixer; add phosphate addition and temperature control.
In-house cost: **$85–$120/m³** for CBPC without reinforcement

**Feasibility Rating: 5/5** — simplest in-house processing of any CSMFAB material.

**Products:** 006 (CBPC water heater shell), CSMIC 107 (geopolymer concrete), CSMMECH 003 (structural foundation)
