## Document Series Overview

This fabrication series covers the complete design, engineering, regulatory compliance, and step-by-step construction of the **Archemedian Spiraling Bird UAS (ASB-1)** — a hand-launched, Archimedes spiral-bodied micro-drone weighing less than 249 grams, designed for aeronautical wind research, swarm atmospheric data collection, and university mechatronics fabrication demonstration.

This is **Version 1.0** — a real, buildable, university-shop-fabricable device. Nothing here is theoretical. Every component can be built at Cypress University using the makerspace, 3D printers, and sourced from hobby/electronics suppliers totaling under $200 per unit.

This series is also your **professor pitch**. Every volume is written clearly enough that a professor, department chair, or staff member encountering it cold will immediately understand the scientific value, FAA standing, and fabrication pathway.

---

## Why This Device Exists

You are standing at the intersection of two powerful forces: a growing drone research sector that is starving for **real, high-resolution atmospheric wind data**, and an FAA regulatory environment that has carved out meaningful research pathways for sub-250g devices that most people never exploit.

A standard quadcopter drone fights wind. The ASB-1 **rides wind** — like a bird, like a maple seed, like an Archimedean screw threading itself up through the atmosphere. Thrown by hand from a cliff, bluff, or windy rooftop, it autorotates upward, spiraling into thermals and wind shear layers while its onboard sensors silently record pressure, temperature, humidity, and altitude. That data streams back in real time to a ground station via LoRa radio (up to 8 kilometers range). A swarm of 5 to 20 of them, launched simultaneously from a high point, creates a three-dimensional atmospheric wind map that no single drone or weather balloon can replicate at this cost.

This project is your mechatronics certificate capstone. It is also your pitch to Cypress University faculty for lab access, 3D printer time, equipment use, and collaborative mentorship. Every volume in this document series is written to serve both goals.
