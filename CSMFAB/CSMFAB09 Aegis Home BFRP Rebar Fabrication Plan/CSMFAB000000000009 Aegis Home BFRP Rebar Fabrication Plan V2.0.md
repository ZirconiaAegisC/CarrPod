## 2. Theoretical Physics of Failure: The GIC Structural Threat

### 2.1 Geomagnetically Induced Currents and the Ferrous Antenna

During a Carrington-class CME, the impact of solar plasma compresses the Earth's magnetosphere, generating a rapidly time-varying magnetic field (dB/dt) at the surface. Faraday's Law produces an EMF along any conductive path:

$$\mathcal{E} = -\frac{d\Phi_B}{dt} = -\frac{d(B \cdot A)}{dt}$$

In standard reinforced concrete, the steel rebar cage acts as a massive, unintended antenna array extending through the building's foundation. When GICs flow through this lattice:

**Failure Mode 1 — Joule Heating:**

$$P_{Joule} = I_{GIC}^2 \cdot R_{rebar}$$

For a G5 event (estimated I_GIC = 200–500 A in building-scale grounded conductors), and a #6 rebar (nominal resistance ~4 Ω/100m):

$$P_{Joule} = (300 \text{ A})^2 \times 0.04 \text{ Ω} = 3,600 \text{ W per meter of rebar}$$

At 3.6 kW/m of localized heating, steel rebar expands at:

$$\Delta L = L_0 \cdot \alpha_{steel} \cdot \Delta T = 1.0 \text{ m} \times 17 \times 10^{-6} K^{-1} \times 300°C = 5.1 \text{ mm expansion per meter}$$

The surrounding concrete (compressive strength ~35 MPa; tensile strength ~3.5 MPa) **cannot accommodate this expansion**. The steel-concrete interface fails catastrophically in tension, producing explosive spalling.

**Failure Mode 2 — Electrochemical Acceleration:**

The quasi-DC nature of GICs drives electrochemical corrosion at the steel-concrete interface:

$$\text{Fe} \rightarrow \text{Fe}^{2+} + 2e^- \text{ (anodic dissolution)}$$

Iron oxide (rust) occupies 2–6× the volume of the original iron. The resulting internal pressure exceeds concrete tensile strength at:

$$P_{expansion} = \frac{E_{concrete} \cdot \epsilon_{mismatch}}{1 - \nu} \geq \sigma_{tensile} \approx 3.5 \text{ MPa}$$

Structural cracking begins within hours of a sustained GIC event — **orders of magnitude faster than normal corrosion timescales**.

## 3. Basalt Continuous Fiber (BCF): The Volcanic Advantage

### 3.1 Material Origins and Supply Chain

Basalt fiber is produced from **volcanic basalt rock** — igneous material formed 65–100 million years ago, mined from natural deposits. The fiber manufacturing process is simpler than carbon fiber or aramid:

1. Basalt rock crushed and cleaned
2. Melted in platinum-rhodium crucible at 1400–1500°C
3. Drawn through multi-hole bushing to continuous fiber
4. Fiber diameter: 9–24 µm
5. Wound onto bobbins as Basalt Continuous Fiber (BCF)

**Key advantage:** No hydrocarbon precursors required (unlike carbon fiber). No hazardous chemical precursors (unlike aramid). Volcanic rock is abundantly available globally. The supply chain for BFRP is geopolitically resilient.

For FRP-reinforced sections in seismic zones (ACI 440.11-25 Section 18):

**Design Tensile Capacity:**

$$f_{fu}^* = C_E \cdot f_{fu}$$

Where C_E is the environmental reduction factor (C_E = 0.70 for basalt FRP in concrete, per ACI 440.11-25 Table 7.2).

$$f_{fu}^* = 0.70 \times 1000 \text{ MPa} = 700 \text{ MPa}$$

**Minimum Reinforcement Ratio (Seismic Zone D/E):**

$$\rho_{f,min} = \frac{0.35}{f_{fu}^*} \cdot \frac{f_c'}{E_f/E_s}$$

$$\rho_{f,min} = \frac{0.35 \times 35}{700 \times (60/200)} = \frac{12.25}{210} = 0.058 = 5.8\%$$

Note: higher reinforcement ratios are required compared to steel due to the linear-elastic (non-yielding) behavior of BFRP. The 2025 ACI 440.11-25 code provides confinement enhancement factors that partially compensate for the lack of ductile yielding.

## 6. Fabrication Protocol: Pull-Winding Process

### 6.1 Pull-Winding vs. Pultrusion

BFRP rebar for CSMFAB000000000009 is manufactured by **pull-winding** — a hybrid of pultrusion and filament winding:

- **Axial fibers** (0° rovings): provide primary tensile strength
- **Helical fibers** (±12° to ±25°): provide bond surface texture, shear strength, and transverse CTE control
- Result: rebar with both high tensile strength AND mechanical bond surface — eliminating need for metallic deformed bar pattern
