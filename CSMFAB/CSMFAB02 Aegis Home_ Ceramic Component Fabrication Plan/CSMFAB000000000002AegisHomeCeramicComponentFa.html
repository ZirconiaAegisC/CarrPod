In a standard wildfire, heat transfer is relatively indiscriminate. However, the selective liquefaction of a conductive mass like an engine block, in the absence of the total consumption of surrounding insulators, points to a mechanism of Electromagnetic Induction Heating.

The mechanism is explicable through the lens of Maxwell's equations. Aluminum is a highly conductive, non-magnetic metal. In the presence of a rapidly time-varying magnetic field ($dB/dt$)—such as that generated by a massive GIC event, a localized geomagnetic substorm, or high-energy Directed Energy (DE) flux—the metal acts as a susceptor. The magnetic flux induces Eddy Currents within the bulk volume of the metal. The power dissipated ($P$) in the material is defined by the equation:

$$P = \frac{\pi^2 B_{max}^2 d^2 f^2}{6 \rho D}$$

Where:

  • $B_{max}$ is the magnetic flux density.
  • $d$ is the thickness of the material.
  • $f$ is the frequency of the field.
  • $\rho$ is the resistivity of the material.
  • $D$ is the density.

Crucially, because aluminum has very low resistivity ($\rho$), it allows massive currents to circulate. These currents generate internal Joule heating ($I^2R$) that is volumetric—meaning the metal heats from the inside out. This rapid internal heating can exceed the heat of fusion ($L_f$) of the alloy before the thermal energy can be dissipated to the environment or the chassis.

3.1 The Matrix: Silicon Nitride ($Si_3N_4$)

The matrix phase of the composite is Silicon Nitride ($Si_3N_4$), specifically the $\beta$-phase.

  • Dielectric Strength: $Si_3N_4$ is an excellent electrical insulator with a resistivity $>10^{14} \Omega\cdot cm$ and a high breakdown voltage.2 This property is non-negotiable; the bulk component must insulate against GICs.
  • Thermal Shock Resistance: Unlike oxide ceramics (like Alumina), $Si_3N_4$ has a low coefficient of thermal expansion ($\sim 3.2 \times 10^{-6} /K$) and high thermal conductivity for a ceramic. This allows it to withstand the rapid temperature changes of a cooking burner without cracking—a critical requirement for replacing cast iron.
  • Fracture Toughness: The microstructure of sintered $Si_3N_4$ consists of interlocking, needle-like (acicular) grains. 3.2 The Reinforcement: Zirconium Diboride ($ZrB_2$)

The reinforcement phase is Zirconium Diboride ($ZrB_2$), an Ultra-High Temperature Ceramic (UHTC).

  • The Conductivity Paradox: Pure $ZrB_2$ is electrically conductive ($\sim 10^7 S/m$). This poses a challenge: how to use a conductive material in a "conductive-less" product?

The solution lies in Percolation Theory. In a composite of conductive particles ($ZrB_2$) dispersed in an insulating matrix ($Si_3N_4$), the bulk material remains electrically insulating as long as the volume fraction of the conductive phase remains below the Percolation Threshold ($\phi_c$).
