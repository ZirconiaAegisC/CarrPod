# Polaris Prototype & Field-Reversed Configuration Technology

**Date:** 15 July 2026
**Classification:** Corporate Dossier — Section 03.1

---

## Technical Summary

Helion Energy's fusion architecture is built on the field-reversed configuration (FRC), a compact toroidal plasma confinement scheme in which a self-organized plasma structure is formed, magnetically compressed in a pulsed cycle, and used to directly induce electrical current in surrounding coils — eliminating the steam turbine entirely.

The Polaris prototype represents the company's 7th-generation machine and the first privately funded device to demonstrate deuterium-tritium (D-T) fusion. It is the direct predecessor to the Orion commercial plant.

---

## Principle of Operation

### Field-Reversed Configuration (FRC)
An FRC is a high-beta plasma configuration in which the plasma's internal magnetic field reverses direction, forming a closed field-line structure that is self-confining. Unlike tokamaks, FRCs:
- Operate at high beta (ratio of plasma pressure to magnetic pressure approaching unity)
- Require no toroidal field coils
- Produce naturally elongated, compact plasma volumes
- Are inherently compatible with pulsed operation

### Pulsed Magnetic Compression
Helion's cycle proceeds in distinct phases:
1. **Formation:** Two FRC plasmoids are formed at opposite ends of a linear vacuum chamber.
2. **Translation:** The plasmoids are accelerated toward the center of the device at high velocity (hundreds of km/s).
3. **Collision / Merging:** The plasmas collide and merge in the central compression section, converting kinetic energy into thermal energy.
4. **Magnetic Compression:** External pulsed magnetic coils rapidly compress the merged FRC, increasing density and temperature to fusion-relevant conditions.
5. **Fusion Burn:** D-He3 (or D-T for testing) fusion reactions occur during peak compression.
6. **Expansion & Direct Energy Recovery:** The plasma expands against the magnetic field, inducing current in the surrounding compression coils — directly converting fusion energy to electricity.

### Thermodynamic Bypass
Conventional fusion approaches (tokamaks, stellarators, laser ICF) produce thermal energy that must be converted to electricity via steam turbines at ~33–40% thermal efficiency. Helion's direct electromagnetic induction recovers expansion work directly as electrical current, theoretically achieving substantially higher conversion efficiency and eliminating the capital cost of steam plant infrastructure.

## D-He3 Fuel Cycle

Helion's commercial architecture targets D-He3 fusion:
- **Reaction:** D + He3 → p (14.7 MeV) + He4 (3.6 MeV)
- **Key advantage:** Aneutronic reaction — produces charged particles (protons and alpha particles), not neutrons, enabling direct electromagnetic energy conversion and minimizing neutron activation of reactor components.
- **Temperature requirement:** Approximately 200 million °C — roughly one order of magnitude higher than D-T ignition.

Deuterium-tritium (D-T) is used for testing and validation because:
- D-T ignites at lower temperature (~100–150 million °C)
- Produces 14.1 MeV neutrons, simplifying diagnostic measurement
- Tritium handling infrastructure provides regulatory experience
- Helium-3 supply at commercial scale remains unresolved

## Tiny Merge

Tiny Merge, announced in spring 2026, is a 1/8-scale derivative of Polaris designed for rapid, low-cost iteration of FRC formation, merging, and compression physics. Its construction signals Helion's recognition that scaling from Polaris to Orion requires parametric optimization across a broader range of plasma conditions than a single full-scale prototype can deliver.

## Engineering Challenges

1. **Helium-3 supply:** No commercial-scale production pathway exists. Lunar mining, tritium decay, and natural gas extraction have been proposed but none are operational.
2. **Pulse repetition rate:** Commercial power requires approximately 1 pulse per second; Polaris operates at lower repetition rates.
3. **Capacitor lifetime:** 50 MJ capacitor banks must sustain millions of charge-discharge cycles with minimal degradation.
4. **First-wall materials:** Although aneutronic D-He3 reduces neutron flux, residual D-D side reactions produce 2.45 MeV neutrons, requiring radiation-resistant materials.
5. **Plasma stability:** FRC configurations are susceptible to tilt, rotational, and n=2 instabilities; active stabilization is required.
