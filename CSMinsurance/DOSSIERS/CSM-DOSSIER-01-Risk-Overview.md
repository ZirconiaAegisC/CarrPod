## SECTION 2: CORE DELIVERABLE — THE CARRINGTON EVENT EXPLAINED

### 1. THE 1859 EVENT — SEQUENCE OF OBSERVABLES

1.1. **1 September 1859, ~11:18 UTC**: Richard Carrington observes white-light solar flare from sunspot group at solar longitude 260°. Duration approximately 5 minutes. First solar flare observation in human history.

1.2. **1 September 1859, ~11:15-11:20 UTC** (retroactively confirmed): Richard Hodgson independently observes same white-light flare.

1.3. **2 September 1859, ~04:45 UTC** (17.6 hours transit): Coronal mass ejection (CME) reaches Earth's magnetosphere. Transit time of 17.6 hours implies CME velocity of approximately 2,300 km/s. Modern Carrington-class events estimate CME velocity range of 1,500-3,000 km/s.

1.4. **Geomagnetic effects observed**: Disturbance Storm Time index (Dst) estimated at -850 nT to -1,050 nT. For comparison, the March 1989 Hydro-Québec blackout event: Dst = -589 nT. The May 2024 Gannon Storm: Dst = -412 nT.

### 2. THE PHYSICS — GEOMAGNETICALLY INDUCED CURRENTS (GIC)

2.1. **CME-magnetosphere coupling**: The coronal mass ejection carries an embedded magnetic field (interplanetary magnetic field, IMF). When IMF Bz (southward component) is strongly negative, magnetic reconnection at the dayside magnetopause transfers solar wind energy into the magnetosphere.

2.2. **Ionospheric current systems**: The energy transfer drives intense auroral electrojet currents (millions of amperes) in the ionosphere at altitudes of 100-300 km.

2.3. **Geoelectric field induction**: The time-varying ionospheric currents (dB/dt rates up to 5,000 nT/min during extreme events) induce geoelectric fields at the Earth's surface. Magnitude: 1-20 V/km during severe storms, potentially up to 50 V/km during Carrington-class events. Direction and magnitude determined by local ground conductivity structure.

2.4. **GIC in grounded conductors**: The geoelectric field drives quasi-DC currents (0.0001-0.1 Hz, functionally DC for 50/60 Hz power systems) into any long, grounded conductor aligned with the field. This includes high-voltage transmission lines, pipelines, railways, and submarine cables.

2.5. **Transformer saturation**: GIC entering the neutral-ground connection of wye-connected transformers causes half-cycle magnetic saturation during each AC cycle. This produces:
   - Harmonic generation (even and odd harmonics)
   - Reactive power consumption increase (50-100 MVAR per transformer during severe storms)
   - Hot-spot heating in windings (can exceed 200°C in minutes)
   - Tank-wall heating from stray flux
   - Voltage collapse across the network as reactive power demand exceeds supply

### 3. SOLAR CYCLE 25 — CURRENT STATE

3.1. **Cycle 25 maximum**: Original NOAA/NASA prediction panel (December 2019): smoothed SSN peak of 115 ± 10 in July 2025. Revised prediction (October 2023): SSN peak of 137, now confirmed by observation.

3.2. **As of July 2026**: Solar Cycle 25 at or just past maximum. Observed SSN monthly values exceeding forecast by approximately 19%. Cycle 25 activity comparable to Cycle 23 (1996-2008, which produced the 2003 Halloween storms and the 2000 Bastille Day event).

3.3. **Comparison to Cycle 24**: Cycle 24 (2008-2019) was the weakest cycle in 100 years (SSN peak: 116). This minimum lull produced widespread complacency in infrastructure planning — a full 11-year cycle with no G5-level events.

3.4. **Carrington-class probability**: Peer-reviewed estimates place the probability of a Carrington-class event at 0.46-1.88% per year (Riley, 2012, Space Weather), 0.7-2.8% per year (Love, 2012, Space Weather), and approximately 10-12% per decade. The 2012 near-miss (STEREO-A observation of extreme CME that passed through Earth's orbital path, July 23, 2012) is considered a 1-in-150-year event that missed by approximately 9 days of Earth's orbital position.
