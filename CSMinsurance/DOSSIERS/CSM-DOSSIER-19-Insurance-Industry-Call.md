### 1. THE ARGUMENT — MORAL AND ACTUARIAL

#### 1.1. The Actuarial Argument
- The insurance industry exists to price and pool catastrophic risk.
- A Carrington-class CME represents the largest unpooled, unpriced catastrophic risk in the world.
- The industry's own surveys, reports, and research organizations have identified the component risks: Business Interruption (#1), Natural Catastrophes (#2), Cyber (#3). A Carrington event triggers all three simultaneously, globally.
- The industry's own loss estimates (Lloyd's, Swiss Re) place the potential insured loss at $0.6-2.6 trillion.
- The cost of prevention (grid hardening, materials research, product development) is $30-50 billion — approximately 1-2% of the potential loss.
- Investing in prevention reduces the modeled loss below industry capacity thresholds, converting an extinction-level event into a manageable (though severe) catastrophe.
- **The actuarial conclusion is unambiguous: fund hardening.**

- A Carrington event is not just a financial catastrophe. It is a humanitarian catastrophe.

- Funding hardening before the event prevents the humanitarian catastrophe. It keeps the hospitals running. It keeps the water flowing. It keeps the food moving. It saves lives — potentially millions of them.
- **The moral conclusion is unambiguous: fund hardening.**
