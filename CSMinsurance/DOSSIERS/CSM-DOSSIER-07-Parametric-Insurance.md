**[HOVER]**

The concept is simple, elegant, and proven. Parametric insurance — sometimes called index-based insurance — has been around for decades. It's used for hurricanes in the Caribbean. Earthquakes in Japan. Drought in Africa. The trigger is not "prove your loss." The trigger is "the index hit the threshold." You don't send an adjuster. You don't argue about causation. You don't wait for proof of loss documentation. The index triggers, the payment goes out. Period.

For hurricanes: wind speed at a specified location, measured by a specified weather service, hits a specified threshold. Payment in 14 days.

For earthquakes: ground acceleration (PGA) at a specified location, measured by a specified seismic network, hits a specified threshold. Payment in 30 days.

For space weather: the Kp index, as measured and published by NOAA SWPC, reaches a specified threshold (say, Kp=9, corresponding to a G5 Extreme geomagnetic storm). OR the Dst index drops below a specified threshold (say, Dst=-500, corresponding to severe storm conditions). OR NOAA issues a G5 warning. Payment within 72 hours of the threshold being met.

**[BURST]**

Why parametric? Four reasons.

One: Speed. After a Carrington-class event, the claims infrastructure — adjusters, loss assessors, the people who show up and say "yes, that transformer is definitely on fire" — will be overwhelmed. If you're waiting for a property adjuster to inspect 2,000 transformers across North America, you're going to be waiting months. Parametric triggers in 72 hours. Cash in hand while the power is still out. That cash buys generators. That cash buys fuel. That cash buys emergency response. That cash keeps hospitals running. Speed is not a feature. Speed is survival.

Two: Basis risk management. Let's be honest — after a Carrington event, the correlation between "the Kp index hit 9" and "your insured sustained a loss" is going to be approximately 1.0. Everyone has a loss. Everyone. The basis risk — the risk that the index triggers but the insured didn't actually suffer damage — is essentially zero for a G5 event. The index is the perfect proxy for the loss because the loss is universal.

Three: No coverage disputes. Is space weather covered under a property policy? Is it excluded? Is it a "named peril" or an "all risk" coverage? The lawyers will feast on those questions for years. Parametric policy: "If Kp hits 9, we pay X." No ambiguity. No litigation. No reservation of rights letter. The index triggers, the payment goes out, everyone moves on to the business of recovery instead of the business of arguing.

Four: Reinsurance efficiency. Reinsurers love parametric triggers because the loss is predefined and capped. There's no tail risk. There's no "the adjusters found additional damage six months later." The payment is the payment. The contract is the contract. The math is clean.

Client: Manufacturers, retailers, logistics companies with documented supplier concentration in high-risk grid regions.
Use case: Compensates for supply chain disruption even when the insured's own facilities are operational. Covers the "my supplier lost power and I can't get parts" scenario.
Premium: 1-4% of limit, depending on supply chain mapping and diversification.

## SECTION 2: CORE DELIVERABLE — PARAMETRIC SPACE WEATHER INSURANCE DESIGN

### 1. PARAMETRIC INSURANCE FUNDAMENTALS

#### 1.1. Definition and Structure
- **Parametric insurance**: Insurance product where payout is triggered by an objective, independently measured index exceeding a pre-defined threshold, rather than by the insured's actual loss.
- **Key components**: (1) Index definition, (2) Trigger threshold, (3) Payout structure, (4) Measurement agency, (5) Payment timeline, (6) Premium.
- **Advantages**: Speed of payment (days, not months), no claims adjustment cost, no coverage disputes, transparency, capital market compatibility (securitization).
- **Disadvantages**: Basis risk (index may trigger without loss, or loss may occur without index trigger). Acceptable basis risk for Carrington-class events due to universal loss correlation.

#### 1.2. Space Weather Indices Available for Parametric Triggers

| Index | Agency | Scale | Carrington-Class Value | Update Frequency |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Kp (Planetary K-index) | NOAA SWPC, GFZ Potsdam | 0-9 (3-hour) | Kp=9 | Every 3 hours |
| Dst (Disturbance Storm Time) | WDC Kyoto, NOAA | nT (hourly) | -850 to -1050 nT (est.) | Every 1 hour |
| NOAA G-Scale | NOAA SWPC | G1-G5 | G5 (Extreme) | Real-time alerts |
| Ap (Planetary A-index) | NOAA SWPC | 0-400 (daily) | >200 (estimated) | Daily |
| AE (Auroral Electrojet) | Various | nT (1-minute) | >3,000 nT | Every 1 minute |
| dB/dt (magnetic field change rate) | USGS, INTERMAGNET | nT/min | >2,000-5,000 nT/min | Real-time |

**Recommended primary trigger**: NOAA G-Scale (G5 = Extreme) combined with Kp=9. The NOAA G-scale is the most publicly accessible, regulatorily recognized, and widely understood space weather classification. Kp=9 provides the quantitative confirmation.

### 2. PRODUCT DESIGN 1: BUSINESS INTERRUPTION LIQUIDITY FACILITY (BILF)

#### 2.1. Product Specifications
- **Trigger**: NOAA issues G5 (Extreme) Geomagnetic Storm Warning, confirmed by Kp=9 observation within 24 hours of warning.
- **Payout**: Lump sum equal to 30 days of Gross Profit (as defined in standard BI policy wording) for the insured, capped at policy limit.
- **Payment timeline**: 72 hours (3 business days) from trigger confirmation.
- **Measurement agency**: NOAA Space Weather Prediction Center (SWPC), Boulder, Colorado.
- **Coverage territory**: Global. Trigger depends on global geomagnetic indices, not location-specific measurements.

#### 2.2. Underwriting Parameters
- Gross Profit verification: audited financial statements, last 12 months.
- Policy limit: $500,000 - $50,000,000 per insured.
- Premium rate: 0.5%-2.0% of limit, depending on:
  - Industry sector (manufacturing: higher exposure; services: lower exposure)
  - Geographic concentration (single grid region: higher premium; multi-region: lower)
  - Backup power capability (generator-equipped: premium credit)
  - Risk engineering assessment (AEGIS-C hardening: premium credit)

#### 2.3. Coverage Period and Renewal
- Annual policy period, aligned with solar cycle conditions.
- Renewable with premium adjustment based on updated solar cycle forecast.

### 3. PRODUCT DESIGN 2: EMERGENCY RESPONSE COST REIMBURSEMENT (ERCR)

#### 3.1. Product Specifications
- **Trigger**: NOAA issues G4 (Severe) OR G5 (Extreme) Geomagnetic Storm Warning.
- **Payment structure**: Option A — Lump sum of 50% of policy limit upon trigger, remaining 50% upon submission of emergency response cost documentation. Option B — 100% lump sum upon trigger (higher premium).
- **Covered costs**: Generator deployment, fuel procurement, temporary facilities, security services, emergency communications, water procurement, medical supplies, emergency food supplies.
- **Payment timeline**: Initial payment within 72 hours; final payment within 30 days of documentation submission.

#### 3.2. Target Clients
- Municipal governments and county governments.
- Hospital systems and healthcare networks.
- Public utility districts and co-ops.
- Data center operators.
- University and research campuses.
- Large industrial facilities with on-site emergency response capabilities.

#### 3.3. Underwriting Parameters
- Emergency response plan documentation: must be current (within 12 months), tested (tabletop or live exercise within 24 months).
- Generator capacity audit: fuel storage capacity, runtime at full load, refueling contracts.
- Total insured value for emergency response: $1,000,000 - $100,000,000.

### 4. PRODUCT DESIGN 3: SUPPLY CHAIN CONTINGENT BUSINESS INTERRUPTION (SC-CBI)

#### 4.1. Product Specifications
- **Trigger**: Kp index reaches 8 (G4) or 9 (G5) as reported by NOAA SWPC.
- **Payment structure**: Three tranches:
  - Tranche 1 (40% of limit): payable upon trigger confirmation.
  - Tranche 2 (30% of limit): payable at T+30 days if supply chain disruption continues.
  - Tranche 3 (30% of limit): payable at T+90 days if supply chain disruption continues.
- **Disruption verification**: Confirmation by insured's Chief Supply Chain Officer or equivalent.

#### 4.2. Supply Chain Mapping Requirement
Insured must provide:
- Tier 1 supplier list with geographic locations (country/region level).
- Supplier concentration analysis: % of revenue or COGS dependent on single-source or single-region suppliers.
- Supplier business continuity plans: existence, testing status, hard copy availability.
- Alternative supplier identification: secondary sources for top 10 suppliers by spend.

#### 4.3. Underwriting Parameters
- Premium: 1%-4% of limit.
- Rate factors: supply chain diversification (more diversified = lower premium), supplier geographic concentration in high-latitude regions (higher GIC exposure), supplier grid hardening status.
- Policy limit: $1,000,000 - $100,000,000 per insured.
