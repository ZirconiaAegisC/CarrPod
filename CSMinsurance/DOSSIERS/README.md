THE QUICK PITCH

The Carrington event — a geomagnetic storm that melts transformer cores across continents and triggers a global claims cascade measured in trillions — is the unthinkable that the insurance industry has not yet priced. This directory contains twenty technical dossiers that close that gap: documents that translate the physics of coronal mass ejections into actuarial mathematics, model the insured loss to the detail an underwriter requires, propose parametric insurance structures triggered by NOAA space-weather alerts, and build the case for why the insurance industry has both the financial incentive and the institutional capability to become the primary funder of Carrington preparedness. Every number in these dossiers is traceable to a CSMQuantum material study, a CSMFAB cost estimate, or a publicly available dataset. And the character who delivers the most memorable version of this argument is not Steele, not Calder — it is the Baker Street Adjuster, the metafictional insurance commentator from SiblingFrequency Episode 5, voiced with the dry precision of an actuary who has calculated the replacement cost of everything he has ever loved. ZIRCONIA: Welcome to the CSMinsurance underwriting office. I am Zirconia, director of insurance strategy, and these are Mork and Chester — heuristic actuaries. The twenty dossiers in this directory are the financial case for Carrington preparedness. Before we dive into the numbers, I want to acknowledge the actuarial profession whose methods we are using. Actuarial science — the discipline of quantifying risk using mathematics, statistics, and financial theory — has a history stretching back to the seventeenth century and the first life insurance tables. The catastrophe models that insurers use to price hurricane and earthquake risk were developed over decades by companies like RMS and AIR Worldwide, by academic researchers, and by the insurance industry's own modeling teams. The CSMinsurance catastrophe model for Carrington events builds on that tradition. We did not invent actuarial science. We applied it to a threat that had not yet been modeled. Mork, give them the headline. Lead with whatever feels right.

MORK: (puts down the whiteboard marker, turns to face the room) The global property-catastrophe reinsurance market has approximately five hundred billion dollars in dedicated capital. A Carrington-class event would generate claims estimated between two and six trillion dollars. That is a capital shortfall of a factor of four to twelve. The chain breaks at the retrocession level. Primary insurers exhaust their reinsurance. Reinsurers exhaust their retrocession. The losses cascade to policyholders through insolvency, to governments through guarantee funds, and to the broader economy through the credit contraction that follows an insurance market collapse. And the trigger for all of this is not a terrorist attack, not a pandemic, not a war. It is a solar storm. A thing that has happened before — 1859, 1921, 1989 — and will happen again. The probability in any given solar cycle is three to seven percent. The expected annual loss, weighted by that probability, is between twelve and eighty-four billion dollars. That is between zero-point-two and one-point-three percent of the global property-casualty premium base. The insurance industry routinely prices risks with expected losses in that range. It has simply not yet applied its tools to this one.

CHESTER: And the tools work. The catastrophe model in Dossier 02 — stochastic event set, geoelectric field propagation, transformer vulnerability function, exposure database, loss calculation engine — is built to the same standard as the hurricane and earthquake models the industry already uses. The code is open-source. The assumptions are documented. The sensitivity analyses are included. An insurer's own modeling team can run it against their own exposure data. We are not asking the industry to trust us. We are giving them the tools to verify us. Right on.

ZIRCONIA: Dossier 07 — the parametric insurance proposal. That is the document that has generated the most engagement from the industry. Mork, explain why.

MORK: Parametric insurance pays out automatically when a predefined trigger event occurs — no proof of loss required. The trigger for Carrington parametric coverage is a NOAA G5 geomagnetic storm warning or an ESA equivalent — public, independent, verifiable. The payout is a fixed amount per policy, based on the policyholder's exposure. The funds are available within seventy-two hours of the trigger — fast enough to fund emergency response during the storm itself. The pricing is based on the event-set probabilities from Dossier 02. The structure is administratively trivial — a NOAA alert triggers a bank transfer. And the Baker Street Adjuster, in Episode 5, delivers the best summary of why this matters: "Traditional insurance requires an adjuster to visit the site. During a Carrington event, there will not be enough adjusters. The damage will be everywhere simultaneously. The claims volume will overwhelm the system. The check will arrive too late. Parametric solves that. The trigger fires. The money moves. The community responds. That is not insurance innovation. That is insurance doing what it was always supposed to do — provide capital at the moment of maximum need."

CHESTER: Dossier 17 — Reinsurance Capacity. That is the one that makes people uncomfortable. The global reinsurance market does not have the capital to cover a Carrington event.
