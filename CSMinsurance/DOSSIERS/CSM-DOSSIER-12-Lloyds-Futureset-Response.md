### 3. WHAT THE LLOYD'S SCENARIO DIDN'T INCLUDE — AND WHY IT MATTERS

#### 3.1. Physical Attenuation Data
- The Lloyd's scenario used transformer damage functions based on Kappenman (2010) Metatech modeling, which modeled unmitigated GIC flow into transformers.
- The scenario did not account for GIC reduction through neutral blocking devices, which have been demonstrated to reduce GIC by 70-95% (Gaunt, 2014; industry case studies from Hydro One, National Grid UK, and others).
- The scenario did not account for the operational advantage of GIC monitoring systems that remain functional during the storm (via AEGIS-C shielding), enabling preemptive action.
- **Impact**: The Lloyd's scenario overstates transformer damage by 3-10x compared to a hardened grid.

#### 3.2. Parametric Insurance Products
- The Lloyd's scenario modeled the insurance response as standard indemnity-based claims processing — adjusters, proof of loss, coverage determination, payment. This process takes months and creates a liquidity crisis.
- The scenario did not model parametric products that pay within 72 hours of a defined trigger event, providing immediate liquidity.
- **Impact**: The Lloyd's scenario overstates business closure and economic cascade because it assumes no parametric liquidity. With parametric products, the economic cascade is partially arrested.

#### 3.3. Premium Credit-Driven Hardening
- The Lloyd's scenario modeled the grid in its current (vulnerable) state at the time of the event.
- The scenario did not account for a world in which insurance premium incentives have already driven hardening investment across the grid.
- **Impact**: The Lloyd's scenario implicitly assumes no pre-event hardening. If the insurance industry creates hardening incentives now, the grid at the time of a future event will be substantially more resilient than the current grid.
