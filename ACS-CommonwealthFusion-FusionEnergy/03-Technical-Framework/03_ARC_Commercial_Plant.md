# ARC Commercial Fusion Power Plant

**Dossier Reference:** ACS-CFS-FE-2026-TECH-003
**Subject:** ARC (Affordable, Robust, Compact) Commercial Reactor
**Target Site:** Chesterfield County, Virginia, United States

---

## 1. Design Philosophy

The ARC commercial reactor translates the physics and engineering validated by SPARC into a grid-connected power generation platform. The design acronym—Affordable, Robust, Compact—encapsulates the engineering objectives: capital cost reduction relative to government-led fusion programs, operational reliability sufficient for utility-scale deployment, and physical compactness enabled by HTS magnet technology.

ARC is designed as a 400 MW electric (MWe) net-output plant, representing a scale appropriate for mid-sized utility generation and industrial offtake. The plant is engineered for modular construction, rapid maintenance turnaround, and compatibility with existing transmission infrastructure.

## 2. Site Selection

## 3. Plant Architecture

### 3.1 Reactor Core

The ARC tokamak shares the fundamental compact high-field architecture demonstrated by SPARC, scaled to commercial power output:

- Toroidal field magnets: REBCO HTS, 20+ tesla operational field
- Plasma volume: Scaled from SPARC for 400 MWe output
- Fusion power: ~1–1.5 GW thermal for 400 MWe net electrical, assuming thermal conversion efficiency of 35–40%
- Fuel cycle: Deuterium-tritium (D-T)
- Tritium breeding: Lithium-bearing blanket for self-sufficient tritium production

### 3.2 Balance of Plant

The fusion thermal output is converted to electricity through a conventional steam Rankine cycle:

1. **First wall/blanket:** Absorbs fusion neutrons, converts kinetic energy to thermal energy in coolant (pressurised water or helium)
2. **Primary heat exchanger:** Transfers thermal energy to secondary steam loop
3. **Steam turbine:** Multi-stage turbine driving electrical generator
4. **Condenser:** Heat rejection via cooling water (once-through or evaporative tower)
5. **Generator:** Synchronous generator producing 400 MWe at transmission voltage
6. **Step-up transformer:** Grid interconnection at appropriate transmission voltage

### 3.3 Tritium Systems

Self-sufficient tritium breeding is essential for commercial viability. The lithium-bearing blanket captures fusion neutrons to breed tritium via the reactions:

- Li-6 + n → T + He-4 + 4.8 MeV
- Li-7 + n → T + He-4 + n' - 2.5 MeV

Tritium extraction, purification, and reinjection systems maintain closed-loop fuel cycle operation, minimising external tritium supply requirements after initial inventory loading.

### 3.4 Maintenance & Availability

A defining feature of ARC's commercial design is the modular replacement of in-vessel components:

- The vacuum vessel and first-wall/blanket system is designed for segmented removal and replacement
- Remote handling systems enable maintenance without full reactor dismantlement
- Target availability exceeds 80% annual capacity factor
- Scheduled maintenance intervals aligned with utility outage planning cycles

## 5. Google Power Purchase Agreement

Google LLC has executed the **first corporate fusion power purchase agreement in history**, committing to purchase 50% of ARC's electrical output. This PPA represents a watershed moment in energy markets, establishing precedent for:

- Corporate procurement of fusion-generated electricity
- Long-term offtake pricing structures for fusion power
- Credit enhancement for project financing
- Market signal validating commercial fusion as a bankable energy source

The specific terms—price per MWh, contract duration, take-or-pay provisions—remain commercially confidential but establish the template for subsequent fusion PPAs.

## 6. Grid Integration

### 6.1 Interconnection

ARC will connect to the PJM Interconnection grid via Dominion Energy's transmission network. Interconnection studies will assess:

- Fault current contribution and short-circuit analysis
- Voltage regulation and reactive power capability
- Frequency response and inertial contribution
- Transmission capacity adequacy for 400 MWe injection

### 6.2 Dispatch Characteristics

As a baseload thermal generator, ARC is expected to operate continuously at rated power, analogous to nuclear plants. Load-following capability is technically feasible but will be secondary to baseload operation in initial deployment. Fusion plants are inherently load-followable via tritium injection rate modulation, offering flexibility beyond traditional nuclear baseload constraints.

## 7. Regulatory Pathway

Fusion energy regulation in the United States is under active development:

- The Nuclear Regulatory Commission (NRC) has affirmed jurisdiction over fusion facilities
- A fusion-specific regulatory framework is being developed, distinct from fission reactor licensing (10 CFR Part 50/52)
- Key regulatory considerations: tritium handling and release limits, neutron activation of structural materials, safety analysis for worst-case accident scenarios (loss of plasma control, magnet quench, coolant loss)
- CFS is engaged with the NRC through pre-application interactions

The fusion regulatory framework is expected to be less burdensome than fission licensing due to the inherent safety characteristics of fusion: no criticality risk, minimal decay heat, and orders-of-magnitude lower radionuclide inventory.

## 9. Fleet Ambition

CFS's stated objective is the eventual deployment of **"thousands" of ARC reactors** globally. This scale ambition predicates:

- Standardised, repetitive manufacturing of reactor modules
- Establishment of multiple magnet production facilities
- Development of a fusion construction and services supply chain
- Licensing standardisation across multiple jurisdictions
- Cost reduction through volume manufacturing and operational experience
