# SPARC Demonstration Reactor — Technical Design & Status

**Dossier Reference:** ACS-CFS-FE-2026-TECH-001
**Subject:** SPARC Tokamak — Net Energy Demonstration Platform
**Status:** Dry Dress Rehearsal (July 2026)

---

## 1. Mission & Design Objectives

SPARC is a compact, high-field tokamak designed to achieve the first controlled net-energy fusion reaction in history—generating more thermal energy from fusion than the electrical energy required to heat and confine the plasma. The design philosophy eschews the ITER paradigm of enormous physical scale in favour of magnetic field intensity, leveraging HTS magnet technology to achieve fusion conditions in a machine volume approximately 1/40th that of ITER.

### 1.1 Performance Targets

| Parameter | Target Value |
|-----------|-------------|
| Fusion power output | 50–100 MW (thermal) |
| Energy gain factor (Q) | > 1 (minimum); > 10 (design) |
| Plasma temperature | > 100 million °C |
| Toroidal field strength | 20 tesla (at magnet) |
| Operational mode | Deuterium-tritium (D-T) |
| Pulse duration | Initially short-pulse; ARC-class steady-state design validation |

## 2. Physical Configuration

### 2.1 Site
SPARC is assembled within a purpose-built facility on CFS's 60-acre campus in Devens, Massachusetts. The tokamak hall accommodates the vacuum vessel, magnet systems, cryogenic infrastructure, RF heating systems, and diagnostic apparatus.

### 2.2 Magnet System

Eighteen toroidal field (TF) magnets form the backbone of the confinement system. Each magnet:

- Generates a peak field of 20 tesla
- Weighs approximately 24 tons
- Utilises REBCO (rare-earth barium copper oxide) HTS tape conductor
- Operates at cryogenic temperature of approximately -253 °C (20 K)
- Carries current exceeding 30,000 amperes
- Is manufactured as a single monolithic winding

The poloidal field coil set, central solenoid, and correction coils complete the magnet configuration necessary for plasma shaping, current drive, and positional control.

### 2.3 Vacuum Vessel & First Wall

The vacuum vessel encloses the toroidal plasma volume and provides structural support for in-vessel components. First-wall materials are engineered to withstand neutron flux and thermal loading during D-T operation. The divertor region manages heat exhaust and impurity control.

### 2.4 Heating & Current Drive

Radio-frequency (RF) heating systems provide the primary plasma heating mechanism:

- Ion cyclotron resonance heating (ICRH) for bulk ion heating
- Potential lower hybrid current drive for non-inductive current sustainment
- Commissioning of RF systems underway during DDR phase (July 2026)

### 2.5 Cryogenic System

The cryogenic plant provides liquid helium cooling to maintain HTS magnet operational temperatures. The system is fully operational as of the DDR phase, having been commissioned and validated against thermal load specifications. Thermal shielding and cryostat design minimise heat ingress.

### 2.6 Power Systems

Pulsed power and steady-state electrical systems supply the magnet coils, RF heating, and auxiliary plant loads. Power supply testing constitutes a core component of the DDR phase, validating voltage regulation, current ramp profiles, and fault protection systems.

## 3. Operational Phases

### 3.1 Construction Complete — Summer 2026

Installation of all 18 TF magnets marks the mechanical completion of the SPARC tokamak. The first magnet was installed in January 2026; the full complement of 18 is expected by summer 2026. Associated structures—cryostat, vacuum vessel, in-vessel components, diagnostic ports—are populated in parallel.

### 3.2 Dry Dress Rehearsal — July 2026 (Active)

The DDR phase encompasses integrated systems testing without plasma:

- Cryogenic plant cooldown and thermal stability validation
- Magnet energisation to operational current (30 kA+) with quench detection verification
- RF system commissioning and impedance matching
- Power supply sequencing and fault-mode testing
- Vacuum system bakeout and leak-rate certification
- Control system integration and data acquisition validation

### 3.3 First Plasma — Late 2026 / Early 2027

Initial plasma generation utilises hydrogen or helium feedstock for commissioning diagnostics and control systems. Plasma initiation, position control, and basic confinement are validated before transition to deuterium operation.

### 3.4 Deuterium-Tritium Campaign — 2027

Following successful deuterium plasma characterisation, tritium is introduced for D-T fusion operations. The campaign objective is Q > 1 achievement with progressive power escalation toward the design Q > 10 target at 50–100 MW fusion output.

## 4. Diagnostic Suite

SPARC incorporates a comprehensive diagnostic package for plasma characterisation:

- Magnetic diagnostics: flux loops, Mirnov coils, Rogowski coils for equilibrium reconstruction
- Thomson scattering: electron temperature and density profiles
- Charge exchange recombination spectroscopy: ion temperature and rotation
- Interferometry/polarimetry: line-integrated density
- Bolometry: radiated power distribution
- Neutron diagnostics: fusion yield, neutron spectroscopy
- Infrared thermography: first-wall and divertor thermal loading
- Spectroscopy: impurity content and transport

## 5. Physics Validation

Five peer-reviewed publications define the ARC-class physics basis, establishing:

- Confinement scaling laws validated for high-field, compact geometry
- Stability boundaries for the SPARC operating space
- Divertor heat-flux handling predictions
- Alpha-particle confinement and heating dynamics
- Disruption mitigation strategies

These papers, coupled with SPARC's empirical results, form the evidentiary basis for the ARC commercial plant design.

## 6. Risk Mitigation

| Risk | Mitigation Approach |
|------|-------------------|
| Magnet quench during operation | Active quench detection; distributed protection; individual magnet testing during DDR |
| Plasma disruption | Disruption prediction algorithms; massive gas injection; shattered pellet injection |
| Tritium handling | Licensed tritium facility; closed-loop processing; ALARA protocols |
| First-wall erosion | Replaceable plasma-facing components; erosion monitoring diagnostics |
| Schedule delay | Parallel workstreams; DDR de-risks integration prior to plasma |

## 7. Significance

SPARC's successful achievement of Q > 1 would mark the first controlled fusion reaction producing net energy, validating the compact high-field approach and unlocking the commercial fusion pathway embodied in the ARC design. The demonstration is positioned to catalyse the fusion energy industry from experimental science to deployable power generation.
