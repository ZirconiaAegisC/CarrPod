## 1. Summary of Capitalisation

Commonwealth Fusion Systems has accumulated approximately **$3 billion** in total funding since its founding in 2018, establishing it as the most capitalised private fusion energy enterprise globally. The company's fundraising trajectory reflects escalating investor confidence as technical milestones have been sequentially achieved, transitioning from early-stage venture capital to strategic corporate and sovereign wealth investment.

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Total funding raised | ~$3.0B |
| Number of funding rounds (disclosed) | 3 major rounds |
| Investor syndicate size | 35+ institutional and strategic investors |
| Largest single round | $1.8B Series B (2021) |
| Most recent round | $863M Series B2 (August 2025) |
| Valuation (estimated, post-Series B2) | Not publicly disclosed; likely in excess of $5B |

## 2. Funding Round Detail

### 2.1 Seed and Early-Stage (2018–2020)

| Detail | Information |
|--------|-------------|
| Amount | Undisclosed |
| Period | 2018–2020 |
| Lead/Notable Investors | Breakthrough Energy Ventures, The Engine |
| Purpose | Company formation; initial HTS magnet R&D; team building |

The seed phase capitalised CFS's spinout from MIT and funded the critical early work demonstrating that REBCO HTS magnets could achieve the field strengths required for compact tokamak fusion. Breakthrough Energy Ventures, the climate-technology fund established by Bill Gates and including prominent technology and energy investors, provided institutional validation from inception.

### 2.2 Series B (2021)

| Detail | Information |
|--------|-------------|
| Amount | $1.8 billion |
| Date | 2021 |
| Notable Participants | Temasek Holdings, Equinor, Breakthrough Energy Ventures, and multiple climate-technology and sovereign wealth investors |
| Purpose | SPARC construction; magnet manufacturing facility; Devens campus buildout; ARC preliminary design |

The $1.8B Series B round represented the largest single private investment in fusion energy to date and signalled institutional conviction that fusion was transitioning from scientific research to industrial development. Temasek, Singapore's sovereign wealth fund, contributed significant capital, as did Equinor, the Norwegian state energy company.

Use of proceeds:
- Construction of the 60-acre Devens, Massachusetts campus
- Establishment of the HTS magnet manufacturing facility
- Procurement of REBCO tape and magnet component materials
- SPARC tokamak assembly, cryogenic plant, and power systems
- Expansion of engineering and scientific personnel
- ARC conceptual and preliminary design studies

| Detail | Information |

The Series B2 round was strategically significant beyond its capital quantum. The round brought the total investor syndicate to over 35 institutions.

- Chesterfield County, Virginia site development

### 3.2 Strategic Rationale by Investor Type

**Climate-tech venture capital:** Direct alignment with decarbonisation thesis; fusion represents the highest-impact energy technology investment category.

**Sovereign wealth funds:** Long-duration investment horizon compatible with fusion development timeline; portfolio diversification into transformative energy technology.

**Strategic corporate investors:** Google and Nvidia's participation reflects (a) corporate demand for firm, 24/7 carbon-free power for data centre operations, (b) technology integration opportunities (digital twin, HPC), and (c) strategic positioning in the emerging fusion supply chain.

**Institutional asset managers:** Energy transition allocation mandates; seeking exposure to technology with asymmetric return potential.

**Energy sector strategic investors:** Equinor's participation signals recognition by incumbent energy producers that fusion represents a long-term competitive threat—and opportunity.

## 4. Capital Efficiency

CFS's capital efficiency compares favourably to the only government-led benchmark, ITER:

| Metric | CFS/SPARC+ARC | ITER |
|--------|--------------|------|
| Cumulative capital to date | ~$3B | >$22B (estimated, all sources) |
| Machine volume (approximate) | ~1/40th of ITER | ~840 m³ plasma volume |
| Design field strength | 20 tesla | 5.3 tesla |
| Target Q | >10 | 10 |
| First plasma timeline (from founding) | ~8–9 years | ~20+ years (projected) |

This capital efficiency is the direct consequence of the compact high-field approach enabled by HTS magnet technology. By achieving fusion-relevant confinement at dramatically reduced physical scale, CFS compresses both capital requirements and construction timelines relative to the low-temperature superconductor paradigm.

## 5. Financial Outlook

### 5.1 Remaining Capital Requirements

| Phase | Estimated Cost | Funding Status |
|-------|---------------|----------------|
| SPARC commissioning and operation | $200–400M | Funded from existing capital |
| ARC definitive design and licensing | $300–500M | Funded from Series B2 |
| ARC construction | $2.5B+ | Partially funded; will require additional equity and/or project finance |
| Magnet manufacturing scale-up | $200–500M | Funding pathway under development |

### 5.2 Future Funding Scenarios

**Scenario A — Equity Financing:** Additional private capital rounds, potentially including IPO, to fund ARC construction. Fusion sector momentum and SPARC success would support favourable valuation.

**Scenario B — Project Finance:** Non-recourse or limited-recourse debt financing secured against ARC PPA revenue streams. Google PPA provides credit enhancement; additional PPAs would strengthen the financing case. Generally available only after technology risk is substantially retired (post-SPARC Q > 1).

**Scenario C — Government Support:** Department of Energy loan guarantees (Title XVII, LPO), infrastructure grants, or production tax credits. The Inflation Reduction Act and ADVANCE Act provide statutory basis for federal support of fusion deployment.

**Scenario D — Strategic Partnership:** Joint venture or co-investment structure with utility or industrial partners for ARC deployment, reducing CFS's direct capital burden.

## 6. Risk Factors

| Risk | Description | Probability | Impact |
|------|-------------|------------|--------|
| Technology failure | SPARC fails to achieve Q > 1 | Low-Medium | Critical: ARC pathway blocked |
| Capital availability | Inability to raise ARC construction financing | Medium | High: Delays deployment |
| Cost escalation | ARC construction exceeds $2.5B estimate | Medium-High | Medium: Reduces investor returns |
| Regulatory delay | NRC licensing timeline extends beyond projections | Medium | Medium: Delays grid connection |
| Competition | Alternative fusion approach achieves commercial viability first | Low-Medium | Medium: Reduces first-mover advantage |
| PPA counterparty risk | Google or subsequent offtakers exercise contract termination rights | Low | Medium: Revenue uncertainty |
