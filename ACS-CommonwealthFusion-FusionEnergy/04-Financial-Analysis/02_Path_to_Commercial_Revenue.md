## 1. Revenue Pathway Architecture

CFS's path to commercial revenue is structured across four interdependent channels, sequenced to provide revenue generation prior to, concurrent with, and following fusion breakeven. This multi-channel architecture hedges technology execution risk by ensuring that HTS magnet sales and licensing revenues can sustain the enterprise even if fusion electricity sales are delayed.

| Channel | Revenue Type | Timing | Risk Profile |
|---------|-------------|--------|-------------|
| HTS magnet sales | Product revenue | 2026+ | Low: existing market demand |
| Electricity sales (PPA) | Recurring revenue | Early 2030s | Medium: conditional on ARC completion |
| Technology licensing | Royalty revenue | 2030s+ | Low-Medium: contingent on international deployment |
| Carbon credits / policy incentives | Supplementary revenue | 2030s+ | Medium: dependent on policy continuity |

## 2. Channel I: HTS Magnet Sales (2026+)

### 2.1 Revenue Model

HTS magnets manufactured at the Devens facility are saleable into adjacent markets independent of fusion energy commercialisation. This channel provides near-term revenue and validates manufacturing capability.

### 2.2 Addressable Adjacent Markets

| Market | Application | Estimated TAM ($B) | Time to Revenue |
|--------|------------|-------------------|-----------------|
| Offshore wind | Direct-drive HTS generators | $2–5B annual | 2028–2030 |
| Medical imaging | High-field MRI magnets | $1–2B annual | 2027–2029 |
| Scientific instrumentation | NMR, particle physics magnets | $0.5–1B annual | 2026+ |
| Electric propulsion | HTS motors for marine/aviation | $1–3B annual (emerging) | 2030+ |
| Grid infrastructure | Fault current limiters, SMES | $0.5–1B annual | 2028+ |

### 2.3 Competitive Position in Adjacent Markets

CFS's magnet manufacturing capability—validated by the production of 18 full-scale tokamak-grade TF magnets—represents a capability that no entity in the HTS magnet supply chain currently possesses at equivalent scale and demonstrated reliability. This positions CFS to capture premium pricing in applications requiring the highest-field, highest-reliability HTS magnets.

The manufacturing learning curve accumulated through tokamak magnet production directly translates to cost competitiveness in adjacent markets. As production volume increases, REBCO tape procurement costs decrease, winding process yield improves, and testing overhead is amortised.

## 3. Channel II: Electricity Sales (Early 2030s+)

### 3.1 Google PPA Structure

The Google PPA for 50% of ARC output establishes the commercial revenue baseline. While specific terms are confidential, indicative analysis based on comparable clean-energy PPAs suggests:

| Parameter | Indicative Range |
|-----------|-----------------|
| Contract duration | 15–25 years |
| Pricing structure | Fixed base price with escalation; possible two-part tariff |
| Volume commitment | 50% of net output (~140 MWe average) |
| Delivery obligation | As-available (fusion plant dispatch) |
| Credit support | Investment-grade offtaker (Google/Alphabet) |

The PPA with an investment-grade counterparty provides the credit enhancement necessary for project finance of ARC construction, reducing the weighted average cost of capital.

### 3.2 Subsequent PPA Development

The Google PPA establishes a template for subsequent offtake agreements. Target offtakers include:

- **Hyper-scale data centre operators:** Microsoft, Amazon (AWS), Meta — all have announced 24/7 carbon-free energy commitments and face growing difficulty meeting them with intermittent renewables alone
- **Industrial consumers:** Steel, cement, chemicals, and hydrogen production facilities requiring firm, carbon-free process heat and power
- **Utilities:** Investor-owned utilities with coal retirement schedules and net-zero mandates, seeking dispatchable zero-carbon replacement capacity
- **Government procurement:** Federal and state agencies with clean energy procurement mandates

### 3.3 Pricing Trajectory

| ARC Unit | Estimated LCOE ($/MWh) | PPA Price Range ($/MWh) | Margin |
|----------|----------------------|------------------------|--------|
| 1st unit (FOAK) | $120–180 | $100–150 | Negative to thin |
| 5th unit | $80–120 | $80–120 | Break-even to margin |
| 20th unit (NOAK) | $50–80 | $60–90 | Positive margin |
| 100th unit | $40–60 | Market-competitive | Strong margin |

The first-of-a-kind (FOAK) ARC unit may not be profitable on a standalone electricity-sales basis; its economic value lies in establishing the cost-learning trajectory, validating the technology at commercial scale, and satisfying PPA obligations that unlock subsequent project finance.

## 4. Channel III: Technology Licensing (2030s+)

### 4.1 Licensing Model

CFS's stated ambition for "thousands" of ARC reactors globally cannot be executed through direct construction and ownership alone. International deployment will be enabled through licensing agreements under which:

- CFS provides the ARC reactor design, HTS magnets, and key proprietary components
- In-country licensees provide site development, construction, grid interconnection, operations, and local regulatory compliance
- CFS receives upfront licensing fees and ongoing royalties based on electricity output or revenue

### 4.2 Target Licensing Jurisdictions

| Region | Rationale | Probability |
|--------|-----------|------------|
| United Kingdom | Established fusion regulatory framework; net-zero mandate | High |
| Japan | Fusion leadership ambition; limited domestic fossil resources | High |
| South Korea | KSTAR experience; industrial manufacturing capability | High |
| European Union | Green Deal; nuclear-inclusive taxonomy | Medium-High |
| Middle East (UAE, Saudi Arabia) | Energy diversification; sovereign capital availability | Medium |
| Southeast Asia | Growing electricity demand; limited renewable resources | Medium |

### 4.3 Licensing Economics (Indicative)

| Revenue Component | Indicative Value per Unit |
|-------------------|--------------------------|

| Magnet supply (18 TF + PF coils) | $300–500M |

## 5. Channel IV: Carbon Credits and Policy Incentives

### 5.1 Applicable Mechanisms

| Mechanism | Jurisdiction | Estimated Value |
|-----------|-------------|-----------------|
| Production Tax Credit (PTC) | US (Inflation Reduction Act §45Y) | $25–30/MWh (technology-neutral clean electricity PTC) |
| Zero-Emission Renewable Energy Credits (ZRECs) | US state-level | $5–15/MWh |
| Carbon credits (voluntary market) | International | $5–50/tonne CO₂e |
| EU Emissions Trading System | European Union | €50–100/tonne CO₂e |
| UK Carbon Price Support | United Kingdom | £18/tonne CO₂e (minimum) |

### 5.2 Policy Risk

The Inflation Reduction Act's technology-neutral PTC extends through 2032 or until power sector emissions decline by 75% from 2022 levels, whichever is later. Subsequent administrations may modify or extend these provisions. CFS's revenue model does not assume indefinite policy support, but near-term credits enhance FOAK ARC economics during the cost-learning phase.

## 7. Investment Thesis Summary

CFS represents a rare investment profile combining:

1. **Technology risk retirement:** Imminent SPARC first plasma and Q > 1 demonstration
2. **Near-term revenue catalyst:** HTS magnet sales to adjacent markets independent of fusion success
3. **Asymmetric upside:** Fusion electricity market measured in trillions of dollars
4. **Strategic partnership depth:** Google PPA, Nvidia technology integration, Dominion site lease
5. **Regulatory tailwind:** Fusion-specific regulatory framework development in US and internationally
6. **Capitalisation advantage:** ~$3B funding provides multi-year runway and competitive moat

The primary investment risk is technology execution: SPARC must demonstrate Q > 1 to unlock the ARC pathway. The secondary risk is capital availability for ARC construction, partially mitigated by the HTS magnet revenue channel and the credit-enhancement effect of the Google PPA.
