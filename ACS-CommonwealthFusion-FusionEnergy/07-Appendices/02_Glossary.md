# Glossary of Terms

**Dossier Reference:** ACS-CFS-FE-2026-APPX-002
**Subject:** Technical Terminology, Acronyms, and Definitions
**Date:** July 2026

---

## A

**Alpha Particle:** A helium-4 nucleus (two protons, two neutrons) produced in D-T fusion reactions. Alpha particles carry approximately 20% of the fusion energy and are confined by the magnetic field, providing self-heating of the plasma.

**ALARA:** "As Low As Reasonably Achievable" — a radiation safety principle requiring that radiation exposure and releases be minimised to the extent practical.

**ARC:** "Affordable, Robust, Compact" — CFS's commercial fusion power plant design, targeting 400 MWe net electrical output.

## B

**Balance of Plant (BOP):** All supporting systems of a power plant outside the reactor core, including steam turbines, generators, cooling systems, electrical switchgear, and auxiliary systems.

**Blanket:** The structure surrounding the fusion plasma that absorbs neutron energy (converting it to thermal energy) and, in a breeding blanket, produces tritium through neutron interaction with lithium.

**Burning Plasma:** A plasma in which the majority of heating is provided by fusion-produced alpha particles rather than external heating systems. Achieving a burning plasma is a primary objective of SPARC.

## C

**Central Solenoid (CS):** The solenoid magnet at the centre of a tokamak that induces plasma current through transformer action.

**Compact High-Field Approach:** CFS's design philosophy using HTS magnets operating at 20 tesla to achieve fusion conditions in a physically compact machine, avoiding the massive scale of ITER-class tokamaks.

**Critical Temperature (Tc):** The temperature below which a material becomes superconducting. For REBCO HTS, Tc exceeds 90 K.

**Cryostat:** The vacuum-insulated vessel that houses superconducting magnets and maintains cryogenic temperatures.

## D

**DDR (Dry Dress Rehearsal):** Integrated systems testing of the tokamak without plasma, including magnet energisation, cryogenic cooldown, RF system commissioning, and control system validation.

**Deuterium (D):** A hydrogen isotope with one proton and one neutron. Together with tritium, forms the D-T fuel for fusion reactors. Abundant in seawater (~33 grams per cubic metre).

**Deuterium-Tritium (D-T) Reaction:** The fusion reaction D + T → He-4 (3.5 MeV) + n (14.1 MeV), the primary reaction for first-generation fusion reactors due to its high cross-section at achievable temperatures.

**Digital Twin:** A virtual representation of a physical system—in this context, a full-physics simulation of the SPARC or ARC reactor—used for design iteration, predictive maintenance, and operational optimisation.

**Disruption:** A sudden loss of plasma confinement in a tokamak, potentially resulting in large electromagnetic forces and thermal loads on reactor components.

**Divertor:** The region of a tokamak designed to exhaust heat and helium ash from the plasma,承受 the highest heat flux of any plasma-facing component.

## E

**Engineering Current Density (Je):** The current per unit cross-sectional area of a superconducting winding including structural materials, insulation, and coolant channels—as distinct from the current density in the superconductor alone.

## F

**First Plasma:** The initial creation of a plasma within the tokamak vacuum vessel, marking the transition from construction to operations.

**First Wall:** The innermost surface of the vacuum vessel directly facing the plasma,承受 neutron and thermal flux.

**Fusion Energy Gain Factor (Q):** The ratio of fusion power output to external heating power input. Q = 1 represents breakeven (fusion power equals heating power). Q > 1 represents net energy gain. SPARC targets Q > 1, with design Q > 10.

## H

**High-Temperature Superconductor (HTS):** A material that becomes superconducting at temperatures above approximately 30 K. For CFS, specifically REBCO (rare-earth barium copper oxide), which superconducts above 90 K and is operated at ~20 K for fusion magnet applications.

**Hydrogen (H):** The simplest element. Hydrogen plasmas are typically used for initial tokamak commissioning before transitioning to deuterium and tritium.

## I

**Ion Cyclotron Resonance Heating (ICRH):** A plasma heating method using radio-frequency waves at the ion cyclotron frequency to transfer energy to plasma ions.

**ITER:** "International Thermonuclear Experimental Reactor" — the multinational fusion experiment under construction in Cadarache, France, using niobium-tin low-temperature superconductor magnets at 5.3 tesla. Originally projected to cost ~$5 billion; current estimates exceed $22 billion.

## L

**Levelised Cost of Energy (LCOE):** The per-unit cost of electricity over the lifetime of a generating asset, accounting for capital costs, fuel, operations and maintenance, and financing costs. Expressed in $/MWh.

**Lithium (Li):** Element used in the breeding blanket to produce tritium through neutron capture. Li-6 + n → T + He-4 + 4.8 MeV; Li-7 + n → T + He-4 + n' - 2.5 MeV.

**Lorentz Force:** The electromagnetic force on a current-carrying conductor in a magnetic field. In tokamak magnets, Lorentz forces are enormous, requiring high-strength structural casings.

**Low-Temperature Superconductor (LTS):** Superconducting materials that operate at temperatures below approximately 20 K, such as niobium-titanium (NbTi, Tc ≈ 9 K) and niobium-tin (Nb3Sn, Tc ≈ 18 K). Used in ITER and predecessor tokamaks.

## M

**Magnetohydrodynamics (MHD):** The study of the behaviour of electrically conducting fluids (such as plasmas) in magnetic fields. MHD instabilities are a primary cause of plasma disruptions.

**MWe:** Megawatts electric — the net electrical power output of a power plant. 1 MWe = 1,000 kilowatts.

**MWth:** Megawatts thermal — the thermal power output of a reactor before conversion to electricity. ARC's 400 MWe output requires approximately 1–1.5 GWth fusion power.

## N

**Net Energy:** The condition in which a fusion reactor produces more energy (thermal output) than is consumed by its heating, magnet, and auxiliary systems. SPARC targets the first demonstration of controlled fusion net energy.

**NRC (Nuclear Regulatory Commission):** The United States federal agency responsible for licensing and regulating nuclear facilities, including fusion energy systems.

**Nth-of-a-Kind (NOAK):** The cost and performance characteristics of a mature technology after manufacturing learning and construction experience have been accumulated through multiple units. Contrast with FOAK (First-of-a-Kind).

## P

**Plasma:** A state of matter in which electrons are separated from atomic nuclei, forming an electrically conductive ionised gas. Fusion plasmas are confined by magnetic fields and heated to temperatures exceeding 100 million degrees Celsius.

**Plasma-Facing Component (PFC):** Any material surface directly exposed to the plasma, including the first wall and divertor targets. PFCs must withstand extreme heat, neutron bombardment, and erosion.

**Poloidal Field (PF) Coils:** Magnets that produce the poloidal (vertical plane) magnetic field component for plasma shaping and position control.

**Power Purchase Agreement (PPA):** A long-term contract between an electricity generator and a purchaser (offtaker) specifying price, volume, and delivery terms. Google's PPA with CFS is the first corporate fusion PPA.

## Q

**Q:** See Fusion Energy Gain Factor.

**Quench:** The sudden transition of a superconducting magnet to the normal (resistive) state, typically triggered by a local temperature rise. Quench protection systems must safely dissipate the stored magnetic energy without damaging the magnet.

## R

**REBCO:** Rare-Earth Barium Copper Oxide — the high-temperature superconductor used in CFS's magnets, typically YBa₂Cu₃O₇₋ₓ or GdBa₂Cu₃O₇₋ₓ.

**Remote Handling:** The use of robotic systems to perform maintenance and component replacement within the activated environment of a fusion reactor, avoiding human entry.

## S

**SPARC:** The compact, high-field tokamak built by CFS to demonstrate net-energy fusion (Q > 1). Sited at Devens, Massachusetts.

**Superconductivity:** The property of certain materials to conduct electricity with zero resistance when cooled below their critical temperature.

## T

**Tesla (T):** The SI unit of magnetic flux density. 1 tesla = 10,000 gauss. Earth's magnetic field is approximately 50 microtesla. SPARC magnets operate at 20 tesla.

**Tokamak:** A magnetic confinement fusion device using a toroidal (doughnut-shaped) configuration with both toroidal and poloidal magnetic field components. The most advanced and well-characterised fusion concept.

**Toroidal Field (TF) Coils:** The magnets that produce the primary toroidal (horizontal plane, around the torus) magnetic field for plasma confinement. SPARC and ARC each use 18 TF coils.

**Tritium (T):** A radioactive hydrogen isotope with one proton and two neutrons, with a half-life of 12.3 years. Together with deuterium, forms the D-T fusion fuel. Tritium is bred from lithium in the reactor blanket.

## V

**Vacuum Vessel:** The sealed chamber that contains the plasma at ultra-high vacuum conditions, preventing contamination of the fusion plasma by atmospheric gases.
