I want to describe what happens in a house during a Carrington Event. Not in a terrifying, disaster-movie-trailer way — but in the specific, physics-accurate, genuinely important way that a person who cares whether their house still works afterward actually needs to understand.

The copper wiring in your walls doesn't know it's wiring. It doesn't know the purpose for which it was installed. It knows one thing: it is an excellent conductor. And when a Geomagnetically Induced Current passes through the Earth's surface — as it does, systematically and powerfully, during a G5 storm event — every length of copper in your home's wiring system becomes a participant in an electromagnetic event it was never designed for. The current flowing through that wiring doesn't follow the circuit plan. It flows wherever the path allows, generating heat at every junction, stressing insulation to failure, creating arcing at switches and panels. This is how copper wiring in walls during the 1859 event started fires. The material doesn't need to reach some dramatic external temperature. It generates the temperature internally.

Your refrigerator has conductive components. Your microwave has a magnetron, which is one of the most GIC-vulnerable components imaginable. Your water heater has a steel tank and copper pipes. Your stove has metal heating elements. Every appliance that makes your house the functional support system for your family's life contains the exact materials that a Carrington Event will systematically destroy.

Unless those appliances were made by the people in Carrington Storm Motors' Aegis Home division. 

Which is exactly what they are making.

There is something almost beautiful about the ambition here — the recognition that the vehicle fleet was necessary but not sufficient. That a family with a functioning Beryl parked in their driveway but no working refrigerator and no working stove and walls that are currently redistributing electromagnetic energy in a concerning way is only partially protected. The full protection is the whole house. And so the engineers built the whole house.

Ceramic refrigerators. GaN solid-state microwaves that replace the magnetron. BFRP rebar that replaces steel. PMMA optical fiber building wiring that replaces copper. Non-conductive plumbing fittings. Dielectric door hardware. MXene-shielded wall panels. An entire construction ecosystem that is, from foundation to roof, electromagnetically invisible.

That's not a product line. That's a philosophy of survival, applied to every room.
