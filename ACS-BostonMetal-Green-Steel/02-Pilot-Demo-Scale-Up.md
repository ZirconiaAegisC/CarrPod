# Boston Metal — Pilot Plant and Demonstration Scale-Up

## Woburn Pilot Facility

Boston Metal operates its primary pilot plant in Woburn, Massachusetts, approximately 12 miles north of Boston. The facility houses multiple generations of MOE cells, ranging from laboratory-scale test cells producing hundreds of grams of iron per run to semi-industrial pilot cells producing tonnes per month. The Woburn site serves multiple functions: cell design iteration, anode lifetime testing, electrolyte chemistry optimization, process control development, and operator training.

The Woburn facility employs approximately 120 engineers, scientists, and technicians as of late 2025. The site includes materials characterization laboratories, electrochemical testing bays, a metallurgical analysis suite, and mechanical fabrication workshops. Cell components are designed, machined, and assembled on-site, enabling rapid iteration cycles.

Key milestones achieved at Woburn include:
- Demonstration of multi-tonne iron production from a single cell run
- Validation of anode lifetimes exceeding 12 months under continuous operation
- Confirmation of iron purity exceeding 99% without secondary refining
- Integration of automated tapping and casting systems for continuous operation
- Testing of over 30 different ore feedstocks from 8 countries

## Commercial Demonstration Plant — Brazil

In 2024, Boston Metal selected a site in Brazil for its first commercial-scale demonstration facility. The choice of Brazil reflects several strategic considerations: Brazil's predominantly renewable electricity grid (approximately 85% renewable, primarily hydropower with growing wind and solar), abundant iron ore reserves in Minas Gerais and Pará states, proximity to maritime export infrastructure, and supportive federal and state-level industrial decarbonization policies.

The Brazilian demonstration plant will house the first commercial-scale MOE cells, each with a production capacity measured in thousands of tonnes of iron per year. The initial deployment targets 6–10 cells operating in parallel, with a combined annual capacity of approximately 25,000–40,000 tonnes of liquid iron. Construction is scheduled in phases:
- Phase 1 (target 2026): First commercial-scale cell commissioned
- Phase 2 (target 2027–2028): Full cell array operational
- Phase 3 (target 2029–2030): Scale-up to full commercial steel production

## Cell Scaling Challenges

Scaling from pilot to commercial cells involves non-trivial engineering challenges:
- **Thermal management**: Larger cells generate proportionally more resistive heat; maintaining uniform temperature distribution requires advanced thermal modeling and active cooling systems.
- **Current distribution**: Ensuring uniform current density across larger electrode surfaces is critical for efficiency and anode lifetime.
- **Mass transport**: Iron droplet coalescence and collection must be optimized at scale to maintain Faraday efficiency.
- **Anode fabrication**: Manufacturing inert anodes at commercial dimensions (approximately 1–2 meters in dimension) requires development of specialized sintering and quality control processes.
- **Electrolyte management**: Maintaining electrolyte composition over multi-year campaigns requires monitoring and replenishment systems.

## Modular Scale-Up Economics

The modular architecture of MOE technology fundamentally changes the capital expenditure profile of steelmaking. A conventional integrated steel mill (blast furnace + BOF) requires capital investment of $1–2 billion and takes 3–5 years to construct. MOE cells can be deployed incrementally:
- A single commercial cell represents a capital outlay of approximately $15–25 million
- Cells can be added to match demand growth
- Capacity can be phased in over months rather than years
- De-bottlenecking is simpler than in continuous-process plants

This modularity also reduces financing risk, as investors can commit to initial cell deployment and expand based on operational performance data.

## Technology Transfer to Operating Partners

Boston Metal's commercialization strategy involves licensing its technology to steel producers rather than becoming a steel producer itself. The Brazilian demonstration plant serves as a reference facility where licensees can observe operations, train personnel, and validate performance guarantees. This asset-light business model is analogous to how UOP licenses refining technology or how ARM licenses processor designs.
