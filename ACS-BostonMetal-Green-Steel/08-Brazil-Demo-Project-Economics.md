# Boston Metal — Brazil Commercial Demonstration: Site Selection and Project Economics

## Strategic Rationale for Brazil

Boston Metal's selection of Brazil as the location for its first commercial-scale MOE demonstration facility reflects a convergence of favorable factors spanning resource availability, energy economics, market access, and regulatory environment.

### Iron Ore Resources

Brazil is the world's second-largest iron ore producer, with approximately 440 million tonnes produced in 2024, primarily from the Minas Gerais (Iron Quadrangle) and Pará (Carajás) regions. The Carajás mine complex, operated by Vale, produces some of the world's highest-grade iron ore but also generates substantial volumes of mid-grade and lower-grade ores that are currently blended or stockpiled. MOE's ability to utilize these lower-grade feedstocks creates a valorization opportunity for material that is sub-optimal for conventional blast furnace or DRI routes.

Vale, the world's largest iron ore producer outside of Australia, has publicly stated its commitment to reducing scope 3 emissions (emissions from downstream processing of its products). MOE technology aligns with this commitment by enabling zero-emissions processing of Vale's ores, regardless of grade.

### Electricity Economics

Brazil's electricity grid is among the world's cleanest and most cost-competitive for industrial consumers:
- Approximately 85% renewable generation (hydroelectric ~60%, wind ~15%, solar ~5%, biomass ~5%)
- Industrial electricity tariffs in the range of $25–40 per MWh for long-term contracts, with some hydropower contracts priced below $25/MWh
- Expanding wind capacity in the Northeast region (Bahia, Piauí, Rio Grande do Norte) offers additional low-cost renewable electricity at capacity factors exceeding 50%
- Solar photovoltaic resources are among the world's best, with capacity factors exceeding 25% in the Northeast

At $25–30/MWh electricity costs, MOE's electricity component is approximately $90–120 per tonne of iron — competitive with the coal cost in conventional blast furnace ironmaking even before accounting for carbon pricing.

### Maritime Export Infrastructure

Brazil has established maritime export infrastructure for iron ore and steel products through ports including:
- Tubarão (Vitória, Espírito Santo): Vale's primary iron ore export terminal
- Ponta da Madeira (São Luís, Maranhão): Deep-water port handling Valemax vessels (400,000 DWT)
- Sepetiba (Rio de Janeiro state): Steel products export terminal

Proximity to these export hubs enables cost-effective shipping of MOE-produced iron or steel to markets in Europe (where CBAM carbon pricing advantages apply), North America, and Asia.

### Regulatory and Policy Environment

Brazil's federal government has implemented several policies favorable to industrial decarbonization:
- RenovaBio program provides a regulatory framework for carbon credit generation
- National Hydrogen Program (PNH2) includes provisions for industrial decarbonization
- State-level incentives in Minas Gerais and Espírito Santo for clean industrial investment
- Free trade zone benefits in certain regions

## Project Structure and Phasing

### Phase 1 — First Commercial Cell (Target 2026)
- Single commercial-scale MOE cell
- Production capacity: ~5,000–8,000 tonnes/year of iron
- Capital expenditure: $25–40 million (including balance of plant)
- Primary objective: Validate commercial cell performance (production rate, anode lifetime, iron quality, energy consumption) under continuous industrial operation
- Secondary objective: Establish operating procedures, train operator workforce, demonstrate regulatory compliance

### Phase 2 — Multi-Cell Array (Target 2027–2028)
- 6–10 MOE cells operating in parallel
- Production capacity: ~25,000–40,000 tonnes/year
- Incremental capital: $100–200 million
- Primary objective: Demonstrate multi-cell operation, process automation, and plant-level availability metrics
- Secondary objective: Produce iron for downstream steelmaking qualification by customers

### Phase 3 — Integrated Steel Production (Target 2029–2030)
- Integration of MOE ironmaking with downstream steelmaking (electric arc furnace or basic oxygen furnace)
- Production capacity: ~100,000–250,000 tonnes/year of finished steel
- Estimate capital for integration: $150–300 million
- Primary objective: Demonstrate full green steel value chain and achieve commercial product qualification

## Capital Efficiency of Modular Deployment

The phased approach to the Brazil demonstration plant illustrates the capital efficiency advantages of MOE's modular architecture. A conventional integrated steel mill requires approximately $1–2 billion in upfront capital before the first tonne of steel is produced. The MOE phased approach:

1. De-risks technology before committing to full-scale investment
2. Generates operating data to inform subsequent phase design
3. Produces revenue from initial cells to partially fund expansion
4. Allows course correction based on operating experience
5. Reduces financing cost by matching capital deployment to demonstrated performance

## Economic Sensitivity Analysis

The following table presents estimated levelized cost of iron (LCI) for the Brazil demonstration plant under various scenarios:

| Scenario | Electricity Cost ($/MWh) | Anode Life (years) | Capacity Factor | LCI ($/tonne iron) |
|----------|-------------------------|-------------------|-----------------|---------------------|
| Optimistic | 25 | 5+ | 95% | 160–180 |
| Base Case | 30 | 3–4 | 90% | 200–230 |
| Conservative | 40 | 2–3 | 85% | 260–290 |
| Pessimistic | 50 | 1–2 | 80% | 340–380 |

For reference, conventional BF-BOF hot metal cost is approximately $300–350 per tonne, and with a $100/tonne CO₂ carbon price, approximately $480–550 per tonne.

At the base case and optimistic scenarios, MOE iron is cost-competitive with conventional hot metal even without carbon pricing. At the conservative scenario, MOE is competitive only with moderate carbon pricing or green steel premiums ($50–100/tonne). At the pessimistic scenario, MOE requires carbon pricing above $100/tonne or green steel premiums above $100/tonne to be competitive.
