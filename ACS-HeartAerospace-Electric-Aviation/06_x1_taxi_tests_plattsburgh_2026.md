# X1 Taxi Tests — Plattsburgh, New York, May 2026

## Test Site Selection

Heart Aerospace conducted low-speed taxi tests of the X1 demonstrator
at Plattsburgh International Airport (KPBG) in Plattsburgh, New York,
completing the test series in May 2026. The choice of Plattsburgh,
rather than Gothenburg, reflects the company's strategic relocation
of its headquarters to Los Angeles in 2025 and its desire to conduct
flight test activities within FAA jurisdiction, where certification
cooperation with U.S. authorities is paramount.

Plattsburgh's advantages as a test site include:

- **Runway length:** The primary runway (17/35) is 11,759 feet long,
  far exceeding the 1,100-meter requirement and providing ample safety
  margin for aborted test runs.
- **Low traffic density:** Plattsburgh is a former Air Force base with
  minimal commercial service compared to major Northeast airports,
  reducing conflicts with scheduled operations.
- **Weather diversity:** The Champlain Valley provides a range of
  temperature and wind conditions suitable for testing propulsion,
  battery thermal management, and crosswind handling.
- **Proximity to aerospace industry:** Within driving distance of major
  aerospace engineering centers in Montreal (Pratt & Whitney Canada,
  Bombardier) and within the U.S. Northeast aerospace corridor.
- **FAA cooperation:** Plattsburgh tower and the FAA Albany Flight
  Standards District Office could coordinate directly with Heart
  Aerospace test personnel.

## Taxi Test Objectives

The low-speed taxi tests, conducted in May 2026, were the first
movement of the X1 under its own power. The test objectives included:

### Propulsion System Verification

Each of the four electric motors was individually brought to power
and validated for thrust response, RPM governance, and thermal
behavior. Motor controllers, inverters, and battery power distribution
were tested in real-time communication with ground telemetry stations.

### Steering and Braking

The nose-wheel steering system was tested through a range of turn
angles at low speed to validate geometry, actuator authority, and
pilot workload. Carbon brake performance was assessed during
accelerate-stop simulations, with brake temperature monitoring
confirming that energy absorption met design predictions.

### Battery Thermal Management at Ground Power

Electric taxiing places a unique thermal demand on battery packs:
high current draw at low airspeed with minimal ram-air cooling. The
X1's liquid-cooled battery thermal management system was validated
under these worst-case conditions, with coolant flow rates, pump
power consumption, and cell temperature uniformity all recorded.

### Avionics and Fly-by-Wire Integration

The taxi tests exercised the full avionics suite including primary
flight displays, multi-function displays, engine indication and crew
alerting system (EICAS) for the electric powertrain, and the
fly-by-wire control laws at ground speeds up to taxi velocity.
Communication radios, transponder, and ADS-B Out were tested against
Plattsburgh ground and approach facilities.

### Electromagnetic Compatibility

High-power electric propulsion systems generate electromagnetic fields
that can interfere with navigation and communication equipment. The
taxi tests included electromagnetic compatibility verification,
confirming that the X1's shielding, bonding, and grounding design
prevented interference with VHF comm, VOR/ILS navigation, GPS, and
transponder signals.

## Test Results Summary

Heart Aerospace reported successful completion of the taxi test
campaign in May 2026. All primary test objectives were met. Specific
observations shared publicly include:

- The four electric motors delivered symmetrical thrust response
  with no unexpected torque ripple or harmonic vibration.
- Battery thermal management maintained cell temperatures within
  design limits during repeated taxi-run cycles at maximum
  continuous ground power.
- Fly-by-wire control laws behaved as predicted from simulation,
  with no handling-quality anomalies at taxi speeds.
- The 32-meter wingspan and wide-track landing gear provided stable
  ground handling, with no tendency toward wing-rock or excessive
  yaw sensitivity during taxi maneuvers.

## Path to First Electric Flight

Completion of the taxi test series clears the X1 for low-speed flight
operations. Heart Aerospace must now complete:

- High-speed taxi tests up to rotation speed (Vr).
- Formal flight readiness review with FAA and EASA observers.
- Issuance of a special airworthiness certificate in the experimental
  category from the FAA.
- Final flight-test instrumentation calibration and telemetry
  ground-station validation.

First electric flight of the X1 is targeted for 2026. When achieved,
it will mark the first flight of a European-designed, 30-seat class,
clean-sheet electric aircraft—and a significant milestone for the
electric aviation industry globally.
