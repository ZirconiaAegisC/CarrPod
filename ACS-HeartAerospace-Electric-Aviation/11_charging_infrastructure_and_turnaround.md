# Charging Infrastructure & Turnaround

## Thirty-Minute Rapid Charging

One of the ES-30's most operationally significant capabilities is the
30-minute full-charge time. This enables multiple daily flight cycles
on an all-electric schedule, with ground turnaround times comparable
to short-haul turboprop operations. The 30-minute charge requires
dedicated high-power ground charging infrastructure at operating
airports.

### Charging Power Requirements

To charge the estimated 800–1,200 kilowatt-hour battery pack in 30
minutes, the ground charger must deliver approximately 1.6–2.4 megawatts
of continuous DC power. This assumes:

- 100% charge from a fully depleted state (worst case; operational
  practice would typically charge from a 20–30% reserve).
- Approximately 90% charger and battery charging efficiency.
- Active thermal management of the battery during charging to handle
  the heat generated by a 2C charge rate.

The 1.6–2.4 megawatt power level is substantially higher than current
automotive fast-charging standards (Tesla Supercharger V4: 350 kW;
CCS: 350 kW maximum) and comparable to the Megawatt Charging System
(MCS) standard being developed for heavy-duty electric trucks.

### Charger Standard and Interface

Heart Aerospace has not publicly specified the charging connector
standard for the ES-30. The Megawatt Charging System (MCS), standardized
under ISO/IEC and CharIN, is the most likely candidate because:

- It is designed for up to 3.75 megawatts continuous DC.
- It includes liquid cooling for the connector and cable, necessary for
  sustained megawatt-level power transfer without cable overheating.
- It is an open industry standard with multiple equipment manufacturers.

Alternatively, a proprietary aerospace connector may be developed if
the MCS form factor is unsuitable for aircraft integration. The charging
receptacle on the ES-30 is expected to be located in the lower fuselage
or belly fairing, accessible from ground level.

## Airport Charging Infrastructure

Deploying megawatt-level charging at regional airports presents a
significant infrastructure challenge. Regional airports typically have
electric service sized for terminal lighting and HVAC, not megawatt
vehicle charging. Upgrades may require new transformers, medium-voltage
service extensions, on-site battery storage for grid buffering, and
combined charging/ground-power units.

Heart Aerospace is expected to partner with charging infrastructure
companies, airport authorities, and utilities to plan the initial
network. Rollout would begin at airline partner hubs—United hubs at Denver,
Chicago O'Hare, and Houston, and Air Canada hubs at Toronto Pearson and
Montreal—before expanding to destination airports.

## Operational Turnaround Concept

Ground turnaround for an all-electric ES-30 mission:

| Phase | Duration | Activity |
|-------|----------|----------|
| Arrival and taxi-in | 5 min | Aircraft taxis to gate or charging stand |
| Passenger deplaning | 5 min | 30 passengers exit |
| Charger connection | 2 min | Cable connection and handshake |
| Battery charging | 25 min | 800–1,200 kWh delivered at megawatt power |
| Cabin servicing | Overlaps | Cleaning during charging |
| Passenger boarding | 10 min | 30 passengers board |
| Charger disconnect | 2 min | Cable stowed |
| Preflight and pushback | 6 min | Cockpit checks, clearance, pushback |
| **Total turnaround** | **55 min** | Comparable to regional turboprop turnaround |

Cabin servicing overlapping with charging minimizes the incremental time
added by electric operations. The 55-minute turnaround supports 8–10
flights per day per aircraft on short-haul routes.

## Regulatory Considerations

Megawatt charging at airports introduces regulatory issues that Heart
Aerospace, airport authorities, and regulators must address:

- **Electrical safety:** High-voltage DC connections in outdoor, all-
  weather airport ramp environments require robust safety interlocks,
  ground-fault detection, and arc-flash protection.
- **Fire safety:** Battery charging at megawatt power levels requires
  airport fire services to develop procedures for battery thermal
  events, which differ from fuel fires in suppression method and
  duration.
- **Electromagnetic compatibility:** Megawatt power electronics must
  not interfere with airport navigation aids, communication systems,
  or aircraft avionics during charging.
- **Standardization:** Airlines operating multiple aircraft types will
  require standardized charging interfaces to avoid a proliferation of
  incompatible connectors and chargers on the ramp.

## Battery Lifecycle and Replacement

Airlines will require clarity on battery replacement intervals and
costs. Automotive lithium-ion experience suggests that cells cycling
daily at 2C charge/discharge rates between 20% and 90% state of charge
will retain approximately 70–80% of original capacity after 1,500–2,000
cycles. At five flights per day, this translates to battery replacement
every 12–18 months for high-utilization aircraft.

Battery replacement is a known operational cost that must be factored
into lease rates or operator budgets. Heart Aerospace is expected to
offer battery-as-a-service or power-by-the-hour programs similar to
engine maintenance programs, where the operator pays per flight hour
and the manufacturer manages battery health, replacement, and disposal.
