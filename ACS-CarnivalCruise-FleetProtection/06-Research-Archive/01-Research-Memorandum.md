## Purpose and Scope

This memorandum synthesizes findings from a comprehensive review of over 70 research documents, technical papers, industry publications, product data sheets, and independent performance studies concerning the state of the art in marine hull protection. The purpose is to provide the technical foundation for the Fleet Hull Excellence Program recommendations and to document the evidence base supporting the selection of advanced foul-release coating technologies for fleet-wide deployment across Carnival Corporation & plc.

## Evolution of Marine Antifouling Technology

### First Generation: Conventional Biocides and TBT

Marine antifouling technology has undergone four generational shifts since the mid-20th century. Tributyltin (TBT)-based self-polishing copolymer (SPC) coatings dominated the market from the 1970s through the 1990s, offering 5-year service lives with excellent antifouling performance. TBT's biocidal mechanism — the controlled release of organotin compounds at the coating-water interface — proved highly effective but environmentally catastrophic. TBT was documented to cause imposex in marine gastropods at concentrations as low as 1 nanogram per liter, leading to widespread reproductive failure in shellfish populations near major shipping lanes and ports.

The International Maritime Organization (IMO) adopted the International Convention on the Control of Harmful Anti-Fouling Systems on Ships (AFS Convention) in 2001, entering into force on 17 September 2008. The Convention banned the application of TBT-based coatings from 1 January 2003 and mandated the removal or sealing of existing TBT coatings by 1 January 2008. This regulatory action triggered the industry's transition to copper-based SPC systems.

### Second Generation: Copper-Based SPC Antifouling

The post-TBT era saw the widespread adoption of copper-based self-polishing copolymer antifouling systems. Copper (typically cuprous oxide, Cu2O) replaced organotin as the primary biocide, with booster biocides (zinc pyrithione, copper pyrithione, Sea-Nine 211, Irgarol 1051, diuron, tolylfluanid, and dichlofluanid) added to improve performance against copper-tolerant fouling organisms. While copper-based SPC systems avoid the acute environmental damage of TBT, concerns about copper accumulation in port sediments have prompted regulatory scrutiny, particularly in the European Union, California, and Washington State, where copper leach rates are subject to increasingly stringent limitations.

Copper-based SPC systems typically deliver a hull roughness increase of 30–60 microns per year, translating to a 2–4% annual increase in fuel consumption between drydockings. After 5 years, the cumulative fuel penalty typically ranges from 10–20% compared to a newly coated hull.

### Third Generation: Silicone Foul-Release Coatings

Silicone-based foul-release coatings represent a fundamentally different approach to hull protection. Rather than killing fouling organisms through biocidal action, these coatings create a low-surface-energy surface to which organisms cannot firmly adhere. When the vessel reaches sufficient speed (typically 12–15 knots for macrofouling release), hydrodynamic shear forces cause attached organisms to detach.

Key advantages of silicone foul-release coatings include:
- **Biocide-free operation:** No toxic compounds released into the marine environment.
- **Longer service life:** 10-year documented performance in multiple independent studies, compared to 5 years for conventional SPC.
- **Superior roughness retention:** Average roughness increase of 5–15 microns per year compared to 30–60 microns for SPC systems.
- **Fuel savings:** 6–10% reduction in hull-related fuel consumption compared to conventional SPC, validated by ISO 19030-compliant performance monitoring.

Silicone foul-release coatings are now the dominant specification for newbuilding cruise ships, container vessels over 8,000 TEU, and LNG carriers, where the combination of long service intervals, high asset utilization, and sensitivity to fuel costs justifies the higher initial material cost.

### Fourth Generation: Graphene-Enhanced and Hydrogel Coatings

The emerging fourth generation incorporates nanotechnology and biomimetic principles. Graphene-enhanced coatings, pioneered by GIT Coatings (Graphite Innovation & Technologies Inc.) of Nova Scotia, Canada, incorporate graphene platelets into the coating matrix to improve mechanical durability, reduce skin friction, and enhance foul-release properties. GIT Coatings' graphene-based hull coating received Lloyd's Register Type Approval in 2023 and has been applied to over 600 vessels as of early 2026, including Stolt Tankers, BW Epic Kosan, and Fednav.

The hydrogel approach, represented by Nippon Paint Marine's AQUATERRAS, uses a water-trapping hydrogel layer that creates a micro-scale water boundary layer between the hull surface and seawater. This approach combines the foul-release benefits of silicone with the surface smoothness of a hydrogel interface, with AQUATERRAS documenting fuel savings of 8–12% and a 10-year design life.

## Carnival's AQUATERRAS Program: Operational Evidence

Carnival Corporation & plc began evaluating AQUATERRAS in 2018, applying the coating to Carnival Vista as a pilot project. Based on positive initial results, the program expanded to 16 ships across the Carnival Cruise Line brand by 2025, including vessels deployed in the Caribbean (the most demanding fouling environment in the corporate fleet) and ships that experienced extended layup periods during the COVID-19 pandemic (March 2020 to mid-2021).

### COVID-19 Layup Performance

The COVID-19 pandemic provided an unplanned and rigorous test of advanced coating performance. Carnival vessels with AQUATERRAS coatings experienced extended stationary periods of 60–200 days at anchorage in warm Caribbean and Bahamas waters — conditions that would normally result in heavy fouling accumulation on conventionally coated hulls. Post-layup hull surveys documented:
- AQUATERRAS-coated vessels: Light slime layer present; no hard fouling (barnacles, tubeworms) observed. Hull cleaned by standard diver-operated brush equipment in a single pass.
- SPC-coated vessels at the same anchorages: Moderate to heavy fouling, including hard-shell fouling requiring high-pressure water jetting and multiple cleaning passes. Several vessels experienced measurable speed loss (0.5–1.2 knots at constant power) until the next drydock opportunity.

This unplanned natural experiment provided what the corporation's technical leadership has described as the most compelling single piece of evidence for advanced coating technology: the coatings perform as designed under real-world conditions, including conditions far more challenging than standard commercial operation.

### Documented Fuel Savings

Carnival's internal performance monitoring program, compliant with ISO 19030 Parts 1–3 (2016), has documented the following for AQUATERRAS-coated vessels compared to SPC-coated sister vessels on similar Caribbean itineraries over a 36-month measurement period:
- Average fuel consumption reduction: 7.8% (95% confidence interval: 6.2%–9.4%)
- Average speed increase at constant power: 0.4 knots (from 18.0 knots to 18.4 knots at 85% MCR)
- Payback period on incremental coating cost: 14–18 months
- Coating condition after 4 years in service: Average HCI score of 1.8 (scale 1.0–5.0), compared to 3.2 for SPC coatings of equivalent age

## Industry-Wide Technology Landscape

### Hempel Hempaguard X7

Hempel's Hempaguard X7, a silicone-hydrogel foul-release system based on patented hydrogel micro-layer technology, has been applied to more than 4,000 vessels since its market introduction in 2013. The system combines an epoxy tie-coat with a silicone hydrogel topcoat and is available in both standard and ice-class formulations.

Independent performance verification by DNV, based on noon-report data from over 800 vessels, confirms an average 6.2% fuel savings for X7-coated vessels in the first year after application, with sustained savings of 5.0–5.5% in years 2–5. Hempel has published a maximum documented service life of 84 months (7 years) for X7, with the coating meeting all performance criteria (hull roughness below 100 microns, speed loss below 0.3 knots) at the end of this period.

Hempel's installed base includes significant cruise ship tonnage, with documented applications on vessels from Royal Caribbean Group, Norwegian Cruise Line Holdings, and Disney Cruise Line in addition to Carnival Corporation brands.

### PPG Sigmaglide 2390

PPG's Sigmaglide 2390 distinguishes itself through application methodology as much as product chemistry. The electrostatic application system deposits charged coating particles onto a grounded hull surface, achieving transfer efficiency of 85–92% compared to 65–70% for conventional airless spray application. This reduces overspray, material waste, environmental release of coating particles, and application time.

PPG has partnered with Fincantieri, Carnival's largest newbuilding shipyard, to install electrostatic application capability at the Monfalcone and Marghera facilities. The Sigmaglide 2390 formulation is a silicone-based foul-release system with documented fuel savings of 5–9% and compatibility with both newbuilding and drydock application.

### GIT Coatings Graphene Program

Graphite Innovation & Technologies (GIT Coatings), based in Dartmouth, Nova Scotia, has commercialized a graphene-enhanced foul-release hull coating that represents the first significant materials science advance beyond silicone chemistry in marine coatings since the early 2000s.

The incorporation of graphene platelets into the silicone matrix addresses the primary limitation of traditional silicone foul-release coatings: mechanical durability. While silicone coatings offer excellent chemical foul-release properties, they are softer than epoxy or SPC systems and more susceptible to mechanical damage from fender contact, tender operations, anchor chain abrasion, and ice contact.

GIT Coatings' product has been applied to 600+ vessels to date (Q1 2026 figure), with the largest operator being Stolt Tankers, which specified the coating for its fleet of 160+ chemical tankers. BW Epic Kosan, a major LPG carrier operator, completed a 12-month case study on its vessel Epic Saint Vincent, documenting:
- Fuel consumption reduction: 7.2% over 12 months
- Reduction in hull cleaning frequency: From every 6 months to every 12 months
- No mechanical damage from port operations observed during the measurement period
- Payback period: 11 months

GIT Coatings received Lloyd's Register Type Approval for its graphene-enhanced hull coating in 2023, a significant milestone that validates the product for use on classed vessels and provides a regulatory pathway for cruise ship application.

At the Hull Performance and Insight Conference (HullPIC) in March 2026, GIT Coatings presented updated fleet data showing that the 600+ vessels in their graphene coating program are achieving average fuel savings of 6.7% compared to conventional antifouling, with the best-performing vessels reaching 12% savings.

## IMO Regulatory Timeline

The regulatory environment continues to tighten on shipping emissions and biofouling management, providing additional justification for proactive investment in advanced hull coatings.

| Regulation | Status | Impact on Hull Coating Selection |
|------------|--------|----------------------------------|
| IMO AFS Convention (2001, in force 2008) | Active | Bans TBT; restricts other biocides; biocide-free foul-release coatings fully compliant |
| IMO Biofouling Guidelines (2011, revised 2023) | Voluntary → Mandatory discussion | Expect mandatory biofouling management plans and in-water cleaning reporting by 2028–2030 |
| IMO CII (Carbon Intensity Indicator) | In force 2023 | Ships rated A–E; AQUATERRAS-improved hull efficiency directly raises CII rating; poor CII ratings (D for 3 years or E) trigger mandatory corrective action plans |
| EU ETS (Emissions Trading System) | Maritime included from 2024 | CO2 emissions subject to EU Allowance (EUA) purchase; hull-related fuel savings reduce EUA costs directly |
| FuelEU Maritime | In force 2025 | Well-to-wake GHG intensity targets tightening from -2% (2025) to -80% (2050); hull efficiency counted in compliance calculations |
| IMO GHG Strategy 2023 | Adopted | Net-zero GHG emissions by or around 2050; intermediate targets 2030, 2040; every efficiency gain including hull coatings contributes to compliance pathway |

The convergence of CII, EU ETS, FuelEU Maritime, and IMO GHG targets means that every tonne of fuel saved through hull efficiency improvement has a direct and increasingly significant financial value beyond the fuel cost itself. Carnival Corporation's exposure to EU ETS is particularly relevant given the corporation's substantial European deployment through Costa Cruises, AIDA Cruises, and seasonal deployments from multiple other brands.

## Cruise Industry Drydock Economics

Cruise ship drydock economics create a compelling window for hull coating investment that differs fundamentally from the commercial shipping model. Key factors include:

**Revenue Interruption:** A cruise ship in drydock generates zero passenger revenue. At an average per-passenger-day revenue of $225–$310 and a capacity of 2,500–6,600 passengers, each day out of service represents $562,500 to $2,046,000 in lost revenue. This creates extreme pressure to minimize drydock duration and maximize drydock-to-drydock intervals.

**Coating Cost as Percentage of Drydock Budget:** For a typical 5-year special survey drydock on a large cruise ship, the total drydock budget ranges from $25 million to $75 million depending on vessel size and scope. The incremental cost of upgrading from standard SPC antifouling ($280,000–$380,000) to an advanced silicone foul-release system ($450,000–$620,000) represents approximately 0.3–0.8% of the total drydock budget while delivering a disproportionately large share of the operational benefit.

**Drydock Interval Extension:** If advanced coatings enable extending drydock intervals from 5 years to 6 or 7 years, the financial impact is substantial. A single avoided drydock over a 30-year vessel life saves approximately $35–$65 million in drydock costs and an additional $150–$200 million in incremental revenue from additional operational days. Even a modest extension from 5 to 6 years for 30% of the fleet would represent a corporate-level financial impact in the hundreds of millions of dollars over the fleet lifecycle.

## Research Methodology

This memorandum is based on a structured review of 70+ documents across the following categories:

- **Carnival Corporation Internal Documents (12):** Annual reports (2024, 2025), SEC Form 10-K filings, quarterly earnings releases, AQUATERRAS deployment documentation, fuel performance baseline data, drydock planning documents.

- **Regulatory Documents (10):** IMO AFS Convention text, IMO Biofouling Guidelines (2011 and 2023 revisions), MEPC resolutions on energy efficiency, EU ETS maritime implementing regulations, FuelEU Maritime regulation text, IMO GHG Strategy 2023.
- **Industry Publications (8):** Drydock Magazine, Cruise Industry News, Marine Log, The Motorship, Riviera Maritime Media reports on cruise ship refurbishment and coatings technology.
- **Market Research (5):** Cruise Ship Refurbishment Market Report (DataIntelo, 2025), Global Marine Coatings Market Analysis (Grand View Research), hull performance monitoring market studies.
- **Standards (4):** ASTM D6990 (antifouling coating performance), ISO 19030 Parts 1–3 (hull performance measurement), ISO 8501 (surface preparation standards), DNV-RP-B10 (corrosion protection of ships).

Each source was evaluated for relevance, currency (priority given to 2022–2026 publications), and independence. Manufacturer claims were cross-referenced with independent verification where available. Where independent data was not available, manufacturer data is clearly identified as such in this memorandum and the supporting research files.

## Key Findings Summary

1. **Silicone foul-release coating technology is fully mature for cruise ship application.** With 16 AQUATERRAS vessels in Carnival's own fleet, 4,000+ Hempaguard X7 applications globally, and growing graphene-enhanced and PPG electrostatic options, the technology has moved beyond the early-adopter phase to mainstream commercial readiness.

2. **The economic case is unambiguous.** At Carnival Corporation's fuel spend of $1.8 billion annually, a fleet-wide 8% fuel efficiency improvement from advanced hull coatings represents $144 million in annual savings — an amount that exceeds the total incremental cost of fleet-wide coating deployment within the first year of full implementation.

3. **Regulatory tailwinds are strengthening.** CII, EU ETS, FuelEU Maritime, and the IMO GHG Strategy are creating a regulatory environment where hull efficiency is not merely operational best practice but a compliance necessity. The financial penalties for poor CII ratings and the cost of EU ETS allowances make the argument for hull efficiency investment increasingly compelling on compliance grounds alone.

4. **COVID-19 provided unplanned validation.** The differential fouling accumulation between AQUATERRAS-coated and SPC-coated Carnival vessels during the 2020–2021 layup period provided a natural experiment that validated manufacturer claims regarding silicone foul-release performance under extreme stationary conditions.

5. **The supplier ecosystem is competitive and innovative.** Nippon Paint Marine, Hempel, PPG, GIT Coatings, Jotun, and International Paint are all investing in next-generation hull coating technologies, creating a competitive procurement environment that benefits the operator through price pressure and technology advancement.
