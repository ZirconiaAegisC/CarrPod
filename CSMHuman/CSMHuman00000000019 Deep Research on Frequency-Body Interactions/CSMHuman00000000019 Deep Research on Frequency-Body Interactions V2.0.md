## 1. Complementary Diagnostic Framework: Oscillopsia and VOR Testing

V1.0's monograph on 20–800 Hz acoustic interaction with the eye-orbit system focused on diagnostic applications for characterizing soft-tissue biomechanical state. V2.0 adds an adjacent, actively-used clinical diagnostic domain operating in an overlapping conceptual space: **oscillopsia** assessment via Vestibulo-Ocular Reflex (VOR) testing. Where V1.0's acoustic method probes tissue mechanical properties directly, VOR/oscillopsia testing (video head-impulse test, videonystagmography) probes the *functional consequence* of impaired gaze stabilization — providing a clinically complementary rather than competing diagnostic axis. Functional head-impulse testing (fHIT) studies report approximately 78% abnormal-result correlation with oscillopsia severity in bilateral vestibulopathy patients, reinforcing that low-frequency mechanical/vestibular interactions with the eye-orbit system remain an active, clinically-relevant research area.

## 2. Low-Frequency Reference Point: 12.5 Hz Visual-Acuity Decrement

V1.0's 20–800 Hz diagnostic acoustic band is now explicitly bounded on its low-frequency side by the classical whole-body-vibration visual-acuity decrement finding (Ishitake et al. 1998): maximum acuity reduction at 12.5 Hz seated vertical vibration. This is not a diagnostic acoustic signal in V1.0's sense, but it clarifies that the eye-orbit system's frequency sensitivity extends continuously from sub-diagnostic-band mechanical vibration (12.5 Hz) into V1.0's 20–800 Hz diagnostic window, with no discontinuity in biomechanical relevance.
