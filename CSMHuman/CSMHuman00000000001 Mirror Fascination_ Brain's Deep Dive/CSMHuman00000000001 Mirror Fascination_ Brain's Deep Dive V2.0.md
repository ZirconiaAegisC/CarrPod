## 1. Causal Confirmation: Mirror-Gazing Impairs Sense of Agency (2026)

V1.0 established, via Caputo's classic paradigm, that prolonged mirror fixation produces dissociative strange-face apparitions in 100% of healthy subjects. That data was correlational — it described *what* is experienced but not whether the mirror is the causal driver of the deeper cognitive disruption. A two-phase controlled study (Bregman-Hai & Soffer-Dudek, Ben-Gurion University of the Negev, PMC12919786) closes this gap:

- **Study 1 (N=98):** Mirror-gazing (with or without a suggestive cue) versus a video-watching control. Mirror-gazing induced dissociation and *decreased explicit (self-reported) sense of agency*, but primarily in individuals with high trait dissociation.
- **Study 2 (N=199):** Added an improved control (article-reading, which does not confound with "absorption") and an *implicit* agency task. Mirror-gazing impaired **implicit sense of agency across the general population**, not just high trait-dissociators — bypassing the self-report bias that limited Study 1 and V1.0's interpretive reach.

**V2.0 implication:** The "eight-pane" progression described in V1.0 (subcortical trigger → self-face lock → dialogical emergence → Troxler fading → pareidolia → strange-face → dissociative break → recursive loop) is not merely an experiential cascade — Panes 7–8 involve a measurable, causally-induced *loss of the felt sense of authorship over one's own actions*. This reframes mirror-lock from a curiosity into a quantifiable neuro-safety variable anywhere a human must retain rapid behavioral agency near a reflective or reflective-adjacent (camera-display) surface — including a moving vehicle.

## 2. Revised Self-Face Network: From Two Nodes to Seventeen (2024–2026 Synthesis)

V1.0 modeled self-face processing around two hubs: the MedPFC (self-integration) and ACC (self/other salience). The 2024 cross-disciplinary synthesis *Self-Face Recognition and the Brain* (Keenan, Quevedo & Hopkins, Routledge) consolidates lesion, fMRI, and comparative-primate data showing self-face recognition recruits **17 distinct regions** (fusiform face area, superior temporal sulcus, inferior parietal lobule, MedPFC, ACC, right DLPFC, posterior insula, and others), first mapped comprehensively by Ramachandran (2007).

| Finding | V1.0 Model | V2.0 Update |
|---|---|---|
| Network scale | 2 primary hubs (MedPFC, ACC) | 17-region distributed network |
| Redundancy | Not addressed | Damage to any single region does not reliably eliminate recognition — the system is "everywhere and nowhere" |
| Clinical dissociation | Single mechanism (Troxler + pareidolia) | Documented double-dissociation: procedural mirror-use (grooming) can remain intact while declarative recognition ("that is me") fails independently |
| Comparative basis | Human-only citations | Extended to chimpanzee mirror self-recognition individual differences (Hopkins et al.) |

**V2.0 note on the Denisovan/Dieselpunk image framework:** the eight-pane matrix remains valid as a narrative and illustrative device; the underlying neuroscience citation set is updated to reflect the redundant, distributed model rather than a two-node bottleneck.

## 3. Applied Engineering: Camera-Monitor-System (CMS) Dashboard Safety

The V1.0 optical-physics section (infinite reflection, Law of Reflection, index of refraction) is retained unchanged, but V2.0 adds a direct engineering application prompted by the accelerating shift from analog mirrors to electronic Camera-Monitor Systems in the automotive sector — directly relevant to Aegis-C cabin design.

**Regulatory divergence (2025–2026):**
- **United States (FMVSS 111):** As of November 2025, physical rearview/side mirrors remain mandatory; NHTSA's CMS rulemaking (Docket NHTSA-2018-0021) remains an open Advance Notice of Proposed Rulemaking, with an active data-collection extension (OMB Control No. 2127-0756, extended May 2025) studying driver lane-change behavior on CMS versus analog mirrors.
- **European Union (ECE R46, 07-series amendments, effective 2020):** CMS is already a permitted equivalence-tested alternative to physical mirrors.

**V2.0 design guidance derived from Sections 1–2 (Mammalian First Responder + Self-Face Lock + Troxler Fading):**
1. **Superior-colliculus over-trigger risk:** A small, high-refresh CMS display in the driver's peripheral field can trigger the same subcortical "magnetic saccade" lock described in Pane 1 more aggressively than a fixed analog mirror, because digital refresh introduces additional discrete motion events. Aegis-C CMS units are specified with motion-smoothing and refresh ≥60 Hz to minimize spurious peripheral saccade triggers.
2. **Troxler-fading blind-spot risk:** A static, low-contrast CMS overlay (e.g., a blind-spot icon) held in fixed peripheral position for extended highway driving risks perceptual fading identical to the mechanism in Pane 4. Aegis-C HMI specification mandates icon micro-motion (sub-2° dither) to prevent adaptation fade.
3. **GIC/EMP flicker as a novel dissociation trigger (Solar Cycle 25 addendum):** Under geomagnetically induced current (GIC) transients associated with G4–G5 storms (Solar Cycle 25 observed SSN ~137 peak), unshielded CMS display drivers can exhibit brief flicker or frame-hold artifacts. Because Section 1 confirms mirror-analog stimuli can induce measurable agency disruption even in brief laboratory exposures, an unpredictably flickering driver-facing display during a storm event is treated in the Aegis-C design basis as a **safety-relevant failure mode**, not merely a cosmetic one. Mitigation: Aegis-C retains a physical, GIC-immune (non-electronic) mirror as the certified fallback field of view in all markets, with CMS as a supplemental — never sole — visual channel.
