The Biological Trigger

Before you even recognize "that's me," your brain has already reacted. The mirror presents a unique stimulus: Synchronized Biological Motion.

Pane 1: The Primitive Alert

The Amygdala detects motion as a potential threat or mate. Because the reflection moves perfectly with you, it bypasses standard filtration, triggering a "False Positive" alert in Area V5 (Visual Motion).

Pane 2: The Social Imperative

Humans are obligate social mammals. The Fusiform Face Area (FFA) is hardwired to lock onto eyes. A mirror provides the ultimate "Direct Gaze" stimulus, which is biologically impossible to ignore.

Once the primitive brain is engaged, the conscious mind struggles to catch up, leading to deep cognitive dissonance.

Pane 3: Theory of Mind

"Is that me, or another?" The Right Temporoparietal Junction (rTPJ) spikes in activity as it calculates perspective. You are simultaneously the subject (feeling inside) and the object (seeing outside).

Pane 4: The Uncanny Valley

Mirrors reverse images. Since no face is perfectly symmetrical, your reflection looks "almost" right but slightly alien compared to photos. This creates an Uncanny Valley effect that demands scrutiny to resolve.

The Addiction Loop

Why do we linger? It's a chemical cocktail. Primate grooming instincts hijack the Ventral Striatum (Reward System). Checking your reflection releases small hits of dopamine (relief) or stress (cortisol), creating a reinforcement loop.

Simultaneously, Proprioception (body awareness) locks into the visual feed. Moving your hand and seeing it move provides a deeply satisfying "Sensory Sync."
