Frequency-Dependent Biodynamics and Pathophysiology in Hand-Arm Vibration Syndrome: An Exhaustive Technical Analysis

1. Introduction: The Etiology, Epidemiology, and Clinical Complexity of Hand-Arm Vibration Syndrome

The interaction between the human biological system and mechanical vibration is a subject of profound complexity, sitting at the intersection of occupational medicine, biomechanical engineering, and cellular physiology. Hand-Arm Vibration Syndrome (HAVS) represents the cumulative pathological outcome of this interaction, a multifaceted disorder comprising vascular, sensorineural, and musculoskeletal injuries resulting from the transfer of energy from vibrating tools to the hand-arm system. While historically categorized under various nomenclatures—including Raynaud’s Phenomenon of Occupational Origin, Vibration-Induced White Finger (VWF), and Traumatic Vasospastic Disease—the modern medical consensus, as reflected in the Workplace Safety and Insurance Appeals Tribunal (WSIAT) medical discussion papers and international standards, recognizes HAVS as a systemic syndrome with distinct, yet interrelated, pathophysiological components.1

The prevalence of HAVS in industrially developed nations remains alarmingly high, despite decades of regulatory intervention. Epidemiological data indicates that the prevalence in cohorts of workers regularly exposed to hand-transmitted vibration (HTV) ranges from 6% to 100%, with an average prevalence of approximately 50%.2 This variance is largely attributable to differences in the intensity of vibration, the duration of exposure, the specific spectral content of the vibration source, and individual susceptibility factors. The latency period—the duration between the onset of exposure and the first clinical manifestation of symptoms—exhibits a similarly wide range, from as little as six weeks in cases of extreme exposure to over 14 years for lower-level insults.2 This latency is critically dependent on the "dose" of vibration energy absorbed by the tissue, a metric that is currently the subject of intense scientific debate.

The core of this debate, and the primary focus of this report, is the frequency dependence of injury. Traditional risk assessment models, codified in standards such as ISO 5349-1, rely on frequency weighting curves ($W_h$) that heavily prioritize low-frequency vibration (below 25 Hz) while attenuating higher frequencies.3 However, a convergence of evidence from biodynamic modeling, physiological experimentation, and molecular biology suggests that this model is fundamentally flawed. The human hand is not a passive mass; it is a complex dynamic structure with specific resonance frequencies in the 100 Hz to 300 Hz range.5 It is within this high-frequency band that vibration power absorption is maximized, endothelial shear stress peaks, and the most severe sensorineural and vascular damage occurs.

This report provides an exhaustive technical examination of the human body's interaction with vibration frequencies. It synthesizes data from medical discussion papers, biodynamic studies, and cellular mechanotransduction research to construct a comprehensive model of HAVS pathogenesis. By deconstructing the physics of transmissibility, the mechanics of energy absorption, and the molecular responses of endothelial tissue, we elucidate why high-frequency vibration—often underestimated by current standards—is the primary driver of the vascular and neurological devastation seen in HAVS.

2. Clinical Pathology of the Hand-Arm Vibration Syndrome

To understand the mechanisms of vibration injury, one must first appreciate the clinical reality of the syndrome. HAVS is not a singular injury but a triad of disorders affecting the blood vessels, nerves, and musculoskeletal structures of the upper extremity. The WSIAT medical discussion paper establishes the case definition and diagnostic criteria used in medico-legal contexts, distinguishing HAVS from constitutional Raynaud's disease and other neuropathies.2

2.1 The Vascular Component: Secondary Raynaud’s Phenomenon

The vascular component, historically termed Vibration-Induced White Finger (VWF), is the most visible manifestation of the syndrome. It is characterized by episodic digital vasospasms, typically triggered by cold exposure or emotional stress, which result in the blanching of one or more fingers.2

2.1.1 Pathophysiology of Vasospasm

The underlying pathology involves both functional and structural alterations in the digital arteries and arterioles.

  • Functional Alterations: In the early stages, there is a hyperactivity of the central sympathetic nervous system or increased local sensitivity to circulating catecholamines, leading to exaggerated vasoconstriction in response to cold.7
  • Structural Remodeling: With continued exposure, the vessel walls undergo hypertrophy of the muscular layer (medial hypertrophy) and damage to the endothelial lining. This results in a narrowing of the lumen and a permanent reduction in blood flow, even in the absence of vasospasm.7

The "attack" itself is a triphasic event:

  1. Pallor (White): Complete closure of the digital arteries leads to ischemia.
  2. Cyanosis (Blue): As the spasm relaxes slightly, deoxygenated blood enters the capillaries.
  3. Rubor (Red): Reactive hyperemia occurs as blood flow is restored, often accompanied by throbbing pain.8

2.1.2 Grading and Classification

The severity of the vascular component is graded using the Stockholm Workshop Scale (vascular), which replaced the older Taylor-Pelmear scale. The Stockholm scale separates vascular and sensorineural symptoms, recognizing that they may progress independently.9

  • Stage 0: No attacks.
  • Stage 1 (Mild): Occasional attacks affecting only the tips of one or two fingers.
  • Stage 2 (Moderate): Occasional attacks affecting the distal and middle phalanges of one or more fingers.
  • Stage 3 (Severe): Frequent attacks affecting all phalanges of most fingers.
  • Stage 4 (Very Severe): Trophic skin changes (ulceration, gangrene) in the fingertips.9

Crucially, the Stockholm scale emphasizes the extent of blanching (distal vs. middle vs. proximal phalanges) rather than just the frequency of attacks, providing a more anatomical correlate to the cumulative vibration dose.9

2.2 The Sensorineural Component: Vibration Neuropathy

The sensorineural component often precedes vascular symptoms and can be more disabling due to the loss of manual dexterity. It manifests as intermittent or persistent numbness, tingling (paresthesia), and reduced tactile sensitivity.2

2.2.1 Mechanoreceptor Dysfunction

The glabrous skin of the hand is innervated by specialized mechanoreceptors, each tuned to specific frequencies of mechanical deformation. The damage in HAVS is frequency-specific:

  • Meissner’s Corpuscles: Located in the dermal papillae, these receptors respond to low-frequency vibration (flutter) in the 10 Hz to 50 Hz range. They are responsible for texture perception and grip control.11
  • Pacinian Corpuscles: Located deeper in the dermis and subcutaneous tissue, these are large, onion-like structures tuned to high-frequency vibration, with peak sensitivity around 250 Hz (range 40–500 Hz).11

Because many power tools generate intense vibration in the 100–300 Hz range (e.g., the meshing frequency of gears, motor rotation, pneumatic percussion), the Pacinian corpuscles are subjected to the highest mechanical loads. Consequently, the earliest sign of sensorineural HAVS is often a threshold shift in the perception of high-frequency vibration (e.g., at 125 Hz), reflecting damage to the Pacinian system.12

2.2.2 Nerve Trunk Pathopathology

Beyond the receptors, vibration causes damage to the digital nerve trunks themselves. Pathological studies have revealed:

  • Demyelination: Loss of the myelin sheath, slowing nerve conduction velocity.
  • Axonal Degeneration: Loss of nerve fibers.
  • Perineural Fibrosis: Thickening of the connective tissue surrounding the nerve bundles.13

This neuropathy contributes to the loss of thermal sensitivity and fine motor control, increasing the risk of acute accidents in the workplace.

2.3 The Musculoskeletal Component

The musculoskeletal effects of HAVS are diverse and frequency-dependent.

  • Osteoarthritis and Bone Cysts: Low-frequency vibration (<40 Hz) is transmitted efficiently through the wrist and elbow, leading to degenerative changes in the joints and the formation of bone cysts (vacuoles) in the carpal bones.2
  • Muscle Fatigue and Tendinitis: Vibration in the 80–100 Hz range triggers the Tonic Vibration Reflex (TVR), an involuntary contraction of the flexor muscles. This sustained contraction leads to rapid muscle fatigue, metabolic depletion, and chronic strain on the tendons (e.g., lateral epicondylitis or "tennis elbow").15
  • Grip Strength Reduction: A hallmark of advanced HAVS is a measurable reduction in grip strength, a consequence of both muscle pathology and the neurological loss of motor unit recruitment.2

3. The Physics of Vibration Transmission in the Human Upper Extremity

To understand why specific pathologies occur at specific anatomical sites, we must analyze the hand-arm system as a biomechanical structure. It is not a rigid body but a complex system of masses, springs, and dampers (viscoelastic tissues) with non-linear dynamic properties. The transmission of vibration through this system is governed by the principles of transmissibility, impedance, and resonance.

3.1 Transmissibility Characteristics: The Low-Pass Filter Effect

Transmissibility is defined as the ratio of vibration magnitude at a specific anatomical location (response) to the vibration magnitude at the driving point (input).

$$T(f) = \frac{a_{response}(f)}{a_{input}(f)}$$

Where $a$ is acceleration. A transmissibility of >1.0 indicates amplification (resonance), while <1.0 indicates attenuation (isolation).

Experimental mapping of the hand-arm system using 3-D laser vibrometry has revealed a distinct "low-pass filter" characteristic:

  1. Low Frequencies (1 – 25 Hz): The hand-arm system acts as a rigid unit. Vibration is transmitted with very little attenuation (transmissibility $\approx$ 1.0) from the hand to the wrist, elbow, and shoulder. In some postures (extended arm), resonance can even amplify vibration at the shoulder.14 This explains the association between low-frequency tools (e.g., rammers, road breakers) and proximal musculoskeletal disorders like neck and shoulder pain.
  2. Intermediate Frequencies (25 – 50 Hz): Transmission to the shoulder begins to drop off, but significant energy is still transmitted to the elbow and wrist. The wrist typically exhibits a resonance peak in this band (see Section 3.2).
  3. High Frequencies (> 50 Hz): A critical decoupling occurs. The soft tissues of the palm and fingers begin to isolate the proximal limb. Transmissibility to the wrist drops below 0.5, and transmission to the elbow becomes negligible.5
  4. Very High Frequencies (> 100 Hz): Vibration is effectively confined to the hand and fingers. The energy is not transmitted away; instead, it is absorbed locally by the soft tissues of the digits.16 This local absorption is the thermodynamic basis for the vascular and sensorineural injuries of the fingers.

3.2 Resonance Frequencies of Substructures

Resonance is the tendency of a system to oscillate with greater amplitude at specific frequencies. The hand-arm system exhibits distributed resonance, meaning different parts resonate at different frequencies.

3.2.1 The Finger Resonance (100 – 300 Hz)

The fingers are lightweight, stiff structures relative to the arm. Laser vibrometry studies have consistently identified the fundamental resonance of the fingers in the 100 Hz to 300 Hz range.5

  • Transverse/Shear (X and Y axes): The resonance is typically lower, around 100 Hz. In the Y-axis (radial-ulnar direction), transmissibility magnitudes can reach 3.0, meaning the finger tissue is accelerating at three times the magnitude of the tool handle.16
  • Longitudinal/Compressive (Z axis): The resonance is higher, typically around 200 Hz, due to the higher stiffness of the finger column in compression.5

This resonance is catastrophic for the tissues. It implies that a tool operating at 150 Hz is not merely shaking the finger; it is driving the finger structure into a high-amplitude standing wave, generating immense internal shear stresses.

3.2.2 The Hand-Dorsum and Wrist Resonance (30 – 50 Hz)

The combined mass of the hand and the stiffness of the wrist joint create a resonance in the 30 Hz to 50 Hz range.5

  • Similar to the fingers, the Y-axis (transverse) often shows the highest amplification, exceeding 2.5 times the input.16
  • This frequency band corresponds to the operation of many percussive tools (e.g., chipping hammers), explaining the high incidence of wrist osteoarthritis and Kienböck’s disease in workers using these tools.

3.2.3 The Forearm and Arm Resonance (< 30 Hz)

The forearm resonates between 16 Hz and 30 Hz, while the upper arm and shoulder resonate even lower, between 7 Hz and 12 Hz.14 These low-frequency resonances are associated with "whole-arm" vibration syndromes but are less relevant to the specific digital pathologies of VWF.

3.3 The Role of Coupling Forces and Posture

The biodynamic response is modulated by how the worker interacts with the tool.

  • Grip Force: Increasing grip force increases the contact stiffness between the hand and the handle. This stiffening effect tends to push resonance frequencies higher and increases the transmission of high-frequency vibration into the deeper tissues.18 A tighter grip essentially bypasses the natural damping of the skin, coupling the tool more efficiently to the skeleton.
  • Push Force: Increasing push force primarily affects the apparent mass of the system at lower frequencies, altering the transmission to the shoulder.14
  • Posture: An extended arm (locked elbow) creates a rigid transmission path, facilitating the transfer of low-frequency shock to the shoulder and neck. A bent arm acts as a mechanical filter, attenuating transmission to the shoulder but potentially increasing the load on the elbow muscles.14

4. Energy Absorption and Biodynamic Stress: The Strain-Stress Theory

While transmissibility tells us where vibration goes, Mechanical Impedance (MI) and Vibration Power Absorption (VPA) tell us how much energy is causing damage. The "Strain-Stress Theory" posits that tissue damage occurs where mechanical energy is dissipated, leading to structural fatigue and cell death.20

4.1 Mechanical Impedance Distributions

Mechanical Impedance ($Z$) is the ratio of force ($F$) to velocity ($v$) at the driving point:

$$Z(f) = \frac{F(f)}{v(f)}$$

It represents the resistance of the hand to motion.

Research decomposing impedance into "Finger" and "Palm" components reveals a critical crossover:

  • < 40 Hz: The palm and arm dominate the impedance. The effective mass is large (the whole arm is moving).
  • > 100 Hz: The impedance of the palm drops, but the impedance of the fingers remains significant. Above 160 Hz, the mechanical impedance of the fingers becomes comparable to or even greater than that of the palm.6
  • Implication: At high frequencies, the tool is essentially "driving" only the fingers. The massive inertia of the arm holds the palm relatively still, while the fingers vibrate intensely against the handle.

4.2 Vibration Power Absorption Density (VPAD)

Vibration Power Absorption ($P_{abs}$) is the real part of the energy flow:

$$P_{abs} = \text{Real}\{F \cdot v^*\}$$

While the total power absorbed by the hand might be higher at low frequencies (due to the large mass of the arm involved), the Power Absorption Density (power per unit volume of tissue) tells a different story.

Because the volume of soft tissue in the fingers is very small ($\approx 20-30 \text{ cm}^3$) compared to the arm, the energy density in the fingers at high frequencies is enormous.

  • Studies utilizing hybrid Finite Element models indicate that VPAD in the fingertips peaks in the 125 Hz to 300 Hz range.20
  • This peak coincides with the resonant frequency of the fingers.
  • At these frequencies, the energy is dissipated primarily in the skin and subcutaneous tissues of the fingertips, generating heat and, more importantly, cyclic shear strain.20

This high VPAD is the "smoking gun" for VWF. It explains why vascular damage is localized to the digits and why it correlates with exposure to high-speed tools, even if the "weighted" acceleration (which ignores these frequencies) appears low.

5. Cellular Mechanotransduction: The Molecular Basis of Injury

The physical stress defined by VPAD translates into biological injury through the process of mechanotransduction. The endothelial cells (ECs) lining the digital microvasculature are the primary targets.

5.1 Endothelial Shear Stress (ESS) and Frequency Specificity

Endothelial cells are exquisitely sensitive to fluid shear stress and mechanical strain. Under normal physiological conditions (heart rate $\approx$ 1–2 Hz), pulsatile shear stress promotes vascular health. It stimulates the expression of Endothelial Nitric Oxide Synthase (eNOS), leading to the production of Nitric Oxide (NO), a potent vasodilator and anti-atherosclerotic agent.23

However, the response of ECs to vibration is highly frequency-dependent:

  • Low Frequency (1–2 Hz): Promotes cell alignment, anti-inflammatory gene expression, and NO release. This mimics the natural cardiac cycle.23
  • High Frequency (30–100 Hz and above): The cellular response becomes pathological.
  • Turbulence and Oscillatory Flow: High-frequency vibration induces turbulent, oscillatory flow patterns in the microvessels. This "disturbed flow" is known to be pro-inflammatory and pro-thrombotic.25
  • Gene Expression Shift: At frequencies like 100 Hz, ECs exhibit distinct gene expression profiles compared to steady flow or low-frequency stimulation. For example, 100 Hz vibration has been shown to induce the expression of Syndecan-4 and VEGF.27 While VEGF promotes angiogenesis, its chronic overexpression in damaged tissue can lead to vascular remodeling and thickening (medial hypertrophy), a key feature of VWF.

5.2 The Oxidative Stress Pathway

A leading hypothesis for vibration-induced endothelial dysfunction is the uncoupling of eNOS.

  1. High-frequency mechanical strain generates excessive Reactive Oxygen Species (ROS), specifically superoxide anions ($O_2^-$).28
  2. In the presence of high ROS, eNOS uncouples: instead of producing NO, it begins to produce more superoxide.
  3. This creates a vicious cycle of oxidative stress (peroxynitrite formation), which damages the endothelial cytoskeleton, reduces the bioavailability of NO, and promotes chronic vasoconstriction.7

5.3 LncRNA and Epigenetic Changes

Recent research highlights the role of long non-coding RNAs (lncRNAs) in this process. Studies have observed a vibration-induced decrease in the expression of lncRNA MEG3 after stimulation at frequencies like 63 Hz and 250 Hz.27 MEG3 is involved in regulating endothelial function and autophagy; its downregulation may contribute to the failure of cellular repair mechanisms in the face of chronic vibration stress.

6. Frequency Weighting and Risk Assessment: The Standards vs. Science

The central controversy in HAVS prevention is how to measure and assess the risk of vibration exposure. The current international standard (ISO 5349-1) employs a frequency weighting curve that is increasingly viewed as scientifically obsolete for vascular risk assessment.

6.1 The ISO 5349-1 ($W_h$) Standard

The $W_h$ weighting curve is derived principally from studies of equi-sensation (psychophysics)—how effectively humans perceive vibration—rather than how vibration damages tissue.20

6.1.1 Mathematical Definition

The $W_h$ curve is defined by a transfer function in the Laplace domain ($s = j2\pi f$), combining a band-limiting filter ($H_b$) and a weighting filter ($H_w$):

$$H(s) = H_b(s) \cdot H_w(s)$$

The weighting filter $H_w(s)$ is a simple filter defined as:

$$H_w(s) = \frac{(s + 2\pi f_3) \cdot 2\pi K \cdot f_4^2}{(s^2 + \frac{2\pi f_4 s}{Q_2} + 4\pi^2 f_4^2) f_3}$$

Where $f_3 = 15.915$ Hz ($100/2\pi$), $f_4 = 15.915$ Hz, and $Q_2 = 0.64$.4

6.1.2 The Implication of $W_h$

The result is a curve that is roughly flat up to 16 Hz and then rolls off at -6 dB/octave (constant velocity) for higher frequencies.

  • 16 Hz: Weighting factor = 1.0
  • 160 Hz: Weighting factor = 0.1
    This implies that 160 Hz vibration is ten times less dangerous than 16 Hz vibration of the same acceleration magnitude.
  • Critique: This contradicts the biodynamic evidence (resonance at 100–300 Hz), the energy absorption evidence (VPAD peak at 125–300 Hz), and the cellular evidence (pathological gene expression at 100 Hz). By suppressing high frequencies, $W_h$ drastically underestimates the risk of tools like die grinders, dental drills, and impact wrenches.3

6.2 The Brammer-Taylor Model ($W_{hf}$ / $W_{hT}$)

Recognizing the failure of $W_h$ to predict VWF latency in workers using high-speed tools, researchers Brammer and Taylor developed alternative weightings based on epidemiological and vascular models.31

Their analysis compared cohorts exposed to low-frequency tools (motorcycles, pavement breakers) vs. high-frequency tools (chainsaws, rock drills). They found that to normalize the risk between these groups, the frequency weighting needed to be flat (frequency-independent) across the primary hazard range.

6.2.1 Characteristics of the Brammer Curve

  • Range 25 Hz – 500 Hz: Flat response (Weighting $\approx$ 1.0).
  • Slopes: 12 dB/octave rolloff below 25 Hz and above 500 Hz.29
  • Significance: This model treats all frequencies in the 25–500 Hz band as equally damaging. This aligns with the broadband nature of finger resonance and energy absorption. When applied to epidemiological data, this weighting yields a much stronger correlation with the prevalence of VWF than $W_h$.31

6.3 ISO/TR 18570 and the $W_p$ Curve

In 2017, the ISO released Technical Report 18570, introducing the $W_p$ (hand-arm vascular) frequency weighting. This represents the formal standardization of the scientific consensus on high-frequency risk.3

6.3.1 Comparison: $W_h$ vs $W_p$

The $W_p$ curve is significantly more aggressive in retaining high-frequency content.

  • While $W_h$ attenuates frequencies above 16 Hz, $W_p$ maintains a high weighting factor well into the 250 Hz range.
  • Mathematical Concept: Though the exact polynomial coefficients are complex, the curve effectively shifts the focus from "whole-arm shaking" (<25 Hz) to "finger energy absorption" (31.5 – 250 Hz).
  • Validation: In the EU VIBRISKS project, the daily exposure calculated using $W_p$ (denoted as $A_p(8)$) was a superior predictor of VWF symptoms and cold response compared to the standard $A_h(8)$.3

7. Diagnostic, Legal, and Prevention Implications

The disconnect between the regulatory standard ($W_h$) and the biological reality ($W_p$/VPAD) creates significant challenges in the diagnosis, compensation, and prevention of HAVS.

7.1 The "Safe Tool" Fallacy

Tools are currently marketed with vibration emission values declared according to ISO 5349. Manufacturers often design tools to shift vibration energy into higher frequencies (>100 Hz) to reduce the $W_h$-weighted acceleration value.

  • The Problem: A tool might have a declared emission of $2.5 \text{ m/s}^2$ (low risk), but an unweighted or $W_p$-weighted acceleration of $20 \text{ m/s}^2$ (extreme risk).
  • Consequence: Workers are exposed to high levels of damaging high-frequency energy while the risk assessment indicates safety. This may explain the continued incidence of HAVS in workplaces that are technically "compliant" with exposure limits.30

7.2 Diagnostic Challenges

The WSIAT discussion paper highlights the difficulty in diagnosing HAVS, particularly in separating it from constitutional Raynaud's or carpal tunnel syndrome (CTS).2

  • Objective Testing: Because subjective history is unreliable, objective tests are crucial.
  • Cold Provocation Test with Plethysmography: Measures the recovery of finger systolic blood pressure or temperature after cold stress.
  • Vibrotactile Thresholds: Measures the threshold of sensation at specific frequencies (typically 31.5 Hz and 125 Hz). A threshold shift at 125 Hz is a specific indicator of Pacinian corpuscle damage, consistent with high-frequency exposure.12
  • The CTS Confounder: HAVS and Carpal Tunnel Syndrome (CTS) often coexist. However, the WSIAT paper notes that CTS is distinct from the neurological component of HAVS. CTS is a median nerve compression at the wrist, while HAVS neuropathy is a diffuse, distal axonopathy affecting the digital nerves. The high transmissibility of 30–50 Hz vibration to the wrist likely contributes to the etiology of CTS in vibrating tool users.2

7.3 Prevention Strategies: The Role of Gloves

Anti-vibration (AV) gloves are a common control measure, certified under ISO 10819. However, their efficacy is frequency-dependent.

  • Low Frequencies (< 25 Hz): AV gloves often amplify vibration due to the resonance of the glove material itself.35
  • High Frequencies (> 100 Hz): AV gloves can provide significant attenuation.
  • Paradox: Since ISO 5349 ($W_h$) prioritizes low frequencies, the "weighted" transmissibility of gloves often appears poor. However, if we accept the Brammer/ISO 18570 view that high frequencies are the true vascular hazard, then AV gloves may be more effective clinically than the current standards suggest, provided they are not used with low-frequency tools.36

8. Conclusion: Toward a Biologically Relevant Standard

The comprehensive analysis of the frequency-human body interaction reveals a stark dichotomy. On one side stands the established regulatory framework (ISO 5349-1), built on a model of vibration perception that heavily weights low frequencies. On the other side stands a formidable body of biodynamic, physiological, and epidemiological evidence indicating that intermediate and high frequencies (30 Hz – 300 Hz) are the primary drivers of the specific tissue pathologies that constitute Hand-Arm Vibration Syndrome.

The mechanism is clear:

  1. Resonance: The fingers resonate between 100 Hz and 300 Hz.
  2. Absorption: This resonance leads to maximal Vibration Power Absorption Density (VPAD) in the digital soft tissues.
  3. Transduction: The resulting high-frequency shear stress and strain trigger pathological gene expression (eNOS uncoupling, inflammatory cytokines) in the endothelial cells and structural damage to Pacinian corpuscles.

For the medical and safety community, the implications are profound. Reliance on the single $A(8)$ value derived from $W_h$ is insufficient and potentially negligent. Effective protection requires a spectral approach to risk assessment—one that recognizes the unique lethality of high-frequency vibration and prioritizes the mitigation of energy in the 30–300 Hz band. Until standards evolve to reflect this biological reality (e.g., by adopting $W_p$), the "silent" epidemic of HAVS will likely persist, driven by the invisible, high-frequency energy that current models choose to ignore.
