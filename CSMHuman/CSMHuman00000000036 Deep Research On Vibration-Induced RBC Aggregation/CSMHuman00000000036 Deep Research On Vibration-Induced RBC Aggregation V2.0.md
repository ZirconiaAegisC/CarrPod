## 1. Aggregation Mechanism: 2025 Single-Cell Confirmation

V1.0's erythrocyte-scale analysis addresses the debated mechanism of RBC aggregation (macromolecular "bridging" vs. depletion-force models). A 2025 single-cell-level study (laser tweezers combined with microfluidic technology and fluorescence microscopy) provides new direct evidence: immediate adsorption of approximately 10⁴ molecules of 70 kDa dextran per cell was observed, refining the quantitative basis for the depletion-force model this document evaluates.

---

## 2. New Clinical Reference Threshold: Critical Shear Rate

A June 2025 clinical hemorheology study establishes a **critical shear rate of 446 s⁻¹** required to fully disaggregate RBC aggregates in healthy-control blood samples, using a microfluidic Aggregation-Area-Indicator (AAIH) method. This provides V1.0's whole-body-to-erythrocyte-scale analysis with a concrete, clinically-derived quantitative threshold (distinct from, but complementary to, the vibration-frequency thresholds this document otherwise emphasizes) — and a pathological comparison point, since diseased (chronic lymphocytic leukemia) samples show significantly elevated resistance to shear-induced disaggregation at this same threshold.

## 3. Frequency-Specific Vibration Threshold

A controlled whole-blood study confirms that vibration frequencies exceeding **10 Hz** (specifically, 11 Hz vs. 5 Hz/0 Hz controls) produce a statistically significant decrease in erythrocyte and platelet counts — directly relevant to this document's own thesis that specific frequencies can act as a pathogenic mechanical force on blood cellular elements, and providing a concrete frequency threshold value that V1.0's more general frequency-dependency argument lacked.

## 4. In Vivo Human Complement

A 2025 whole-body-vibration exercise study (elderly women, Smovey-device protocol) measured changes in red blood cell count, hemoglobin, platelet count, and both aggregation index (AI) and elongation index (EI) following a 3-month training period — providing an in vivo human physiological data point that complements this document's more mechanistic/in vitro erythrocyte-scale focus.
