Neural Overload

When facing a mirror, the brain does not just "see." It simultaneously assesses threats, checks social status, and attempts to predict the movement of the "other." This creates a spike in cognitive load across multiple brain regions, effectively locking attention.

  • Amygdala: Immediate threat assessment (Is it safe?).
  • Visual Cortex: Processing the "Uncanny" movement synchronization.
  • Prefrontal Cortex: Social logic and self-correction.

The Magnetic Pull

Why is it so hard to look away? The combination of motion synchronization and facial recognition creates a "Super-Stimulus."

Studies show that even when driving—a high-stakes environment—the glance at the rear-view mirror lingers significantly longer if the driver makes eye contact with themselves compared to checking traffic.

Insight: The "Mirror (Self)" category commands attention nearly 6x longer than a standard window view.
