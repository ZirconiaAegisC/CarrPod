## 1. From Dissociation to Agency-Loss: The 2026 Causal Study

V1.0's "Chrono-Psychology" framework treated the mirror-gaze compulsion as an evolutionary-survival artifact that terminates, under prolonged fixation, in dissociative/depersonalization states (per Caputo 2010 and related work). V2.0 incorporates a decisive causal advance: **Bregman-Hai & Soffer-Dudek (PMC12919786)** ran a two-phase experiment isolating mirror-gazing as the causal driver of impaired **sense of agency** — the felt experience of being the author of one's own actions — rather than dissociation being a terminal, disconnected end-state.

| Study | Design | Key Finding |
|---|---|---|
| Study 1 (N=98) | Mirror-gazing (± suggestive cue) vs. video-watching control | Mirror-gazing induced dissociation and reduced *explicit* agency, concentrated in high trait-dissociators; video control was confounded (also raised "absorption") |
| Study 2 (N=199) | Mirror-gazing vs. article-reading control (improved), plus an implicit agency task | Mirror-gazing impaired *implicit* agency broadly across the sample — the effect is not confined to people who already report high dissociative tendencies |

**Reframing for V2.0:** the mammalian compulsion described in V1.0 is not simply "unable to look away" — it is a mechanism that, past a duration threshold, actively degrades the observer's felt control over their own behavior. This matters clinically (see §2) and operationally (see §3).

## 2. Clinical Cross-Reference: Mirror-Confrontation in Dissociative Disorder

V1.0 focused on healthy-subject phenomenology. V2.0 adds the clinical boundary condition: in diagnosed dissociative disorder (DD) patients, facial mirror-confrontation (CFM) produces significantly more subjective stress and acute dissociation than in healthy controls, **but with blunted autonomic (sympathetic/parasympathetic) reactivity** measured via impedance cardiography — i.e., the psychological distress is not matched by a proportional physiological stress response. This autonomic blunting is consistent with the agency-loss model in §1: the DD patient's mirror image is processed as psychologically threatening while the normal "fight/flight" authorship signal is disconnected, mirroring the implicit-agency finding in healthy subjects at a pathological extreme.

**Therapeutic implication (unchanged from clinical literature, newly cross-referenced in V2.0):** controlled, brief mirror-exposure protocols are being explored as a diagnostic/therapeutic tool for DD precisely because the effect is reproducible and measurable, not because it is benign.

## 3. Applied Extension: Autonomous-Vehicle Handoff Agency Loss

The most significant V2.0 addition is the extension of the agency-loss model to a modern operational context relevant to Aegis-C platform design: **Level 3 conditional-automation handoff.**

During Level 3 operation, the driver is a passive monitor (often gaze-fixed on a forward display or instrument cluster) who must reacquire full behavioral agency within a bounded time window when the system issues a takeover request. The mirror-gaze literature's central finding — that passive, fixed visual monitoring of a "responsive" or semi-autonomous visual field measurably degrades the *implicit* sense of agency even in psychologically healthy people — is treated in V2.0 as a directly analogous risk profile:

| Mirror-Gaze Finding (§1) | Level 3 Handoff Analogy |
|---|---|
| Fixed gaze on a synchronized, responsive visual stimulus | Fixed gaze on a forward display responding to real-time driving state |
| Implicit agency degraded broadly, not just in high-dissociators | Any driver, regardless of baseline psychological profile, may show slowed re-engagement |
| Explicit agency loss concentrated in high trait-dissociators | Individual variability in takeover-request response time may correlate with dissociative-trait screening (research question, not yet validated for this specific application) |

**Aegis-C design implication:** takeover-request (TOR) alerts are specified to use multi-modal (haptic seat pulse + auditory + visual) redundancy rather than visual-only escalation, precisely because the visual channel is the one shown in the mirror-gaze literature to be associated with agency suppression under sustained fixation.

## 4. Solar Cycle 25 Addendum: Instrument-Cluster Blackout as an Agency-Loss Event

During a G4–G5 geomagnetically induced current (GIC) event (Solar Cycle 25 observed SSN ~137 peak), an unshielded digital instrument cluster or ADAS display may blank or flicker. V2.0 flags this as materially distinct from a simple "display failure": per §1 and §3, the moments immediately following a sudden loss of a fixed visual monitoring target are precisely when re-engagement of full behavioral agency is required, and the psychological literature gives no assurance this transition is instantaneous.
