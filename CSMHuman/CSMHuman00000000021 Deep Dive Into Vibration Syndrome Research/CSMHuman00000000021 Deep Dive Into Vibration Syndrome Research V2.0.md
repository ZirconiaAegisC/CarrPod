## 1. Molecular Vascular Mechanism: Piezo1 Confirms the 1994 Clinical Model

V1.0's foundation — the Pelmear & Taylor (1994) clinical baseline establishing HAVS as a multifaceted vascular/sensorineural/musculoskeletal disorder — described the vascular component (digital vasospasm, Vibration-Induced White Finger) without a confirmed cellular mechanism. **2025 research directly closes this gap:** vibration exposure drives Piezo1-mediated upregulation of angiogenic chemokines (CCL2, CCL5, CXCL1, CXCL2, CXCL10) in vascular smooth muscle via NF-κB/p65 signaling (*Nature Communications Biology*, Jan 2025). This gives the 30-year-old clinical classification a specific, testable molecular pathway: chronic Piezo1 activation from repetitive hand-transmitted vibration plausibly drives the chronic inflammatory vascular remodeling underlying progressive VWF, rather than vasospasm alone.

| Pelmear & Taylor (1994) Clinical Category | V2.0 Molecular Correlate |
|---|---|
| Vascular (VWF) | Piezo1 → NF-κB/p65 → chemokine-driven vascular smooth muscle remodeling |
| Sensorineural | Not directly addressed by Piezo1 data; retained from V1.0 without molecular update |
| Musculoskeletal | Not directly addressed by Piezo1 data; retained from V1.0 without molecular update |

## 2. Standards and Field-Monitoring Updates

- **ISO 5349-3:2025** adds shock/impulse vibration measurement (Vibration Peak Magnitude, 8–1000 Hz octaves) to the continuous-exposure ISO 5349-1 framework V1.0 relies on.
- **Wearable monitoring (arXiv:2509.16536, Sept 2025):** a transfer-function correction method allows wrist/upper-arm wearable accelerometers to produce ISO-5349-comparable exposure estimates without handle-mounted instrumentation, validated on hammer-drilling trials.
