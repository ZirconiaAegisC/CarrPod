## 1. Impedance Modeling: From Mechanical-Only to Mechanical + Molecular

V1.0's mechanical-impedance treatment models the body as coupled masses, springs, and dampers with frequency-dependent energy absorption. V2.0 adds a second impedance-like layer at the molecular scale: Piezo1 and Piezo2 channels behave as **frequency filters** with transduction efficiency dependent on stimulus frequency, waveform, and duration (Duke University heterologous expression studies) — functionally analogous to an electrical impedance that varies with drive frequency, but operating on mechanotransduction rather than energy dissipation. This gives the vibro-acoustic coupling analysis in this document a two-tier impedance model:

| Tier | Physical Analogue | Frequency Dependency |
|---|---|---|
| Macroscopic (V1.0 baseline) | Mechanical impedance (mass/stiffness/damping) | Segment-specific resonance (e.g., NASA Kaplan & Rubinstein lateral-vibration/vision coupling) |
| Molecular (V2.0 addition) | Channel gating "impedance" | Piezo1/Piezo2 inactivation-kinetics-dependent frequency filtering |

## 2. Applied Case Study: Aegis-C BFRP Chassis Helmholtz Tuning

The vibro-acoustic coupling principles in this document are directly realized in the Aegis-C platform's cabin design: the BFRP monocoque panel fundamental resonance is tuned to 178 Hz (versus ~85 Hz for a typical steel floorpan), and the cabin height (1,050 mm, BERYL sf=1.0 reference) creates a first Helmholtz cavity mode at approximately 163 Hz. Both figures sit well above the 8–16 Hz visual/vestibular risk band and the sub-80 Hz therapeutic vibroacoustic window used elsewhere in the CSM Aegis product line, demonstrating V1.0's coupling theory applied as a deliberate structural design target rather than an incidental property.
