The Biodynamic and Pathophysiological Interface of Hand-Arm Vibration Syndrome: A Comprehensive Synthesis of Frequency-Dependent Human-Body Interactions

1. Introduction: The Etiological Complexity of Hand-Arm Vibration

The interaction between the human body and mechanical vibration represents one of the most complex interfaces in occupational medicine, a domain where Newtonian physics collides with cellular mechanotransduction to produce a spectrum of debilitating pathologies collectively known as Hand-Arm Vibration Syndrome (HAVS). While the industrial application of vibrating tools—from pneumatic drills to chainsaws—has driven infrastructural and economic progress for over a century, the biological cost is measured in the progressive, irreversible dysregulation of the vascular, neurological, and musculoskeletal systems of the upper limb. This comprehensive report serves as an exhaustive technical synthesis of the current state of knowledge regarding HAVS, integrating data from the Workplace Safety and Insurance Appeals Tribunal (WSIAT) medical discussion papers, advanced biodynamic research, and recent molecular findings regarding mechanosensitive ion channels.

At its core, HAVS is not merely a consequence of "shaking"; it is a specific failure of biological adaptation to high-frequency energy transfer. The traditional reductionist view, which quantified exposure simply by acceleration magnitude, has been superseded by a more nuanced understanding of biodynamics—specifically, how the mechanical impedance of the hand, the resonance of tissue substructures, and the frequency-dependent filtering of the arm determine the site and severity of injury. The discordance between the physical reality of vibration transmission—particularly the resonance phenomena occurring between 30 Hz and 500 Hz—and the regulatory frameworks used to assess risk remains a central tension in the field.

This analysis proceeds by dissecting the vibration-body interaction from the macroscopic to the molecular level. It traces the propagation of energy from the tool handle, through the non-linear viscoelastic tissues of the palm and fingers, into the resonance-prone structures of the digital neurovasculature, and finally to the genetic and protein-level responses that drive endothelial remodeling and neuropathy. In doing so, it addresses the critical role of frequency weighting, exposing the limitations of current ISO standards and proposing a bio-physically grounded model of injury that aligns with the "frequency human body interaction" observed in clinical and experimental settings.

1.1 Historical Context and Clinical Definition

The clinical entity now known as HAVS has evolved through various nomenclatures, reflecting a deepening understanding of its pathophysiology. Originally termed "Raynaud’s phenomenon of occupational origin" or "Vibration White Finger" (VWF), these descriptors focused primarily on the dramatic vascular presentation—the episodic blanching of fingers upon cold exposure.1 However, the seminal work of researchers such as Taylor, Pelmear, and Brammer expanded this definition to encompass the triad of vascular, sensorineural, and musculoskeletal components that constitute the full syndrome.2

The WSIAT Medical Discussion Paper establishes a rigorous case definition: HAVS refers to the vascular, neurological, and musculoskeletal health effects that occur due to hand-arm vibration exposure, which may occur separately or in combination.1 The vascular effect is secondary Raynaud's phenomenon; the sensorineural effect is digital neuropathy characterized by numbness and reduced tactile sensitivity; and the musculoskeletal effect involves decreased grip strength and potential degenerative changes in the bones and joints.1 This definition is critical because it acknowledges that a worker may suffer from profound neurological disability—such as the inability to manipulate small objects or feel temperature—without ever manifesting the classic white finger attacks, or vice versa.1

The prevalence of the syndrome in exposed populations is staggeringly high, ranging from 6% to 100%, with an average of approximately 50% in groups using high-vibration tools.1 The latency period—the time from first exposure to the onset of symptoms—is inversely proportional to the intensity of the vibration. While latencies of 5 to 10 years are common for moderate exposures, high-intensity inputs (acceleration > 10 m/s²) can precipitate advanced pathology in as little as 6 months to 2 years.1 This varying susceptibility underscores the importance of the dose-response relationship, a concept that is complicated by the fact that the "dose" is not simply acceleration, but the energy absorbed by the tissue at specific, damaging frequencies.

2. Biodynamics of the Hand-Arm System: The Physics of Energy Transfer

To understand why vibration causes injury, one must first understand how the hand-arm system behaves as a mechanical structure. The arm is not a rigid body; it is a complex system of masses (bones), springs (ligaments, muscles), and dampers (viscoelastic soft tissues).5 When exposed to oscillatory force, this system exhibits mechanical impedance, defined as the ratio of the driving force to the resulting velocity at the point of interaction.

2.1 Mechanical Impedance and Non-Linearity

The mechanical impedance ($Z$) of the hand-arm system is the primary determinant of how much vibration energy enters the body. Research indicates that impedance is highly non-linear and dependent on several variables: frequency, grip force, push force, and posture.6

At low frequencies (below 25 Hz), the impedance of the hand-arm system is dominated by the mass of the arm. The vibration is transmitted efficiently past the wrist and elbow to the shoulder, causing the entire limb to move in sync with the tool. This explains why low-frequency tools, such as rammers and breakers, are associated with proximal disorders like shoulder tendinitis and neck pain; the energy is not absorbed in the hand but is transmitted up the kinetic chain.8

However, as frequency increases into the range of 30 Hz to 500 Hz, the dynamics shift dramatically. The impedance of the hand increases, and the "spring" and "damper" characteristics of the finger and palm tissues become dominant. The hand begins to decouple from the arm, and the vibration energy is increasingly dissipated (absorbed) within the soft tissues of the hand itself.9 This phenomenon is critical: injury occurs where energy is absorbed. The high impedance of the hand at these frequencies means that the energy driving the tool is converted into heat and mechanical strain within the delicate structures of the fingers, rather than being harmlessly transmitted to the body mass.

2.2 The Role of Grip Force

A crucial variable in this interaction is grip force. Experimental data consistently demonstrates that increasing grip force significantly increases the mechanical impedance of the hand-arm system across the frequency spectrum, particularly between 30 Hz and 200 Hz.5 A tighter grip effectively "stiffens" the coupling between the hand and the vibrating handle.

This has two deleterious effects:

  1. Increased Transmission: A tighter coupling improves the transmissibility of high-frequency vibration into the deep tissues of the fingers, bypassing the natural damping properties of the skin.7
  2. Static Load Ischemia: The muscular contraction required to maintain a strong grip increases internal tissue pressure, compressing capillaries and reducing blood flow. This creates a state of relative ischemia, rendering the tissues more vulnerable to the metabolic stress induced by the vibration energy.3

Consequently, the ergonomic advice to "let the tool do the work" is not merely about reducing fatigue; it is a biodynamic intervention intended to lower the mechanical impedance and reduce the total energy flux into the hand.

2.3 Vibration Transmissibility and Resonance

Transmissibility is the ratio of vibration magnitude at a specific anatomical location (e.g., the wrist or elbow) to the magnitude at the source (the handle). A ratio greater than 1.0 indicates resonance—a condition where the tissue vibrates with greater amplitude than the source itself. A ratio less than 1.0 indicates attenuation or isolation.

The frequency response of the upper limb reveals a segmented pattern of transmission and isolation:

  • Shoulder and Head (< 25 Hz): Vibration in the 4–10 Hz range can excite whole-body resonances and is transmitted efficiently to the shoulder and head. This is the domain of Whole-Body Vibration (WBV) but is relevant to HAVS in the context of musculoskeletal pain in the upper back and neck.11
  • Elbow and Forearm (16–40 Hz): The forearm and elbow structures show resonance peaks in this range, correlating with the risk of elbow osteoarthrosis in workers operating percussive tools.12
  • Fingers and Hand (> 50 Hz): Above 50 Hz, and particularly above 100 Hz, the vibration is effectively isolated from the proximal arm. The transmissibility to the elbow drops to near zero. However, this isolation comes at the cost of the hand.

The Finger Resonance Phenomenon:

The most critical finding in recent biodynamic research is the identification of specific resonant frequencies in the fingers. Using 3-D scanning laser vibrometers, researchers have mapped the vibration transmissibility of human fingers and found profound resonances:

  • Transverse/Shear Directions (X/Y axes): Major resonance occurs at approximately 100 Hz.13
  • Compressive Direction (Z axis): Major resonance occurs at approximately 200 Hz.13

At these resonant frequencies, the vibration amplitude within the finger tissue can be three times higher than the input vibration from the tool. This amplification creates localized "hotspots" of intense mechanical stress. The tissue is literally shaking itself apart at the cellular level. This biodynamic reality explains the distribution of pathology: VWF and digital neuropathy are diseases of the fingers because that is where the high-frequency energy is trapped and amplified by resonance.10

2.4 Vibration Power Absorption (VPA)

Given the limitations of measuring acceleration alone, researchers have proposed Vibration Power Absorption (VPA) as a superior metric for injury risk. VPA represents the actual energy dissipated per unit time in the biological system.15 Unlike acceleration, which is a kinematic quantity, VPA is a dynamic quantity that accounts for the force, velocity, and the phase angle between them (the damping capacity).

Studies mapping the distribution of VPA have shown that at frequencies above 100 Hz, energy absorption is almost exclusively confined to the fingers and the contact surface of the palm. The "Power Absorption Density"—the energy absorbed per volume of tissue—is highest in the fingertips, exactly correlating with the anatomical loci of the most severe vascular and neurological damage.17 This energetic perspective provides the causal link between the physics of the tool and the biology of the injury: the absorbed power is the agent of damage, driving the heating, shearing, and metabolic exhaustion of the tissue.

3. Pathophysiology of the Vascular Component: Molecular Mechanotransduction

The vascular component of HAVS, VWF, is characterized by the hyper-responsiveness of digital arteries to cold. While earlier theories focused on autonomic dysregulation, modern molecular biology has elucidated a direct mechanism of vascular injury driven by the mechanical forces of vibration.

3.1 The Piezo1 Channel and Shear Stress

A breakthrough in understanding the molecular basis of HAVS is the identification of Piezo1, a mechanosensitive cation channel expressed in endothelial cells and vascular smooth muscle cells (VSMCs). Piezo1 is a trimeric protein that sits in the cell membrane and opens in response to mechanical tension, specifically fluid shear stress and membrane stretch.19

High-frequency vibration (100–300 Hz) generates rapid oscillatory shear stress within the blood vessels of the fingers. This mechanical stimulus forces Piezo1 channels into a state of chronic activation. Upon opening, Piezo1 allows a massive influx of Calcium ions ($Ca^{2+}$) into the cytoplasm. Calcium is a ubiquitous signaling molecule, and its dysregulation is catastrophic for vascular health.

The Piezo1-NF-κB Axis:

Recent research (2025) has delineated a specific pathway linking Piezo1 activation to vascular remodeling. The influx of calcium activates the NF-κB/p65 signaling pathway, a master regulator of inflammation.21 This activation leads to the transcriptional upregulation of pro-inflammatory chemokines, including:

  • CCL2 (MCP-1): Monocyte Chemoattractant Protein-1, which recruits immune cells to the vessel wall.
  • CXCL1 and CXCL10: Chemokines involved in neutrophil recruitment and angiostasis.21

This chronic inflammatory signaling promotes the proliferation of VSMCs, leading to medial hypertrophy—a thickening of the muscular wall of the artery. A thickened vessel wall encroaches on the lumen, reducing the vessel's diameter and its capacity for blood flow. This structural remodeling provides the anatomical substrate for Raynaud's phenomenon: a narrowed vessel requires only a minor degree of constriction to close off completely.22

3.2 Endothelial Dysfunction and the NO/ET-1 Imbalance

The endothelium—the inner lining of the blood vessel—regulates vascular tone by balancing vasodilators (Nitric Oxide - NO) and vasoconstrictors (Endothelin-1 - ET-1). Vibration exposure disrupts this balance fundamentally.

Endothelin-1 (ET-1):

ET-1 is one of the most potent vasoconstrictors in the human body. Studies consistently show elevated plasma levels of ET-1 in workers with HAVS, with concentrations correlating with the severity of the staging.24 The mechanical stress of vibration directly stimulates endothelial cells to synthesize and release ET-1. Furthermore, the thickened smooth muscle (driven by Piezo1) is hyper-sensitive to ET-1, creating a vicious cycle of constriction.

Reactive Oxygen Species (ROS) and Nitric Oxide Scavenging:

The metabolic demand of vibration exposure, coupled with the oscillatory stress on mitochondria, leads to the generation of Reactive Oxygen Species (ROS), such as superoxide anions ($O_2^-$) and hydrogen peroxide ($H_2O_2$).26

  • NO Scavenging: Superoxide anions react rapidly with Nitric Oxide to form peroxynitrite ($ONOO^-$). This reaction effectively removes NO from the system, eliminating the vessel's primary mechanism for dilation and relaxation.25
  • Src Kinase Activation: ROS can directly activate Src-family kinases in VSMCs, which facilitate calcium sensitization and contraction, further biasing the vessel toward spasm.26

The net result is a "primed" vessel: structurally narrowed, deficient in vasodilators, and bathed in vasoconstrictors and inflammatory mediators. When such a vessel is exposed to cold (a sympathetic stimulus), it clamps down catastrophically, resulting in the blanching and ischemia characteristic of VWF.

4. Pathophysiology of the Neurological Component: Sensorineural Frequency Selectivity

The neurological component of HAVS is not a generalized neuropathy but a frequency-specific failure of the somatosensory system. The glabrous skin of the hand is populated by four main types of mechanoreceptors, each tuned to a specific frequency band. The damage profile of HAVS mirrors the resonance profile of the fingers.

4.1 Mechanoreceptor Specialization and Vulnerability

To understand sensorineural HAVS, one must distinguish between the receptor types:

  1. Merkel Disks (SAI): Sensitive to static pressure and very low frequencies (0–3 Hz). They are responsible for form and texture perception.
  2. Meissner’s Corpuscles (FAI): Rapidly adapting receptors located in the dermal papillae. They are tuned to low-frequency "flutter," with peak sensitivity around 30–50 Hz.28
  3. Pacinian Corpuscles (FAII): Large, onion-like structures located deep in the dermis and subcutaneous tissue. They are the primary receptors for high-frequency vibration, with an extremely high sensitivity peaking between 100 Hz and 400 Hz.30

The "Pacinian Failure" Hypothesis:

Biodynamic studies show that vibration transmission to the fingers is maximal in the 100–300 Hz range due to resonance. This frequency band overlaps perfectly with the physiological tuning of the Pacinian corpuscle. Consequently, the Pacinian corpuscle is subjected to the highest mechanical load of any neural structure in the hand.

  • Metabolic Exhaustion: The continuous high-frequency firing demands immense metabolic energy (ion pumping to restore gradients). Ischemia induced by the vascular component exacerbates this, leading to receptor failure.
  • Structural Disintegration: Animal studies exposing rat tails to 250 Hz vibration (the Pacinian resonance) have shown physical destruction of the nerve endings, demyelination, and axonal swelling.15
  • Clinical Correlation: This explains why the earliest and most profound sensory deficit in HAVS is often a loss of vibration perception at high frequencies (125–500 Hz), while two-point discrimination (mediated by Merkel cells) may remain intact until later stages.32

4.2 Demyelination and Axonopathy

Beyond the receptors, the nerve trunks themselves are damaged. Vibration acts as repetitive micro-trauma to the peripheral nerves. Histological examination of vibration-exposed nerves reveals:

  • Perineurial Fibrosis: Thickening of the connective tissue sheath surrounding the nerve fascicles.
  • Segmental Demyelination: Loss of the myelin sheath, particularly near the Nodes of Ranvier, which slows conduction velocity.15
  • Anterograde Degeneration: In severe cases, the distal parts of the axon degenerate (dying-back neuropathy).

Recent findings implicate the same oxidative stress pathways seen in the vasculature. ROS produced during vibration exposure attack the lipid-rich myelin sheaths (lipid peroxidation) and disrupt mitochondrial function in the axons, leading to energy failure and apoptosis of Schwann cells.34

5. Clinical Assessment and Staging: The WSIAT Framework

The assessment of HAVS requires a structured clinical approach that separates the vascular and neurological components, as established by the Stockholm Workshop and adopted by WSIAT.

5.1 The Stockholm Workshop Scale (1987)

The Stockholm Workshop Scale (SWS) replaced the older Taylor-Pelmear scale and remains the international gold standard for staging.3 It is crucial to stage each hand separately.

5.2 Diagnostic Modalities

Diagnosis relies on a combination of occupational history, physical examination, and objective testing.

1. Vibrotactile Perception Threshold (VPT):

This is the cornerstone of neurological assessment. The patient places a finger on a vibrating probe, and the amplitude is increased until felt.

  • Frequency Specificity: Testing must be performed at multiple frequencies. A shift at 31.5 Hz suggests Meissner corpuscle damage, while a shift at 125 Hz or 500 Hz indicates Pacinian corpuscle damage.
  • Early Detection: Bovenzi (2025) highlights that VPT shifts at 500 Hz are the most sensitive early indicator of HAVS, consistent with the high-frequency vulnerability of the Pacinian corpuscle.32

2. Thermal Perception Thresholds:

Testing the ability to detect hot and cold stimuli assesses the function of small unmyelinated (C-fibers) and thinly myelinated (A-delta) fibers. This helps distinguish HAVS from large-fiber neuropathies.

3. Cold Provocation Test:

To assess the vascular component objectively, the hands are cooled (e.g., immersion in 10°C water) while finger systolic blood pressure (FSBP) is monitored. A significant drop in FSBP in the cooled fingers compared to a reference finger indicates a vasospastic defect. The "finger rewarming test" measures how quickly the fingers return to baseline temperature; delayed rewarming is a hallmark of HAVS.

4. Differential Diagnosis:

A critical task is distinguishing HAVS from Carpal Tunnel Syndrome (CTS). Both cause numbness and tingling.

  • HAVS: typically diffuse, affecting all fingers (or those most exposed), often sparing the thumb. Symptoms are often worse during or immediately after vibration.
  • CTS: affects the median nerve distribution (thumb, index, middle, half of ring), usually worse at night, and associated with Phalen’s/Tinel’s signs.
  • Overlap: Vibration is an independent risk factor for CTS. Many workers have both HAVS and CTS ("HAVS complicated by CTS").1 Nerve conduction studies (NCS) are essential to separate the focal slowing of CTS at the wrist from the diffuse distal slowing of HAVS.

6. The Regulatory Controversy: ISO 5349 and the Failure of $W_h$

A central theme in the scientific literature, and a critical point for any deep analysis of HAVS, is the growing consensus that the current international standard for risk assessment—ISO 5349-1—is scientifically flawed and fails to protect workers from the specific frequency components that cause the most damage.15

6.1 The ISO Frequency Weighting Curve ($W_h$)

ISO 5349-1 defines the method for measuring and reporting hand-transmitted vibration. It utilizes a frequency-weighting curve known as $W_h$.

  • The Shape of $W_h$: The curve assigns a weighting factor of 1.0 to frequencies between 8 Hz and 16 Hz. Above 16 Hz, the weighting decreases linearly with frequency at a slope of -6 dB per octave.
  • The Implication: This means the standard assumes that vibration at 16 Hz is the most dangerous, and that danger decreases rapidly as frequency increases. By 250 Hz, the weighting factor is approximately 0.063. In other words, the standard assumes that 250 Hz vibration is roughly 15 times less harmful than 16 Hz vibration of the same acceleration amplitude.37

Origins of $W_h$:

The $W_h$ curve was derived decades ago, largely based on subjective comfort studies and whole-body vibration data. It assumes that "displacement" is the key injury metric (since constant velocity implies decreasing displacement as frequency rises). However, biological tissue damage is driven by strain, energy absorption, and velocity, not just displacement.

6.2 The Biological Mismatch

The $W_h$ curve stands in direct contradiction to the biodynamic and pathological evidence presented in this report:

  1. Vascular Evidence: Epidemiological studies of workers (e.g., stone cutters, forestry workers) consistently show that VWF prevalence correlates best with exposure to intermediate and high frequencies (30–500 Hz), not low frequencies.40
  2. Resonance Evidence: As established, the fingers resonate and absorb maximum power between 100 Hz and 300 Hz. The $W_h$ curve effectively filters out this critical "damage band," assigning it a negligible weight.
  3. Neurological Evidence: The Pacinian corpuscle, the primary sensor of vibration, is most sensitive (and thus most vulnerable) at 250 Hz. The standard down-weights this frequency, failing to predict sensorineural risk.43

The Consequence of Using $W_h$:

The continued use of ISO 5349 results in the "clearing" of dangerous tools. A high-speed grinder operating at 150 Hz might produce a low ISO-weighted acceleration value (because the 150 Hz component is multiplied by ~0.1). However, that same 150 Hz vibration is driving massive energy absorption in the fingers, activating Piezo1 channels, and destroying nerve endings. This regulatory blind spot likely contributes to the persistence of HAVS in industry despite compliance with exposure limits.38

7. Prevention, Control, and the Role of PPE

Given the irreversibility of advanced HAVS, prevention is the only cure. Control strategies must be hierarchical, prioritizing engineering over personal protective equipment (PPE).

7.1 Engineering and Administrative Controls

  • Source Reduction: The most effective control is the selection of low-vibration tools. This requires looking beyond the ISO-weighted value and considering the unweighted frequency spectrum. Tools with dominant frequencies in the 100–300 Hz range should be avoided or damped.
  • Damping: Viscoelastic materials on handles can reduce high-frequency transmissibility. However, increasing handle diameter can increase the required grip force, which, as noted in the biodynamics section, increases impedance and injury risk. A balance must be struck.
  • Work Cycles: Reducing trigger time is essential. Because the biological response (e.g., oxidative stress) is cumulative, breaks allow for reperfusion of ischemic tissues and replenishment of antioxidant reserves.

7.2 The Biodynamics of Anti-Vibration (AV) Gloves

The utility of AV gloves is a subject of nuanced debate, often misunderstood due to the frequency-dependence of their performance.

  • High-Frequency Attenuation: Certified AV gloves (ISO 10819) are generally effective at attenuating frequencies above 150 Hz. Since this report has identified the 100–500 Hz band as critical for VWF and Pacinian injury, AV gloves do offer meaningful protection against these specific mechanisms.18
  • Low-Frequency Amplification: AV gloves typically provide no attenuation below 30 Hz and may even amplify vibration due to the resonance of the glove material itself (a "mass-spring" system on the hand).43 For low-frequency tools like rammers, gloves are biodynamically useless or detrimental.
  • The Thermal Factor: Perhaps the most critical function of gloves is thermal insulation. Keeping the hands warm prevents the background vasoconstriction that exacerbates vibration ischemia. Warm tissue is also more compliant (lower stiffness), potentially reducing the mechanical stress on the cells.44

7.3 Medical Surveillance Strategy

A robust surveillance program must be frequency-aware.

  • Early Detection: Relying on workers to report "whiteness" is too late. Periodic testing of VPT at 125 Hz and 500 Hz is the most effective way to detect early, reversible physiological changes.32
  • Tiered Screening: Annual questionnaires should be supplemented by objective clinical testing for anyone reporting symptoms. The use of the Stockholm Workshop Scale allows for the standardized tracking of progression.

8. Conclusion

This deep research analysis culminates in a unified theory of Hand-Arm Vibration Syndrome that bridges the gap between physics and pathology. HAVS is not a monolithic entity but a frequency-specific cascade of biological failures.

The low frequencies (<25 Hz) pass through the hand to assault the joints and proximal structures of the arm. The high frequencies (100–500 Hz), however, are the true silent killers of the hand. They are trapped by the resonance of the fingers, amplified by the mechanics of the grip, and absorbed by the very tissues—Pacinian corpuscles and endothelial cells—that are most vulnerable to them.

The identification of molecular mechanisms like the Piezo1-NF-κB axis provides the "missing link" that explains how mechanical energy is transduced into chronic vascular inflammation. Meanwhile, the biodynamic data on finger resonance exposes the critical flaw in current ISO 5349 regulations, which systematically undervalue the hazard of high-frequency vibration.

The path forward requires a paradigm shift: from measuring "shaking" to measuring "energy absorption"; from minimizing ISO numbers to minimizing high-frequency resonance; and from treating HAVS as a mystery of occupational medicine to managing it as a preventable failure of bio-mechanical interface design. The "Giant Read" of the evidence is clear: the frequencies we ignore are the ones that hurt us most.

Appendix: Key Equations of Hand-Arm Biodynamics

1. Mechanical Impedance ($Z$):

$$Z(f) = \frac{F(f)}{v(f)}$$

Where $F(f)$ is the dynamic force and $v(f)$ is the velocity at frequency $f$. A higher $Z$ implies greater resistance to motion and higher force transmission for a given velocity.

2. Vibration Power Absorption (VPA):

$$P_{abs} = \text{Re}\left[ F(f) \cdot v^*(f) \right]$$

Where $v^(f)$ is the complex conjugate of velocity. This represents the real energy dissipated in the tissue per second (Joules/sec or Watts).*

3. ISO 5349 Frequency-Weighted Acceleration ($a_{hv}$):

$$a_{hv} = \sqrt{\sum_{i} (W_{h,i} \cdot a_i)^2}$$

Where $a_i$ is the r.m.s. acceleration in the $i$-th one-third octave band, and $W_{h,i}$ is the weighting factor for that band. Note that $W_{h,i}$ decreases by half for every doubling of frequency above 16 Hz.
