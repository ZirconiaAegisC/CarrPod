# Mantle Customer Pipeline — Automotive Sector

## Market Opportunity

The automotive tooling market represents Mantle's largest addressable revenue
opportunity. A single vehicle program requires 2000 to 5000 injection molds
for interior, exterior, and under-hood plastic components. The US automotive
tooling market exceeds 2 billion USD annually, dominated by Tier 1 suppliers
and captive toolrooms at the major OEMs. Mantle targets the rapid prototyping,
bridge tooling, and low-to-medium volume production segments where speed-to-market
and tooling cost reduction create the strongest value proposition.

## Key Customer: General Motors

### Relationship Overview

General Motors represents Mantle's most strategically important automotive
customer. GM's tooling organization, which manages thousands of molds across
dozens of vehicle programs, began evaluating Mantle technology for interior
trim components in 2021 and has since expanded usage across multiple programs.

### Application Profile

GM's Mantle insert programs focus on:

- **Interior trim**: Dashboard bezels, center console components, door handle
  surrounds, speaker grilles
- **Under-hood components**: Wire harness clips, fluid reservoir caps, sensor
  housings, and bracket components
- **Low-volume programs**: Tooling for special edition vehicles, performance
  variants, and commercial fleet-specific trim packages where traditional
  tooling amortization over low volumes is uneconomical

Materials are predominantly H13 for production tooling and P20 for prototype
and bridge applications. Insert sizes range from small connector housings
(30 mm by 20 mm) to large trim panel inserts (200 mm by 150 mm).

### Value Drivers for GM

GM's primary value drivers for Mantle adoption include:

- **Program acceleration**: Reducing tooling lead time from 8-16 weeks to
  1-2 weeks compresses the overall vehicle development timeline by 4 to 8
  weeks, enabling late-stage design changes without delaying launch
- **Bridge tooling economics**: For low-volume programs (5000 to 50000 units
  annually), Mantle inserts at 2500 to 5000 USD per insert are 60 to 80
  percent less expensive than conventionally manufactured tooling
- **Iterative design**: Rapid insert turnaround enables 3 to 5 design-test-
  refine cycles during development, improving part quality at launch while
  reducing total development tooling spend
- **Supply chain resilience**: Onshoring tooling production to Mantle's US
  facility reduces dependence on overseas tooling sources that were disrupted
  during recent global supply chain events

### Production Validation

GM has completed extensive production validation of FMP inserts across
multiple molding materials including ABS, polypropylene, glass-filled nylon,
and TPO. Results demonstrate equivalent or better performance compared with
conventional tooling in terms of dimensional stability, surface finish
retention, and tool life through production runs of 100000 to 500000 cycles.

## Broader Automotive Pipeline

Mantle's automotive customer pipeline extends beyond GM to encompass:

### Tier 1 Suppliers

Major Tier 1 suppliers are evaluating Mantle technology for:

- **Interior systems**: Instrument panels, door panels, center consoles
  from suppliers such as Faurecia, Yanfeng, and Magna
- **Exterior systems**: Bumper fascias, grilles, trim from suppliers such
  as Plastic Omnium and SRG Global
- **Powertrain**: Air intake manifolds, engine covers, fluid reservoirs from
  suppliers such as MAHLE and MANN+HUMMEL

Tier 1 relationships are structured similarly to the OEM approach, with
benchmark projects progressing to pilot programs and eventual enterprise
deployment. However, Tier 1 customers often move faster than OEMs because
their tooling procurement is decentralized to individual program teams
with P&L accountability for tooling cost and timing.

### Electric Vehicle Programs

EV manufacturers and their suppliers represent a growth vector for Mantle:

- **New entrants**: EV startups without legacy tooling infrastructure are
  more open to innovative tooling approaches than established OEMs with
  entrenched processes
- **Program velocity**: EV programs typically run on compressed development
  timelines of 24 to 36 months versus 36 to 48 months for conventional
  vehicles, creating greater demand for rapid tooling
- **Lower volumes**: Initial EV production volumes of 10000 to 50000 units
  annually make bridge tooling economics particularly attractive
- **Component proliferation**: EVs introduce new plastic-intensive components —
  battery enclosures, high-voltage connector housings, thermal management
  components — that require new tooling programs

Mantle has active engagements with multiple EV manufacturers, though these
relationships are at earlier stages than the established GM partnership.

## Tool and Die Industry Partnership Strategy

Mantle positions the P-200 not as a replacement for tool and die shops but
as a productivity tool that enables existing shops to increase throughput
and accept more work. The company's partnership strategy includes:

- **Job shop program**: P-200 systems placed at independent tool and die
  shops that serve as Mantle-certified production partners for customers
  who prefer to outsource insert production rather than operate their own
  P-200 systems
- **Cooperative marketing**: Mantle-certified shops receive lead referrals
  from Mantle's sales team for customers in their geographic region
- **Capacity buffer**: The network of job shops provides Mantle with a
  scalable production capacity buffer that can absorb demand spikes without
  requiring capital investment in additional company-owned production
  facilities

This partnership model addresses the concern that Mantle's technology might
threaten traditional tool and die businesses. By enabling these shops to
increase revenue per employee and accept more programs than their skilled
workforce constraints would otherwise allow, Mantle positions the P-200 as
a force multiplier rather than a competitive threat.
